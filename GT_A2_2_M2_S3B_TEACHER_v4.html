§1 Warm-Up
No warm-up today — Speak NOW
8–10 MIN
🚀 Production starts now. Tell your partner a real problem from your work. Your partner advises using should / shouldn't / could. You have 60 seconds. Go.
⚡ Switch! After 60 seconds, roles reverse. Partner B describes a problem. Partner A advises. No notes. No planning. Just speak.
📌 Teacher setup: This warm-up sets the pace for the whole session. Tell students clearly: "Today there are no notes, no tables, no supports. You speak — I listen. Mistakes are fine. Stopping is not." The discomfort IS the lesson. Move quickly into Interview Simulations.
🎯 S3B Mindset: S3A gave you the tools. S3B gives you real pressure. In meetings, job interviews, and client calls — nobody waits for you to remember the grammar rule. They need your advice NOW. This session trains that reflex.

🎬 How to run Simulations:
(1) Assign pairs. (2) ONE student plays the PROBLEM — describes the situation. (3) Other student is the ADVISOR — gives 4+ pieces of advice using should / shouldn't / could. (4) NO notes, NO planning. Just speak. (5) Don't interrupt for grammar errors — mark them silently. (6) After 2 min: SWITCH roles. (7) Move fast. One simulation every 3 minutes maximum.

Extended Tasks
Longer Narratives · No Notes
20–25 MIN

Students speak continuously for 2–4 minutes. No written supports. Timers below each task.

Extended Task 1 · 2 minutes · Career Coach
You are a career coach. A nervous person arrives before a big interview. Give them 5–6 pieces of advice: preparation, mindset, clothing, during the interview, and what to do after. Use should / shouldn't / could for each. No notes.
💡 Opener: "Congratulations on the interview. Here's my advice…" — then keep talking. Partner can ask follow-up questions to keep you going.
2:00
Extended Task 2 · 2 minutes · Real Story
Tell the story of a time you gave someone important advice at work. What was the problem? What did you say (use should / shouldn't / could)? Why was it good advice? What happened in the end? Speak naturally — 2 minutes minimum.
💡 This is real from YOUR life. No perfect grammar needed — just tell the story. Past tense + advice structures mixed naturally is fine.
2:00
Extended Task 3 · 3–4 minutes ⭐ CHALLENGE · Speech
You're speaking at a company event: "How to Succeed in Your First Year." Cover: (1) What you should prepare before starting, (2) mistakes you shouldn't make, (3) who you could talk to for help, (4) one difficult situation and your advice, (5) what comes next. No notes. A classmate can interrupt with questions.
💡 You're on stage. Your audience is new employees. You're the expert. Mistakes? Keep going. Pauses? Keep going. Stop only when time is up.
3:30
🎙️ Extended Task Rules:
(1) Student speaks WITHOUT notes. (2) Use the timer button — it counts down visibly. (3) Partner or teacher can ask questions to extend. (4) FLUENCY over accuracy. Mistakes → keep talking. (5) Record if possible — students can listen back. (6) This is the closest activity to real professional speaking in English.
Model Listening
How Advice Sounds Naturally
8–10 MIN

Listen to 2 native speakers giving advice. Notice: natural flow, pauses, connecting words, confidence.

Model 1 — Career Coach Before an Interview
Speaker: Emma (British English) · Speed 0.85 · ElevenLabs
"Right, so you're interviewing next week. Here's my advice. You should definitely prepare examples from your past work — that's non-negotiable. Because interviewers want to see how you actually think and behave, not just what you say. You should also research the company thoroughly. Look at their news, products, and culture. You shouldn't go in blind. You could also prepare three or four questions to ask them about the role — that shows you're interested and professional.

On the day itself, you shouldn't stay up late the night before. Sleep is crucial. You need your brain sharp. You could wear formal business clothes or smart casual — depends on the company — but you shouldn't wear jeans. During the interview, listen carefully and ask if something isn't clear. You should make eye contact and speak slowly. After the interview, you should send a short thank-you email within 24 hours. And finally — you shouldn't stress too much. You've prepared. You're ready."
📎 ElevenLabs: Emma · British English · Speed 0.85 · Pitch 1.0 · A2.2 M2 S3B
Model 2 — Manager Advising a Stressed Employee
Speaker: David (American English) · Speed 0.82 · ElevenLabs
"I can see you're stressed, and I want to help. First: you shouldn't try to do everything at once. That's just not possible. You should prioritize. Pick the three most important things on your list today and focus only on those. Let the rest wait.

You could delegate some tasks. Who on the team could take something off your plate? You could also contact clients early and ask for more time — they often say yes when you communicate proactively. You should definitely talk to me about your workload. Don't just accept everything silently. That's not sustainable.

You shouldn't work late every night. I need you healthy and focused, not burned out. You could take a day off this week — I'm serious. Rest. And finally: you should think about whether this role fits your current life. If something needs to change, we can talk about it. What would help most right now?"
📎 ElevenLabs: David · American English · Speed 0.82 · Pitch 1.0 · A2.2 M2 S3B
👂 What to notice:
(1) Always base verb after should/shouldn't/could — "should prepare" NEVER "should to prepare" · (2) Natural thinking sounds ("Right...", "And finally...") · (3) Connecting words: because / also / and / but / finally · (4) They give REASONS — not just "you should do X" but "you should do X because Y" · (5) Mix of structures — they don't repeat the same one 5 times
Session Assessment
Can You Give Advice?
10 MIN
🎯 Final Speaking Task — 5 Minutes · No Notes
Choose a REAL situation: a job interview, a difficult client, too much work, a career decision, or something from your own life. Advise the other person naturally for 5 minutes. Teacher or partner marks the rubric below.
✓
Grammar accuracy — 40%
Should/shouldn't/could + base verb. No "should to"? No "-ed" forms? Every instance counts.
✓
Vocabulary & reasons — 20%
Do you use career/advice words from this module? Do you explain WHY you give each piece of advice?
✓
Fluency & natural flow — 25%
Do you speak continuously? Connect ideas naturally? Sound like you believe your own advice?
✓
Communication effectiveness — 15%
Would someone actually follow this advice? Did you cover should, shouldn't, AND could?
5:00
📋 Scoring Guide:
90–100% — Confident, accurate, natural. Uses all 3 structures. Gives reasons. · 80–89% — Accurate grammar, good fluency, 3+ pieces of advice. · 70–79% — Some grammar errors but speaks continuously. Idea is clear. · Below 70% — Review base verb rule next session. This is the #1 priority.
