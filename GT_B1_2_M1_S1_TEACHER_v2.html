In Mexico, warmth and relationship-building come first. In international contexts, directness wins. Which approach have you been using? Does it work?

"You only get one chance to make a first impression." Is this still true in a hybrid / remote world? What's changed?
💡 B1.2 push: argue a position, then concede something. No simple answers — qualify, nuance, push back.

§ 04
Grammar — Present Perfect Continuous
have/has + been + verb-ing · Ongoing actions with emotional weight · B1.2 precision
04
12 min
⏳
Ongoing Duration
"I've been working on my executive presence for months."
"She's been dealing with this since January."
"We've been trying to solve it for weeks."
💡
Explaining a Current Situation
"He looks exhausted — he's been managing three projects alone."
"She seems tense — the team has been questioning her credibility."
❓
Questions (PPC)
"How long have you been feeling this way?"
"Have you been noticing the same thing?"
"What has she been projecting in meetings?"
⚠️
PPC vs PP Simple
✅ "She's been managing" = STILL doing it now, ongoing process
✅ "She's managed" = experience, result — may be complete
⚠️ The difference is DURATION + EMOTIONAL WEIGHT
Present Perfect — Simple
"I've worked here for 2 years."
Neutral fact — duration without ongoing implication
"She's managed difficult clients."
Experience — general, may be past
"He's been promoted twice."
Result — the promotion is the focus
Present Perfect Continuous
"I've been working here for 2 years."
Still happening NOW + emotional weight or context
"She's been managing difficult clients lately."
Ongoing situation — implies it's still a challenge
"He's been trying to get promoted."
Process — not done yet, still in progress
💡 The key rule: "I've read his file." = completed, result matters. "I've been reading his file." = ongoing or recently ongoing — implies I'm still forming a view, or the process took significant effort. Choose based on whether the ACTION or the RESULT is your focus.
⚠️ Most Common B1.2 Errors
❌ "I've been work here"→✅ I've been working here (need -ing)
❌ "She's been manage the situation since"→✅ She's been managing the situation since…
❌ "How long you have been dealing?"→✅ How long have you been dealing with this?
❌ "I've been working here since 3 months"→✅ I've been working here for 3 months
✏️ Activity 1 — Choose the Correct PPC Form
Select the answer that uses Present Perfect Continuous correctly.
The team _____ under a lot of pressure since the new director arrived.
He looks exhausted — he _____ all three client accounts alone since March.
Which sentence uses PPC correctly to explain a current state?
How long _____ you _____ that he was wrong about you?
✏️ Activity 2 — True or False
Is this statement about PPC correct?
"I've been reading his file" implies the process is still ongoing or recently unfinished — more emotional weight than "I've read it."
"She's been managing" and "she's managed" are interchangeable — both mean the same thing.
"How long have you been feeling this way?" is a correct PPC question — auxiliary have comes before subject.
"I've been work there" is correct because the verb follows "been."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q3 is the most important — student must identify why the PPC version is better: "it's starting to show" = the ongoing process has a visible current result. This is the B1.2 nuance target.
  • T2 is the critical misconception: "she's managed" = completed experience, no emotional continuity. "she's been managing" = still happening, explains current state. This distinction defines B1.2 vs B1.1 grammar precision.
  • After activities: speed round. Teacher gives a context, student produces PPC in 8 seconds. "She looks overwhelmed →" / "He's been late 3 weeks →" / "The team is exhausted →"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Speed round: I give you a situation, you explain it with PPC in one sentence. 8 seconds. Ready?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In M2, Reported Speech adds a new layer: 'She said she had been managing it alone.' Same PPC — one tense back. Today's foundation makes that natural."
§ 05
Reading — The 30-Second Executive
A professional reflects on what she's been projecting — and what she's been getting wrong
05
8–10 min
Valentina Cruz
Senior Director · NorTech Solutions · Guadalajara
LinkedIn Article
Watch for: PPC (green) · PP Simple (blue) · S1 vocabulary (highlighted) · Colloquial phrases (bold)

For years, I have been projecting something I thought was confidence. I'm not gonna lie — I only realised recently that what I have actually been projecting is urgency. Two very different things.

I have been arriving to meetings early, speaking first, filling silences. I thought that was professionalism. What the room has been reading — I found out from a coach — is anxiety. It's a bit much, she told me. Fair enough.

The problem is authenticity. I have never felt completely natural in high-stakes environments. So I have been performing a version of myself that has been working against me. The gap between what I project and what people read has been growing for months.

What has changed since working with the coach? I have been practising pausing before I respond. I have been deliberately not filling every silence. And something interesting has happened — the room has been giving me more space. My credibility has been increasing not because I say more, but because I say less. Honestly though — that took me three years to learn.

✏️ Comprehension — PPC Focus
Answer using information from Valentina's article.
What has Valentina been projecting — and what has the room actually been reading?
What specific behaviour has she been practising with her coach?
"The gap has been growing for months" — PPC is used here because the problem is ongoing, not finished.
Valentina says credibility increased because she started saying MORE in meetings.
GT Originals — The Cultural Context
Valentina's situation is extremely common for Mexican professionals operating in international contexts. The tendency to fill silences, speak first, and demonstrate urgency is often a cultural reflex — in Mexican professional culture, it signals energy and commitment. In US/UK corporate settings, it can read as insecurity. The lesson: adapting isn't betraying yourself. It's code-switching — something bilingual professionals have been doing in Spanish their entire careers.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Count PPC instances: 10+. Ask student to identify them before answering comprehension questions.
  • RQ2 precision: the answer must include BOTH elements — pausing AND not filling silences. If only one: "Can you be more specific? What two things has she been practising?"
  • After comprehension: "Tell me Valentina's story in your own words — 60 seconds, no looking. Use PPC throughout."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Valentina says: 'I say less and get more space.' Does that match your experience — or the opposite? Have you been filling silences more than you should in English?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Valentina's article is the model for §7's speaking challenge. Her PPC story structure — 'I've been doing X → which has been causing Y → I've been changing it → Z has been happening' — is what 2 minutes of B1.2 speaking sounds like."

Executive Coaching — First Impressions Audit
B1.2 · M1 · S1 · Natural pace · ~90 sec · Dense PPC · American English

🔈 Audio placeholder. Replace with Google Drive embed.
ElevenLabs: Sarah · Rate: 0.88 · Stability: 0.62 · Natural conversational pace

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

I've been working with executives on first impressions for over fifteen years. And the pattern I've been seeing most consistently lately is this: people have been confusing energy with presence.

The client I've been working with for the past two months — let's call him R — has been struggling with something very specific. He's been talking too much in meetings. Not because he has nothing to say — he's been leading a team of twelve people effectively for years. It's that he hasn't been reading the room. He's been filling every pause. He's been interpreting silence as failure.

What we've been doing in our sessions is practising what I call "earning the room." You don't own the room by talking the most. You earn it by saying things that land. By letting your credibility build without announcing it.

After six weeks, R's been doing something I've been wanting to see from the beginning: he's been pausing before he responds. And the team has been responding to that differently. His first impression has been shifting. Not because his skills changed — they haven't. Because the way he's been projecting them changed.

⏱ Target: 85–95 sec · PPC density: I've been working / I've been seeing / he's been struggling / he's been talking / he hasn't been reading / he's been filling / we've been doing / he's been pausing / the team has been responding / has been shifting / he's been projecting

✏️ Listening Activities — PPC Identification
Listen and answer. Focus on which PPC forms you hear — not just content.
What has the coach been seeing as the most consistent pattern in executives?
What change has the coach been noticing in R after 6 weeks?
"He's been filling every pause" — PPC is used because this is an ongoing pattern, not a one-time action.
The coach says R's skills changed completely after the coaching sessions.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Focus task: "Count the 'has been / have been' sentences. Answer: 11 PPC instances in 90 seconds." This is the density benchmark for B1.2 professional English.
  • L2 distinction: the coach says explicitly "not because his skills changed — they haven't." Student must catch this negation. If they say "his skills improved" → "Listen again from 'After six weeks…'"
  • After activities: student plays the coach. They describe a real professional in their life using PPC: "He's been… She hasn't been… The team has been…" 60 seconds.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "The coach says 'you earn the room by saying things that land.' Have you been doing this in your professional life — or have you been filling pauses too?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Homework: listen 3×. Write every PPC sentence. "11 in 90 seconds — that's the density of professional B1.2 English on a specific topic."
