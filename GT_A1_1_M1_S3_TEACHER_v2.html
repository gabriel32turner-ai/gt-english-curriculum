Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Expected vocabulary: any job title + "to be". Accept all attempts — activation only, no correction yet.
  • Q3 targets possessives (his/her) — note which form they use. If wrong, don't correct now: "Great — we'll perfect that in §4."
  • If student can't name a job: point to vocab cards in §2. Preview is fine here.
  • Timing: max 7 min. Keep it conversational and light.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "You already know how to describe jobs — today we make the grammar perfect."
  • If student uses "a engineer" — smile and note it: "Almost! We'll nail that in grammar today."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Your job title in English is the first thing an international contact reads in your email signature. Today we get it exactly right."

§ 04
Grammar — A/An + Possessives
12–15 min
Rule 1 — A / AN before job titles
Use A before a consonant sound: a manager · a doctor · a teacher · a lawyer · a designer.
Use AN before a vowel sound: an engineer · an accountant · an assistant.
❌ I am a engineer.→✅ I am an engineer.
❌ She is an manager.→✅ She is a manager.
Rule 2 — Possessive Adjectives
My
I → my
"My name is Ana."
"My job is coordinator."
Your
You → your
"What is your job?"
"Your company is big."
His
He → his
"His name is Carlos."
"His company is Toyota."
Her
She → her
"Her name is Ana."
"Her job is director."
❌ Ana is a director. His job is in Celaya.→✅ Her job is in Celaya. (she → her)
Activity — Choose the correct word
Click the correct option to complete each sentence.
She is ___ engineer in our company.
What is ___ job? Are you a manager? (asking the student)
Carlos is ___ assistant. (Carlos = he)
___ name is Ana. She is a coordinator.
I work at Grupo Norte. ___ company is in Celaya.
Robert is the manager. ___ department is Sales. (Robert = he)
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Most common error: his/her confusion. Students apply Spanish logic (the gender of the object, not the person). Reinforce: "his = he, her = she. Always follow the person."
  • After activity: student makes 2 sentences about real colleagues using his/her. Personalisation seals the rule.
  • A/AN: if student still errors here, go back to §3 and flip 2 more cards together.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Every professional LinkedIn profile uses these exact words. You're writing real business English right now."
  • When student gets all 6 correct: "You just mastered two grammar points in one session. That's A2 territory."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "A/AN and possessives stay forever — every tense, every level. One rule, unlimited application."
  • Upsell: "In A1.2 you'll use these to talk about your daily routine: 'My morning starts at 7. Her team meeting is at 9.'"
§ 05
Reading
8–10 min
Read · ~90 words · A1.1 level

My name is Ana García. I am a coordinator at Grupo Norte. My company is in Celaya. It is a manufacturing company.

My manager is Carlos Ramírez. He is a director. His office is on the third floor. He is not from Celaya — his city is Monterrey.

My colleague is Mariana. She is an accountant. Her department is Finance. Tom is also in our team. He is an engineer from Canada. His Spanish is very good!

We are a great team at Grupo Norte. What is your job?

Comprehension
Read the text. Answer the questions.
1. Ana is a director.
2. Carlos's office is on the third floor.
3. Mariana is an engineer.
What is Ana's job?
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Ask student to find all possessives in the text: "his office", "his city", "her department", "his Spanish". Count them together (4).
  • Ask student to find all A/AN uses: "a coordinator", "a director", "an accountant", "an engineer". Count (4).
  • Reading aloud: student reads one paragraph at a time. Correct A/AN mispronunciations only.
  • Q1 is a trap: Ana is a coordinator, not a director (Carlos is the director). Many students miss this.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Does Ana's team look like yours? Tell me one difference and one similarity."
  • "Tom is Canadian but his Spanish is very good — just like you'll speak English very well soon."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Imagine sending an email like this — professional, clear, and all in English. That's your S5 speaking challenge."

Wrap-Up

  • I use A before consonants, AN before vowels.

Key Summary
A / AN Rule
A + consonant: a manager, a doctor
AN + vowel: an engineer, an accountant
Possessives
My · Your · His (he) · Her (she)
Remember
His/Her follows the person, not the thing. Ana → her. Carlos → his.
