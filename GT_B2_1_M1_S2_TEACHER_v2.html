Warm-Up · S2 Rotation

This or That

Instant opinions on feedback scenarios — no right answers, 30-second justifications

⏱ 10 min
This or That: Feedback Edition
S2 Rotation — all 8 cards are new. Students choose a side instantly and defend in 30–60 sec. Fluency activation only — no grammar correction here.
10:00
Warm-Up
Teacher Instructions

Read each card aloud. Students signal immediately — "this" or "that." Call on 2–3 per card for a quick justification. Move fast: 70–80 seconds per card maximum. Energy, not depth.

CARD 01
When a colleague makes a mistake at work, what's better?
CARD 02
When you receive negative feedback, your instinct is to…
CARD 03
Giving feedback to your boss — you would…
CARD 04
Which feedback channel is more effective at work?
CARD 05
Negative feedback should be delivered…
CARD 06
Which type of feedback is actually more useful?
CARD 07
Honest feedback, even if it hurts, is…
CARD 08
The ideal piece of feedback is…
🔴 Facilitation
🟡 Challenges
🟢 Extension
  • Fluency activation only — grammar correction during This or That is counterproductive. Save it for the Grammar section.
  • Cards 03 and 07 carry the most cultural weight. In Mexican corporate culture, many students default to "stay quiet." Don't push back — note it as data for the Debate.
  • Bridge to the session topic after Card 08: "Today we go deeper into exactly these moments — when feedback is uncomfortable, necessary, and complicated."
  • S2 Rotation: all 8 cards are new. Confirm with the group if they completed S1 previously.
  • Students can't decide: Prompt — "If you had 3 seconds, no thinking — which one?" B2.1 students sometimes over-analyze binary options.
  • Justifications stay abstract: Push — "Give me a real example. Has this actually happened to you at work?"
  • One student dominates: Freeze technique — stop them mid-sentence, ask another student to finish the thought.
  • Card 05 creates conflict: Good. Let it breathe 10 extra seconds. Public vs. private disagreement is a natural bridge to the Debate motion.
  • Ask advanced students to argue for the side they personally disagree with — direct preparation for Debate format.
  • After Card 07: "Think of a moment when honest feedback — even painful — actually changed the outcome. What would have happened if it hadn't been given?" — seeds Third Conditional thinking before the Grammar section.

Vocabulary · Approved List

10 + 10

10 nouns · 10 verbs · feedback lexical set — click any word to reveal definition

⏱ 10 min
Vocabulary: Approved Set
Teacher-paced. Click each word to reveal its contextual definition. In Student Mode, definitions are hidden for controlled reveal during class.
10:00
Vocabulary
Nouns (10)
feedbackInformation given to someone to help them improve performance or behavior
toneThe emotional quality or attitude in the way you communicate
reactionHow someone responds — emotionally or behaviorally — to what they hear
patternA repeated behavior or situation that becomes noticeable over time
timingChoosing the right moment to say or do something for maximum effect
gapThe distance between what was expected and what actually happened
strengthA positive quality or skill that someone demonstrates consistently at work
outcomeThe result that follows from an action, decision, or conversation
approachThe method or general strategy used to handle a situation
tensionStress or discomfort between people caused by an unresolved issue
Verbs (10)
addressTo deal with a problem or topic directly rather than avoiding it
avoidTo deliberately stay away from a difficult conversation or situation
challengeTo respectfully question or push back on someone's idea or approach
deliverTo communicate feedback in a structured, intentional, and effective way
improveTo become better as a direct result of feedback, practice, or reflection
frameTo present a message in a way that shapes how it will be received
reactTo respond emotionally or instinctively to something you hear or see
acknowledgeTo recognize and accept what someone has said, done, or felt
redirectTo shift a conversation toward a more constructive or productive direction
follow upTo check back after a feedback conversation to ensure change is happening
Activation Activities
Activity A — Definition Race (3 min)

Teacher reads a definition aloud. First student to call out the correct word scores a point. After 5 rounds, switch — a student reads, teacher or class guesses.

Sample Sequence

"The emotional quality in your voice when you speak…" → tone

"To present a message in a way that influences how it lands…" → frame

"The distance between what was expected and what actually happened…" → gap

Activity B — Sentence Builders (4 min)

Each student makes ONE sentence connecting a noun + a verb from the list in a real work context. Challenge: include a third word as a bonus.

Target Example — B2.1

"The timing of the feedback completely changed how he reacted — there was no tension at all after that."

Activity C — Personalization Round (3 min)

Ask each student: "Which three words from the list best describe your personal experience giving or receiving feedback at work?" — 30 seconds per student. No judgment, maximum honesty.

🔴 Facilitation
🟡 Challenges
🟢 Extension
  • Click-to-reveal is teacher-paced. In Student Mode, definitions are hidden — keep student screens face-down during presentation phase.
  • Priority verbs: frame, address, redirect, follow up — these appear in Grammar practice and the Register Switch. Spend extra time here.
  • Follow up = two words as a phrasal verb. One word ("followup") is the noun. State this once, explicitly.
  • Time target: Activity A = 3 min / B = 4 min / C = 3 min. Do not exceed 10 min total.
  • "Approach" vs. "method": Approach = general angle/style. Method = specific technique. "We could change our approach without changing the method."
  • "Gap": Reinforce with — "A performance gap — the space between expected and actual results." Use hand gesture (two palms, pull apart).
  • "Tension" vs. "stress": Tension is interpersonal — lives between people. Stress is individual — lives inside you. Quick mime works well.
  • "Redirect": Clarify the conversational meaning vs. the IT/URL meaning.
  • Collocations challenge: "What words go with 'feedback'? Constructive / actionable / unsolicited / timely?" — extends range beyond the approved list.
  • Grammar bridge: ask strong students to use a vocab word in the past perfect — "I had avoided giving feedback for months because I didn't know how to frame it."
04
Listening · Natural Monologue

Talking Up

Giving feedback to someone higher in the hierarchy — a real, challenging voice

⏱ 10 min
"Talking Up" — Audio Monologue
Challenging rhythm. First-person authentic register. B2.1 comprehension in 3 task stages. Web Speech API — use Chrome for best voice quality.
10:00
Listening
Pre-Listening Activation (1 min — no prep)

"Has anyone here ever wanted to tell their manager something honest — and held back? What happened as a result?" Take 2–3 quick answers. Do NOT pre-teach vocabulary. Let the monologue speak first.

Script · "Talking Up"

So — here's the thing nobody tells you when you first join a team: giving feedback upward? It is one of the most politically loaded things you can do. I've watched careers stall, relationships fall apart, entire projects derail — just because someone either said the wrong thing, or — more often — didn't say anything at all.

I had a situation, about two years in at this company. My department head had this pattern — she'd change the brief at the last minute. Every single time. And no one said anything, because, you know... tension. Hierarchy. You just adapt, right? So eventually, I decided to address it. I didn't go in accusatory. I framed it as a question: "I've noticed our timelines tend to shift late in the process — is there a way we could flag those changes earlier?" And she looked at me — and I honestly couldn't tell if she was about to thank me or reassign me.

Turns out, she actually appreciated it. But here's what I've learned since then: tone is everything. Timing is everything. The way you frame something determines whether it lands as a gift or a threat. Most people go straight to thinking about the reaction. That's backwards. Delivery comes first. Always.

Speed: 0.86×
Comprehension Tasks
Task 1 — First Listen: Gist (2 min)
Question for Students

"What is the speaker's main argument? Summarize it in one sentence."

Target: Giving feedback upward is risky — but whether it works depends almost entirely on how you deliver it (tone, timing, framing), not on the content itself.

Task 2 — Second Listen: Detail (3 min)

Play again at 0.78× speed. Students answer:

Comprehension Questions
  • What was the specific recurring problem the speaker noticed with her department head?
  • How did she decide to raise the issue? What language did she use exactly?
  • What three things does the speaker say are "everything" when giving feedback?
  • What mistake does she say most people make?
Answers
  • The manager changed the brief at the last minute — every single time. A recurring pattern.
  • Framed as a question: "I've noticed our timelines tend to shift late in the process — is there a way we could flag those changes earlier?"
  • Tone, timing, and the way you frame something.
  • Thinking about the reaction before thinking about the delivery — she says that's backwards.
Task 3 — Speaking Response (3 min)
Discussion Prompt

"The speaker says: 'Delivery comes first. Always.' Do you agree? In your company, is giving upward feedback expected, accepted — or avoided?"

Push for vocabulary use — each student uses at least 2 words from the approved list. This is the natural bridge to the Third Conditional: when students say "If she had said it differently..." — capture those moments as grammar hooks.

🔴 Facilitation
🟡 Challenges
🟢 Extension
  • Rate target: 0.86× for First Listen (natural but challenging), 0.78× for Second Listen (Task 2). Never use 1.0× — Web Speech synthesis sounds robotic at full speed.
  • Highlighted words in the script are session vocabulary. Use as recognition bridges: "Did you catch how she used 'framed' there? That's exactly the word we practiced."
  • First-person script creates strong identification: "Has anyone here been in this exact situation? Who was in the role of the department head?"
  • Task 3 is the grammar bridge. When students produce "If she had..." — write it on the board: "That's exactly where we're going next."
  • "Frame" as a verb is missed: Used twice. After the second listen, point to both instances and ask students to use it in a sentence.
  • Speed complaints: Validate without lowering the bar — "Yes — this is real-world speed. In your job, native speakers aren't slowing down for you. We're training for that."
  • Task 1 answers too vague: Prompt — "More specific — what does she say about delivery versus the content of the message?"
  • Ask strong students to reconstruct the exact framing question the speaker used — then generate their own version for a real situation from their workplace.
  • Third listen (if time): Play at 1.0× — students focus only on where the speaker pauses and why. Builds prosodic awareness.
05
Grammar · Third Conditional

If You Hadn't...

Negative forms & question forms — past hypotheticals in real feedback scenarios

⏱ 10 min
Third Conditional: Neg + Questions
Structures students rarely produce but hear constantly in professional English. Priority: oral production, not written exercises.
10:00
Grammar
Form A · Negative Third Conditional
If + subject + hadn't + past participle, subject + wouldn't have + past participle
If you hadn't avoided that conversation, the tension wouldn't have escalated.
If she hadn't framed it carefully, he wouldn't have reacted so defensively.
If they hadn't followed up, the gap wouldn't have closed at all.
Form B · Question — Would you have...?
Would + subject + have + past participle + if + subject + hadn't + past participle?
Would you have addressed it earlier if you hadn't felt the tension?
Would she have followed up if the outcome hadn't been so poor?
Would the approach have changed if he hadn't challenged it openly?
Form C · Inverted Question — Advanced / Passive Recognition
Hadn't + subject + past participle, would + subject + have + past participle?
Hadn't she addressed the pattern, would the gap have grown?
Formal written / legal register — introduce for passive recognition only at B2.1.
Controlled Practice

Students say the full sentence aloud before you reveal. Click "Show" to check.

1. If you _______ (not / avoid) the conversation, the outcome _______ (be) very different.
If you hadn't avoided the conversation, the outcome would have been very different.
2. _______ you _______ (follow up) if she _______ (not / react) so badly to the feedback?
Would you have followed up if she hadn't reacted so badly to the feedback?
3. If he _______ (not / challenge) the approach, the gap _______ (not / close).
If he hadn't challenged the approach, the gap wouldn't have closed.
4. _______ the tension _______ (escalate) if you _______ (not / redirect) the conversation?
Would the tension have escalated if you hadn't redirected the conversation?
5. If she _______ (not / acknowledge) the pattern, _______ anything _______ (improve)?
If she hadn't acknowledged the pattern, would anything have improved?
6. The outcome _______ (be) better if the manager _______ (not / avoid) delivering the feedback for so long.
The outcome would have been better if the manager hadn't avoided delivering the feedback for so long.
Free Production — Speaking Round (3 min)

Each student recalls a real situation at work involving feedback — given, avoided, or mishandled. They share using the Third Conditional.

Prompt for Students

"Think of a moment when feedback wasn't given — or wasn't delivered well. What would have been different? Use the Third Conditional: negative or question form."

Target: each student produces one negative AND one question form. If students default to affirmative, prompt: "Now say it in the negative — what if it hadn't happened that way?"

🔴 Facilitation
🟡 Challenges
🟢 Extension
  • Link to Listening first: "Remember when she said 'I decided to address it'? What would have happened if she hadn't? That's the Third Conditional."
  • Form A (negative) is the priority. Introduce Form B only after students are solid on A. Form C = recognition only.
  • Write only the structure on the board — don't write the rule: hadn't + PP → wouldn't have + PP. Say the sentences; don't explain them.
  • Practice items 1–6: oral-first always. Written transcription only if time permits.
  • Question word order errors: Students produce "You would have...?" without inversion. Drill: "Would you have... / Would she have..."
  • Confusing 2nd and 3rd Conditional: "Did it actually happen? No → Third Conditional. Is it still possible? No → Third Conditional."
  • "If I would have": Most common B2 error globally. Rule: no "would" ever in the if-clause. Repeat it visually.
  • "Hadn't" contraction: Model the spoken contraction — both full form and contraction are acceptable in writing, but the contraction is natural in speech.
  • Mixed conditional for very strong students: "If you hadn't avoided that conversation, things would be different now." — past cause, present result. Not Third Conditional, but shows range.
  • Form C (inverted) appears in formal business writing and legal documents. Worth mentioning as passive recognition for advanced B2.1 students.

Speaking · Debate Format · S2 Rotation

Debate

Live scoreboard · register switch · structured argumentation on giving feedback upward

⏱ 15 min
Debate: 360° Feedback Policy
Students argue FOR or AGAINST the motion. Scored live using the Debate Scoreboard. Register Switch used after opening statements to reflect on language choices.
15:00
Debate
Debate Motion
"Companies should require all employees to formally evaluate their managers through a mandatory 360-degree feedback policy."
B2.1 · Opinion + Argument + Rebuttal · 15 minutes total
FOR the Motion

360° policy creates accountability, improves leadership, and builds psychological safety across the organization.

Seed Arguments
  • Managers need feedback to recognize blind spots in their own leadership style
  • Accountability flows both ways — reduces abuse of power and creates balance
  • Anonymous systems protect employees who give upward feedback
  • Major companies (Google, Microsoft, Deloitte) already use 360° reviews effectively
AGAINST the Motion

Mandatory upward feedback creates political tension, power imbalances, and cultural resistance — especially in hierarchical environments.

Seed Arguments
  • Employees may not be honest — even anonymously — out of fear of retaliation
  • Junior staff lack perspective to evaluate strategic management decisions
  • Anonymous feedback can be weaponized or used vindictively
  • In hierarchical cultures (including Mexican corporate context) this creates structural tension
Debate Structure — 15 min
  • 2 min — Prep: teams organize 2–3 main arguments with examples
  • 3 min — FOR team opens: main argument + real example or data point
  • 3 min — AGAINST team opens: main argument + real example or data point
  • 4 min — Open rebuttal: each side challenges the other's points directly
  • 2 min — Closing statements: 1 min per side
  • 1 min — Teacher scores final round + announces winner via Scoreboard

Register Switch

Real phrases from real feedback conversations. Toggle each between Formal and Informal to feel the register shift. Use after opening statements — ask students which register they used and why.

Context: Opening a feedback conversation
I'd like to share some observations about the way we're managing the project timeline.
FORMAL
Context: Pointing out a specific problem
Your current approach to this may benefit from some reconsideration.
FORMAL
Context: Requesting a feedback conversation
I'd appreciate the opportunity to discuss your recent performance on this project.
FORMAL
Context: Pushing back on feedback you received
I acknowledge your concerns — however, I'd like to provide some additional context before we proceed.
FORMAL
Context: Following up after a feedback conversation
I wanted to follow up on our previous conversation and assess whether any progress has been made.
FORMAL
Register Switch — Teaching Notes
  • Reveal one phrase at a time. Ask students to predict the informal version before toggling — activates active lexical retrieval.
  • Discussion: "In which situations at your company would you use the formal version? When would informal feel more natural or more authentic?"
  • Key insight: Informal doesn't mean unprofessional. It means reduced social distance. In many Mexican professional contexts, informal register actually reduces tension and increases trust.
  • Bridge to Debate debrief: which register did each student use in their opening argument? Was it intentional? Did it affect how their points landed?
🔴 Facilitation
🟡 Challenges
🟢 Extension
  • Assign debate sides randomly — not based on personal opinion. Forces critical thinking and richer language output.
  • Use the Scoreboard actively and visibly — students perform measurably better when they can see real-time scoring. Point to it. Make it theatrical.
  • Register Switch works best immediately after opening statements — students have just spoken and can reflect on their own language choices while the output is fresh.
  • If Debate runs long, compress Register Switch to 3 phrases. Phrases 1, 2, and 4 are highest-yield.
  • Students argue from opinion, not evidence: Push — "Can you give a specific example? A company? A real situation? Numbers?"
  • Weak rebuttals: Students repeat their own argument instead of responding to the other side. Model: "They said X. But they're not considering Y. And in fact, Z suggests the opposite."
  • Register Switch confusion: Some students perceive informal as "incorrect." Reinforce: both are grammatically correct — they serve different relationship contexts.
  • Post-debate grammar integration: ask each student to produce ONE sentence using Third Conditional + a vocab word — what would have happened if the 360° policy had (or hadn't) been in place at their company.
  • Register Switch extension: challenge advanced students to write a NEW phrase on the same feedback theme in both registers. Compare and discuss.

Wrap-Up

Exit round · global teacher note · preview of S3

⏱ 5 min
Exit Round
Each student completes one prompt using session vocabulary and Third Conditional. Non-negotiable — always run this, even if abbreviated.
5:00
Wrap-Up
Exit Round — Protocol

Each student completes ONE prompt using vocabulary from this session. No repeats — each student picks a different one. No preparation time: they speak immediately.

Exit Prompt A
"Something I would have done differently at work if I hadn't avoided the conversation was..."
Exit Prompt B
"If I hadn't attended this session today, I wouldn't have realized that feedback requires..."
Exit Prompt C
"Would I have addressed that tension with my manager if I'd known the outcome? Honestly..."
Exit Prompt D
"One thing I want to follow up on — either at work or in my English — is..."
Global Feedback Note — Teacher Script

After the exit round, deliver ONE group-level observation — under 45 seconds:

"Today, the group really [STRENGTH — specific, name the language used]. One area to keep working on is [GAP — constructive, not critical]. In the next session, we'll push deeper into [PREVIEW]."

Do not correct individuals at this stage. Make the note collective, specific, and forward-looking.

🟡 Challenges
🟢 Extension
  • The Exit Round is non-negotiable — it's the single most important fluency production moment of the session. If you're running late, cut 2 minutes from the Debate, not from this.
  • If a student freezes: "Just say the first word — 'If you hadn't...' " — the sentence usually follows once the opening is given.
  • The Global Feedback Note must be specific: avoid "Great job today!" Use: "I noticed the group really improved their use of 'frame' and 'redirect' — two words that appeared multiple times in natural production."
  • Everyone picks Prompt D (easiest): Fine — it still generates production. If this happens, assign Prompts A, B, and C to specific students before the round starts.
  • Silence on the exit: Use countdown pressure — "You have 10 seconds to begin." The time constraint usually unlocks output that hesitation blocks.
  • Ask strong students to complete Prompt A or C AND integrate one phrase from the Register Switch into their exit sentence.
  • Pre-work framing for S3: "Before the next session, think of one real situation at work involving conflict — not feedback, but actual conflict between people. We'll use your real examples in S3."
