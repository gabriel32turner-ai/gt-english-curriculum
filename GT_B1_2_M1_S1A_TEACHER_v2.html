📍 Scenario — Ethical Dilemma at Work
Your manager asks you to send a client report that contains a calculation error — an error that makes the results look better than they actually are. The client presentation is in two hours. Your manager says: "Nobody will notice. Just send it."
What would you do? Would you send the report? Would you push back? Would you say something to the client directly? — Answer freely. Don't worry about grammar. Just respond in English as if this were happening right now.

💬 M1 Colloquial Phrases — How Native Speakers Talk About Difficult Situations
"drop the ball"
To fail to complete something you were responsible for. — "If I dropped the ball on this report, I would lose the client's trust permanently."
"bite the bullet"
To do something difficult or unpleasant that you have been avoiding. — "If I had to confront my manager, I would just bite the bullet and do it."
"play it by ear"
To decide what to do as a situation develops, without a fixed plan. — "If the conversation got tense, I would play it by ear and adapt."
"rock the boat"
To do or say something that disturbs a stable or comfortable situation. — "If I spoke up right now, I would rock the boat — but maybe that's necessary."
"throw (someone) under the bus"
To blame someone else unfairly to avoid taking responsibility yourself. — "If I threw my colleague under the bus, I would save myself — but destroy the relationship."

Grammar — 2nd Conditional
If + simple past · would + base verb · Hypotheticals, dilemmas, and "what if" thinking · 2 activities only
04
15–18 min
Affirmative
+
If + subject + past simple, subject + would + base verb

"If I had more authority, I would address this directly."
"If she were in charge, she would set clearer boundaries."
Negative
−
If + subject + did not + base verb, subject + would not + base verb

"If I did not speak up, the problem would not go away."
"If they did not escalate, it would not be resolved."
Question Form
?
Would + subject + base verb + if + subject + past simple?

"Would you escalate if you discovered this?"
"Would she push back if the manager asked her to lie?"
Special: "were" (formal)
!
Use "were" for all subjects in formal writing and advanced speaking.

"If I were you, I would bite the bullet."
"If he were more transparent, the team would trust him more."

⚡ CRITICAL CONTRAST — Know Which Conditional to Use

1st Conditional — Real / Likely
Structure: If + present simple, will + base verb
Meaning: This is possible. It might actually happen.
Example: "If I have time tomorrow, I will call HR." → (I probably will have time)
Context: Real plans, realistic scenarios, near future
2nd Conditional — Hypothetical / Unlikely
Structure: If + past simple, would + base verb
Meaning: This is imaginary, unlikely, or contrary to current reality.
Example: "If I had time, I would call HR." → (I don't have time — this is hypothetical)
Context: Dilemmas, "what if" thinking, advice, imaginary situations
💡 Key signal: When you say "what would you do if...?" — you are always in the 2nd conditional. The "if" + past simple tells the listener: this is hypothetical. The "would" in the main clause delivers the imaginary consequence. These two parts work together — never separate them.
⚠ B1.2 Common Errors — 2nd Conditional
❌ "If I would have more time, I would call you."→✅ "If I had more time, I would call you."
"Would" NEVER goes in the if-clause. The if-clause uses past simple only.
❌ "If I am you, I would push back."→✅ "If I were you, I would push back."
Use "were" after "if" for all subjects in formal or advanced contexts. "Was" is acceptable in informal speech.
❌ "If I speak up, he would get angry." (mixing tenses)→✅ "If I spoke up, he would get angry."
Both clauses must match: past simple in if-clause, would in main clause. No mixing with present simple + would.

What Would You Do? A Framework for Navigating Workplace Dilemmas

Every professional, at some point, faces a moment where they must choose between what is easy and what is right. These moments — ethical dilemmas, impossible deadlines, uncomfortable confrontations — define careers. According to recent Gallup research, however, most professionals avoid difficult conversations rather than address them directly.

The Hypothetical Framework

Consider this scenario: if your manager asked you to omit critical risk data from a client report, what would you do? Organizational psychologists argue that transparency, however uncomfortable, protects both team integrity and long-term professional credibility. If professionals practiced hypothetical decision-making — that is, if they regularly asked themselves "what would I do if this happened?" — they would be significantly better equipped when a real dilemma arrived.

That being said, speaking up is rarely straightforward. If someone pushed back on your concerns, how would you navigate that? On the other hand, silence has its own consequences. If you chose not to act, accountability would eventually fall somewhere — and it might fall on you. Dropping the ball on an ethical issue is not neutral. It is a decision.

The Language of Difficult Decisions

Perhaps the most important professional skill is not the decision itself — it is the ability to articulate it clearly. Saying "I would escalate this to HR" or "I would address it with the client directly" requires both grammatical precision and personal clarity. Notice that all the questions above use the same structure: if + past simple, would + base verb. This is not accidental.

Leaders who think in hypotheticals — "What would I do if...?" — are better prepared when real situations arise. Integrity, the ability to defuse tension, and knowing when to push back are not personality traits. Although they feel natural in some people, they are learnable skills. And like all skills, they improve with deliberate practice — even if that practice is hypothetical.

"The most effective professionals prioritize one question above all: What would I do if I knew no one was watching? That answer defines your professional character."

MORGAN
Alex, I want to run something by you — hypothetically. If you discovered that someone on your team was inflating expenses to cover project overruns, what would you do?
ALEX
Honestly? I would not react immediately. I think the first thing I would do is gather all the facts. I would not want to escalate without being completely sure.
MORGAN
I hear you. But — if you waited too long and it came out later, you would own that silence. That is your accountability right there.
ALEX
You have a point. That being said, if I confronted the person directly before speaking to HR, I might create more tension than necessary. I think I would set up a private conversation first — just to hear their side.
MORGAN
That is reasonable. But — and this is the hard part — if that person pushed back and said you were wrong, how would you handle that?
ALEX
If they pushed back, I would stay calm. I would say: I have the numbers in front of me. I am not here to accuse — I am here to address this together. If we could not resolve it privately, I would escalate to management.
MORGAN
That is how I would handle it too. No drama, but no silence either. Biting the bullet is not comfortable — but it is the right move. Integrity means acting even when it costs you something.
ALEX
Exactly. And if I were in your position — knowing you — you would already be on the phone with HR.
MORGAN
Probably. I would not let it sit. But I see your point about facts first. Maybe we both have something to learn here.
ALEX
The real dilemma is not the numbers. It is figuring out how much transparency you owe different people at different times.

I am not sure that makes me a good colleague. But if integrity means anything, it means acting even when it is uncomfortable — and that is what I would do."

GT Originals — S1A Exit Notes
The 2-minute debate timer: Start it AFTER the student has said their first full sentence — not before. This prevents over-prepared openings and forces real improvisation for the middle and end. If student finishes under 90 seconds: "Now argue the opposite. Same 2nd conditional, opposite position. Go."

Model answers: Reveal only ONE model answer — the one that contrasts most with the student's answer. "Your approach was X. Here's the other side. Where do you agree?" This creates critical thinking, not imitation.

B1.2 benchmark: 3+ correct 2nd conditionals + 2 colloquial phrases + a defended position = S1A passed. Note the count. If student hits 2/3 — S1B warm-up starts there.

Upsell trigger: "Say one sentence — the most honest one you have — about a real dilemma you are currently navigating at work. Use the 2nd conditional. Right now, no notes." That sentence is the testimonial.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Benchmark: 3+ correct 2nd conditional instances + 2 colloquial phrases in 2 minutes = S1A passed at B1.2 standard. If student reaches 2 out of 3 criteria — note for S1B warm-up.
  • Model answers: show the version that contrasts most with student response. Ask: "What would you keep from this model? What would you change?" — Forces critical evaluation, not copy-paste.
  • Flashcard wall: do not skip. Even if time is tight, do 5 cards minimum. The ritual of the GT Flashcard Wall is part of the product identity — it creates closure and reinforces vocabulary in a low-pressure moment.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "You just debated one of the most uncomfortable professional situations — in English, with hypothetical reasoning, with colloquial language — in two minutes. That is what B1.2 English sounds like when it works."
  • Closing ritual: "Give me one sentence. The most honest one. About a real dilemma at work right now. 2nd conditional. No notes. Go."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Upsell: "S1B next week takes everything from today and removes the scaffolding. You will be the manager — live role-play, no script, no model to follow. The question is: will you be ready? Based on today? Yes."
  • Testimonial trigger: "Say it again — your honest 2nd conditional sentence about your real work situation. I want to hear it clean, no hesitation. That sentence proves to you that B1.2 is already happening." → If student nails it: pause. "That. That's your testimonial."
  • Retention prompt (optional — if time): "This week, when a difficult situation comes up at work — any tension, any dilemma, any pressure — ask yourself: 'What would I do if...' Finish that sentence in English, in your head. That's your practice."
