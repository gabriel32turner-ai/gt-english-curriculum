Hot Take — 60 Seconds Under Uncertainty
Andrea's project is in danger · Express possibility precisely · No notes · Timer on
⏱ 5 MIN
🔥 The Situation
It's Monday morning. Andrea's team has 3 open situations: (1) The key client might cancel the quarterly review. (2) The digital inventory project may be delayed because the IT team is understaffed. (3) The budget might not be approved until next month. The CEO just walked in and asks: "Andrea, how's next week looking?"
You are Andrea. You have 60 seconds to give the CEO an honest, professional update. Use will, might, may, and might not. No notes. Be specific — not just "we'll see."
Hot Take Timer
01
Use will for the one confirmed thing — show the CEO what is solid
02
Use might / may for the uncertain situations — no "maybe," use the modal
03
End with "I'll keep you posted" or "TBD" — close the loop professionally
📘 Pedagogical Note
This Hot Take specifically targets the "maybe" default. Listen for students who say "maybe the client cancels" and gently redirect: "That's 'the client might cancel' — try it with the modal." The correction should happen after the timer, not during.
§2
Pitch Round — Optimist vs Pessimist
Two views on the same uncertain week · might vs might not · 3 minutes each
⏱ 10 MIN
📋 How to run this: Same scenario — Andrea's uncertain week. Side A argues things will probably go well (optimist). Side B argues things might not go as planned (pessimist). Both must use may/might/might not correctly. The winner is judged on grammar accuracy AND argument quality. 3 minutes per side, then class vote.
Side A — The Optimist
Things may go better than expected
  • "The client may confirm the review after all — they usually do."
  • "The IT team might find a solution faster than we think."
  • "The budget may be approved earlier — finance has been efficient lately."
  • "Things will work out — the project will start even with delays."
  • "It depends on how Andrea manages the week — she's done it before."
Side B — The Pessimist
Things might not go as planned
  • "The client might not confirm — they've rescheduled twice already."
  • "The project may not start Monday — the IT team is already overloaded."
  • "The budget might not arrive — it depends on the director's schedule."
  • "The delivery might not arrive — transport issues are real this week."
  • "TBD on all fronts — this might be a very difficult week for the team."

§3
Relay — Possibility Chain
Transform certainty into uncertainty — 5 situations, one spectrum · Each student shifts the modal one level
⏱ 8 MIN
💬 How it works: Each chain has 5 versions of the same sentence — from won't (0%) to will (100%). Student 1 reads the "will" version. Student 2 must step it down one level to "may." Student 3 steps to "might." Student 4 goes to "might not." Student 5 reaches "won't." Then the class discusses: in what situation would each level be realistic?
Chain 1 — The Client Visit
will100%
Context: It's confirmed — tickets booked, agenda sent.
"The client will visit our office on Thursday."
→ Student 1 reads this. Student 2: transform to "may."
may~60%
Context: They said yes but haven't confirmed the exact time.
"The client may visit our office on Thursday."
→ Student 2 says this. Student 3: transform to "might."
might~40%
Context: They're travelling — it's 50/50.
"The client might visit our office on Thursday."
→ Student 3 says this. Student 4: transform to "might not."
might not~70% NO
Context: They just rescheduled twice. Probably not happening.
"The client might not visit our office on Thursday."
→ Student 4 says this. Student 5: transform to "won't."
won't0%
Context: They cancelled. Officially. It's done.
"The client won't visit our office on Thursday."
✅ Full chain complete. Class discussion: when does each level apply at your job?
🎤 Chain 2 — Mini Version (class does this together)
Starter: "The Q4 budget will be approved this week."
The class transforms it step by step. Teacher asks: "What happened between 'will' and 'won't'? What changed the certainty?"
Target: Students internalize that the modal choice reflects how much information you have — not just how you feel.

Your flight to Monterrey on Friday isn't confirmed yet — 50% chance. How do you tell your team?
✅ "I may travel to Monterrey on Friday — I'll confirm by Thursday."
Target modal: may (~50%)
The project deadline is officially set for next Friday. How do you communicate this?
✅ "The project will finish by next Friday — it's confirmed."
Target modal: will (100% certain)
The supplier is probably not going to make it — there's a 70% chance of delay. How do you update the manager?
✅ "The delivery might not arrive on time — I'd suggest preparing a backup plan."
Target modal: might not (70% NO)
You're not sure if the client will join Thursday's call — it depends on their director's availability.
✅ "The client might join Thursday's call — it depends on her director. TBD."
Target modal: might (~35–40%)
You sent a proposal 3 days ago. No reply yet. Your manager asks if the client is interested.
✅ "They might be interested — I haven't heard back yet. I may follow up tomorrow."
Target: might + may (two levels of uncertainty)
The all-hands meeting is in the calendar for Monday — everyone confirmed attendance. How do you announce it?
✅ "The all-hands meeting will happen on Monday at 10 AM — attendance is confirmed."
Target modal: will (confirmed, 100%)
There's a storm forecast for Tuesday. The supplier usually drives from Guadalajara. What do you tell the production team?
✅ "The delivery may be delayed due to weather — we might need to use emergency stock."
Target: may (first risk) + might (consequence)
The finance director is on holiday until Thursday. The budget needs his signature. When does Andrea get it?
✅ "The budget might not be approved before Thursday — it depends on when the director returns."
Target: might not (blocked by external factor)
The board presentation was supposed to be Wednesday — but two board members are possibly unavailable. Update the team.
✅ "The board presentation may be rescheduled — two members might not be available. I'll keep you posted."
Target: may (change) + might not (reason)
The client officially sent a cancellation email for Thursday's meeting. Zero chance of happening now. What do you say?
✅ "The Thursday meeting won't happen — the client cancelled. I'll reschedule for next week."
Target: won't (0% — it's certain it won't happen)

Solo Challenge — 2 Minutes · Full Spectrum
Combine will + may + might + might not in one coherent professional update · No notes
⏱ 8 MIN
🎯 Solo Challenge
You are a team leader. Your manager just called and asked: "Give me a quick status on next week — what's confirmed, what's at risk, and what definitely won't happen?" You have 2 minutes. Be precise with your modals.
  • Mention at least 1 thing that WILL happen — confirmed, certain
  • Mention at least 1 thing that MAY happen — 50–60% likely
  • Mention at least 1 thing that MIGHT happen — 30–40%, uncertain
  • Mention at least 1 thing that MIGHT NOT happen — more likely to be delayed or cancelled
  • Use one colloquial phrase: "I'll keep you posted," "TBD," or "It depends on…"
Solo Challenge Timer
💰 Retention & Upsell Note
After the Solo Challenge, tell the class: "Everything you just did — expressing uncertainty, mixing modals, using professional phrases — is exactly the language of M4. In M4 we add Present Continuous for future ('I'm meeting the client Friday') which makes this even more precise." One sentence plants the next module's value proposition.

Source Analysis — Modals in the Wild
4 real-format sources · Identify modal choice · Evaluate accuracy and register
⏱ 8 MIN
💬 How to run this: Students read each source and answer the metacognitive question. Key focus: is the modal choice accurate given the context? Could a different modal change the message significantly?
💬 WhatsApp — Team Group
"Hey team — quick update. The client visit might be rescheduled. I may not be in the office Thursday. The Friday delivery will arrive — confirmed. I'll keep you posted. 🙏"
Q: The writer uses three different modals in four sentences. Are they all correctly chosen? What does the switch from "might" to "will" communicate to the reader? Does "I'll keep you posted" add value here?
📧 Email — Project Update
"Dear team, I wanted to flag that the IT infrastructure upgrade may affect our system access next Tuesday. The impact might last 2–4 hours. Critical processes will be prioritized and might not be affected. I'll confirm on Monday. TBD for less critical tools."
Q: This email uses four modals in professional context. Which modal signals the highest confidence? What would change if the writer used "will" instead of "may" in the first sentence? How does "TBD" function differently from "I'll keep you posted"?
💼 LinkedIn Post
"Career tip: When you don't know if something will happen, say 'it might.' When you think it's likely, say 'it may.' When it's confirmed, say 'it will.' Three words. Three levels of certainty. One of the most underused tools in professional English."
Q: This LinkedIn post teaches exactly what we studied today. Is the explanation accurate? What's missing from this post — what would you add to make it more complete? Could you write a similar post in English today?
📄 Internal Memo
"Due to current supply chain conditions, the Q4 product launch may be delayed by 2–3 weeks. Some components might not arrive before October 15th. Marketing campaigns will proceed as planned. A revised timeline may be shared by end of week. Status: TBD."
Q: This memo mixes certainty (will) with uncertainty (may/might not). Does the structure feel reassuring or worrying? Rewrite the first sentence using "will" instead of "may" — how does that change the tone of the whole memo?

🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases — Use Them This Week
PHRASE 1
"The meeting will happen — the client might be late, but it won't be cancelled."
Will + might in one sentence — you signal certainty AND prepare your team for a minor risk. This is mature, professional language that separates strong communicators from the rest.
PHRASE 2
"The budget may not arrive this week — TBD. I'll keep you posted."
Three uncertainty tools in one sentence: may not (negative possibility) + TBD (status flag) + "I'll keep you posted" (commitment to follow up). Use this in any email or WhatsApp where something is unresolved.
PHRASE 3
"It depends on the client — they might confirm, or they might not. We'll see."
The most complete expression of genuine uncertainty in professional English. "It depends on" + two modal options + "we'll see" covers everything. Honest, clear, and completely natural. Use when someone presses you for a definitive answer you don't have.
