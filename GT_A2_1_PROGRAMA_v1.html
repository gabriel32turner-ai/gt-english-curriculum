Who is this program for?

  • Students who completed A1.2 at Gabriel Turner English Training
  • Professionals who can communicate in the present but struggle with past and future
  • Corporate employees who need to report, plan and negotiate in English
  • Adults ready to move from basic survival English to real business conversations

By the end of A2.1, students will be able to:

  • Describe past work experiences using Simple Past (regular and irregular verbs)
  • Tell business stories and explain events, trips and meetings
  • Chair and participate in basic meetings and give short presentations
  • Talk about future plans and intentions using be going to
  • Make offers, promises and predictions using will
  • Communicate with clients across past, present and future tenses

01
Work in the Past
Daily work actions · Past time expressions · End-of-day reports
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Describing completed work tasks from yesterday or last week
  • Reporting daily work activities to a manager or colleague
  • Writing and reading end-of-day work reports
  • Asking and answering about past work using did/didn't
  • Talking about work outcomes: on time, delayed, cancelled
  • Using past time expressions with precision
Grammar
Simple Past — regular verbs (aff. / neg. / ?) Spelling rules: work→worked / study→studied / stop→stopped Did / Didn't as past auxiliaries Short answers: Yes, I did. / No, I didn't. Past time expressions: yesterday / last week / two days ago / in + year Simple Present (consolidation — contrast with past)

Functional English
  • What did you do yesterday? — I attended two meetings and reviewed the report.
  • Did you send the email? — Yes, I did. / No, I didn't — I forgot.
  • We finished the project last Friday.
  • The client confirmed the order two days ago.
  • I didn't work last Monday — it was a holiday.
  • When did you submit the report? — I submitted it this morning.
  • Did she call? — No, she didn't. She sent an email instead.
Everyday English
  • "I got it done." — I finished it
  • "It went well." — positive result
  • "Not bad." — acceptable outcome
  • "We ran out of time."
  • "I totally forgot."
End-of-Day Report (chunks)
  • "Today I completed…"
  • "I reviewed and submitted…"
  • "I didn't finish — I'll do it tomorrow."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 📅 Past time expression system: difference between "yesterday" (specific) and "last week" (general) — high error rate at A2
  • 🔴 False Friend: "I assisted the meeting" → I attended the meeting — assist = help, not attend
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: regular past endings — /t/ (worked) / /d/ (called) / /ɪd/ (attended) — three distinct sounds, never just "ed"
  • ⚠️ Common error: "I did finish the report" (wrong emphasis) vs "I didn't finish it" (correct negative)
  • 💼 Corporate use: end-of-day report language — "Today I completed / reviewed / submitted…" — immediate professional output
Exit Task M1
Describe what you did at work last week. Use at least 5 regular past verbs with different time markers. Answer 3 teacher questions in Simple Past. (Speaking · 4 min)

Stories & Events
Business trips · Client visits · Corporate events · Past narratives
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Narrating a business trip, conference or client visit
  • Describing what happened at a corporate event
  • Asking Wh- questions about past events
  • Talking about the duration of past events
  • Reporting what someone said or did in the past
  • Expressing positive and negative outcomes
Grammar
Simple Past — irregular verbs (high-frequency business list) Wh- questions in Simple Past: What did you say? / Who did you meet? Negative with irregular verbs: I didn't go / She didn't have (infinitive rule) How long did it take? — duration in the past Simple Past regular (consolidation from M1)

Functional English
  • We had an excellent meeting with the client in Monterrey last Tuesday.
  • She gave a presentation to the board. It went really well.
  • I went to the Chicago office last month. I met the regional director.
  • What happened at the meeting? — We made a decision about the new supplier.
  • How long did the conference take? — It took three days.
  • He didn't come to the office yesterday. He sent a message.
  • Who did you speak to? — I spoke to the HR manager.
Everyday English
  • "How did it go?" — asking about a past event
  • "It went really well."
  • "It didn't go as planned."
  • "Long story short…" — to summarize
  • "Better than expected."
Storytelling (chunks)
  • "So, what happened was…"
  • "First… then… finally…"
  • "In the end, we…"
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🧠 Irregular verb memory system: group by pattern (meet/met, get/got / make/made, take/took / write/wrote, speak/spoke) — faster acquisition
  • ⚠️ Critical error: "I didn't went" → I didn't go — infinitive after didn't — the most common A2 mistake globally
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "met" /met/ vs "meet" /miːt/ — past/present confusion in speaking, drill in context
  • 💼 Storytelling structure: "So, what happened was…" — used in business to explain delays or results to managers concisely
  • 🌍 Cultural note: in US/UK business, past narratives should be concise — "bottom line up front" before the story
Exit Task M2
Tell the story of a real or imaginary business event (client visit, meeting, or trip). Use 5 irregular verbs + 2 Wh- questions in Simple Past. Answer follow-up questions. (Speaking · 4 min)

Functional English

  • I'd like to present our Q3 results. As you can see in this slide…

Chair Language (chunks)

  • "Let's move on to the next point."
  • "We'll pick that up next time."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 📋 Meeting minutes template A2.1: Date / Attendees / Action Points / Next meeting — real corporate output students can use immediately
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "agenda" /əˈdʒendə/ — stress on second syllable / "minutes" (of a meeting) = written record, NOT time
  • ⚠️ Was/Were error: "The meeting were good" → The meeting was good — singular subject, always was
  • 💼 Chair language: how to open, transition and close a meeting in English — high-value corporate skill at A2.1
  • 🌍 Cultural note: raising a point in a US/UK meeting — "I'd like to add something" — not interrupting directly
Exit Task M3
Chair a 5-minute mini-meeting with the teacher: open → 2 agenda points → action items → close. Then describe what happened using Simple Past + was/were. (Role play · 5 min)

Future Plans: Going To
Work plans · Project intentions · Corporate schedules · Future time expressions
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Talking about planned work activities for next week or month
  • Describing project plans and personal intentions at work
  • Asking about and confirming colleagues' plans
  • Discussing what will happen with a delayed or changed plan
  • Using future time expressions with precision
  • Contrasting going to with fixed schedule (Simple Present)
Grammar
Be going to — full system (aff. / neg. / ? / short answers) I'm / She's / We're going to + infinitive Negative: I'm not going to / He isn't going to Questions: Are you going to attend? / What are you going to do? Going to vs Simple Present: personal plan vs fixed schedule Future time expressions: next week / tomorrow / this Friday / in two weeks

Functional English
  • We're going to launch the new product in March.
  • Are you going to attend the conference in Monterrey?
  • I'm not going to be in the office next Monday — I'm going to visit a client.
  • What are you going to do about the delay?
  • The project is going to finish ahead of schedule.
  • We're going to reschedule the presentation — the client cancelled.
  • Is the team going to meet the deadline? — Yes, we are.
Everyday English
  • "We're good to go." — everything is ready
  • "It's happening." — confirming a plan
  • "We're on track."
  • "That's the plan."
  • "Let's make it happen."
Project Update (chunks)
  • "I'm going to send it by Friday."
  • "We're going to meet the deadline."
  • "I'm going to follow up on that."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🔵 Going to vs Will: introduce the contrast clearly here — going to = plan already in your head / will = decision right now — drill with real situations
  • 💼 Project update email A2.1: "I'm going to send the report by Friday. We're going to meet the deadline." — real corporate writing output
  • ⚠️ Natural speech: "I'm going to go" is correct but sounds awkward — often replaced with "I'm going" — teach both forms
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "going to" → natural speech = "gonna" — awareness only, not production at A2.1
  • 📊 Corporate schedule reading: "In March, we're going to launch X. In April, we're going to review Y." — immediate practical use
Exit Task M4
Present your work plan for the next month using be going to. Include 3 specific plans with dates. Answer questions from the teacher about your plans and intentions. (Speaking · 4 min)

10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics

  • Giving promises and commitments to clients and colleagues
  • Making predictions about business results and timelines
  • Handling basic client communication: quotes, offers and follow-ups
  • Writing follow-up emails using will and going to

Grammar
Will — full system (aff. / neg. / ? / short answers) Contractions: I'll / She'll / We'll / They'll / Won't Short answers: Yes, I will. / No, I won't. Will vs Going to — systematic contrast (key A2.1 skill) Predictions with will: I think it'll be ready by Friday. Going to (consolidation from M4)

Functional English
  • I'll call you back this afternoon. — Great, I'll wait for your call.
  • Will you be available on Thursday? — Yes, I will. / I'll check my calendar.
  • I'll send you the proposal today and follow up on Friday.
  • I think the delivery will be ready next week.
  • We won't meet the original deadline, but we'll deliver by the 20th.
  • I'll give you a 10% discount if you confirm the order today.
  • Will it be ready for the launch? — I think it will, but I'll confirm tomorrow.
Everyday English
  • "I'll take care of it."
  • "I'll handle it."
  • "That works for me."
  • "I'll get back to you."
  • "Leave it with me."
Client Call (chunks)
  • "I'll send that over today."
  • "I won't be able to…, but I'll…"
  • "I'll copy you on that email."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🔵 Will vs Going to — the definitive contrast: use 3 real business situations to test the difference — students always mix these at A2
  • 💼 Client call script A2.1: receive → discuss → offer → confirm — will is the dominant tense throughout, very natural
  • ⚠️ Error: "I will to call you" → I'll call you — will + bare infinitive, never "to"
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "I'll" /aɪl/ sounds identical to "aisle" — drill in context / "won't" /wəʊnt/ — not "will not" in natural speech
  • 📧 Email with will: "I'll send / I'll confirm / I won't be available" — most common future tense in business emails at A2.1
