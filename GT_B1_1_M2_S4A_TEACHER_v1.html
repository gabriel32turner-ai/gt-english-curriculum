Today's Topic
Workplace Culture — Have You Seen the Best and Worst?
Have you ever worked somewhere with a truly great culture — or one that was quietly toxic? What separates them? And what have you already learned about what matters most?
Round 1 · 2 min
Describe the culture of your current (or most recent) workplace. Use 3 specific examples.
💡 Use: "I've noticed…" / "They've already started…" / "We still haven't…"
Round 2 · 2 min
Have you ever experienced a toxic workplace? What were the red flags? What did you — or didn't you — do about it?
💡 Use: "I've never seen…" / "I'd already noticed the signs…" / "I still haven't forgotten…"
Round 3 · 2 min
What's one thing a company could do to genuinely improve its culture — starting now? What have the best companies you've seen already done differently?
💡 Use: "They've just introduced…" / "It's the first time I've seen…" / "Most companies haven't tried yet…"
"I've worked in two very different environments. My first company — I'd describe it as a pressure cooker. The turnover was high and psychological safety was basically zero. I've never felt so burned out. Nobody had ever given us honest feedback — it was all top-down.

My current role is different. They've just introduced a quarterly culture survey, which I think is a good sign. Management has already acknowledged some problems publicly — that's rare. We still haven't solved the work-life balance issue completely, but at least it's on the agenda.

'If you can't handle the heat, get out of the kitchen' — I've heard that. But the best cultures I've seen create kitchens where people actually want to cook. That's the difference."

§4 — Colloquial
Real Workplace Talk — Culture in Action
4 phrases · How native speakers actually talk about culture · Integrate with PP grammar
8–10 MIN
🗣️
Colloquial
Culture red flags · Euphemisms · Workplace reality talk
8–10 MIN
"We're a family here."
🚩 Red flag phrase. Sounds warm — actually means: "We expect you to sacrifice personal time because that's what families do." Common in companies with poor boundaries.
"They've always said 'we're a family' — but I've never heard of a family where you work 60 hours a week unpaid."
+ PP: "I've heard this at every toxic company I've ever worked at."
"We have a culture of feedback."
📢 Can be genuine — or a euphemism for "your manager criticizes you constantly." The real test: is feedback bidirectional? Can you give feedback UP the hierarchy?
"They've just implemented 360° reviews — I've already seen people give honest feedback to their managers for the first time."
+ PP: "Have you ever worked somewhere where feedback actually went both ways?"
"We move fast."
⚡ Startup language. Positive: agility, opportunity. Negative: "We've never thought through the consequences." Often hides lack of process and high stress.
"They move fast — but they've broken so many things along the way that nobody's trusted the process in years."
+ PP: "I've just started somewhere that 'moves fast' — I haven't decided if it's exciting or exhausting yet."
"My door is always open."
🚪 Classic management phrase. Reality check: have you ever actually used that door? A truly open door policy means people feel safe to walk through — even with bad news.
"She's always said her door is open — and people have actually used it. I've seen real problems get solved because of it."
+ PP: "I've never worked somewhere where that was truly the case — until now."
  • ACCIÓN: Pregunta al alumno: "Have you ever heard any of these phrases at work — in Spanish or English?" Casi todos los han escuchado. Ese reconocimiento personal hace que las frases sean memorables.
  • "We're a family" — Esta es la frase coloquial más cargada de la sesión. En México, el concepto de empresa-familia es culturalmente más aceptado. Señala el contraste: en contexto anglosajón, esta frase es una red flag reconocida. No es juicio — es inteligencia cultural.
  • El patrón PP integrado (+PP) en cada tarjeta es la conexión directa con §5 Grammar. Pide que el alumno lea el ejemplo en voz alta y te diga cuál adverbio usa ("just", "already", "never", "yet").
§5 — Grammar
Present Perfect Precision — Just · Already · Yet · Still · Never · Ever
Referential — never drilling · Meaning in context · Quick Poll + Activities
12–14 MIN
⚡
Grammar — Referential
PP adverbs in workplace culture context · Use, don't drill
12–14 MIN
Key principle for B1.1: These adverbs don't change the grammar — they change the meaning and timing of the Present Perfect. The form is always have/has + past participle. The adverb tells us when in the speaker's experience the event sits.
AdverbPositionMeaning in contextWorkplace culture example
justbetween have & past participlevery recently completed"They've just launched a new culture survey — it started yesterday."
alreadybetween have & pp (or end)sooner than expected"She's already changed the meeting structure — we didn't expect it so fast."
yetend of negative/questionexpected but not happened"HR hasn't addressed the issue yet." / "Have they published results yet?"
stillbefore haven't / before havesurprising continuation"They still haven't changed the policy — after three years of complaints."
neverbetween have & ppnot at any time in experience"I've never felt this level of psychological safety in a workplace before."
everbetween have & pp in questionsat any time in your life"Have you ever worked somewhere with genuine transparency from management?"
⚠️ The "still / yet" trap: Both mean something expected hasn't happened — but still emphasises surprise or frustration (before haven't), while yet is more neutral (at the end). "They still haven't fixed it" = I'm frustrated. "They haven't fixed it yet" = neutral report.

  • ACCIÓN: Antes de las actividades, pide al alumno que diga UNA oración verdadera sobre su empresa usando cada adverbio. "Just — dime algo que acaba de pasar. Already — algo que pasó antes de lo esperado." El contexto real ancla la gramática mejor que cualquier tabla.
  • Award Badge 3 (⚡ Grammar Pro) when student answers Quick Poll 3/3 correctly OR completes both activities with 6+/8 correct.
  • El error más común en B1.1 con hispanohablantes: "I have never ever seen…" (doble emphasis) — no es incorrecto pero suena enfático/informal. "I've never seen" es más neutro y profesional.
  • "Still haven't" vs "haven't yet" — la distinción pragmática es más importante que la gramatical. Practica con intonación: "They STILL haven't fixed it" (frustrante) vs "They haven't fixed it YET" (neutro, informativo).

Culture Check — Two Companies, Two Stories
B1.1 · M2 · S4A · Voice: Sarah (American English) · Speed: 0.84
Interviewer: "Rodrigo, you've worked in two very different companies. Can you tell me about the culture difference?"

Rodrigo: "Absolutely. My first company — I'd call it a classic pressure cooker. They've never once celebrated a team success publicly. The hierarchy was rigid, and nobody had ever challenged a manager's decision without consequences. I hadn't realised how toxic it was until I left."

Interviewer: "And what changed?"

Rodrigo: "Everything. At my current company, the CEO has just launched an anonymous feedback platform — it's only been two weeks. Managers have already started responding publicly to comments. I've never seen anything like it. People have already submitted over 200 responses. HR still hasn't published all the results yet — but they've promised to."

Interviewer: "What's the most important thing a company can do to build real culture?"

Rodrigo: "Address the issues they've already seen and haven't fixed yet. Everyone knows what the problems are. The question is: have they ever actually done something about it? I've worked places that have talked about change for years and still haven't moved. That's the difference."

Question 1 — Culture Audit
Give your company a culture score from 1–10. Explain 3 specific reasons using PP adverbs. What has already improved? What still hasn't changed? What have you never experienced there that you wish you had?
💡 Target: "just / already / yet / still / never / ever" — at least 4 different adverbs in your answer.
Question 2 — Red Flag Spotter
Your friend has just accepted a job at a new company. They share these facts: "They said we're a family. The CEO hasn't met the team yet. They move fast." What red flags have you spotted? What advice would you give?
💡 Use: "I've seen that before and it usually means…" / "I've never trusted companies that…" / "Have you ever asked them about turnover?"
Question 3 — Two-Minute Pitch
You're pitching to a candidate why they should join your company. Make the culture sound genuinely appealing — using REAL examples, not PR language. What has your company actually done? What hasn't it done yet but is working on?
💡 Authenticity test: your pitch should include at least one honest "we haven't solved X yet but…" — that's what B1.1 sounds like.
🎲 Hot Seat Dice — 6 prompts · Click to reveal · 20 seconds each · No prep
Teacher picks a card (or student picks randomly). Student answers immediately — 20 seconds, no thinking time. Award Badge 4 when 4+ cards answered. Click card to reveal model prompt.
🎲 1
Have you ever walked out of a job because of the culture?
Force a YES/NO then expand. "I've never left — but I've thought about it because…" or "I left once after I'd already realised…" Minimum 3 sentences.
🎲 2
Name one company you've heard of that has an amazing culture. What have they already done that others haven't?
Target vocabulary: "They've already eliminated…" / "They've just introduced…" / "I've heard they've never tolerated…" Real company = more authentic.
🎲 3
Have you ever challenged your manager? What happened — and what have you learned since?
High emotional value — personal story forces authentic PP use. "I'd already tried indirect approaches before I finally said something directly." Watch for: simple past vs PP confusion.
🎲 4
Your company has just introduced mandatory 7 AM meetings every Monday. What do you do?
Opinion question — forces hypothetical + PP. "I've never accepted that kind of imposition without…" / "I'd already been thinking about this kind of thing — I think the company has crossed a line."
🎲 5
What's the most toxic thing you've ever seen normalized at a workplace?
Narrative question. Target: "It had been going on for years and nobody had ever complained officially." Encourages complex PP + past perfect combinations — stretch goal for strong students.
🎲 6
⭐ Has your company ever asked employees what kind of culture they actually want? What happened?
This is the most sophisticated prompt. "They've surveyed us, but the results still haven't changed anything." Forces the "still haven't + yet" contrast. Award Badge 4 here if not already earned.

🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"Have you ever worked somewhere with genuine psychological safety?"
Use this in your next networking conversation or job interview. It signals B1.1+ professional vocabulary and shows you understand what culture actually means beyond the buzzwords.
PHRASE 2
"They've already started, but they still haven't solved the core issue yet."
The complete nuance sentence — progress acknowledged + problem identified. Use when evaluating a company's culture change efforts. "Already" and "still/yet" in one sentence = B1.1 precision.
PHRASE 3
"I've never felt more — or less — trusted as a professional than at [place]."
A narrative opener that works in interviews, conversations and LinkedIn. "Never felt more" (positive story) or "never felt less" (growth lesson). The comparative + PP combination sounds sophisticated and natural.
