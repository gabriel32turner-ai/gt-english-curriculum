Capa 1 · Pedagogical & Error Redirection
  • This is a diagnostic moment. Let the student speak for 45 seconds without correction. Note: what opinion language do they already use? "I think", "I prefer", "I feel"? That is your baseline for §4 depth-calibration.
  • DO NOT offer vocabulary yet. §2 is the injection point. If the student struggles now, that creates productive frustration — the vocab in §2 will feel like a genuine tool, not a list.
  • Key observation: does the student justify their choice (because / since / so that) or just describe? Justification is the B1.1 target. Push: "Why do you prefer that? Can you give me a reason?"
  • Common errors at B1.1 warm-up: (a) "I like because…" → missing "it": "I like it because…" (b) "The office is more comfortable" → push for comparison: "More comfortable than what?" (c) "I prefer the home" → "I prefer working from home" — gerund is the target form.
  • Pronunciation: "environment" /ɪnˈvaɪrənmənt/ — 4 syllables. Students often say "en-vi-RON-men-TO" (5 syllables, Spanish transfer). This word appears frequently in §3 and §4. Model it now once.
Capa 2 · Motivation & Engagement
  • Script: "Today we're talking about something everyone has strong opinions about — where you work and how that affects everything: your mood, your output, your sense of purpose. Let's start visually. Pick the photo, then tell me."
  • Micro-celebration: If the student produces a complete opinion sentence unprompted — "Perfect. That's exactly the kind of answer we're building toward today. More of that."
  • If the student picks the remote/flexible option: ask "Is that your ideal or your current reality?" — creates a rich contrast that feeds directly into §3 and §4.
Capa 3 · Context & Upsell
  • In the Bajío industrial corridor (Guanajuato, SLP, Querétaro), most students work in manufacturing, engineering, or operations — structured, deadline-driven environments. This topic is highly relevant. Expect strong opinions about open offices vs. plant floors vs. corporate settings.
  • Upsell signal: If the student struggles to express their preference → "This is exactly the gap we close in M2. By the end of this session you'll be able to describe your ideal environment in 2–3 full, precise sentences. That's the target." Plant the expectation early.

Group A · Opinion Expressions — How to Frame Your Views
I think… /aɪ θɪŋk/ The most common, neutral way to share a personal opinion. "I think a flexible schedule is essential."
In my opinion… /ɪn maɪ əˈpɪnjən/ More formal opener — signals a clear personal perspective. "In my opinion, company culture matters more than salary."
I prefer… /aɪ prɪˈfɜːr/ Shows preference between two options — compare explicitly. "I prefer working independently rather than in a large open space."
I believe… /aɪ bɪˈliːv/ Signals a deeper conviction — stronger than "I think." "I believe that where you work shapes who you become professionally."
From my perspective… /frɒm maɪ pəˈspektɪv/ Marks your viewpoint as personal — great for contrast. "From my perspective, remote work is more productive."
Personally… /ˈpɜːrsənəli/ Emphasizes you speak for yourself, not in general. "Personally, I cannot focus in noisy, open environments."
To be honest… /tuː biː ˈɒnɪst/ Signals a candid, sometimes surprising opinion. "To be honest, I have never really liked open-plan offices."
I feel strongly that… /aɪ fiːl ˈstrɒŋli ðæt/ Shows high intensity — used when you are very certain. "I feel strongly that every employee deserves a quiet space."
Group B · Work Environment Descriptors — Words to Describe Where You Work
collaborative /kəˈlæbərətɪv/ Involving people working together toward a shared goal. "We have a very collaborative team culture."
flexible /ˈfleksɪbl/ Adaptable — not fixed or rigid in schedule or location. "I need a flexible schedule to manage my responsibilities."
structured /ˈstrʌktʃərd/ Organized with clear rules, roles, and procedures. "Some people need a structured environment to be productive."
autonomous /ɔːˈtɒnəməs/ Having independence and freedom to make decisions. "I prefer an autonomous role where I control my own time."
supportive /səˈpɔːrtɪv/ Providing help, encouragement, and backing — a culture of care. "My manager is really supportive — she listens when there are problems."
competitive /kəmˈpetɪtɪv/ Involving strong rivalry and a drive to outperform others. "The sales department has a very competitive culture — only results matter."
fast-paced /ˈfɑːst peɪst/ Moving quickly with constant change and high workload. "It is a fast-paced environment — decisions have to be made immediately."
deadline-driven /ˈdedlaɪn ˈdrɪvən/ Organized around strict due dates and time pressure. "We work in a deadline-driven industry — delays affect everything."
dynamic /daɪˈnæmɪk/ Energetic, constantly changing, full of new activity. "I prefer a dynamic environment over a routine, predictable one."
creative freedom /kriˈeɪtɪv ˈfriːdəm/ The space to think independently and try new ideas at work. "I left my last job because there was no creative freedom at all."
⚡ Rapid Personalisation: After each descriptor, teacher asks: "Is your current workplace [collaborative / flexible / structured]? Why or why not?" Student answers with a complete opinion sentence using a Group A expression.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical & Error Redirection
  • Key B1.1 distinction: Group A (opinion openers) are FRAMES. Group B (descriptors) are CONTENT. The skill is combining both: "I think [frame] + [descriptor] + because [reason]." That three-part structure is the B1.1 speaking target for this session.
  • "autonomous" — stress on second syllable: /ɔːˈTÒNəməs/. Students over-stress the first syllable. Drill: click 🔊, repeat twice, then use in a sentence. Don't move on until it's natural.
  • "collaborative" — stress: /kəˈLÆBərətɪv/. Students often say "coLABorativo" (Spanish transfer). The stress shift is the fix.
  • "deadline-driven" — this compound adjective (noun + past participle) is a B1.1 pattern worth flagging: "What other -driven compounds can you think of?" (data-driven, results-driven, customer-driven). Expands naturally to B1.2.
  • If a student has NO vocabulary to describe their environment after §1, Group B gives them 10 new tools. By the end of §2 target: "My workplace is [descriptor] and [descriptor], but I think I'd prefer something more [descriptor]."
Capa 2 · Motivation & Engagement
  • Script: "You now have the language. These 8 opinion starters and 10 descriptors are your toolkit for the next 45 minutes. Use them — not just today, but any time you need to express a preference in English at work."
  • Pronunciation win: "personally" /ˈPÜRsənəli/ — strong first syllable, reduced -ally ending. Practice it as a sentence opener with rising then falling intonation. One round of drilling this one word pays dividends all session.

Capa 1 · Pedagogical & Error Redirection
  • The binary choice is a cognitive shortcut — it forces immediate commitment and removes decision fatigue. Once a choice is made, the student MUST justify it. That justification IS the B1.1 output: opinion + reason + example.
  • Minimum acceptable response per item: 2 complete sentences with a because / since / so clause. If the student gives a one-word answer: "Good — now tell me why. And give me an example from your own experience."
  • If the student switches their choice mid-answer: "You changed your mind! That's fine — tell me why. Use: 'Actually, I think I prefer… because I realized that…'" — this generates even richer output.
  • Items 4 and 5 are more complex (team vs solo / startup vs corporate) because they involve trade-offs that invite conditional language: "I prefer X, but if I had to choose between Y and Z, I would say…" — this is the B1.2 bridge.
  • Common error: "I prefer the office more" → redundant "more" with prefer. Correct: "I prefer the office" or "I prefer working at the office." Model it once, don't over-correct.
Capa 2 · Motivation & Engagement
  • Devil's advocate script: "After you answer, I'll give you the opposite position and you have to argue it. This is called devil's advocate. Ready? Let's go." — turns any item into a 2-minute extended output drill.
  • Vocabulary accountability: If the student doesn't use any Group B descriptors, prompt: "Can you use one of today's words — collaborative, structured, autonomous — in your answer right now?"
§ 04
🃏 Question Cards — 8 Questions, Progressive Depth
Basic → Medium → Advanced · Answer first, then reveal model
04
12 min

Answer each question out loud first. Then click Reveal Model Answer to see a B1.1-level example. Cards progress from basic to advanced — do them in order.

01
Basic
Do you prefer working in a team or working alone? Why?
Use: "I prefer… because…" — try to give at least two reasons.
B1.1 Model Answer — "Team vs. Solo"
"Personally, I prefer working in a collaborative team environment, but it depends on the type of task. For creative projects, I think working with others generates better ideas — you get different perspectives and you challenge each other. However, for analytical or detailed work, I believe I am more productive alone, without interruptions. From my perspective, the ideal situation is a flexible arrangement where you can choose based on what the task actually requires."
🏷 Key patterns: opinion opener + preference + conditional ("it depends on") + contrast connector ("however") + ideal vision at the end. Target: 45–60 seconds of connected speech.
02
Basic
Is a flexible schedule important to you? Give two specific reasons.
Try: "I think flexible hours are important because… Also, without flexibility…"
B1.1 Model Answer — "Flexible Schedule"
"To be honest, yes — a flexible schedule is very important to me. First, I think people are not equally productive at the same hours. I am much more focused in the morning, so I want to do my most complex work then — not at 4 PM in a meeting. Second, without flexibility, managing family responsibilities or unexpected situations becomes very stressful. In my opinion, flexibility signals that a company actually trusts its employees — and that trust improves morale significantly."
🏷 Two distinct reasons (personal productivity + trust/morale), connected by "First… Second…". The final opinion connects the practical to the cultural — that is B1.1 depth.
03
Basic
Describe your ideal work environment using exactly 3 adjectives from today's vocabulary. Explain each one.
Pattern: "My ideal environment would be [adj1], [adj2], and [adj3]. [adj1] because… [adj2] because…"
B1.1 Model Answer — "3 Adjectives"
"I think my ideal work environment would be autonomous, supportive, and dynamic. Autonomous because I believe people produce their best work when they are trusted to manage their own time and decisions — micromanagement kills creativity. Supportive because, even with autonomy, you still need a team and a manager who are there when you face real problems. And dynamic because personally, I lose motivation when every day is exactly the same — I need challenges and change to stay engaged."
🏷 Three-part structure with parallel justifications. "Micromanagement kills creativity" is a B1.1 opinion marker — confident, specific, not generic. Push students toward this kind of concrete claim.
04
Medium
What is one thing you would change about your current workplace? Explain the impact that change would have.
Use: "If I could change one thing… it would be… because… The impact would be…"
B1.1 Model Answer — "One Change"
"If I could change one thing about my workplace, it would be the communication structure. Right now, I feel that information does not flow efficiently — decisions are made at the top and we find out much later than we should. I believe that more transparent, horizontal communication would improve team morale dramatically. When people understand why something is happening, not just what is happening, they feel more committed. The impact would be a more collaborative and engaged team — and probably fewer misunderstandings."
🏷 "If I could change…" is a Second Conditional chunk — teach it as a fixed phrase here, not grammar. The why/what contrast shows B1.1 analytical thinking.
05
Medium
Do you think company culture is more important than salary? Defend your position.
Don't just agree or disagree — give a nuanced answer: "It depends on… but I generally think…"
B1.1 Model Answer — "Culture vs. Salary"
"I think it genuinely depends on your stage of life and your financial situation. From my perspective, salary matters more when you are starting your career or supporting a family — you cannot ignore economic reality. However, I believe that beyond a certain point, more money without a supportive culture becomes meaningless. I have seen people leave high-paying jobs because the environment was toxic — and they described it as the best decision of their lives. Personally, I would not stay in a competitive, unhealthy environment for a 20% salary increase."
🏷 The nuanced structure (it depends on + however + personal limit) is the signature of B1.1 opinion expression. Students who answer only "yes" or "no" need to be pushed to add "but" or "however."
06
Medium
Some people work better under pressure. Do you agree? Give an example from your own experience.
Use a personal story: "There was a time when… and that experience taught me that…"
B1.1 Model Answer — "Working Under Pressure"
"I think there is a distinction between productive pressure and destructive pressure. I believe that short, defined deadline-driven moments can actually sharpen focus — you eliminate distractions and prioritize fast. But chronic, constant pressure with no recovery time is different — it leads to burnout, not performance. Personally, I experienced both. There was a product launch where I had three days to prepare a technical report that would normally take two weeks. The pressure was intense, but I was focused and proud of the result. That experience showed me that I can work well under pressure — but only if it is temporary, not a permanent condition."
🏷 "Distinction between X and Y" — a B1.1 analytical pattern that shows nuanced thinking. The personal anecdote demonstrates extended narrative — target: 60–75 seconds.
07
Advanced
In your opinion, what single factor makes a work environment truly motivating? Not just comfortable — truly motivating.
Challenge: go beyond the obvious answers (salary, good boss). Find the deeper factor.
B1.1 Model Answer — "The Motivating Factor"
"From my perspective, the single most motivating factor is a sense of meaning — the feeling that your work actually matters and that someone notices it. Not recognition in a superficial way — not just a certificate or a performance bonus. I think the real motivator is when your manager or your team shows that your specific contribution changed something: a decision, a result, a process. I feel strongly that people can tolerate a lot — a fast-paced environment, a structured culture, even long hours — as long as they feel that what they do is worth something. Without that sense of purpose, no amount of free coffee or team-building events will matter."
🏷 "Not X but Y" contrast + "People can tolerate X as long as Y" conditional = B1.1 rhetorical sophistication. The final punchline about "free coffee" shows personality — encourage students to have a distinctive voice.
08
Advanced
How has your idea of an ideal work environment changed since you started your career? What changed — and why?
Use: "When I first started… I thought… But now I think… because I've learned that…"
B1.1 Model Answer — "How My Views Changed"
"When I first started my career, I thought the most important thing was a prestigious company with a high salary. I wanted the title, the brand name, the sense of professional success. But over time, my priorities shifted completely. I now believe that the quality of the people around you matters far more than the company's reputation. A supportive, collaborative team in a medium-sized company is far more motivating than a toxic environment in a global corporation. Personally, I also learned that creative freedom — the ability to propose ideas and actually see them implemented — is something I cannot work without. It took a few years and a few mistakes to figure that out."
🏷 "When I first… but now I…" connects past and present perspective — naturally invites Present Perfect structures ("my priorities have shifted"). The final humility adds authenticity — push students toward this kind of honest voice.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical & Error Redirection
  • Never reveal model answers before the student attempts. The gap between what the student produces and what the model shows IS the learning. Even if their answer is shorter, that comparison creates the desire to improve.
  • Q1–Q3 (Basic): any B1.1 student should reach 2 sentences with a reason. If not — return to §2 vocabulary and repeat one round.
  • Q4–Q6 (Medium): look for topic sentence + 2 supporting reasons. If the student gives only 1: "Can you give me another angle? What else?"
  • Q7–Q8 (Advanced): look for personal narrative, nuanced contrasts, opinion structures that go beyond "I think X is good." Target for Q8: 60–90 seconds of connected speech.
  • Follow-up pattern for every card: after the student answers, ask ONE follow-up from Capa 2. This is the B1.1 conversation extension drill.
Capa 2 · Follow-Up Questions Bank
  • Q1: "Has that always been your preference? Can you think of a situation when the opposite worked better for you?"
  • Q3: "Of those 3 adjectives, which one is non-negotiable — you absolutely could not work without it?"
  • Q5: "What is the minimum salary you would accept to work in a great culture? And the maximum cultural tolerance for a great salary?"
  • Q7: "Has a manager ever made you feel truly motivated? What specifically did they do — one concrete action?"
  • Q8: "What do you think you will value in 10 more years? Is the trend continuing or do you see it reversing?"

Capa 1 · Pedagogical & Error Redirection
  • The Sentence Builder is not a completion exercise — it is a PRODUCTION scaffold. The goal is that the student says this sentence out loud, clearly, at natural speed, with confident intonation. That is the measurable output of §5.
  • Once built, ask the student to say the full sentence twice — first reading it, then without looking. The second attempt reveals whether the vocabulary is truly acquired or just read.
  • Then extend: "Now add one more clause: 'I prefer… because… and I think… is what makes me most… — and in my current workplace, that means…'" This bridge to their real context is the B1.1 production goal.
  • If the student built a sentence that reflects their real situation — keep a note. That sentence becomes the anchor for S2B, which extends opinion-giving to defended, extended spoken discourse.
Capa 2 · Transition to §SP
  • Bridge script: "You have just built a sentence that expresses your real professional values in English. In §SP, you will defend that position in a structured conversation — push back, disagree politely, and extend your answer. That is the next level."
Capa 3 · Value & Retention
  • "The phrases you practiced today — 'from my perspective', 'I feel strongly that', 'I prefer because' — appear in every English job interview, every performance review, every cross-cultural business conversation. You are building professional fluency, not just vocabulary."

Capa 1 · Pedagogical & Error Redirection
  • §SP is the core of the session. Everything before was preparation for this. Do not rush it or skip prompts for time — if needed, cut one Question Card from §4 instead.
  • Role-Play framing: when you argue the opposite position (devil's advocate), use phrases the student will likely need to counter — "But research shows that open offices increase collaboration" / "Most successful companies require 5-day office presence." This forces real B1.1 pushback language.
  • Key errors to watch for in SP: (a) "I don't agree" → upgrade to "I completely disagree" or "I'm afraid I don't agree with that." (b) "For me is important…" → "For me, it is important…" (c) Pausing and saying "¿Cómo se dice…?" in Spanish → redirect: "In English, what would be the closest word?" and wait.
  • If the student struggles with SP Card 2 (manager pushback), pivot to a safer version: "Imagine you are writing a polite email to your manager. What would you say?" — lower stakes, same vocabulary target.
  • SP Card 5 is the B1.1→B1.2 bridge: "If I were in your position" is second conditional — introduce it as a useful chunk, not grammar. Students who produce it spontaneously are ready for S2B advancement.
Capa 2 · Motivation & Engagement
  • After SP Card 4 (90-second pitch without looking): "That was your most complex English output today. Did you feel the difference? In S2B you'll do this for 2 minutes with pushback. That is the next level."
  • Vocabulary count celebration: after the challenge, tell the student how many vocab items you counted. "You used 6 today's words in 90 seconds. That's B1.1 mastery in action."
Capa 3 · Growth & Upsell
  • Post-SP upsell: "The skill you just practiced — expressing, defending, and extending your professional opinion in English — is what separates a B1 from a B2 speaker in job interviews and international meetings. S2B takes this to the next level. That's where you start talking for 3–4 minutes without support."
  • Testimonial trigger: "If this session felt more challenging than S1A, that's by design. You're being pushed toward B1.1 ceiling. Would you say your English confidence around work topics has improved since we started M2?"
