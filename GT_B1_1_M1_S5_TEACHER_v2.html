⚡ 8 Nouns — Conflict Vocabulary
conflict
noun
/ˈkɒnflɪkt/
A serious disagreement or clash between people with opposing views
"I've been in situations where conflict was avoided for so long it became much harder to resolve."
tension
noun
/ˈtɛnʃən/
The feeling of stress or strain between people — often before conflict breaks out
"I've learned to address tension early — waiting always makes it worse."
communication
noun
/kəˌmjuːnɪˈkeɪʃən/
The exchange of information — or the breakdown of it, which causes most conflicts
"I've found that most conflict I've experienced came from a breakdown in communication."
approach
noun
/əˈproʊtʃ/
The way you choose to deal with a situation or person
"I tried a direct approach — and I've learned it's not always the right one."
style
noun
/staɪl/
A characteristic way of doing things — especially how someone handles conflict
"My conflict style has changed — I've become more patient over the years."
solution
noun
/səˈluːʃən/
A way of resolving a problem or conflict that works for both sides
"We eventually found a solution — but I've regretted how long it took to get there."
situation
noun
/ˌsɪtʃuˈeɪʃən/
The set of circumstances at a specific moment — what was happening
"I've been in situations where I had to choose between being right and being effective."
outcome
noun
/ˈaʊtkʌm/
The result or consequence of a situation — what happened in the end
"The outcome wasn't perfect — but I've learned more from it than from my successes."
🔵 8 Verbs — Conflict Resolution
handle
verb
/ˈhændəl/
To deal with a situation — successfully or not
"Looking back, I've handled that situation better than I thought I could."
avoid
verb
/əˈvɔɪd/
To stay away from a situation — often making it worse over time
"I've learned not to avoid difficult conversations — they don't disappear."
confront
verb
/kənˈfrʌnt/
To address a situation or person directly — often uncomfortable but necessary
"I decided to confront the issue directly — I've found that approach builds more respect."
resolve
verb
/rɪˈzɒlv/
To find a satisfactory solution to a problem or conflict
"We resolved the tension by having a structured one-on-one — I've used that approach since."
manage
verb
/ˈmænɪdʒ/
To control or handle a difficult situation — sometimes without fully resolving it
"I managed the tension professionally — I've never let personal conflict affect my delivery."
address
verb
/əˈdrɛs/
To deal with something formally — more neutral than "confront"
"I tried to address the issue calmly — I've found that tone matters as much as content."
communicate
verb
/kəˈmjuːnɪkeɪt/
To express thoughts clearly — especially when it's difficult or sensitive
"I chose to communicate my concerns in writing first — I've found it reduces defensiveness."
disagree
verb
/ˌdɪsəˈɡriː/
To have a different opinion — the professional version of "not agreeing"
"I disagreed with his approach — but I've learned how to do that without it becoming personal."
💡 Register — "confront" vs "address" Both mean dealing with something directly. "Confront" = more confrontational, emotional register. "Address" = neutral, professional, controlled. In a performance review or feedback conversation: "address" is almost always better. "I decided to address the issue" sounds professional. "I decided to confront him" sounds dramatic.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "communicate" — appears in S1 (sharing info) and S5 (communicating in conflict). Point this out: "In S1 you learned 'communicate' as a general skill. Today it means communicating UNDER PRESSURE — same verb, higher stakes."
  • "approach" — noun and verb. "My approach was…" (noun) vs "I approached him" (verb). Both B1.1 appropriate. Drill both.
  • "outcome" — distinguish from "result." "Result" is neutral. "Outcome" often implies a process or decision. "The outcome of the conversation was…" sounds more reflective and professional.
  • Production: connect vocabulary to the student's story from §1. "Tell me about your conflict story — using 'tension', 'approach', and 'outcome' in your first three sentences."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Which of the 8 verbs best describes how you ACTUALLY handled your story? Pick one — and tell me what you did in one sentence."

GT Originals — No Wrong Answer in the Simulator
The Conflict Resolution Simulator has no "correct" approach — all three are valid in different situations. The point is the PP justification: "I've always found that…" / "I've been in situations where…" / "I've learned that…". Score only the PP quality, not the choice. Capa 1 · Pedagogical

S5 Grammar — The Three-Part Narrative Arc
1
🟢 Setup
Present Perfect
"Have you ever had to deal with a difficult colleague?"
"I've been in situations where tension was affecting the whole team."
"I've worked with people who were very different from me."
2
🟡 Action
Simple Past
"I decided to address it directly."
"I tried to stay calm and listen first."
"I asked for a private meeting."
"I chose not to react immediately."
3
🔵 Reflection
Present Perfect
"I've learned that tone matters more than content."
"What I've taken from that is patience."
"I've never forgotten how uncomfortable it felt."
"I've handled similar situations differently since."
💡 Why PP for Setup and Reflection?
Setup: "Have you ever had to deal with…?" uses PP because it's asking about life experience — no specific time.
Action: Simple Past for the SPECIFIC events — what you decided, tried, said. These happened at a specific moment.
Reflection: PP again — "I've learned" shows this lesson is still relevant NOW. It connects the past story to the present you.
❓
PP Setup — Open the story
"Have you ever had to deal with someone who…?"
"I've been in a situation where…"
"I've worked with someone who made things very difficult."
⚡
Simple Past — The action
"I tried to address it privately."
"I decided to stay calm."
"I chose to bring it to my manager."
"I asked for a direct conversation."
💡
PP Reflection — What you've taken
"I've learned that direct is usually better."
"I've understood that avoidance has a cost."
"I've never forgotten what that experience taught me."
🔄
The Full Arc — in one paragraph
"I've been in situations where tension was silently affecting the team. I decided to address it — I asked for a one-on-one. The outcome wasn't perfect, but I've learned that naming a problem is always better than pretending it doesn't exist."
⚠️ Most Common Errors — S5 Narrative
❌ "I have decided to address it." (the specific decision)→✅ I decided to address it. (specific action → Simple Past)
❌ "I have tried to stay calm at that moment."→✅ I tried to stay calm. (specific moment → Simple Past)
❌ "I learned that avoidance costs more." (current lesson)→✅ I've learned that avoidance costs more. (lesson still relevant now → PP)
❌ "Have you ever dealt with someone difficult in 2022?"→✅ Did you deal with someone difficult in 2022? (specific time → Simple Past Q)
✏️ Activity 1 — Narrative Tense: PP or Simple Past?
Choose the correct tense for each part of the story.
Story opener: "___ you ever had to handle a difficult situation with a colleague?"
Specific action: "I ___ to address the tension in a one-on-one meeting."
Reflection: "I ___ that addressing issues early always saves time in the long run."
Which sentence correctly mixes PP and Simple Past?
✏️ Activity 2 — True or False: Narrative Grammar
Are these statements about storytelling grammar correct?
"I decided to address it" uses Simple Past because this was a specific decision at a specific moment — not an ongoing pattern.
"I've learned from that experience" is wrong — since the experience is finished, it should be "I learned from it."
"Have you ever had to deal with…?" opens a story correctly — it uses PP to ask about life experience before a specific story follows.
In a narrative, ALL verbs should be Simple Past — mixing tenses is a grammar error.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q4 is the most complex: "I've been in that situation — I decided to stay calm and address it directly." This correctly uses PP for context (I've been) + Simple Past for actions (decided, address). The incorrect options both use PP for specific actions, which is wrong.
  • T2 is the critical conceptual error. "I learned" = that lesson is in the past and done. "I've learned" = that lesson is STILL relevant to who I am now. The PP reflection is what makes a story resonate — it connects past experience to present growth.
  • T4: explicitly addresses the misconception that narratives must be all Simple Past. B1.1 target = controlled tense mixing in storytelling.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Oral drill: I give you a verb — you tell me which part of the story it belongs to. 'Decided' → action. 'Have learned' → reflection. 'Have been in' → setup. 10 verbs, 5 seconds each."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "This three-part arc (PP setup → Simple Past action → PP reflection) is the structure of every good professional anecdote in English — in interviews, in speeches, in leadership conversations."

A professional narrates a real conflict — PP + Simple Past narrative arc in action

Miguel Vargas
Operations Manager · Vertex Solutions · Monterrey
LinkedIn Post
Watch for: PP setup (orange) · Simple Past actions (gold) · PP reflection (cyan) · S5 vocabulary (highlighted)

I've worked with a lot of different people over the years. Most I've gotten along with. But there was one colleague — I'll call him R — where the tension was there from the first week.

R and I communicated very differently. He was direct to the point of being abrupt. I preferred written communication and structured meetings. The conflict started small — he dismissed my ideas in team meetings, interrupted me, and took credit for work we'd done together. I've never reacted well to that kind of behaviour.

For three months, I avoided the situation. I told myself it would resolve on its own. It didn't. The tension grew until it was affecting the whole team's dynamic.

Finally, I decided to address it. I asked R for a private conversation. I didn't accuse him of anything — I described the impact his behaviour had had on me. He was surprised. He hadn't realised. We agreed on a different way of working together.

What I've taken from that experience is simple: avoidance is never a solution. I've learned to address tension early — not aggressively, but directly. And I've never regretted having a difficult conversation. I've always regretted not having one.

✏️ Comprehension — Narrative Arc
Answer using information from Miguel's post. Focus on which tense is used and why.
What did Miguel do before deciding to address the conflict — and for how long?
What approach did Miguel take in the private conversation with R?
"I decided to address it" uses Simple Past correctly — it describes a specific decision at a specific moment in the past.
"I've always regretted not having one" — this PP shows the regret is ongoing and applies to Miguel's whole career, not just this situation.
The text uses ONLY Simple Past — because the story is finished and all verbs should be in the past.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Color coding: orange = PP (setup + reflection), gold = Simple Past (actions), cyan = PP reflection. Ask student to count how many of each appear. PP: ~10 instances. Simple Past: ~12 instances. Both tenses working together = B1.1 narrative.
  • R3 is the critical comprehension trap: students who haven't internalized the PP/SP distinction will say TRUE. The text mixes both — that's the point. Use this to go back to §4's narrative arc if needed.
  • After comprehension: "Retell Miguel's story in your own words — without looking at the text. 60 seconds. Use 'I've been in a situation where…' to open." Then: "Now replace Miguel's story with YOURS."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Miguel says: 'I've always regretted not having a difficult conversation — never having one.' Do you agree? Have you ever regretted staying silent? Tell me."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Miguel's text is the model for §7's exit task storytelling. His structure: PP setup → Simple Past actions → PP reflection. Your story should follow the same arc."

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

I've managed teams for eight years, and in that time I've had to deal with a lot of difficult situations. But there's one I've never forgotten.

I had a team member — let's call her T — who was technically excellent. But she'd started to become disruptive in meetings. She interrupted people, dismissed ideas before they were finished, and made others reluctant to speak up. The tension was affecting the whole team.

I decided not to address it in a group setting. I asked T for a one-on-one. I didn't tell her she was being difficult — I described what I'd observed: specific behaviours, specific moments. I told her how it was affecting others. She was defensive at first. But she listened.

Over the next month, things changed. Not perfectly — but enough. She became more conscious of her impact.

What I've taken from that experience is this: the most effective conversations are specific, not general. "You're difficult" achieves nothing. "I've noticed that when you interrupt before someone finishes, it shuts them down" — that's something someone can act on. I've used that approach every time since. And I've never regretted it.

⏱ Target: 85–95 sec · PP: I've managed / I've had to / I've never forgotten / I've noticed / I've taken / I've used / I've never regretted · Simple Past: decided / asked / told / described / was / listened / changed

✏️ Listening Activities — Narrative Tense Focus
Listen and answer. Focus on WHICH tense is used for which part of the story.
What specific approach did the speaker take in her conversation with T?
What lesson has the speaker taken from the experience?
"I decided not to address it in a group setting" uses Simple Past — because this was a specific decision at a specific moment, not an ongoing pattern.
"I've never regretted it" — this PP negative means the speaker still feels this way now — the lesson continues to be true.
The speaker uses ONLY Simple Past throughout — because conflict stories should be entirely in the past tense.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical

  • L3 addresses the most common mistake: "all past = all Simple Past." The speaker uses BOTH — Simple Past for actions, PP for setup and reflection. Confirm with a second listen if needed.
