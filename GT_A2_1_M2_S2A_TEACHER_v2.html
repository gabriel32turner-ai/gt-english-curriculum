§ 01
Warm-Up — This or That?
5–7 min
Pick your side — no wrong answer. The teacher asks one follow-up question per choice. Use Simple Past to answer. This activates yesterday's irregular verbs while diagnosing how spontaneous the production is.
01 · How do you tell a story?
📝 Start at the beginning
from the very first thing that happened
OR
💡 Start with the result
bottom line first, then explain
Tell me about the last time you explained something at work. How did you start?
02 · After a trip or event
📧 Write an email
sent a follow-up or summary
OR
💬 Talked directly
told the team in person or on a call
What did you write or say? What was the most important thing to communicate?
03 · Last business event you attended
🤝 You talked a lot
networked, presented, made connections
OR
🎧 You mostly listened
attended sessions, took notes, observed
Who did you meet? What did you hear or learn?
04 · When something went wrong
🔧 Fix it immediately
took action right away
OR
📋 Plan before acting
thought it through first
What happened? What did you do first? And then what?
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • DIAGNÓSTICO: ¿ya usa "First… then…"? Si no, esta sesión se los da. Si sí, la sesión los lleva a "after that / subsequently / finally" con más precisión.
  • S2A introduce un set nuevo de irregulares + los conectores de secuencia. El alumno viene de S1A/B con los 12 core verbs. Aquí añades: buy/bought · bring/brought · think/thought · know/knew · tell/told · become/became · lose/lost · win/won · choose/chose · keep/kept.
  • Pair 04 es clave: la respuesta naturalmente usa "first… then…" — si el alumno lo produce espontáneamente, nota que ya tiene el patrón. Si no, eso es exactamente lo que §3 enseña.
  • Timing: 5–7 min. Muévete rápido. La densidad está en §2 y §3.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Interesante — en S1A y S1B ya narrabas lo que pasó. Hoy agregamos los conectores que hacen que tu historia fluya como un profesional. La diferencia entre 'it was good' y una narrativa que el cliente quiere escuchar."
Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
  • "Al final de S2A+S2B vas a poder escribir Y decir un trip report completo. Eso es comunicación corporativa real en inglés."

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • FOCO: buy/bought · bring/brought · think/thought — el patrón -ought es el más confuso para hispanohablante. Ponlos en un grupo y hazlos decirlos 3 veces en cadena. Sonido: /ɔːt/.
  • win/won — pronunciación trampa: "won" suena exactamente igual que "one". Señálalo explícitamente o creerán que es un error.
  • Los Pattern Groups son el sistema de memoria GT para irregulares. Úsalos como chunks mentales, no como lista individual. "¿Cuáles son los -ought?" → buy, bring, think. Drill 3 veces.
  • Micro-Speaking: escucha si usa los nuevos verbos O cae de vuelta a los de S1A/B. Los que no usa en micro-speaking son los que necesitan más drill en §4.
Capa 2 · Engagement

§ 03
Sequence Connectors — First, Then, After That, Finally
8–10 min
You already know the verbs. Now you need the connectors. These words turn a list of past actions into a professional narrative — the kind managers and clients want to hear.
The Business Story Timeline
1st
FIRST
opening action · sets the scene
2nd
THEN
next action · moves the story
3rd
AFTER THAT
another step · builds tension or progress
+
LATER
time has passed · new development
END
FINALLY
resolution · outcome · wrap-up
First
opening action
"First, I went to the client's office in León."
Then
next step
"Then, we had a meeting with their team."
After that
following step
"After that, they brought us to the plant."
Later
time passed
"Later, I thought about their proposal."
Finally
result / close
"Finally, we chose them as our supplier."
ConnectorPosition in storyBusiness example
FIRSTOpening — what happened first"First, I met the director and introduced the team."
THENNext action — no pause specified"Then, we gave a product overview. They asked questions."
AFTER THATFollowing step — implies a pause or new moment"After that, they brought us to the production floor."
LATERTime passed before next event"Later, the director told us they needed more time."
FINALLYLast step OR outcome / resolution"Finally, we sent the proposal and won the contract."
Key rule: All connectors below go at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a comma.
"First, I went… Then, we had… After that, they showed us… Finally, we agreed."
Note: "then" is informal and very flexible. "After that" sounds more professional. Both are correct.
⚡ Micro-Speaking — 2 minutes
Teacher says an event → Student builds a 4-sentence sequence using connectors:
"Tell me what happened at your last project meeting." → Student: "First… then… after that… finally…" — minimum 4 connectors, minimum 4 irregular verbs.
Push: "Now tell me what went wrong." → Student uses the negative: "didn't go / didn't know / didn't tell us / lost / chose the wrong option"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • El objetivo no es que memoricen "first/then/finally" — ya los saben. El objetivo es que los usen AUTOMÁTICAMENTE en producción oral. La tabla y el timeline son para la mente, el micro-speaking es para el hábito.
  • Error típico hispano: "And then and then and then…" — el alumno usa "and then" en loop en vez de variar. Corrección gentil: "Usa 'after that' aquí — suena más profesional."
  • "Finally" error: muchos alumnos lo usan para decir "por fin / at last" (con emoción positiva). En inglés es neutral — solo indica el último evento. Señálalo.
  • El timeline visual es crítico. Haz que el alumno señale en qué punto de su historia está cuando habla. Esto construye conciencia narrativa.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "¿Ves la diferencia entre 'I went, we met, we gave, we chose' y 'First I went. Then we met. After that we gave a presentation. Finally we chose'? La gramática es igual — el efecto es completamente diferente. Eso es narrativa profesional."
Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
  • "Estos conectores son los mismos que usarás en B1 para connected narrative y en B2 para academic writing. No los cambias — los desarrollas. Todo conecta."
§ 04
Grammar — Sequencing in Simple Past
10–12 min
The problem: after a trip, you have 6 things to say — but they come out as a random list. The tool: Simple Past + sequence connectors = a professional narrative that flows.
Structure: Ordered Narrative
First, [subject] + [irregular past] + [object]. → opening action
Then / After that, [subject] + [irregular past] + [complement]. → what happened next
Finally, [subject] + [irregular past / result]. → outcome or closing
Full example: "First, I went to the site and met the operations director. Then, they brought me to the production floor and I took notes. After that, we had lunch and talked about pricing. Finally, I chose to send a formal proposal — I thought they were the best option."
⚠ Most Common Errors — Sequence + Irregulars
❌ "I buyed the material."→✅ I bought the material.
buy is irregular — not buy+ed
❌ "She thinked it was too expensive."→✅ She thought it was too expensive.
think → thought (the -ought pattern)
❌ "And then and then we went."→✅ Then… / After that… / Later…
vary connectors — don't repeat "and then"
❌ "We didn't knew the answer."→✅ We didn't know the answer.
didn't + BASE FORM — always (even for irregular verbs)
❌ "Finally we choose them."→✅ Finally, we chose them.
past form: chose / don't forget the comma after "finally"
Activity 1 — Choose the Correct Form
Click the correct option.
She ___ the new supplier was competitive on price.
First, they ___ us to the main office. Then, we had the negotiation.
The team ___ the contract — the competitor was cheaper.
Finally, we ___ the local distributor as our partner.
The situation ___ critical — we needed a solution fast.
We ___ tell them about the delay — it was a difficult conversation.
Activity 2 — True or False: Sequence Rules
Is this statement correct?
"Finally" always implies a happy ending — a positive result.
"After that" sounds more formal and professional than "then".
Connectors go at the beginning of a sentence and need a comma after them.
"We didn't knew" is the correct negative of "we knew".
Buy, bring and think all share the same irregular pattern in the past.

      Spot as you read: new irregular past verbs · sequence connectors · time expressions · email structure (subject → summary → outcome → next steps)
      FromSofía Reyes <s.reyes@protechlatam.com> Tomanagement@protechlatam.com CCsales@protechlatam.com SubjectTrip Report — Guadalajara Trade Show & Supplier Meetings DateFriday, November 8, 2024
      Dear team,

      I'm back from Guadalajara. Here is a quick summary of what happened during the two-day trip.

      First, I went to the EXPO Guadalajara trade show on Wednesday morning. I knew several of the exhibitors from previous events, which made the conversations easier. I brought our new product catalog and gave a brief overview to three potential distributors. They all thought our pricing was competitive.

      Then, in the afternoon, I met with NovaTech — our potential new supplier. Their representative told me they chose to discontinue one of their product lines. This became an important factor for our decision. I kept notes throughout the entire meeting.

      After that, I went to dinner with the regional manager. He brought up the idea of a co-marketing agreement. I thought it was worth exploring. We didn't make any formal commitment — I told him I needed to consult with the team first.

      Finally, on Thursday morning, I visited one more exhibitor — a small tech startup. They won an award at the show for innovation. I understood why — their solution came with impressive specs and a strong support model. I took their contact details.

      Main outcome: We should follow up with NovaTech and the startup by next Wednesday. I will send a full written summary with recommendations by Monday.

      Best regards,
      Sofía
      Comprehension — Sequence & Details
      Click the correct answer.
      What did Sofía bring to the trade show?
      What important news did NovaTech tell Sofía?
      Why did the startup win an award?
      What are the next steps according to Sofía?
      How many sequence connectors can you find in the email?
      Capa 1 · Pedagogical
      • Dos lecturas. Primera: global — ¿qué pasó? Segunda: con marcadores — subrayar mentalmente los irregular verbs en azul y los conectores en verde.

      • Post-lectura oral: "¿Cuántos irregular verbs nuevos de §2 reconociste? thought / brought / knew / told / chose / became / kept / won / understood / came — 10 de los 12 están en el email."
      • Comprehension Q5: el alumno debe contar manualmente — esto activa análisis lingüístico activo, no solo comprensión pasiva.
      Capa 2 · Engagement
      • "Nota que Sofía usa First / Then / After that / Finally exactamente como los practicamos. Y el email tiene un resultado claro: 'Main outcome + next steps.' Así se escribe un trip report en inglés corporativo real."
      Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
      • "¿Cuántos trip reports escribes al año? Imagina escribir el próximo en inglés — con esta estructura. S2B lo convierte en un output real que puedes usar inmediatamente."

      DIALOGUE — DEBRIEF CALL — MARKERS SHOWN FOR TEACHER

      Marcos: Roberto, so — how did it go? Tell me everything.

      Roberto: OK, so. First, I went to their main office. Their operations director came to meet us at reception. Good start.

      Marcos: And the meeting itself?

      Roberto: Then we had a two-hour meeting. I brought the updated pricing sheet — they thought the numbers were fair. They also told us they chose us over two other suppliers.

      Marcos: That's excellent. What about the contract?

      Marcos: Good. So what happened in the end?

      Roberto: Finally, we didn't sign anything — but they understood our position. I sent a follow-up email the same evening. I kept it short and professional. I think we won the relationship, even if we didn't win the signature yet.

      Marcos: That works. Good job, Roberto.

      § 07
      Role Play — Debrief Interview
      8–10 min
      Role Play — Post-Event Debrief
      Situation: The student just returned from a business trip, trade show, or important client meeting. The teacher plays the manager/boss who wants a full update. The student must tell the story using: sequence connectors (First / Then / After that / Finally) + at least 4 irregular past verbs from today's session + the negative form ("didn't + base") at least once.
      Person A — Manager (Teacher)
      Ask about the trip. Use these questions to drive the story forward:
      • "So — how did it go? Tell me everything."
      • "What happened first? Who did you meet?"
      • "And then what? Did anything go wrong?"
      • "What did they think about the pricing / proposal?"
      • "Did you win the contract / deal? What are the next steps?"
      • "How long did the whole thing take?"
      Person B — You (Student)
      Tell the story of your trip. Use this structure:
      • "First, I went to [place] and [met/gave/had…]"
      • "Then, we [talked/brought/thought/chose…]"
      • "After that, [a problem or a development]"
      • "I didn't [know/tell/sign] — because…"
      • "Finally, [result or next steps]"
      Teacher success criteria: 4+ irregular past verbs used correctly · All 4 sequence connectors used · 1+ negative form · Story flows without stopping · Minimum 7–8 sentences · Duration: 3–4 minutes student speaking time.
      Optional Extension Game — 5 min
      Verb Chain Story


      Format: Random Wheel or Open the Box — each card shows a new irregular verb. Student must continue the story using that verb in the correct past form + a sequence connector.

      Example: Card shows "become" → Student: "After that, the situation became more complicated because…"
      Challenge mode: no pausing — instant production.
      Works for 1-on-1 and group sessions. For groups: each student picks up the story from where the last one left off. Fastest, most accurate storyteller wins.
      Capa 1 · Pedagogical
      • El role play de §7 es el punto de evaluación de S2A. Si el alumno usa 4+ irregulares + conectores con fluidez → listo para S2B. Si no → §3 necesita refuerzo antes de avanzar.
      • No interrumpas en mitad del relato. Escucha completo, anota errores, da feedback al final. Feedback sandwich: Strength → Error → Fix → Strength.
      • Si el alumno se queda atascado: tu pregunta de rescate es "And then what?" — activa el siguiente conector naturalmente.
      • El scenario puede ser real (su último viaje) o imaginado. Permite ambos — lo importante es la estructura narrativa, no la veracidad.
      Capa 2 · Engagement
      • "Este role play es exactamente la conversación que tendrías con tu director después de un viaje. No es práctica artificial — es el inglés que necesitas el lunes."
      Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
      • "En S2B lo llevas al nivel siguiente: storytelling más complejo, más verbos, más conectores, debate de estrategia. Y el output final es un email real que puedes mandar."
      • Testimonial trigger: "¿Cuál de los verbos nuevos de hoy — bought, brought, thought, knew, told, chose — ya vas a usar esta semana?" — Ancla la retención + genera conversación de upsell orgánico.
