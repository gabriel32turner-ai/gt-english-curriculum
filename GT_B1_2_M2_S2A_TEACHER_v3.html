§ 01
Warm-Up — The Salary Taboo
5–7 min
Quick Fire — Answer immediately. No prep.
"Do you know what your colleagues earn? Do they know what you earn?"
"Has anyone ever asked you directly how much you make? What did you say?"
"Imagine you found out your colleague doing the exact same job earns 30% more. What would you do?"
"Is salary a taboo topic in your workplace? Why?"
This session's speaking topic
"Should companies be required to share salary information openly?"
By the end of this session, you'll debate this topic for 2–3 minutes using 8+ vocabulary words, Reported Speech questions, and at least 2 colloquial phrases. Mexico's most emotionally charged workplace topic.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • This is genuinely the most emotionally loaded topic of M2. In Mexican corporate culture, salary is extremely taboo — more so than in the US or Europe. Students will feel real discomfort and real passion. Both are pedagogically valuable.
  • Note the student's INITIAL POSITION right now before any vocabulary. Pro-transparency? Pro-privacy? Neutral? Write it on the board. At §7 ask: "Has today's session shifted your thinking at all?" The contrast is powerful.
  • The warm-up questions naturally generate Reported Speech context: "Someone asked me how much I made and I said..." — bank these sentences. You'll use them in §5: "Let's put that into proper Reported Speech."
  • "Found out your colleague earns 30% more" — this is the most combustible question. If the student has a real story, let it breathe for 90 seconds. Real narrative = real RS practice.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "This session is about something you deal with at work but never talk about in English — because you barely talk about it in Spanish either. Today you're going to have that conversation."
Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
  • "Salary negotiation, asking for a raise, responding to an offer in English — these require exactly the vocabulary and grammar from today. Reported Speech questions are what you use in formal conversations: 'He asked me if I was satisfied with my compensation.' That's a real sentence from a real evaluation meeting."
§ 02
Speaking Topic — Salary Transparency
8–10 min
M2 · S2 — Money, Power & Choices TABOO TOPIC
"Should we talk about salaries openly?
Transparency: right or invasion of privacy?"
First answer: no preparation, no vocabulary help. Just your honest position right now. This topic has no correct answer — real opinions only.
Push the conversation — 3 rounds
Round 1 — Your personal experience: Have you ever talked about salary with a colleague? Did someone ask you? Did you share? What happened, or why did you avoid it?
Round 2 — Reported Speech bridge: What do people in your company say when the topic of salary comes up? What has your manager told you (or not told you) about pay? What have colleagues asked you? Report the actual conversations.
Round 3 — The big question: Is salary secrecy fair — or does it protect a pay gap? If your company disclosed all salaries tomorrow, what do you think would happen? Would it solve problems or create them?
B1.2 Model Answer — "Salary Transparency" · 2.5–3 min · Natural & Real
"Honestly, salary transparency makes me uncomfortable — but I think that's exactly the problem. The discomfort itself tells you something is wrong with the system.

In my company, salary is completely secret. No one discloses anything. A colleague once told me she had been trying to negotiate a raise for months. She said her manager had told her the budget was limited and asked her if she was satisfied with her current package. That's such a mood — because she clearly wasn't, but what can you say? He asked her if she had any offers from other companies. She mentioned she had been looking around. They gave her a 5% increase the next week. I'm not gonna lie — that told her everything about how the system works.

The issue with full transparency is resentment. If I know a colleague with the same experience and the same role earns more, that creates tension — but it also reveals a pay gap that probably already exists. Fairness requires information. You can't claim there's equity if no one can check.

My position? I think companies should at least disclose salary standards by role — not individual numbers, but ranges. That way you know if your negotiation has any real power. Right now, it's a game where only one side has the data. Fair enough? No. Not at all."
🏷 Colour key: teal = S2 vocab · cyan = Reported Speech · gold = colloquial phrases · Count: 9 vocab words, 5 RS uses (told me / said / asked / mentioned / told her), 3 colloquial phrases
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.2 diagnostic: student speaks FIRST. No vocabulary yet. The initial position — pro-transparency or pro-privacy — is less important than the quality of the reasoning. Target: 45 seconds minimum unprompted.
  • Round 2 is the RS gold mine. "What has your manager told you about pay?" → Any answer involving reported conversation is pre-loaded RS. Write the key verb on the board: "He TOLD me…" "She ASKED me…" — you'll formalise this in §5.
  • If student has a real salary resentment story — LET IT RUN. Real emotion + real narrative = the most authentic RS practice of the session. Don't interrupt for corrections; note them for after.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "This is the conversation you've had in Spanish a thousand times. Today you're going to have it in English — and by the end, you'll have the exact language to negotiate, push back, and report what happened, precisely."

Vocabulary — 10 Nouns · 10 Verbs · Workplace & Money
10–12 min
B1.2 rule: Every word is usable in today's debate. After each word, produce one sentence from your own workplace experience — real company, real situation.
10 Nouns — Workplace & Money
salary/ˈsæləri/
fixed regular payment for a job — "Salary discussions are completely avoided at my company."
transparency/trænsˈpærənsi/
openness; sharing information that others can see — "Salary transparency means everyone knows what everyone earns."
pay gap/ˈpeɪ ɡæp/
the difference in pay between groups — "The gender pay gap is often hidden behind salary secrecy."
equity/ˈekwɪti/
fairness; everyone being treated justly — "Pay equity requires data — you can't be fair if you hide the numbers."
negotiation/nɪˌɡəʊʃiˈeɪʃn/
a discussion to reach an agreement — "She went into the negotiation without knowing the salary range."
resentment/rɪˈzentmənt/
bitter feeling of being treated unfairly — "Finding out a colleague earns more creates real resentment."
standard/ˈstændərd/
a level of quality or pay used as a norm — "What's the market standard for this role in Mexico?"
disclosure/dɪˈskləʊʒər/
the act of making information public — "Salary disclosure is mandatory in some countries."
fairness/ˈfeənəs/
the quality of being just and unbiased — "Fairness is impossible when only management knows the numbers."
comparison/kəmˈpærɪsn/
looking at two things to see similarities or differences — "Salary comparison sites have changed how people negotiate."
10 Verbs — Salary & Negotiation
disclose/dɪˈskləʊz/
to make information known — "The company refused to disclose the salary bands."
compare/kəmˈpeər/
to look at similarities and differences — "People compare salaries on LinkedIn and Glassdoor all the time."
negotiate/nɪˈɡəʊʃieɪt/
to discuss to reach an agreement — "She had never negotiated her salary — she just accepted the first offer."
earn/ɜːn/
to receive money for work — "What someone earns should reflect what they contribute."
hide/haɪd/
to keep secret — "Companies hide salary information to keep their power in negotiations."
reveal/rɪˈviːl/
to make something known — "He revealed his salary and everyone was surprised."
demand/dɪˈmɑːnd/
to ask firmly, as if it's a right — "Employees are starting to demand pay transparency."
justify/ˈdʒʌstɪfaɪ/
to give a good reason for something — "How can HR justify paying two people differently for the same work?"
discuss/dɪˈskʌs/
to talk about something openly — "We never discuss salaries — it's an unspoken rule."
close (the gap)/kləʊz ðə ɡæp/
to reduce the difference — "Transparency is the only way to close the pay gap."
⚡ B1.2 Rapid Personalisation: For each word → produce a sentence using a real situation from your workplace or professional life. "My manager told me the company couldn't justify a higher salary right now…" / "She asked if I had researched the market standard…" — This is pre-loading Reported Speech questions before §5.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • 20 words in ~12 minutes. The Rapid Personalisation technique generates RS naturally: "My manager told me..." / "She asked me if..." — note these sentences on the board. In §5 you'll convert them formally.
  • "equity" vs "equality" — worth 30 seconds. Equality = same for everyone. Equity = fair for everyone based on context. In salary debates, equity is the more sophisticated concept. B1.2 students should use it precisely.
  • "resentment" — emotionally loaded. Ask: "Have you ever felt this at work? You don't have to say why — just: have you?" Even a nod is enough to make the vocabulary real.
  • "close the gap" — teach as a chunk. Students often try to construct this from parts. "Close the gap" is fixed. Also: "bridge the gap" works the same way.
§ 04
Colloquial English — 5 Phrases · Module 2
7–8 min
Same 5 Module 2 phrases — now in a salary context. B1.2 target: each phrase feels completely natural in a workplace conversation.
"I'm not gonna lie."
Signals an honest, possibly uncomfortable truth is coming. Disarms the other person. Very natural in opinion conversations.
"I'm not gonna lie — when I found out what he was earning, I was furious. Same role, same years of experience, different number."
"That's such a mood."
Expresses deep relating to a feeling or situation — "I completely understand that feeling." Originally Gen Z, now widely used by professionals.
"She said she felt invisible whenever salary review came around. That's such a mood — I've been there."
"Fair enough."
Accepts a point as reasonable, even if you don't fully agree. Concedes without losing your position. Essential for mature debates.
"He said companies need salary secrecy to avoid internal conflict. Fair enough — but that same secrecy hides discrimination."
"No way."
Strong disbelief or refusal. Tone determines whether it's positive surprise or flat rejection.
"They asked me to justify why I deserved more without telling me what anyone else earns? No way is that a fair negotiation."
"It adds up."
The cumulative effect of small things becomes significant. Used for both financial and emotional accumulation.
"A 5% difference seems small. But over 10 years, in pension contributions, in bonuses? It adds up. That's the real cost of the pay gap."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Same 5 phrases as S1A — now in salary context. If the student used them in S1A, push for nuance here: "You used 'fair enough' before — but in salary debates, it's especially powerful because it concedes while holding your ground. Show me how."
  • "It adds up" works brilliantly for the pay gap argument — teach the mathematical + emotional double meaning: salary difference accumulates financially, and resentment accumulates emotionally. Both meanings in one phrase.
  • "Fair enough" is a B1.2 concede-and-counter structure. Ask: "Can you concede something to the other side using 'fair enough' and then push back?" This is debate training embedded in vocabulary practice.
§ 05
Grammar — Reported Questions & Commands
10–12 min
Real Situation — Performance Review
Your manager asks: "Are you satisfied with your current salary? Do you have any offers from other companies?"

Later you tell your partner about it. How do you report those questions in natural English?
❌ Wrong: "He asked me 'are you satisfied?'"
✅ Right: "He asked me if I was satisfied with my salary."
Reported Speech — Questions & Commands
Rule 1 — Yes/No questions: Direct question → reported with if or whether. Question order becomes statement order. Tenses shift back one step.
"Are you happy?" → She asked me if I was happy.
"Do you have other offers?" → He asked whether I had other offers.
Rule 2 — Wh- questions: Keep the question word. Same tense shift. No "did" in reported form.
"Why did you leave?" → He asked me why I had left.
"What do you earn now?" → She asked what I earned. (NOT: what did I earn)
Rule 3 — Commands & requests: Use told me to or asked me to + base verb. Not "said me to."
"Please don't discuss this." → She asked me not to discuss it.
"Send your figures by Friday." → He told me to send my figures by Friday.
Direct questions
"Are you satisfied?"→asked if I was satisfied
"Do you have offers?"→asked if I had offers
"Why did you leave?"→asked why I had left
"What do you earn?"→asked what I earned
Commands & requests
"Don't share this."→told me not to share it
"Send your CV."→asked me to send my CV
"Accept the offer."→told me to accept the offer
"Come to HR first."→told me to come to HR first
Top Errors — Reported Questions
✗ "He asked me 'are you happy?'"→✓ He asked me if I was happy.
Never quote directly in reported speech — convert the tense and use if/whether.
✗ "She asked what did I earn."→✓ She asked what I earned.
No auxiliary "did" in reported Wh- questions — statement word order only.
✗ "He said me to send the report."→✓ He told me to send the report.
"Said" cannot take an object person. Commands use "told me to" or "asked me to."
✗ "She asked me whether I am satisfied."→✓ She asked me whether I was satisfied.
Tense shifts back: am → was / do → did / will → would / have → had.
Activity A — Transform to Reported Speech
Choose the correct reported version of each direct question.
Direct: HR asked: "Do you know what your colleagues earn?"
Direct: My manager said: "Don't discuss your salary with anyone."
Direct: The recruiter asked: "Why are you looking for a new job?"
Direct: She said: "Send me your salary expectations before the interview."
Activity B — Correct or Incorrect?
Identify whether each reported speech sentence is grammatically correct.
"He asked me what salary did I expect."
"She told me not to share the information with anyone."
"My manager asked whether I was considering other offers."
"They said me to justify my request for a higher salary."
"The recruiter asked what I had been doing in my previous role."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • S2A extends RS into questions and commands — the natural next step from S1A statements. The real-world context (salary review, job interview) makes the grammar immediately functional, not academic.
  • The hardest error: "He asked what did I earn" — students apply direct question word order. Counter it physically: write the direct question, cross out "did", rewrite in statement order. "What did I earn?" → "what I earned." Make the contrast visual.
  • Commands: "told me to" vs "asked me to" — the distinction is tone/formality. "Told me to" = instruction from authority. "Asked me to" = polite request. In salary contexts, both are common. Teach the nuance: "Which one would your manager use?"
  • Bridge after activities: "Give me one real RS question from your life at work — something someone asked you about your job, your salary, your plans. Formal Reported Speech. Go." That's the activation.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Reported Speech questions are the grammar of job interviews, salary negotiations, and performance reviews. Every time you report what HR asked, what your recruiter said, what your manager wanted to know — this is what you're using."

MONOLOGUE — HR MANAGER ON SALARY TRANSPARENCY EXPERIMENT — NATURAL B1.2 PACE

"I'm going to tell you about the most uncomfortable week I've had as an HR manager. Our company decided to run a salary transparency experiment — not full disclosure, just salary bands by role. We thought it would be straightforward.

It wasn't. The first thing that happened: people started asking questions. My manager asked me how I had planned to communicate the bands. My director asked whether I thought the team was ready for it. A colleague asked what I had told the engineers specifically. I'm not gonna lie — I hadn't fully thought through those conversations.

The data revealed a real pay gap. Two people in the same role, same years of experience, different salaries. One of them came to me and asked why the comparison showed such a large difference. I had no good answer. I asked her if she had spoken to her manager. She said her manager had told her not to discuss individual salaries. That's such a mood — that's exactly the problem, isn't it?

Eventually, the company asked HR to justify every salary difference. They told us to produce a report on equity within 30 days. Fair enough — it was overdue. The experience was uncomfortable. But it started a real conversation about fairness. It adds up — the cost of keeping information hidden isn't just financial. It's trust."

The student has vocabulary (§3), colloquial phrases (§4), Reported Speech questions and commands (§5), and a real HR story (§6). This is the full 2–3 minute answer. No notes. Real opinions. Natural English.

Are you pro-transparency or pro-privacy? What makes salary secrecy harmful — or necessary? Use: transparency, pay gap, equity, fairness, resentment.
