§ 01
Warm-Up — Scenario
5–8 min
📸 Office Dilemma — Monday Morning

Imagine: You're in a performance review and your manager asks: "What values have guided your professional decisions this year? Tell me — not the company's values, yours."

S1 review first: say 2 things about your professional identity using Present Perfect. Then: what's one value you've always protected at work?

📌 Teacher Follow-Up Questions
"Have you ever had to choose between two of your values? What happened?"
"Has your sense of what matters at work changed since you started your career?"
"What have you learned about professional ethics from a difficult situation?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q1 (S1 review) is a checkpoint — student should produce PP spontaneously. If they say "I always was…" model: "Can you say 'I've always been…'?" Don't over-correct; just model and move on.
  • The value question is a linguistic bridge to §3's Trade-Off Challenge. Listen: does the student try to justify with "I've always believed…" or "I've been in situations where…"? This tells you how ready they are for production in §3.
  • B1.1 push: "What have you learned?" forces reflection rather than description. Push beyond "I learned that honesty is important" to "I've learned that honesty builds trust — and I've seen what happens when that trust breaks down."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Today's session shifts from describing what you DO to expressing what you BELIEVE. 'I've always believed that integrity is non-negotiable' is significantly harder than 'I work in sales.' This is the linguistic step between A2.2 and B1.1."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In S2B you'll defend your values in a live debate format — no notes, no scaffold. Today is the foundation."

Vocabulary — Values & Professional Ethics

8 Nouns — Core Professional Values
integrity
noun
/ɪnˈtɛɡrɪti/
The quality of being honest and having strong moral principles
"I've never compromised my integrity — not once in fifteen years."
fairness
noun
/ˈfɛərnəs/
The quality of treating people equally, without bias or favoritism
"I've experienced unfairness at work — and I've seen what it does to a team's morale."
loyalty
noun
/ˈlɔɪəlti/
A strong feeling of support or allegiance to a person, team, or organization
"I've always valued loyalty — but I've learned it has limits."
ambition
noun
/æmˈbɪʃən/
A strong desire to succeed, achieve, or reach a goal
"I've always had ambition — but I've had to learn that ambition without integrity becomes toxic."
recognition
noun
/ˌrɛkəɡˈnɪʃən/
Acknowledgment or appreciation for someone's work or contribution
"I've worked in environments where effort was never recognized — that's never deserved."
respect
noun
/rɪˈspɛkt/
Admiration for someone's qualities or achievements; treating others with dignity
"I've built every meaningful relationship in my career on mutual respect."
trust
noun
/trʌst/
Firm belief in the reliability or truth of someone or something
"I've seen what happens when trust breaks down in a team — it's very hard to rebuild."
balance
noun
/ˈbæləns/
A situation in which different elements are equal or in the correct proportions
"I've sacrificed balance for ambition before — I've learned the hard way it's not sustainable."
8 Verbs — Professional Ethics in Action
value
verb
/ˈvæljuː/
To consider something important or beneficial
"I've always valued transparency over comfort."
believe
verb
/bɪˈliːv/
To have a firm conviction or opinion
"I've always believed that people perform better when they feel respected."
prioritize
verb
/praɪˈɒrɪtaɪz/
To treat something as more important than other things when forced to choose
"I've had to prioritize fairness over speed — it's not always popular."
appreciate
verb
/əˈpriːʃɪeɪt/
To recognize the value or quality of something
"I've learned to appreciate feedback — even when it's hard to hear."
expect
verb
/ɪkˈspɛkt/
To regard something as likely or required from others
"I've always expected honesty from the people I work with — it's a baseline."
deserve
verb
/dɪˈzɜːrv/
To have earned or be worthy of something
"I've always believed that honest effort deserves recognition — always."
reward
verb
/rɪˈwɔːrd/
To give something in return for good work or behavior
"The best companies I've worked for have rewarded effort, not just results."
respect
verb
/rɪˈspɛkt/
To admire and consider someone's feelings or boundaries
"I've worked for managers who respected boundaries — and some who didn't."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "trust" appeared in S1 as a verb and appears here as a noun. "I trust my team" (verb) vs "There's a lot of trust in my team" (noun) — same word, different role.
  • "prioritize" vs "prefer": these are not synonyms. "I prefer fairness" (I like it more) vs "I prioritize fairness" (I choose it first when I have to choose). Drill the distinction.
  • Production task: "Which 3 verbs have been most relevant to your professional life? Make a true PP sentence with each." Target: "I've always valued / I've had to prioritize / I've never compromised."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Look at the 8 nouns. Rank your top 3 personal values — just in your head. Now: tell me which one you've had to FIGHT for at some point in your career."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "These 8 nouns are the vocabulary of executive leadership conversations in English. When a company talks about its 'values' — integrity, trust, fairness — this is the language they use."

GT Originals — Values as Professional Self-Awareness
At B1.1, students shift from describing what they DO to expressing what they BELIEVE. "I've always believed that integrity is non-negotiable" is significantly harder to produce than "I work in sales." The Trade-Off Challenge forces exactly this shift.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Trade-Off Challenge: marca ✓ PP Correct solo cuando el alumno produce una oración PP gramaticalmente correcta con contenido genuino. "I believe integrity" = no es PP. "I've always believed that integrity matters more than short-term recognition" = SÍ.
  • Target starters: "I've always…" / "I've been in situations where…" / "I've had to…" / "I've learned that…" / "I've seen what happens when…" / "I've prioritized…"
  • Dilemma 3 (Ambition vs Balance) suele generar las respuestas más personales — toca decisiones profesionales reales. Deja que la conversación se desarrolle si el alumno tiene una experiencia fuerte.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "5 dilemmas — no right answers. I'm interested in WHY you chose what you chose. Each justification must use 'I've'. Ready? Dilemma 1."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "The language you just used — 'I've always believed…' / 'I've had to prioritize…' — is exactly the language of a B1.1 professional in an English-speaking environment."
§ 04
Grammar — Present Perfect Extended
10–12 min
S2A Focus — Questions, Reflection & Difficult Choices
Experience questions
"Have you ever had to choose between two values?"
"What have you learned about yourself professionally?"
"How has your approach to [X] changed?"
Duration + values
"I've valued integrity for my entire career."
"I've believed in fairness since I started working."
"I've prioritized balance for the last three years."
Never / Ever
"I've never worked somewhere without trust."
"I've never compromised my integrity."
"Have you ever had to put loyalty above fairness?"
Conviction + PP
"I've always believed that honesty matters most."
"I've found that respect is non-negotiable."
"I've learned that ambition alone isn't enough."
The "What have you learned…?" pattern — key at B1.1:
This PP question forces reflection on experience — the bridge between describing the past and drawing conclusions. "What have you learned about leadership?" · "How has your understanding of fairness changed?" · "What has this experience taught you?"
⚠ New Error Patterns — S2A
✗ "I always believed in integrity."→✓ I've always believed in integrity. (ongoing conviction = PP)
✗ "I have never compromised since always."→✓ I've never compromised my values. (never = no additional time reference needed)
✗ "I've prioritized balance during my whole career."→✓ I've prioritized balance throughout my career. ("throughout" = more natural)
✗ "What did you learn about integrity?"→✓ What have you learned about integrity? (ongoing lesson = PP)
Present Perfect vs Simple Past — Values Context
✅ Present Perfect — ongoing relevance
"I have always believed that integrity matters."
Conviction still true today
"I have learned that ambition alone isn't enough."
Lesson applies now
"I have never regretted being transparent."
Pattern across career — still true
"Have you ever had to choose between loyalty and fairness?"
Experience at any point in life
⏰ Simple Past — completed, specific moment
"In 2018, I chose fairness over team loyalty."
Specific past decision — finished
"Last year, I learned this lesson the hard way."
Specific past event — closed
"She never prioritized balance in her first job."
Pattern in a past period — over
"Did you have to make that decision last quarter?"
Specific past timeframe
The key question: Is the experience / conviction still relevant NOW? → Present Perfect  |  Is it tied to a specific finished moment? → Simple Past
"I've always believed…" (PP — belief continues) vs "I believed that at the time" (SP — past phase, different now)
Activity 1 — Choose the Correct Form
Select the correct Present Perfect form in each values-based sentence.
"I ___ always believed that loyalty and integrity go together."
"What ___ you learned about fairness from your current role?"
"She ___ compromised her integrity — not once in fifteen years."
"I've valued balance ___ I realised I was burning out."
"Have you ever ___ loyalty above fairness at work?"
Activity 2 — Transform the Sentence
Rewrite each sentence using Present Perfect. Di tu respuesta en voz alta, luego revela el modelo.
→ Change to Present Perfect
Simple Past: "She learned that recognition matters more than salary."
✓ She has learned that recognition matters more than salary.
"has learned" implies the lesson is still relevant now — not just a past event.
→ Change to Present Perfect negative
Simple Past: "He never prioritized balance in his previous company."
✓ He has never prioritized balance in any previous company.
Or: "He's never prioritized" — contracted form is more natural in speech.
→ Change to Present Perfect question
Simple Past: "Did you ever have to choose between loyalty and fairness?"
✓ Have you ever had to choose between loyalty and fairness?
"Have you ever had to…?" — a very natural PP question for professional experience. Drill this pattern.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Activity 2 (transformation) requires active production, not just recognition. This reflects the gradual complexity increase across M1 sessions.
  • Transform Item 3: "Have you ever had to choose…?" — "had to" + base verb inside PP. Students often forget "had to" and say "Have you ever choose?" Drill: "Have you ever HAD TO + base verb?"
  • After activities: oral production round. Give a value word + "for/since" → student produces the PP duration sentence. "Integrity — for your whole career." → "I've valued integrity for my whole career." 5 words → 10 seconds each.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Transformation: te doy una oración Simple Past sobre un valor — tú la conviertes a Present Perfect. 10 segundos por oración. ¿Listo?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "'What have you learned…?' appears in 360-degree performance reviews, in leadership coaching, and in English-language job interviews. This pattern is genuinely useful."
§ 05
Reading — The Values I've Lived By
8–10 min
Elena Fuentes
Senior Consultant · Global Advisory Group · Dallas
LinkedIn Article
Watch for: Present Perfect (green) · Simple Past (violet) · negatives (red) · duration FOR/SINCE (gold)

People often ask me what I've learned after fifteen years in consulting. The honest answer is: it's less about technical skills and more about values.

Early in my career, I thought ambition was the most important thing. I have spent years chasing promotions, titles, and recognition. And I have achieved some of those things — but at a cost I didn't fully appreciate at the time.

What I have learned is that integrity isn't just a value — it's a professional asset. I have built every meaningful client relationship in my career on honesty, even when that honesty was uncomfortable. I have never regretted being transparent. I have regretted the times I wasn't.

The second thing I have learned is about fairness. I have managed teams for over a decade, and I have seen clearly what happens when a manager applies rules inconsistently. People don't leave companies — they leave managers who don't respect them. That's something I have never forgotten.

The value I have struggled most with is balance. I have worked on this since 2019, when I realised that my health and my relationships were suffering. The question I now ask in every professional decision is: "Will I be proud of this in ten years?" That's not a perfect filter — but it has never led me completely wrong.

Comprehension — Values + Present Perfect
Answer using information from Elena's article.
By how much has Elena exceeded her operational efficiency targets this year?
What has Elena prioritized over ambition — and since when?
Elena has always regretted being transparent — honesty caused her professional problems.
"I have worked on this since 2019" uses PP + since because the action started in 2019 and continues now.
"I have spent years chasing promotions" means the behavior is still happening right now.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Green = present perfect. Red = negatives. Gold = duration (for/since). Ask student to count all PP uses before answering comprehension questions. Count: ~12+ instances.
  • T3 is critically important: "I have spent years chasing promotions" — this is PP describing a PAST pattern that is no longer ongoing. PP does NOT always mean "still happening now." The learning/reflection framing is what makes it PP, not current activity.
  • After reading: "Write 3 sentences about YOUR professional year using the same patterns — one with 'have learned', one with 'have never', one with 'for/since'."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Elena's question: 'Will I be proud of this in ten years?' Have you ever used a filter like that? What's yours?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In S2B you'll write and deliver YOUR version of this reflection — live, spoken, to the teacher acting as your interviewer. That is the B1.1 milestone for this session."

Listening — A Values Conversation
10–12 min
🎧
Leadership Podcast — "The Values That Have Shaped My Career"
B1.1 · Single speaker · ~100 seconds · American English

🔈 Audio placeholder. Reemplaza con embed de Google Drive.
ElevenLabs voice: Sarah · Rate: 0.85 · Stability: 0.62

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

I've been asked this question many times: what values have shaped your career? And I've always found it hard to give a short answer — because the honest answer is: they've changed.

Early on, I prioritized ambition. I wanted to move fast, prove myself, get recognized. And for a while, that worked. But I've learned — the hard way — that ambition without integrity leads you somewhere you don't want to be.

The value I've committed to most in the last ten years is fairness. Not because it's easy — it isn't. I've had to make decisions that were fair but unpopular. I've lost short-term goodwill to protect long-term trust. I've never regretted those decisions. I've regretted the moments I wasn't fair — they stay with you.

The question I've found most useful in any professional dilemma is: "What would I do if integrity was the only consideration?" It's a simple filter — but it has never led me wrong.

⏱ Target: 95–110 sec · Voice: Sarah · Speed: 0.85 · PP count: 12+ instances · Include: I've learned / I've committed / I've had to / I've never regretted / I've found

Listening Activities
Listen, then answer.
What value has the speaker committed to most in the last ten years?
What has the speaker learned "the hard way" about ambition?
"I've never regretted those decisions" — this PP negative shows the speaker's position is consistent and still true now.
The speaker says the decision filter "What would I do if integrity was the only consideration?" has sometimes led them wrong.
"I've had to make decisions that were fair but unpopular" — "had to" inside PP means this was a requirement, not a free choice.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Listen 1: global — what's the overall message? Listen 2: comprehension questions. Listen 3 optional: count every PP use (12+ in ~100 seconds = real executive English density).
  • LQ2 tests understanding of the "ambition without integrity" insight — students who hear only the ambition word miss the lesson embedded in the PP structure.
  • After: "Give me 60 seconds about YOUR values journey — using at least 4 PP sentences. Model it on the speaker's structure: early on I prioritized X / I've learned that / I've never regretted / I've found."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "That speech is 100 seconds. Count: 12+ present perfect uses. That's the density of real executive English on the topic of values. You understood all of it."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In S2B you'll deliver your own version of this speech — your values, your career, your language. 90 seconds, 6 PP uses minimum."
