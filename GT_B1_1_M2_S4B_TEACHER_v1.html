Debate Round
Top-down vs Bottom-up culture change — assigned sides
15 MIN
📋 Debate topic: "Culture change must come from leadership — or it never happens." Teacher assigns Side A or Side B. You argue your assigned position, even if you personally disagree. 2-minute pitch each side, then open debate.
✅ SIDE A — Leadership drives culture
Culture starts at the top. I've never seen a company with a toxic CEO that had a healthy culture — it just doesn't happen.
Managers model behaviour. If leadership has already normalized overwork, the team has already accepted it. It's invisible.
Resources come from the top. Psychological safety programmes, recognition systems — they need budget. Leadership either funds them or doesn't.
Accountability flows downward. A manager who's never been held accountable will never hold their team accountable either.
⚡ SIDE B — Employees drive culture
Culture lives in daily behaviour. Every interaction between colleagues has already shaped the culture more than any leadership memo.
Bottom-up movements work. Some of the biggest culture shifts I've read about have started with employees — not executives.
Leaders follow pressure. Companies have just changed policies because employees organized and pushed. Management didn't volunteer that.
Middle managers are the real culture. Your daily experience is your team lead — and most haven't even spoken to the CEO.

⏱ 2 Min · Each Side

  • Assign sides before students read the cards. The discomfort of arguing against your own opinion is the productive constraint — it forces creative language use.
  • Target B1.1 language in this debate: "I've never seen…" / "They've already tried…" / "It still hasn't worked because…" — all of these are natural in this debate context.
  • Debate Meter: update it live. When a student makes a strong PP-based argument ("Companies have just changed policies because employees organized"), give them a point visibly. That real-time feedback sharpens the next argument.

🤝
Negotiation Simulation
Manager vs Employee · 3 escalating scenarios · Real time
15 MIN
🎭 Simulation 1 · 3 min
The One-on-One
Context: Employee has just noticed that the team's burnout has increased. They want to raise it without sounding like they're complaining.
Employee goal: Raise the issue using specific examples. "We've already missed two deadlines because people are exhausted. The team still hasn't had a proper break since…"
Manager goal: Listen, respond professionally. Acknowledge what has already changed AND what hasn't been addressed yet.
🎭 Simulation 2 · 3 min
The Exit Interview
Context: A valued team member has just announced they're leaving. The manager has asked why — and wants honest feedback on the culture.
Employee goal: Give honest, professional feedback without burning bridges. Use: "I've never felt fully trusted here" / "The team has never been recognised for…" / "I've already had this conversation with my previous manager and nothing changed."
Manager goal: Don't get defensive. Ask questions. Find what the company has already failed to fix and acknowledge it.
🎭 Simulation 3 — ⭐ The Hard One · 4 min
The Culture Call-Out
Context: An employee has just sent a company-wide email calling out a pattern of micromanagement and lack of transparency. The CEO has called them into a meeting. The employee believes they were right to do it. The CEO has not yet decided whether to discipline or thank them.

Challenge: Both sides must argue their position using Present Perfect adverbs. Employee: "The company has tolerated this for years and still hasn't addressed it." CEO: "You should have come to me directly — we've already started working on this."
4:00
⏱ 4 Min · Simulation 3
Press START when ready
✅ Badge trigger: triple-click the "Simulation 3" label above to award the Negotiation badge when sim completes.
  • Sim 1 is a warm-up. Sim 3 is the real test. Allocate time accordingly: Sims 1+2 = 6 min total, Sim 3 = 4 min + debrief.
  • The exit interview (Sim 2) is emotionally rich — almost every student has had one or wishes they'd had one. Personal investment = authentic language. Let the emotion drive the vocabulary.
  • Sim 3 error to watch: "The company has tolerated this since years" → "for years." Since = specific point in time. For = duration. This distinction is alive in this simulation.

PP Adverb
Your company just launched a culture survey. Use "just" in a sentence.
"The company has just launched a culture survey — the results aren't out yet."
Vocabulary
What's the opposite of high employee engagement?
Disengagement / apathy. "We've never seen engagement this low — people have already stopped caring about results."
PP Adverb
Use "still haven't" to describe a company problem that hasn't been fixed.
"They still haven't addressed the micromanagement issue — and it's been two years."
Red Flag
What does "We're a family here" usually REALLY mean?
"It usually means the company expects you to work unpaid overtime because 'that's what families do.' I've never trusted that phrase."
Vocabulary
Use "psychological safety" in a sentence about your team.
"Our team has already built real psychological safety — people have never been punished for speaking up here."
PP Adverb
Have you ever worked somewhere where burnout was normal? Answer with "ever".
"Have you ever worked somewhere where burnout was considered a badge of honour? I have. I've never gone back to that kind of environment."
Opinion
Is transparency always good for company culture? 15 words max.
"Transparency is essential — but it needs to go in all directions to be real, not just top-down announcements."
Transform
Transform: "Nobody fixed the problem." → Use Present Perfect.
"Nobody has ever fixed the problem." OR "The problem still hasn't been fixed — after all this time."
Coloquial
What does "my door is always open" mean — and when is it NOT true?
"It means managers are available — but it's not true if employees have never actually felt safe enough to walk through. Real open-door = people use it."
PP Adverb
Use "already" to describe something that happened faster than expected at a company.
"They've already promoted her to director — she's only been here 8 months. Nobody expected it that fast."
Debate
One sentence: why does high turnover mean the culture is broken?
"High turnover means people have already decided that staying isn't worth it — the company still hasn't understood why."
⭐ Challenge
Use just / already / yet / never / ever in ONE sentence about workplace culture.
"I've just realized that I've never worked somewhere that has already solved accountability — and most companies haven't yet — but have you ever seen one that did?"
