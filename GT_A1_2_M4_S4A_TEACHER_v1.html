§1
Warm-Up — Safety Signs & Rules at Work
Activate safety vocabulary · Connect imperatives to real workplace context
⏱ 5–8 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note — Pedagogical
S4 is the final discovery session before the module exit task. Students now have: have/has + symptoms, imperatives, possessive pronouns, allergy language, and should/habits. Today adds: safety-specific imperatives (Wear / Report / Don't touch), have to as a fixed chunk for rules, and accident vocabulary. The warm-up connects imperatives from S2 and S3 to a new high-stakes context: safety on the job. Most students in Bajío manufacturing environments see safety signs in English every day — this is immediately relevant.
Question 1 — Signs at Your Workplace
Think of 3 safety signs you see at your job or in public. What do they say? Are any of them in English? What is the imperative form — "Wear / Don't / Keep / Report"?
💡 "At my plant I see: 'Wear Hard Hat.' and 'Do Not Enter.' and 'Keep Area Clear.'"
Question 2 — PPE: What Do You Wear?
PPE = Personal Protective Equipment. What do you have to wear at your workplace? Safety boots? A helmet? Gloves? Safety glasses? Say: "I have to wear [item] because [reason]."
💡 "I have to wear safety glasses because I work near machinery." — Have to = it's a rule, not a choice.
Question 3 — What Happens After an Accident?
If someone has a minor accident at work — a cut, a fall, a burn — what are the first 3 steps? Try to say them in English as imperatives: "First, call... / Then, report... / Don't..."
💡 "First, stop working. Call your supervisor. Report the accident immediately. Don't move the person if they fell."
Question 4 — Rules vs Advice
What is the difference between a RULE and ADVICE at work? "You should wear a helmet" vs "You have to wear a helmet" — which is stronger? How do you know when something is obligatory?
💡 Should = advice/recommendation. Have to = rule, no choice. "You should drink water" vs "You have to wear your badge."
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds
Say this now: "Wear your hard hat. Don't enter without permission. Report all accidents immediately."
Three safety imperatives — affirmative, negative, and action. You will use all three structures today.
§2
Speaking Topic — Safety Rules & Accident Reporting
Real workplace scenarios · Imperatives + have to in urgent context
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note
Pairs. The real goal is to get students to use "have to" as a fixed chunk correctly: "You have to [base verb]." No "have to to" — same rule as should. Also watch for "must" appearing — it is above A1.2 scope. Redirect to have to or should.
Round 1 — New Employee Safety Induction
You are a safety officer giving a new employee their first safety briefing. Give 5 rules: 2 using "You have to..." (mandatory), 2 using imperatives (direct commands), and 1 using "You should..." (advice). Be specific to your real workplace.
💡 "You have to wear safety boots in all production areas. Wear your badge at all times. Don't enter zone 3 without authorization. Report any accident immediately. You should also read the emergency exits map on your first day."
Round 2 — Minor Accident Response
A colleague cuts their hand on machinery. You are the first person there. Give a sequence of 5 actions using imperatives: what to do first, second, third... What do you say to the injured person? What do you say to bystanders?
💡 "Stop the machine. Apply pressure to the cut. Don't remove the cloth. Call the safety supervisor. Don't move her until help arrives."
Round 3 — Write Your Own Safety Sign
Your company needs 3 new safety signs for your specific work area. Write one mandatory rule (have to), one prohibition (Don't), and one recommendation (should). Each sign has: the instruction + a short reason. Present them to the class.
💡 "WEAR EYE PROTECTION — Flying debris is dangerous in this zone. / DO NOT ENTER — Authorized personnel only. / You should check your equipment before every shift — it prevents accidents."

§4
Colloquial — Safety Phrases at Work
Real phrases for reporting, warning, and responding · Corporate safety English
⏱ 5 MIN
"Watch your step."
Imperative warning — used to alert someone to a physical danger right now. Natural, direct, and universally understood in any workplace.
"Watch your step near the loading dock — the floor gets wet when it rains."
🕐 When: alerting a colleague to an immediate physical hazard — steps, wet floors, cables
"Report it right away."
Urgent imperative — "right away" means immediately, without delay. More urgent than "report it soon." Common in safety protocols and accident response.
"If you see a chemical spill, report it right away. Don't wait."
🕐 When: telling someone to take immediate action after an incident or hazard
"Safety first."
The most universal safety phrase in English. Used as a reminder, a closing to safety briefings, and a cultural value. Not an imperative — it's a principle, presented as a fixed phrase.
"I know we're in a hurry, but safety first — put on your hard hat before you go in."
🕐 When: reminding someone who is rushing to follow a safety protocol they want to skip
"Keep the area clear."
Imperative instruction for maintenance of safe zones. "Clear" means free of obstacles. Appears on signs and in verbal instructions. Always with "the area" or a specific zone name.
"Keep the emergency exit clear at all times — it's a fire safety rule."
🕐 When: instructing someone not to block a safety zone, exit, or equipment access point
"Are you okay? Don't move."
The two-part accident response — check condition + issue safety command. These are the first two things you say to an injured colleague. Simple, correct, and potentially life-saving.
"Are you okay? Don't move — wait for the first aid team. I'm calling now."
🕐 When: first on the scene of a workplace accident — the correct first response in English
💡 Real-World Note
Every student in manufacturing or logistics in México has seen English safety signs. They may have never said them aloud or been tested on them. This colloquial section bridges passive recognition to active production. Drill each phrase 3 times fast — the goal is automatic recall, not comprehension.
§5
Grammar — Imperatives for Safety · Have to (chunk) · Should vs Have to
Problem → Tool · Safety Sign Builder (new visual) · 2 activities
⏱ 12–15 MIN
🚨 Real Situation
Your international client visits your plant. The safety officer asks you — in English — to explain 3 rules to the visitor before entering the production floor:

"Tiene que ponerse el casco. No puede entrar sin sus gafas de seguridad. Debería revisar la salida de emergencia al llegar."
What 3 English structures do you need to say this professionally?
✅ The 3 Tools
Tool 1 — Imperative (mandatory command): Wear your hard hat. / Don't enter without glasses.
Tool 2 — Have to (rule, fixed chunk): You have to wear PPE. / She has to sign in. (never "have to to")
Tool 3 — Should (advice/recommendation): You should check the emergency exit. (softer — not a hard rule)

§6
Listening — Monica Reports a Workplace Incident
Monica calls her supervisor after a colleague slips · Imperatives + have to + should throughout
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Listening Note
Two passes: (1) listen for what happened and what Monica did, (2) listen for every imperative and "have to" the supervisor says. Students write them down. This trains students to hear grammar in an urgent, real context. Speed 0.80 — kept slightly slower to allow processing of safety instructions.
Monica Reports a Workplace Incident — Call to Supervisor
A1.2 · M4 · S4A · Voice: Sofia / Roberto (Mx English) · Speed: 0.80
[Monica]: Hello, Roberto? This is Monica. I have to report an incident. It just happened in corridor B.

[Roberto]: Monica, stay calm. Tell me exactly what happened.

[Monica]: Carlos slipped on a wet floor near the break room. He fell and he has pain in his wrist and knee. He can walk but he's very uncomfortable.

[Roberto]: Okay. Don't move him. Keep him sitting and calm. Is anyone else with him right now?

[Monica]: Yes, Ana is with him. I came to call you immediately.

[Roberto]: Good. You did the right thing. Now listen: you have to call the medical team — the number is on the emergency board near the exit. Don't let Carlos walk to the first aid room alone. The medical team has to come to him.

[Monica]: Okay. Should I fill in an incident report now?

[Roberto]: Not yet. First, take care of Carlos. You have to complete the incident report within one hour — that's the safety protocol. Write down the time, the location, and what happened.

[Monica]: The floor was wet because the cleaning team didn't put up a caution sign. I saw it this morning but I didn't report it.

[Roberto]: That's important information. You should always report a hazard immediately — even if it looks small. Don't wait. A wet floor without a sign is a serious hazard.

[Monica]: I understand. I'm sorry. I will report every hazard from now on.

[Roberto]: Don't worry — you acted well today. Call the medical team now. I'm on my way.

§SP
Speaking Practice — Safety Rules in Action
6 rounds · Imperatives + have to + should in urgent workplace production
⏱ 10–12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Monitor for: (1) "have to to" — double to, (2) "don't to" — phantom to after don't, (3) "she have to" — missing 3rd person "has". Award 1 point per round without errors. Switch partners at Q3.
Question 1 — Safety Briefing for a New Colleague
Your new colleague starts today. Give them a 5-rule safety briefing for your area: use "You have to" for 2 mandatory rules, imperatives for 2 commands, and "You should" for 1 recommendation. Be specific to your real work area.
💡 "You have to sign in every day. Wear your badge at all times. Don't enter zone B without authorization. Report every incident immediately. You should also read the emergency exit map on your first day."
Question 2 — Hazard Identification Walk
Teacher describes 4 scenarios in the classroom as your "workplace." Students identify the hazard and say what to do — one imperative + one "have to." Example: teacher points to a cord on the floor → "Don't trip on that cable! We have to keep walkways clear."
💡 Scenarios: cord on floor / blocked fire exit / no safety glasses near chemicals / wet floor without sign. One command + one rule per hazard.
Question 3 — Accident Response Role Play
Partner A plays a colleague who has a minor accident (choose: cut / slip / burn). Partner B is the first responder — give 5 sequential imperatives for what to do. Then call the supervisor (Partner A plays supervisor) and report the incident using "have to."
💡 "Are you okay? Don't move. Apply pressure here. Don't remove your hand. Wait for medical help." → Call: "I have to report an incident. [Name] has a cut on their hand."
Question 4 — Should vs Have to Contrast
Give your partner 6 safety rules from your real workplace — 3 that are mandatory ("have to") and 3 that are recommendations ("should"). Partner guesses which is which before you reveal. Focus on accuracy: correct form of have to/has to.
💡 "All workers have to wear hard hats. / You should report near-misses too. / She has to complete safety training in her first week."
Question 5 — Design Your Safety Sign
Use the Safety Sign Builder from §5. Generate a sign for YOUR real work area. Then present it to the class: read the sign, explain the hazard, and say whether it is an imperative, a "have to," or a prohibition. The class votes: is it a real safety need?
💡 "This sign is for our loading dock: DO NOT ENTER — You have to wear a high-visibility vest. It is a prohibition + mandatory rule."
Question 6 — 60-Second Incident Report ⭐
You are Monica. Report today's incident to Roberto in 60 seconds: what happened, what you did, what the protocol is (have to), and what you should do differently next time. Use all 3 grammar tools. No notes.
💡 "I have to report an incident. [Name] slipped on a wet floor. I called you immediately. We have to complete the report within one hour. I should always report hazards right away — even small ones."

3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
Workplace safety English · Immediate real-world application
PHRASE 1
"You have to wear [PPE] in this area."
The professional way to communicate a mandatory safety rule in English. "Have to" signals obligation — not advice. Replace with the real PPE for your zone. Works with any international visitor or new employee.
PHRASE 2
"I have to report an incident."
The correct opening line for any accident or incident call in English. "Have to" signals urgency and protocol — you are not choosing to call, you are required to. This phrase opens every professional safety report.
PHRASE 3
"You should always report a hazard — even a small one."
Safety advice that sounds professional and culturally appropriate in English — not a command, but a strong recommendation. The word "always" adds weight. Use this when coaching a colleague who almost skipped a report.
§7
Wrap-Up — Session Checklist
3 takeaways · Checklist · My Win Today · Preview S4B
⏱ 5–8 MIN
01
Have to = rule. Should = advice. Both use base verb — no "to" after. "Have to wear" — correct. "Have to to wear" — never. The contrast between mandatory and advisory is the core skill of this session.
02
3rd person: has to, not have to. "She has to sign in." "Carlos has to complete his training." This single change signals grammar control to any native English speaker.
03
Safety imperatives are direct — and that's correct. "Wear / Don't enter / Report" — no softening needed. In a safety context, being direct is professional, not rude. Native speakers expect clarity in safety instructions.
I can give safety instructions using imperatives: "Wear / Don't enter / Report"
I can use "have to" for mandatory rules — with no extra "to" after it
I know "she has to" is different from "you have to" — 3rd person changes have → has
I can report an incident to my supervisor in English using the correct structure
I built a safety sign using the widget and presented it using today's grammar tools
