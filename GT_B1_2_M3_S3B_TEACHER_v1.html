Your job: answer in 60 seconds. Defend a clear position. Use at least 3 Passive Voice structures. No hedging — journalists want a quotable answer.

§2
The Pitch — Defend Your Side
Structured debate · Two positions · Grammar-integrated starters · Timer
⏱ 10 MIN

Half the class defends Side A, half defends Side B. Each side has 3 minutes to build their argument using the sentence starters below. Then open debate for 4 minutes. Teacher: award the ⚡ badge to students who use Passive Voice naturally without being prompted.

🚫 SIDE A — Cancel Culture Works
Cancel Culture is a Necessary Tool
"Without public pressure, powerful people are never held accountable. Cancel culture fills the gap left by institutions that have failed us."
Sentence starters:
"Without social media, these people would never have been exposed..."
"Accountability was finally demanded after years of silence..."
"The evidence was ignored by institutions for too long..."
"When someone is given a massive platform, they must accept..."
"Real consequences are only felt when the public acts together..."
✅ SIDE B — Cancel Culture Is Dangerous
Cancel Culture Destroys Without Due Process
"People are judged, convicted, and destroyed before any facts are verified. This is not justice — it's mob violence with better Wi-Fi."
Sentence starters:
"Due process is being replaced by social media reactions..."
"Context is almost never considered before someone is cancelled..."
"Entire careers are being destroyed based on a single moment..."
"The accused is presumed guilty before any investigation..."
"Forgiveness is never offered — only permanent punishment..."

§3
Cancel Timeline — Relay Round
Interactive chronology of a cancellation · Passive Voice in every event · Click each node to reveal and discuss
⏱ 8 MIN

How it works: This is the story of a real-style cancellation — a famous sports presenter called "Marco". Each event is described in Passive Voice. Click a timeline node to reveal the event and the grammar question. Each student takes one event and must: (1) read it aloud, (2) identify the Passive tense used, (3) say whether the passive hides or reveals the agent — and why that matters. Earning all 5 = 🎤 badge.

Day 0 — 11:47 PM
A private video was shared without Marco's consent.
Passive: "was shared" → Past Simple Passive · Agent hidden — by whom was it shared?
💬 Relay Question: Why did the writer choose "was shared" instead of "someone shared it"? What effect does this create? What was done with Marco's consent?
📱
📣
Day 1 — 6:30 AM
The video had been viewed 4 million times before his network was notified.
Passive: "had been viewed" → Past Perfect Passive · Scale shown without naming who watched
💬 Relay Question: The Past Perfect Passive shows something happened BEFORE another past event. What does "had been viewed" tell us about the timeline? Why does the scale (4 million) matter here?
Day 1 — 2:15 PM
Marco was suspended by the network pending a "full investigation."
Passive: "was suspended by" → agent revealed (the network) · "pending investigation" = bureaucratic distance
💬 Relay Question: Here the agent IS revealed — "by the network." Why? Compare: "The network suspended Marco" vs "Marco was suspended by the network." Which version makes Marco look more like a victim? Why?
🏢
💸
Day 2 — 10:00 AM
Six sponsorship deals were cancelled simultaneously, without any statement being issued.
Passive: "were cancelled" + "being issued" → double passive · Corporate silence as power
💬 Relay Question: Two passives in one sentence — "were cancelled" and "without any statement being issued." What does this silence tell us? Who had more power in this moment — Marco or the sponsors?
6 Months Later
A comeback interview was arranged, but the episode was never aired.
Passive: "was arranged" + "was never aired" → two past passives · Attempted redemption blocked by unknown forces
💬 Relay Question: "was never aired" — by whom? Why is the agent hidden here? What does this passive construction tell us about who truly controls someone's reputation after cancellation?
🔄

Class debrief question: After all 5 events, ask: "Looking at the complete timeline — who truly had power over Marco's story? Was it the public? The network? The algorithm? Or an anonymous person on Day 0?" Use Passive Voice to describe your answer.

⚡ Teacher Note

Award the 🎤 badge collectively when all 5 nodes have been discussed. This is a team achievement — every student must speak at least once. Debrief question generates the session's most sophisticated passive production: "Marco was destroyed not by the public, but by a system that was designed to profit from outrage."

§6
Source Analysis — 4 Voices, 1 Story
Same cancellation · Different sources · Different Passive structures · Different agendas
⏱ 8 MIN

All four sources describe the same event: Marco's cancellation. Each uses Passive Voice differently — to protect, to attack, to distance, or to manipulate. Analyse the grammar AND the agenda.

📱 Twitter / X — @cancel_culture_watch · 2.3M followers
"BREAKING: Marco has been EXPOSED. Receipts were sent to us by multiple sources. His entire career was built on lies. He will be held accountable. This cannot be unseen."
💬 Discussion: Which passives appear here? Which agent is revealed ("were sent to us by multiple sources") — and why? What is the emotional effect of "cannot be unseen"? Is this journalism or activism?
📄 Official Press Release — Network Communications Team
"Marco has been suspended pending a thorough investigation. All appropriate processes will be followed. A statement will be issued when the review has been completed. No further comment will be made at this time."
💬 Discussion: Count the passive structures. Why does the network use passive everywhere? Who is never mentioned as the subject? What does "no further comment will be made" avoid saying? Is this transparency or legal protection?
💬 WhatsApp — Colleague of Marco (private)
"This is insane. The video was taken completely out of context. He was set up. That clip was edited — you can tell. I've known him for 12 years and he's not what he's being made out to be."
💬 Discussion: "was taken out of context" · "was set up" · "was edited" · "being made out to be" — which of these passive claims can be verified? Which are speculation? What is the defender's emotional motivation here?
📰 Long-form Article — Digital Ethics Review
"Marco's case has been cited by academics as emblematic of how due process is being systematically bypassed. The video was amplified by algorithmic systems designed to maximise engagement, not truth. His reputation was destroyed before any investigation was conducted."
💬 Discussion: This is the most "academic" passive use. Which passives here are most credible — and why? "designed to maximise engagement, not truth" — this is the article's hidden argument. How does passive voice support a systemic critique vs a personal one?

Metacognitive Question — earn your 🔍 badge:

After reading all 4 sources: "If you were Marco — which source would you want people to read, and which would you fear the most? Use Passive Voice to explain what each source does TO Marco's reputation. Start: 'In Source [X], Marco is presented as... / His reputation is [damaged/defended/analysed] by...'"

⚡ Teacher Note — Award 🔍

Award the badge when each student has spoken about at least one source. The metacognitive question generates the session's highest-level language — B1.2 students who can analyse HOW passive shapes narrative (not just form it correctly) are operating at the top of their level.

My Win
Phrase 1
"Due process was completely bypassed."
Use this when something was decided too fast, without proper evaluation — at work, in team decisions, or in discussions about fairness. Sounds analytical and professional.
Phrase 2
"The whole thing blew up before anyone could respond."
Perfect for describing fast-moving situations — a complaint, a rumour, a project crisis. Combines colloquial ("blew up") with natural cause-effect structure. Genuine B1.2 fluency.
Phrase 3
"The context was never fully understood."
Essential for any situation where something was misinterpreted — in meetings, emails, or conversations. Passive voice makes it diplomatic: you're not blaming anyone for misunderstanding.
