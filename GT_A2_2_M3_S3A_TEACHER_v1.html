§1
Warm-Up — What would you say?
Activate prior knowledge · Connect advice-giving to real professional situations
⏱ 5–8 MIN
📘 Pedagogical Note
Students arrive with inconsistent awareness of modal verbs. This warm-up surfaces what they already do instinctively in Spanish and makes the gap visible before drilling.
🎯 Your colleague has a job interview next Monday. He asks: "What should I do to prepare?" What are 3 things you would tell him — in English?
Don't worry about perfect grammar. Just try to give real advice!
💼 A friend works 12-hour days and never exercises. He says he feels terrible. What advice would you give him? Try: "You should…" or "You shouldn't…"
Think about health, work-life balance, stress management.
🧠 When someone gives you advice in English, what words do you expect to hear? Write or say any English words you already know for giving suggestions.
Maybe: "you should… / why don't you… / I think…" — anything counts!
⚡ Teacher note: Collect 4–5 advice attempts on the board. Note errors (especially "I suggest you to…" and missing modals). Don't correct yet — use them in §5 Grammar as live examples.
Look for: absent modals, infinitive errors, Spanish calques like "I recommend you to do it."
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds
Right now, say this out loud: "You should prepare some examples before the interview. You shouldn't be late." — Don't think, just say it. Did it come out naturally?
§2
Speaking Topic — Andrea's Career Dilemma
Real advice-giving in a professional context · 3 rounds · Pair discussion
⏱ 8 MIN
Andrea Soto is a career changer. She studied accounting, but now she works in sales at a tech startup in Guadalajara. She loves her new job but has a problem: she got a job offer from her old company — better salary, but no growth opportunities. She doesn't know what to do. She asks you for advice.
Round 1
The salary offer is 20% higher. Should Andrea take the money or stay?
💡 Use: "She should… / She shouldn't… / I think she should…"
Round 2
She could ask her current manager for a raise first. Is that a good idea? What could she say?
💡 Use: "She could try… / Why don't she…" — wait, is that correct? 🤔
Round 3 — Challenge
Andrea asks: "Should I respond to the offer today?" Give her one piece of advice with a reason: "You should/shouldn't… because…"
💡 Add a reason: "…because it shows you're professional."
"Andrea, you shouldn't respond today because you need more time to think. You should ask your current manager for a meeting first. You could say: 'I have an offer on the table — I'd like to discuss my future here before I decide.' That's the professional approach."
🔥 Engagement Note
Round 2 contains a deliberate trap: "Why don't she…" is wrong (→ "Why doesn't she…"). Let students discover this naturally. If no one catches it, point it out at the end and let them fix it. It creates a memorable moment.

§4
Colloquial — Advice in Real English
5 phrases native speakers use when giving advice · Professional & everyday contexts
⏱ 5 MIN
"If I were you…"
Fixed chunk opener — the most common way to frame advice in English without sounding bossy. Use this to soften any recommendation.
"If I were you, I'd update my LinkedIn before the interview."
"Have you thought about…?"
Invites the person to consider something without telling them what to do. Very natural in professional settings. Less direct than "you should."
"Have you thought about asking HR for a performance review?"
"It's worth trying."
Used after making a suggestion — signals that the risk is low and the benefit is high. Very encouraging and motivating for the listener.
"You could apply to both companies — it's worth trying."
"My advice would be…"
More formal version — used in professional or written contexts. Presents advice as considered opinion rather than a command.
"My advice would be to wait one more week before accepting."
"That's not a good idea."
Direct disagreement with someone's plan. Less harsh than "you shouldn't" — sounds like genuine concern rather than a rule. Can soften: "I'm not sure that's a good idea."
"Sending that email today? That's not a good idea — wait until tomorrow."
🔥 Drill Note
Run a 2-minute drill: Teacher gives a scenario ("Your colleague wants to quit by text message"), student must respond with one of the 5 phrases. Rotate through all 5. This section should feel conversational and fast.
§5
Grammar — Should · Shouldn't · Could
Choosing the right strength of advice · Referential grammar + interactive activities
⏱ 12–15 MIN
🎛 Visual — Advice Strength Meter
How strong is your advice?
Select a level below — watch how the modal changes the force of each recommendation
Gentle could A possibility — low pressure
Standard should A recommendation — clear advice
Firm really should Strong advice — urgent
Prohibited shouldn't Negative recommendation
Scenario 1: Andrea hasn't updated her CV in 3 years.
She could update her CV before applying.
Scenario 2: Your colleague sends emails with grammar mistakes to clients.
He could check his emails before sending them.
Scenario 3: The team hasn't had a team-building event in two years.
They could organize a team activity this quarter.
Scenario 4: A colleague is considering quitting without a new job ready.
She could think carefully before making this decision.
FormStructureUseExample
shouldSubject + should + base verbRecommendation — the right thing to do"You should update your CV."
shouldn'tSubject + shouldn't + base verbAdvice against — not a good idea"You shouldn't be late to the interview."
couldSubject + could + base verbGentle suggestion — one option"You could ask your manager first."
Should I/we…?Should + subject + base verb?Asking for advice"Should I send the email today or wait?"
Why don't you/we…?Why don't + pronoun + base verb?Informal suggestion — inviting action"Why don't we schedule a meeting to discuss this?"
⚠️ Top errors — correct immediately:
✗ "I suggest you to update" → ✅ You should update — "suggest" doesn't take "to + infinitive" at A2.2
✗ "I recommend you to apply" → ✅ You should apply / I recommend applying
✗ "You should to prepare" → ✅ You should prepare — NO "to" after modal verbs
✗ "Should vs must confusion" → should = recommendation · must = obligation (from M2)
🗳️ Quick Poll — 30 seconds
A new colleague keeps making the same mistake in client emails. What do you say?
✏️ Activity A — Choose the Best Modal
1. Andrea isn't sure whether to accept the job offer. She ___ take her time and think carefully before deciding.
2. "I have a job interview next week. ___ I prepare some examples of my work?"
3. The meeting is in 5 minutes and your colleague still isn't there. You ___ call him — he might not know about the change of room.
4. You ___ promise a client something you can't deliver — it damages trust completely.
5. "Should we ask the client for more time?" — "_____"
🗣️ Micro-Speaking
Tell your partner one piece of advice for the interview scenario from §2. Use: "You should… / You could… / You shouldn't…" — Add a reason: "…because it shows professionalism."
✏️ Activity B — True or False? Grammar Rules
"You should to prepare" is grammatically correct — "to" is required after "should."
"Could" can be used for a polite suggestion — it doesn't always mean ability or past tense.
"Should" and "must" mean the same thing — they are interchangeable.
"Why don't you call first?" is a correct and natural English suggestion.
§6
Listening — Career Coaching Call
Andrea Soto talks to a career coach · Should, shouldn't, could throughout
⏱ 10 MIN
Career Coaching Call — Andrea & Coach
A2.2 · M3 · S3A · Voice: Sarah (American English) · Speed: 0.82
Coach: Hi Andrea, thanks for calling. So, what's the situation?

Andrea: Hi. Well, I have a job offer from my old company. The salary is 20% higher. But my current job has better growth opportunities. I don't know what to do.

Coach: OK. First, I think you should write a list — the pros and cons of each option. You shouldn't make this decision quickly. Take at least two or three days.

Andrea: That makes sense. But the offer expires on Friday.

Coach: In that case, you could call them and ask for more time. Many companies are flexible. There's no reason you shouldn't ask.

Andrea: Really? I didn't think about that.

Coach: Yes. You should also think about your long-term goals. Where do you want to be in five years? The 20% salary difference sounds great now, but it might not matter if there's no career growth.

Andrea: That's true. My current company just promoted someone from my team. There are definitely opportunities here.

Coach: Then I think you should stay. But you could also use this offer as leverage — talk to your manager. Say you have an offer and ask about your future at the company.

Andrea: Should I mention the exact salary?

Coach: You could. It depends on your relationship with your manager. But you shouldn't lie about the numbers — that could backfire.

Andrea: OK. Thank you — this really helps.

Coach: You're welcome. And Andrea — you shouldn't stress too much about this. You have two good options. That's a good problem to have.

§SP
Speaking Practice — Your Turn to Advise
6 guided questions · From simple recall to full production · Pair activity
⏱ 12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Students give advice using should/shouldn't/could. Partner marks: (1) missing modal, (2) "should to" error, (3) "I recommend you to" calques. Correct immediately. Switch after Q3.
🗳️ Opinion Meter
"When a colleague makes a mistake, you should always tell them directly — even if it's uncomfortable."
50%
Question 1 — Interview Advice
Your colleague has a job interview next week. Give them 3 pieces of advice using should, shouldn't, and could. Partner checks the modal each time.
💡 Use: "You should prepare examples / You shouldn't arrive late / You could research the company website."
Question 2 — Difficult Client
A colleague has a client who is always unhappy and complains about everything. What should they do? Give 2 concrete suggestions — one with "should" and one with "could."
💡 Use: "You should set clear expectations / You could suggest a meeting to clarify the project scope."
Question 3 — Too Much Work
A colleague works until 9 PM every day, skips lunch, and never takes days off. What would you advise? Use all three: should, shouldn't, could.
💡 Challenge: Add a reason to each piece of advice: "…because in the long run it won't be sustainable."
Question 4 — Ask and Answer
Partner A asks: "Should I…?" (3 real questions from their job). Partner B answers "Yes, you should" or "No, you shouldn't" + reason. Switch roles.
💡 Ideas: "Should I send this email to the whole team? / Should I mention the mistake to my manager?"
Question 5 — Real Advice You Received
Think of the best piece of professional advice you ever received. Who gave it? What did they say? Report it: "She told me I should… / He said I shouldn't…" Did you follow it?
💡 This is also a preview of Reported Speech (coming later). Don't explain the grammar — just do it naturally.
Question 6 — Andrea's Full Scenario ⭐ Challenge
You are Andrea's best friend. She shows you the job offer and asks for your honest opinion. Give her a complete 5-sentence recommendation: what she should do, what she shouldn't do, what she could try, and a reason for each. No notes.
💡 Model opener: "Andrea, I think you should… because… You definitely shouldn't… However, you could also…"
💰 Retention & Upsell Note
Question 5 surfaces Reported Speech naturally ("She told me I should…"). If students do this spontaneously, acknowledge it: "That structure is exactly what we'll master in Module 5." This creates forward anticipation and validates their progress.
🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"You should prepare some examples before the interview."
Use this whenever a colleague asks you for pre-interview advice. Specific, actionable, and professional. Far better than "good luck!" — it actually helps them.
PHRASE 2
"You could try calling instead — it's faster."
A gentle suggestion using "could" — perfect for everyday work situations. It offers an option without pressure. Use it when someone is stuck on a problem and needs a fresh approach.
PHRASE 3
"If I were you, I'd wait before sending that."
The professional way to give advice without being pushy. "If I were you…" softens strong recommendations and sounds experienced. Use it in emails, messages, or face-to-face conversations this week.
§7
Wrap-Up — Session Checklist
Confirm what you learned · One more session to go in Module 3
⏱ 5–8 MIN
01
Should = recommendation. The standard advice tool. "You should prepare" — direct, clear, and professional. Never "should to."
02
Could = gentle option. Not ability, not past — a polite suggestion. "You could try calling instead." Less pressure, still helpful.
03
Shouldn't = advice against. "You shouldn't promise something you can't deliver." Prevents mistakes — use it to protect, not to criticize.

I can use informal suggestions: "Why don't you / we…?" in real work conversations
