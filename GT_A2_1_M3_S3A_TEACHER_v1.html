S2 — Speaking Topic
What Happened in That Meeting?
Use was/were and Simple Past to describe past meetings · Valeria's story as anchor
8 MIN
Character · Valeria Torres — Operations Coordinator, Solaris Manufacturing, Celaya
Valeria had to report on last week's supplier review meeting. The meeting was scheduled for 1 hour. It lasted 3 hours. Two suppliers weren't present. The decision was postponed. Her manager wants a full report — in English.
This is a situation every professional in manufacturing knows: the meeting that ran longer than planned and didn't end with a decision. Today you'll learn to describe that meeting clearly and professionally in English.
Round 1 — Describe Your Last Real Meeting
Tell your partner about a real work meeting from last week. Answer: Was it productive? How long was it? Who was there? What was the main topic?
💡 Use: "The meeting was [adjective]. It was on [day]. There were [number] people. The topic was [noun]."
"The meeting was last Tuesday. It was in the morning — about 9 AM. There were six people: my manager, three colleagues, a supplier, and me. The topic was the new delivery schedule. It was very long — about two hours. It wasn't very productive because the supplier didn't have the right data."
Round 2 — The Presentation
Think of a presentation you attended or gave at work. Describe it: What was it about? Was the presenter good? Were the slides clear? What was the result?
💡 Use: "The presentation was about [topic]. The slides were [adjective]. The presenter was [adjective]. The result was [outcome]."
"The presentation was last month. It was about our Q2 results. It was very professional — the slides were clear and the data was well organized. There were about 15 people in the room. The result was positive — we exceeded our Q2 target."
Round 3 — Challenge: The Negative Report
Describe a meeting or presentation that DIDN'T go well. Use at least 2 negative sentences with wasn't/weren't. What went wrong? Who was responsible?
💡 Use: "The meeting wasn't productive because... / The presenter wasn't prepared. / The slides weren't clear. / There weren't enough people."
"The meeting was on Friday but it wasn't productive at all. The agenda wasn't clear — nobody knew the topics. There weren't enough materials. The key manager wasn't present. We discussed the problem for one hour but the decision wasn't made. We scheduled another meeting for the following week."
📚 Pedagogy: Round 3 is the key production moment. Students need wasn't/weren't in negative context — the hardest part of was/were. If they get through Round 3 fluently, S5 will feel like confirmation, not new information.

S4 — Colloquial
Meeting Real Talk
5 phrases professionals actually use in and after meetings · Informal but professional
5 MIN
"Let's get started."
The professional way to open a meeting. Very common in corporate Mexico with international clients. Signals respect for people's time. It was often the first thing Valeria heard her manager say.
"Good point."
Short, powerful acknowledgment. Use when someone raises an important idea. The meeting ran better when people used it to validate each other's contributions.
"Let's move on."
Used to transition between agenda points. Critical for chairing meetings. The chair managed the 3-hour meeting partly because she used this phrase to keep things moving.
"To wrap up…"
The professional signal that you're about to close. Listeners know to pay attention — you're about to give the summary. Very common in US/UK corporate culture.
"I'll follow up on that."
A professional promise — use it when you can't answer immediately but commit to reporting back. The most-used phrase in Solaris Manufacturing's international meetings.
💡 Drill note: Have students pair up and run a 60-second fake meeting using ALL 5 phrases in the right places. Teacher monitors for naturalness. These are the "meeting toolkit" — once students have them, their meeting English improves dramatically.
S5 — Grammar
Was / Were — Past States & Meeting Results
Full system · Negative questions · Simple Past review · Was/were in professional meeting reports
15 MIN
Real Situation — Monday Standup
Your manager asks: "How was the supplier meeting on Friday?"

You want to say: "La reunión fue muy larga. Había seis personas. Dos proveedores no estaban presentes."
Which verb do you use — was or were? And when does it change?
The Tool: Was / Were
"The meeting was very long. There were six people. Two suppliers weren't present."

Rule 1: Use was with I / he / she / it / singular noun
Rule 2: Use were with you / we / they / plural noun

S6 — Listening
Valeria's Meeting Report — What Happened?
Valeria describes last week's supplier review to Daniel · was/were throughout
10 MIN
Valeria's Supplier Meeting Report — To Manager Daniel
A2.1 · M3 · S3A · Voice: Sarah (American English) · Speed: 0.82
Hi Daniel,

Here is my report from last Friday's supplier review meeting. I know you weren't able to attend, so I want to give you a complete picture.

The meeting was scheduled for 1 hour — from 2 PM to 3 PM. In the end, it lasted three hours. There were ten attendees in total: four from our team, three suppliers, and three people from logistics. Unfortunately, two key suppliers weren't present — they sent representatives instead, and those representatives weren't prepared to answer our technical questions.

The main topic was the new delivery schedule for Q4. The agenda was clear, but the discussion was complicated. The suppliers' data wasn't accurate — there were several errors in the figures they presented. As a result, the final decision was postponed to next Friday.

The good news: the attendees who were present were engaged and the outcome was useful. We identified three specific problems and assigned action points. The minutes were sent to all participants this morning. Two action points were assigned to me, and the follow-up meeting was confirmed for next Friday at 10 AM.

Were you available on Friday? I'd like your input before the next meeting.

Best, Valeria

Report the Meeting
Real situations · was/were + Simple Past in authentic meeting reports · Accuracy is the goal

📚 How to run this: Students answer using was/were + Simple Past. Partner marks: (1) was/were agreement errors, (2) "wasn't you" for "weren't you." Correct immediately. Switch after Q3.
Question 1 — Describe a Past Meeting
Tell your partner about a real meeting from last week or last month. Was it productive? Were all the key people there? How long was it? What was the main outcome?
💡 Use: "The meeting was [adj]. There were [n] people. The outcome was [adj]. The decision wasn't made because..."
Question 2 — The Presentation
Describe a presentation you gave or attended. Was the presenter good? Were the slides clear? Was the audience engaged? What was the result?
💡 Challenge: Use at least 2 negative sentences: "The slides weren't ready." / "The data wasn't accurate."
Question 3 — Negative Questions
Partner A asks 3 negative questions: "Wasn't it on Monday? / Weren't you there? / Wasn't the report ready?" Partner B answers honestly. Then switch roles.
💡 Target: "Wasn't + he/she/it...?" and "Weren't + you/they/we...?" with short answers: "No, it wasn't." / "Yes, we were."
Question 4 — Action Points
Think of a meeting where action points were assigned. What were the action points? Were they completed on time? Who was responsible? What was the result?
💡 Use: "There were [n] action points. I was responsible for [task]. It was completed / wasn't completed because..."
Question 5 — Chair a Mini-Meeting
You are the chair. Open a 2-minute meeting: introduce the topic, have one discussion point, assign one action point, and close. Use all 5 colloquial phrases from S4.
💡 Use: "Let's get started. / Good point. / Let's move on. / To wrap up... / I'll follow up on that."
Question 6 — 90-Second Report ⭐ Challenge
Give a complete 90-second meeting report to your "manager" (partner). Include: what the meeting was about, how many attendees there were, what wasn't resolved, and the follow-up plan. No notes.
💡 Model: "The meeting was [topic] — there were [n] people. The agenda was [adj] but the decision wasn't made because [reason]. The follow-up is on [day]."

3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"The meeting was [adj] — there were [n] people."
Use this in any post-meeting update to your manager or follow-up email. Two sentences that tell the whole story of who was there and how it went. Professional, precise, and completely natural.
PHRASE 2
"The decision wasn't made — the follow-up is on [day]."
The honest, professional way to report an inconclusive meeting. Combines a negative with a forward action. This is exactly what Valeria sent Daniel — problem AND next step in one sentence.
PHRASE 3
"Let's move on to the next point."
The single most useful phrase for anyone who chairs or participates in meetings. It keeps things moving, respects time, and signals control. Use it in your next meeting this week.
