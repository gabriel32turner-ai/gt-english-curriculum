§ 01
Warm-Up — Picture Prompt
5–8 min
📸 Look at the photo — answer these questions. Connect to your own professional experience.
1
S1 review: say 2 things about your professional identity using Present Perfect. ("I've always been…" / "I've never…")
Teacher: note whether PP is used spontaneously or not.
2
What does this image say to you about professional values — integrity, loyalty, or making difficult decisions?
"For me, it's all about…" · What have you had to prioritize in this kind of moment?
3
Has your sense of what matters at work changed since you started your career? What have you learned?
"I've learned that…" / "My priorities have changed because…" · What would you tell your younger self?
4
Have you ever had to make a choice between two values — for example, loyalty vs fairness? What happened?
This is the core debate for today. Let student explore before the Trade-Off Challenge in §3.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q1 is an S1 review checkpoint. Student should produce PP spontaneously — if they say "I always was…" → remind: "Can you say 'I've always been…'?" Don't over-correct; just model and move on.
  • Q4 is the linguistic bridge to §3's Trade-Off Challenge. Listen carefully: does the student try to justify their choice with "I've always believed…" or "I've been in situations where…"? This tells you how ready they are for production in §3.
  • B1.1 push: Q3 requires "What have you learned?" — a high-frequency PP question that forces reflection rather than description. Push beyond "I learned that honesty is important" to "I've learned that honesty builds trust — and I've seen what happens when that trust breaks down."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Look at the image again. One word: what value does this moment represent to you? Now — tell me a time you've lived that value at work."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Today you build the professional vocabulary of values — the language of integrity, trust, and recognition. These words appear in performance reviews, leadership discussions, and job interviews. They're also some of the hardest to express in a second language."

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "trust" appears in S1 as a VERB and here as a NOUN. Point this out: "You learned 'trust' last session as a verb — now it's a noun with a different meaning. 'I trust my team' (verb) vs 'There's a lot of trust in my team' (noun). Same word, different role."
  • "deserve" — common error: "I deserve to receive recognition." (correct) vs "I deserve recognition" (also correct, more natural). Drill the more natural form.
  • "prioritize" — British spelling also accepted: "prioritise". B1.1 students often confuse with "prefer" — these are not synonyms. "I prefer fairness" (I like it more) vs "I prioritize fairness" (I choose it first when I have to choose).
  • Production task: 3 verbs → 3 PP sentences about their career. "Which 3 verbs have been most relevant to your professional life? Make a true sentence with each."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Look at the 8 nouns. Rank your top 3 personal values — just in your head. Now: tell me which one you've had to FIGHT for at some point in your career."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "These 8 nouns are the vocabulary of executive leadership conversations in English. When a company talks about its 'values' — integrity, trust, fairness — this is the language they use. Knowing it positions you as someone who understands professional culture, not just tasks."

GT Originals — Values as Professional Self-Awareness
At B1.1, students shift from describing what they DO to expressing what they BELIEVE. "I've always believed that integrity is non-negotiable" is significantly harder to produce than "I work in sales." The Trade-Off Challenge forces exactly this shift — from description to conviction. This is the linguistic step between A2.2 and B1.1.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Trade-Off Challenge: click ✓ PP Correct only when the student produces a grammatically correct PP sentence with genuine content. "I believe integrity" is not a PP sentence. "I've always believed that integrity matters more than short-term recognition" IS.
  • Target starters for each PP justification: "I've always…" / "I've been in situations where…" / "I've had to…" / "I've learned that…" / "I've seen what happens when…" / "I've prioritized…"
  • Dilemma 3 (Ambition vs Balance) often generates the most personal responses — it touches real professional decisions. Let the conversation develop if the student has a strong experience to share.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "5 dilemmas — no right answers. I'm interested in WHY you chose what you chose. Each justification must use 'I've'. Ready? Dilemma 1."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "The language you just used — 'I've always believed…' / 'I've had to prioritize…' — is exactly the language of a B1.1 professional in an English-speaking environment. That's what 'fluent' sounds like for values."
  • Upsell: "In S3 (Strengths & Weaknesses), you'll use this same language to talk about your professional qualities in a real interview context."
§ 04
Grammar — Present Perfect Extended
10–12 min
S2 Focus — Questions, Reflection & Difficult Choices
Experience questions
"Have you ever had to choose between two values?"
"What have you learned about yourself professionally?"
"How has your approach to [X] changed?"
Duration + values
"I've valued integrity for my entire career."
"I've believed in fairness since I started working."
"I've prioritized balance for the last three years."
Never / Ever
"I've never worked somewhere without trust."
"I've never compromised my integrity."
"Have you ever had to put loyalty above fairness?"
Conviction + PP
"I've always believed that honesty matters most."
"I've found that respect is non-negotiable."
"I've learned that ambition alone isn't enough."
The "What have you learned…?" pattern — key at B1.1:
This PP question forces reflection on experience — the bridge between describing the past and drawing conclusions. At B1.1, students should produce this form naturally:
"What have you learned about leadership?"
"How has your understanding of fairness changed?"
"What has this experience taught you?"
⚠ New Error Patterns — S2
❌ "I always believed in integrity."→✅ I've always believed in integrity. (ongoing conviction = PP)
❌ "I have never compromised since always."→✅ I've never compromised my values. (never = no time needed)
❌ "I've prioritized balance during my whole career."→✅ I've prioritized balance throughout my career. ("throughout" = more natural than "during")
❌ "What did you learn about integrity?"→✅ What have you learned about integrity? (ongoing lesson, not finished = PP)
Activity 1 — Choose the Correct Form
Select the correct Present Perfect form in each values-based sentence.
"I ___ always believed that loyalty and integrity go together."
"What ___ you learned about fairness from your current role?"
"She ___ compromised her integrity — not once in fifteen years."
I've valued balance ___ I realised I was burning out.
"Have you ever ___ loyalty above fairness at work?"
Activity 2 — Transform the Sentence
Rewrite each sentence using Present Perfect. Say your answer aloud, then reveal the model.
→ Change to Present Perfect
Simple Past: "She learned that recognition matters more than salary."
✅ She has learned that recognition matters more than salary.
Note: "has learned" implies the lesson is still relevant now — not just a past event.
→ Change to Present Perfect negative
Simple Past: "He never prioritized balance in his previous company."
✅ He has never prioritized balance in any previous company.
Or: "He's never prioritized" — contracted form is more natural in speech.
→ Change to Present Perfect question
Simple Past: "Did you ever have to choose between loyalty and fairness?"
✅ Have you ever had to choose between loyalty and fairness?
"Have you ever had to…?" — a very natural PP question for professional experience. Drill this pattern.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Activity 2 (transformation) is more complex than S1's T/F — it requires active production, not just recognition. This reflects the gradual complexity increase across M1 sessions.
  • Transform Item 3: "Have you ever had to choose…?" — "had to" + base verb inside PP. Students often forget the "had to" and say "Have you ever choose?" Drill: "Have you ever HAD TO + base verb?"
  • After activities: oral production round. Teacher gives a value word + "for/since" → student produces the PP duration sentence. "Integrity — for your whole career." → "I've valued integrity for my whole career." 5 words → 10 seconds each.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Transformation: I give you a Simple Past sentence about a value — you turn it into Present Perfect. 10 seconds per sentence. Ready?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "'What have you learned…?' appears in 360-degree performance reviews, in leadership coaching, and in English-language job interviews. This pattern is genuinely useful."
§ 05
Reading — The Values I've Lived By
8–10 min
Elena Fuentes
Senior Consultant · Global Advisory Group · Dallas
LinkedIn Article
Watch for: Present Perfect (orange) · negatives (red) · duration FOR/SINCE (gold) · values vocabulary (highlighted)

People often ask me what I've learned after fifteen years in consulting. The honest answer is: it's less about technical skills and more about values.

Early in my career, I thought ambition was the most important thing. I have spent years chasing promotions, titles, and recognition. And I have achieved some of those things — but at a cost I didn't fully appreciate at the time.

What I have learned is that integrity isn't just a value — it's a professional asset. I have built every meaningful client relationship in my career on honesty, even when that honesty was uncomfortable. I have never regretted being transparent. I have regretted the times I wasn't.

The second thing I have learned is about fairness. I have managed teams for over a decade, and I have seen clearly what happens when a manager applies rules inconsistently. People don't leave companies — they leave managers who don't respect them. That's something I have never forgotten.

The value I have struggled most with is balance. I have never been naturally good at disconnecting. I have worked on this since 2019, when I realised that my health and my relationships were suffering. It's still a work in progress — but I have improved.

The question I now ask in every professional decision is: "Will I be proud of this in ten years?" That's not a perfect filter — but it has never led me completely wrong.

Comprehension — Values + Present Perfect
Answer using information from Elena's article.
What does Elena say she prioritized early in her career — and what has she learned since?
How long has Elena managed teams? (identify the correct Present Perfect duration)
"I have never regretted being transparent" — "never" makes this a Present Perfect negative. It refers to her entire career up to now.
Elena says she's always been naturally good at disconnecting from work.
"I have worked on this since 2019" uses SINCE correctly — 2019 is a specific point when the change started.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • This text contains 15 PP instances. Ask student to count them BEFORE answering the comprehension questions — forces careful reading and PP identification.
  • RQ2 is a precision test: "for over a decade" (duration = FOR) vs "since 2019" (point in time = SINCE). Both appear in the text — student must match the question to the correct clause. This consolidates the FOR/SINCE distinction from S1.
  • T2 is a negative comprehension trap: Elena says she has NEVER been naturally good at disconnecting. Students who skim often miss the "never" and answer TRUE.
  • After comprehension: "Tell me Elena's story in your own words — without looking at the text. Use at least 4 PP sentences." Then: "Now tell me YOUR values story — what have you learned after [X] years in your career?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Elena asks: 'Will I be proud of this in ten years?' — Do you have a personal filter like this for decisions? What is it? Can you express it in English?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Elena's article is the model for §7's debate prep. Both sides of the debate require 'I've always believed…' / 'I've seen what happens when…' — the same PP of conviction you practiced in §3."

Professional Reflection — Values & Career
B1.1 · M1 · S2 · Monologue · ~90 seconds · American English · Dense Present Perfect

🔈 Audio placeholder. Replace with Google Drive embed.
ElevenLabs voice: Sarah · Rate: 0.85 · Stability: 0.60

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

I've worked in three very different companies over my career, and the one thing I've always looked for — in every single one of them — is fairness. Not just good pay, not great benefits, but genuine fairness. The feeling that the rules apply to everyone equally.

I've seen what happens when that's missing. I've watched talented people leave because they never felt their work was recognized. I've been in meetings where credit was taken from the people who actually did the work. And I've seen managers who applied different rules to different people, depending on who they liked.

I've never stayed long in an environment like that. Life's too short.

The values I've prioritized most are integrity and recognition. I've learned that recognition isn't about ego — it's about feeling that your effort has been seen. I've worked in teams where that happened naturally, and I've worked in teams where it never did. The difference to morale is enormous.

Have I ever had to compromise a value? Yes. I have. I've been in situations where I prioritized loyalty over fairness — and I've regretted it. That experience has taught me to be more careful about whose expectations I carry.

⏱ Target: 85–95 sec · Voice: Sarah · Speed: 0.85 · PP density: I've worked / I've always looked / I've seen / I've watched / I've been / I've never stayed / I've prioritized / I've learned / I've worked x2 / I've been / I've regretted

Listening Activities
Listen and answer. Focus on PP identification.
Which two values does the speaker say she has prioritized most?
Has the speaker ever compromised a value? What happened?
The speaker says recognition is about ego — wanting to be seen as important.
"I've never stayed long in an environment like that" — this PP negative refers to environments without fairness. It implies she has left such environments.
"I've been in situations where I prioritized loyalty over fairness" — this uses PP correctly because she is reflecting on a past experience that still affects her thinking now.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Focus task: "Count how many times you hear 'I've'. Answer: approximately 12 instances. Then count 'have' without 'I've' — "it has taught me" + short answer "Yes, I have." Point out: PP appears naturally throughout reflection — it's not a grammar exercise, it's the natural tense for professional reflection.
  • L3 is the most conceptually challenging: student must understand WHY PP is used — "because it still affects her thinking now." This is the B1.1 conceptual target: PP = past experience with present relevance.
  • After activities: student plays the speaker. They give their own 60-second values reflection — using "I've always…" / "I've seen…" / "I've learned…" / "I've never…" Minimum 4 PP forms.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "She says: 'I've been in situations where I prioritized loyalty over fairness — and I've regretted it.' Have you ever been in a similar situation? Tell me — using 'I've'."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Homework: listen 3× and write every PP sentence. "12+ in 90 seconds — that's the PP density of fluent English when talking about personal experience and professional values."

🗣 Speaking Challenge
Format: Debate

"Is ambition more important than integrity in a competitive professional world?" Take a position and defend it. Use Present Perfect to support your argument with real experience and evidence. Minimum 2 minutes.

Side A — Ambition first
Without ambition, nothing gets done — "I've seen talented people held back by excessive caution."
Competition rewards results — "I've worked in markets where only ambitious people advance."
Integrity can be defined situationally — "I've found that context matters."
Side B — Integrity first (teacher challenges)
Long-term success requires trust — "I've never seen a leader with no integrity maintain their position."
Ambition without integrity destroys teams — "I've seen what happens when results justify the means."
Reputation is the most valuable career asset — "I've spent years building it."
▶ Show B1.1 Model Answer (reveal AFTER student speaks)
"I believe integrity is more important than ambition — not because ambition is wrong, but because integrity is what makes ambition sustainable. I've seen ambitious people achieve a lot very quickly — and then lose it all because they cut corners. I've also worked with people who were less ambitious but deeply honest, and they've built careers that have lasted decades. For me, it's all about the long game. I've always believed that your reputation is your most important asset — and I've never done anything professionally that I wouldn't be comfortable explaining publicly. That's my filter. That's something that's really important to me."
For me, it's all about… I've always believed that… I've seen what happens when… That's really important to me. I've never compromised… It makes a big difference.

GT Originals — S2 Exit
The debate format at B1.1: The goal is not to "win" — it's to sustain a position in English using Present Perfect and values vocabulary for 2+ minutes. If the student only argues for 40 seconds: push with "But have you ever seen the opposite? Tell me a specific situation."

Complexity trajectory M1: S1 = describe yourself (PP of experience). S2 = justify your values (PP of conviction). S3 = evaluate your strengths (PP of professional development). Each session requires longer, more complex PP production.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Debate: teacher takes the OPPOSITE position and challenges with "Have you ever seen…?" and "But what about…?" — this extends the student's answer beyond preparation and forces spontaneous PP production.
  • Evaluate: student uses "I've always believed…" / "I've seen…" / "I've never…" naturally? Or are they reciting isolated sentences? The goal at B1.1 is connected argument with PP throughout, not 3 sentences then silence.
  • Model answer: reveals a full paragraph of connected PP argument. Point out: it doesn't feel like grammar — it feels like a genuine professional opinion. That's the target.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "You just defended a position about values — in English — using Present Perfect throughout. If I met you at a business event and asked 'What values are most important to you at work?' — you now have an answer."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "S3 is Strengths & Weaknesses — the job interview session. Everything from S1 and S2 (personality + values) becomes the foundation of your 'Tell me about yourself' answer in English."
  • Upsell: "By S5 you'll deliver a 3-minute presentation combining personality, values, strengths, and how you've handled a difficult situation — all in Present Perfect. That's the M1 exit."
