Warm-Up — Scenario
One specific moment. React to it. Let the conversation go where it needs to.
6–8 min
The Scenario
Read it. Feel it. Then tell me your reaction in English.
B1.2 · S5 · Scenario
📋 The Scenario: You just got promoted. Everyone congratulates you. Your manager says you've earned it. But that night, you lie awake thinking: "They made a mistake. I'm not ready for this. It's only a matter of time before they figure that out."

Have you ever had a night like that — about a promotion, a new role, a project, a client? What were you telling yourself?
🔍 Go deeper: When you achieve something important at work — your first reaction: pride or doubt? How long has that pattern been following you?
💬 Open question: Do you think imposter syndrome is more common in high performers or low performers? Why? Has the answer to that question been changing as you've learned more about yourself at work?
🎯 Teacher: This is S5 — the final session of M1. Students have been building language all week. This topic should generate the most personal, unscripted English of the module. Create a safe space immediately: "There are no wrong answers here. I'm not evaluating your psychology — I'm giving you the language to talk about it." PPC hook: "How long have you been carrying this feeling?" — write the answer on the board as PPC.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.2 professionals almost universally experience imposter syndrome — the topic reliably generates authentic, emotionally engaged language. Give students a full 2 minutes of silence before they respond if needed. Silence here is productive.
  • Research note (useful to share): Imposter Syndrome was first described by psychologist Pauline Clance in 1978. Studies show 70% of people experience it at some point — and it is MORE common among high achievers and women in leadership.
  • S5 warm-up feeds directly into the §7 Exit Task. Everything they say here is raw material. Note the phrases they produce — you can reflect them back: "You said 'I've been feeling like a fraud since the promotion.' That's your Exit Task sentence."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "I'm going to read you the Scenario — then I want 30 seconds of silence. Think about the last time you felt like the person in that scenario. Then tell me, in English, what you were telling yourself."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Today you're going to name this feeling, describe it in English, and then do something harder: prove it wrong — using your own professional history. That's the Exit Task."

GT Originals — The IS Paradox
Imposter syndrome is more common among people who are objectively competent. Less competent people rarely feel it — they don't have enough self-awareness to know what they don't know. The fact that your student is experiencing it is often evidence that they are doing exactly the right thing — taking on challenges that push them beyond what feels comfortable. That discomfort is the feeling of growing. The language of B1.2 is exactly the same: they've been pushing past what feels safe. And they're improving. Both things are true at the same time.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Imposter Type Meter: the profiles are not clinical diagnoses — they are conversation starters. After revealing: "Which profile feels most accurate? When did this pattern start? Has it been getting better or worse?"
  • Evidence Board: don't let student flip the card and just read the PPC evidence silently. They must produce their OWN version first: "Before I flip — tell me: what evidence do YOU have from your own career for this situation?" The flip is the confirmation, not the starting point.
  • Click "✓ I produced my PPC" to track. The score /5 creates gentle pressure to complete all 5.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "The Evidence Board is the preparation for your Exit Task. By the time you've done all 5 cards, you'll have 5 real PPC sentences about your professional history. That's the first draft of your capstone."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention

💡 The M1 insight: One grammar structure — PPC — covers 5 very different professional situations.
Work history · Office dynamics · How you work · Speaking up · Who you are inside.

The Exit Task combines all five. You don't use five different grammars. You use one — applied with precision and honesty.
⚠️ M1 Final Error Check — Common PPC Mistakes
❌ "I been faking confidence since the promotion."→✅ "I've been faking confidence since the promotion." — need have
❌ "She has been overcome imposter syndrome."→✅ "She has been overcoming imposter syndrome." — need -ing
❌ "How long she has been feeling this way?"→✅ "How long has she been feeling this way?" — auxiliary before subject
✏️ M1 Grammar Integration — Choose the Best PPC
Each situation comes from one of the 5 sessions. Choose the most natural and precise PPC sentence.
[S1 — Who you are] Someone just told you that you seem very confident in meetings. But internally…
[S2 — Office dynamics] There's something happening in your team that hasn't been resolved…
[S4 — Speaking up] You raised an issue three weeks ago and nothing has happened…
[S5 — Imposter syndrome] You've been successful, but the feeling hasn't gone away…
✏️ True or False — M1 PPC Review
Final check on PPC precision.
"I've been faking confidence" is more precise than "I fake confidence" — PPC shows the pattern is ongoing and has weight.
"She has been overcome imposter syndrome" is correct — she's been dealing with it over time.
"How long have you been feeling this way?" is the most natural professional way to ask about ongoing self-doubt.
"I've been doubting myself for years" uses FOR correctly — it marks the length of a period of time.
✏️ Activity 3 — Reorder
Drag into correct dual-PPC sentence order — the target structure for the Exit Task.
  1. ⠿achieving results
  2. ⠿I've been
  3. ⠿I don't deserve them.
  4. ⠿— but I've also been doubting whether
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Visual Key has 4 panels each representing sessions from M1. Point to each and ask student to name a real situation from THEIR week that matches. This is the Exit Task preparation.
  • Q4 is the most important: the compound PPC ("I've been achieving results — but I've also been doubting whether I deserve them"). This dual-PPC structure is the core of the Exit Task model answer. Drill it until it comes naturally.
  • Reorder: this compound PPC is the Exit Task skeleton. Once correct: student says it with their real professional situation substituted in.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Speed round: I say a session topic, you give me a PPC sentence from YOUR real professional life. S1 — who you are. Go." (5 sessions, 5 sentences, 60 seconds total.)
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In §7, the Exit Task uses all 5 PPC patterns in one 2-minute talk. The grammar review we just did is the skeleton. The Exit Task is the muscle."
§ 05
Reading — The Letter I Wrote Myself
A senior manager writes to her younger self about imposter syndrome · PPC throughout
10–12 min
Ana Isabel Cruz
Regional Director · FinTech MX · CDMX
Letter to younger self
Watch for: PPC (green) · vocabulary (teal) · doubt · earn · achieve · overcome · belong · recognition

You have been working so hard for so long — and you still think you don't deserve to be here.

I remember. When you got the first promotion, you spent weeks waiting for someone to realize the mistake. You had been preparing every meeting three times more than anyone else — not because you were more thorough, but because you were terrified. You had been comparing yourself to everyone in the room, and you had been losing every time.

Here is what you had not been seeing: you had been building something real. While you were doubting yourself, the clients had been coming back. The team had been growing under your leadership. The results had been speaking for you, even when you couldn't speak for yourself.

Imposter syndrome doesn't mean you're not good enough. It means you care about being good enough — and you have been caring more than most.

Here's what I want you to understand: belonging isn't something they give you. You have been earning it every day. The recognition you have been waiting for? It has been there all along — in the outcomes, in the relationships, in the fact that people have been choosing to work with you year after year.

Stop waiting to feel ready. You have been ready. You just haven't been believing it.

✏️ Comprehension
Answer based on the text. Focus on the evidence Ana presents.
According to Ana, what had been proving she deserved her position — even while she doubted herself?
What does Ana say imposter syndrome actually shows about the person who has it?
Ana says belonging is something your organization gives you when you are good enough.
"You have been earning it every day" — this PPC shows belonging is not a single moment but an ongoing, daily process.
Ana's message is: wait until you feel ready before you act like you belong.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Count PPC instances before comprehension: 15+ in the letter. Record-high density for M1 — point this out. "This is what it looks like when someone talks about their professional journey with precision and honesty."
  • After comprehension: "Now write YOUR version of the last paragraph — 'You have been [verb-ing] every day. Stop waiting to feel [adjective]. You have been ready.' Use your real professional history." 3 minutes. This is the seed of the Exit Task.
  • RQ2 — the IS insight: "It means you care about being good enough." Ask: "Do you believe this about yourself? Has reading this been changing anything?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Ana says: 'The recognition you have been waiting for? It has been there all along.' In one PPC sentence — what recognition have YOU been waiting for, and where has it actually been showing up?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "The Exit Task in §7 is your version of this letter — applied to right now, your real job, your real situation."

1:1 — Admitting Imposter Syndrome to Your Manager
B1.2 · M1 · S5 · Dialogue · ~95 seconds · Natural pace · M1 vocabulary integration

🔈 Audio placeholder. Replace with Google Drive embed.
ElevenLabs: Sarah + Michael · Rate: 0.87 · Stability: 0.62

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

Manager: I've been noticing you seem a bit overwhelmed lately. How are you actually doing?

Employee: Honestly though — I haven't been doing great. I've been struggling with something I've never really said out loud before. I think I've been experiencing imposter syndrome. Since I got promoted to this role, I've been waiting for someone to realize I'm not qualified for it.

Manager: I appreciate you saying that. How long has this been going on?

Employee: Since the beginning, really. About eight months. I've been overcompensating — working longer than everyone else, checking everything three times. I've been doing it because I'm afraid that if I slow down, people will see that I don't belong here.

Manager: Can I tell you what I've been observing? Because from where I sit, it looks different.

Employee: Please.

Manager: I've been watching you handle situations that even experienced directors struggle with. I've been noticing how the team has been responding to your leadership. And I've been seeing client satisfaction scores that have been improving since you took over. That's not luck. That's not faking it. That's you — doing the work, earning the results, and not recognizing what you've been building.

Employee: I'm not gonna lie — I've been needing to hear that for eight months. I've been waiting for the evidence and not believing it when it showed up.

Manager: Fair enough. But starting today — you start believing it. Deal?

Employee: Deal.

⏱ Target: 90–100 sec · PPC density: I've been noticing / I haven't been doing / I've been struggling / I've been experiencing / I've been waiting / I've been overcompensating / I've been doing / I've been observing / I've been watching / has been responding / have been improving / I've been needing · 13 PPC instances · 3 colloquial phrases: Honestly though / I'm not gonna lie / Fair enough

✏️ Listening Activities
Listen and answer. Focus on the evidence the manager provides.
What has the employee been doing to compensate for their imposter syndrome?
What three pieces of evidence does the manager use to show the employee they belong in the role?
The employee has been performing poorly in the role — that's why they feel like a fraud.
"I've been waiting for the evidence and not believing it when it showed up" — the employee recognizes that IS has been distorting their perception of real results.
The dialogue uses 3 colloquial phrases from M1 naturally in conversation: "Honestly though," "I'm not gonna lie," and "Fair enough."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • L3: the 3 colloquial phrases are "Honestly though," "I'm not gonna lie," and "Fair enough." Ask: "Find them. Where did they appear? Did they sound natural?" This is the payoff of learning colloquial phrases in context.
  • After LQ2: "The manager gives 3 pieces of evidence. Could your manager give 3 pieces of evidence for you right now? What would they be?" → Final preparation for the Exit Task.
  • Roleplay: student plays the employee, teacher plays the manager — using REAL data from the student's professional life. Let it run — this is the Exit Task warm-up.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "The employee says 'I'm not gonna lie — I've been needing to hear that for eight months.' Is there something similar you've been needing to hear — or needing to say — at work? Tell me in PPC."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "The manager's 3 pieces of evidence — the team response, the client scores, the difficult situations — those are PPC sentences: 'The team has been responding to your leadership.' In your Exit Task, YOU produce the evidence about yourself."

Who You Are at Work — A Workplace Situation You've Been Navigating
CEFR Evaluation Rubric
PPC Count
3+ natural, accurate PPC sentences in 2 minutes. Varied: affirmative, negative, and/or question form.
Vocabulary Range
At least 5 words or phrases from M1 across the 5 sessions. Used naturally — not forced.
Colloquial
At least 2 colloquial phrases from the M1 set, used naturally in the right context.
Specificity
Real situations, real feelings, real consequences — not generic descriptions.
Fluency
Speaks for the full 2 minutes without major pauses. Recovers from errors naturally.
Honesty
The talk reveals something real about who they are at work. Not a performance — a reflection.
🎙 Exit Task Prompt
"Describe a workplace situation you've been navigating. Tell me who you are in it, what's been happening, what you've been doing — and what you've been learning about yourself."
Integration guide: Student may touch on personality (S1), office dynamics or gossip (S2), their work style (S3), a decision they challenged (S4), or self-doubt and imposter syndrome (S5). There's no required order. Evaluate what appears — not what's missing. The best answers weave 2-3 sessions together naturally.
