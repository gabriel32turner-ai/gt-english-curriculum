§ 01
Warm-Up — Scenario
5–8 min
The Situation
It's your first week at a new company. Your new manager asks about your previous workplace. The culture was completely different — the old company was very traditional, hierarchical, and rigid. Your new company is flexible, collaborative, and modern. How do you describe the contrast?
1
How did your old company USED TO work? What was the culture like?
Think hierarchy, flexibility, feedback, decisions. Use "used to" or "would".
2
S3 review: what "would" sentence can you make about a repeated action at your old workplace?
Must be an action verb (not be/have/feel). Example: "We would meet every Monday…"
3
What did your old company used to NOT have that your current company has?
"We didn't use to have [flexibility / feedback sessions / flat hierarchy]…"
4
What phrase sounds more natural: "I used to give feedback" or "I would give feedback"? Why?
Both can work for repeated actions — which feels more formal?
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q4 introduces today's collocations organically: "give feedback" is a natural collocation. Ask: "Do we say 'make feedback'? 'do feedback'? No — it's always 'give feedback'." This is the concept of collocations — specific verb+noun combinations.
  • Today's grammar focus (collocations) is NOT new structure — it's the natural word partners that make "used to" sound fluent rather than just correct.
  • Q2 consolidates S3 grammar: "would" for past repeated ACTIONS. Check: student avoids "would be" (state).
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Imagine I'm your new manager. Describe your old company in 4 sentences — using 'used to' or 'would'. I want to understand the culture difference."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Today you learn collocations — the natural word pairs that make English sound native. The grammar is the same. The collocations are what separate intermediate from advanced speakers."

Vocabulary — Work Culture & Change
10–12 min
negotiate
verb
/nɪˈɡoʊʃieɪt/
To discuss and try to reach an agreement with another party
"We used to negotiate every contract directly with the client."
implement
verb
/ˈɪmplɪment/
To put a plan, decision, or system into practice
"The company didn't use to implement feedback from employees."

noun
/ˌfleksɪˈbɪlɪti/

"There didn't use to be any flexibility in the schedule — 8 to 5, no exceptions."
hierarchy
noun
/ˈhaɪərɑːrki/
A system where people are ranked in levels of authority
"The company used to have a very rigid hierarchy — five levels above me."
feedback
noun
/ˈfiːdbæk/
Comments or evaluation given on someone's work or performance
"Managers would give feedback only once a year — now it's monthly."
milestone
noun
/ˈmaɪlstəʊn/
An important achievement or stage in a project or career
"We used to celebrate every major milestone as a team."
initiative
noun/verb
/ɪˈnɪʃɪətɪv/
A new plan or action taken to achieve a goal; also: taking action independently
"Employees didn't use to take initiative — approval was needed for everything."
adapt
verb
/əˈdæpt/
To change to fit a new situation, environment, or requirement
"The team would adapt its approach depending on the client's needs."
Key Collocations — Learn these as fixed pairs
give feedback  ·  receive feedback take initiative  ·  show initiative reach a milestone  ·  meet a milestone implement a policy  ·  implement changes negotiate a deal  ·  negotiate terms adapt to change  ·  adapt quickly
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Collocation strip at the bottom is the KEY pedagogical focus: drill "give feedback" not "make feedback". "take initiative" not "do initiative". These are fixed word pairs.
  • "flexibility" — uncountable noun: "there wasn't any flexibility" (not "flexibilities"). Common error.
  • "initiative" — two meanings: (1) noun: "a new initiative" = a specific project/program. (2) expression: "take/show initiative" = act independently without being asked. Both are A2.2 appropriate.
  • Production task: student makes 1 collocation sentence per pair in the strip — about their own company, using "used to" or "would".
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Look at the 6 collocations in the strip. Which 3 are most relevant to your work life? Make a 'used to' sentence with each. 2 minutes — go."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "'Give feedback, take initiative, reach a milestone' — these 3 collocations alone appear in over 80% of professional English performance reviews. Lock them in."

§ 04
Grammar — Collocations & Work Culture
10–12 min
Natural Collocations with Used To / Would
Common collocations — used to
used to have a strict hierarchy → state
used to follow a rigid process → action
used to work under pressure → action
used to lack flexibility → state
used to take initiative rarely → action
used to celebrate milestones → action
Also possible — would (actions only)
would give feedback once a year → action ✅
would implement changes without notice → action ✅
would negotiate every contract directly → action ✅
would adapt quickly to new clients → action ✅
would be very hierarchical → state ✗
would have no flexibility → state ✗
Work culture contrasts — the full pattern:
"The company used to have a strict hierarchy. Managers would give feedback only once a year. Employees didn't use to take initiative — approval was needed for everything. Now, the culture is much more collaborative."
Work Culture — Then vs Now
AspectThen (used to / would)Now (simple present)
Decision-making"All decisions used to go through the top.""Now teams decide independently."
Feedback"Managers would give feedback once a year.""Now it's continuous and informal."
Working hours"There didn't use to be any flexibility.""Now we have hybrid and flexible options."
Initiatives"Employees didn't use to take initiative.""Now everyone is encouraged to lead projects."
Hierarchy"The company used to have five management levels.""Now it's flat — two levels only."
Activity 1 — Choose the Natural Collocation
Click the most natural word combination.
Managers used to ___ feedback only at the end of the year.
We didn't use to ___ initiative — we always waited for instructions.
The team would ___ every major milestone with a formal dinner.
The company used to ___ changes in policy without consulting anyone.
There used to ___ very little flexibility in the working schedule.
Activity 2 — Collocations: True or False?
Is this collocation natural in professional English?
"Give feedback" is the natural collocation — NOT "make feedback" or "do feedback".
"The company would have a rigid hierarchy" is grammatically correct.
"Take initiative" and "show initiative" are both natural collocations in English.
"I used to adapt to change quickly" is grammatically correct — "adapt" is an action verb.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q5: "There used to be" — this is a fixed structure. "be" here is part of "there is/are" (existential "be"), not a state verb. It's correct and natural. Don't let students confuse this with "would be" (which is a state).
  • T2: "would have a rigid hierarchy" — "have" = state here (possession of a characteristic). NOT allowed with "would". Only "used to have".
  • After activities: oral production drill. Teacher gives a work aspect (feedback / initiative / milestone / hierarchy / flexibility). Student produces a complete "used to" or "would" collocation sentence from their own experience. 30 seconds per aspect.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "5 aspects: feedback, initiative, milestone, hierarchy, flexibility. For each — give me a TRUE sentence about your company using 'used to' or 'would'. Time yourself: under 3 minutes for all 5."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention

§ 05
Reading — Team Culture Report
8–10 min
FROM
Ricardo Mendez <r.mendez@alante.mx>
TO
Leadership Team · Grupo Alante
📋 Team Culture — Then vs Now · Annual Review
Watch for: collocations with used to/would (highlighted) · work culture vocabulary · all three grammar forms from M1

Hi everyone,

As part of our annual review, I want to reflect on how our team culture has evolved over the past five years.

Five years ago, the culture used to be very traditional. Management would give feedback only at the annual performance review — once a year. Employees didn't use to take initiative; every decision required approval from above. We used to follow a strict hierarchy with five management levels.

There didn't use to be any flexibility in how or when we worked. The team would negotiate every client contract through the director — no direct contact at team level. We would implement policy changes without consulting the people affected.

Things changed when we merged with the Dallas group in 2019. Leadership restructured the hierarchy and introduced a flat management model. The culture shifted significantly.

Nowadays, every team member gives and receives feedback monthly. We celebrate every project milestone as a team. Employees actively take initiative and adapt to client needs without waiting for approval. There is full flexibility in working hours and location.

The transformation has not been easy — but I believe it has made us a stronger, more competitive team.

Best regards,

Ricardo Mendez · Operations Director · Grupo Alante
Comprehension — Grammar + Collocations
Answer using information from the email.
How often would managers give feedback before the transformation?
What specific event changed the culture? (Simple Past)
"Would negotiate" in the email describes a repeated action — so "would" is correctly used here.
Before the transformation, employees used to take initiative independently.
Nowadays, the team celebrates project milestones together.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Highlighted collocations (yellow background): 8 collocations in the text. Ask student to find and name each one: give feedback / take initiative / follow hierarchy / have flexibility / negotiate contracts / implement policies / celebrate milestones / take initiative (again). This is a collocation hunt.
  • Both "would give" and "would negotiate" are correctly used — they describe repeated actions, not states. Ask student to confirm why each "would" instance is correct or incorrect.
  • RQ2: "the team used to restructure" = wrong (not what happened — restructuring was a specific event). "The leadership would introduce" = also wrong (specific event). The merger = correct Simple Past event.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Count the highlighted collocations. Can you find all 8? Say each one aloud — then make your own true sentence with it about your company."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "This email is the type of document you might write or read in a senior role in an international company. In §7 you'll present your own version of this analysis — spoken, 3 minutes."

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

Ana: David, you worked at Nortek before this, right? What was the culture like there?

David: Very different. The hierarchy used to be incredibly rigid. Your manager's manager had to approve everything.

Ana: Really? So nobody used to take initiative?

David: Exactly. And managers would give feedback only at the annual review — one meeting a year. That was it.

Ana: That sounds frustrating. Did you use to have any flexibility in your schedule?

David: Not at all. Nine to six, every day, no exceptions. There didn't use to be any remote work option. It was completely different from here.

Ana: Here we adapt constantly. What about milestones? Did you use to celebrate them?

David: Sometimes. The team would celebrate the big ones — a major contract, end of year. But not the small ones.

Ana: And negotiations? Who used to handle those?

David: Only the director. We didn't use to negotiate directly with clients at team level. Everything went through him. Now I negotiate my own contracts — I still can't believe it.

Ana: That's the difference. Here you take initiative from day one.

David: Exactly. It took me six months to adapt. But now I wouldn't go back.

The collocation principle: Learning "give feedback" is more valuable than learning one new vocabulary word — because it unlocks ALL contexts where "give" and "feedback" appear together. This is why collocations multiply a student's effective vocabulary exponentially.

Presentation format: The 5-step structure (past culture → would → change event → present → personal reflection) is the exact format of a "company culture" answer in a B1 speaking test. Students who master it at A2.2 will find B1 preparation significantly easier.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical

  • Minimum length: 3 minutes. If student finishes early — ask "What didn't you use to have in your old company that you have now?" to extend.
