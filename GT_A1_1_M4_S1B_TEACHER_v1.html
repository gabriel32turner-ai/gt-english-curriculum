Hot Take — Your Office in 60 Seconds
Immediate production · There is/are under time pressure · No preparation

Scenario — Solaris Manufacturing, Celaya
Your new international colleague from Chicago asks: "Hey, I'm visiting your office next week — can you describe the building quickly? What's there?" You have 60 seconds on a Teams call.
Describe your real (or imaginary) workplace in 60 seconds. Include: at least 3 "There is/are" sentences, 1 negative, and 1 floor number. No notes — go!

§2
Pitch — Big Office vs Small Office
Argue your side using There is/are · Grammar integrated into real opinion
⏱ 10 MIN
Team A — Big Corporate Office
Bigger is better!
  • "In a big office, there is a cafeteria, a gym, and…"
  • "There are more spaces to work — there is even a…"
  • "There are meeting rooms on every floor, so…"
  • "There isn't a problem with space because…"
Team B — Small Startup Office
Small = focused!
  • "In a small office, there aren't any distractions — there is only…"
  • "There is one great meeting room and that's enough because…"
  • "There isn't a big cafeteria but there is a break room where…"
  • "There are fewer people so there is more… "
⚡ Teacher Note — Badge ⚡

Assign sides randomly (or let students choose). Give 2 minutes to prepare, then each team presents for 2 minutes. Badge ⚡ earned when a student uses "there is/are" + "there isn't/aren't" in the same turn. Count aloud and celebrate. Finish with a class vote: who was more convincing?

§3
Relay — Visitor Guide Challenge
5 transformation prompts · Each student adds one sentence · Full tour builds collaboratively
⏱ 8 MIN
🗺️ Office Map Navigator NEW VISUAL Click each prompt → reveal the model answer after the relay
Prompt 1 — Visitor arrives at entrance
A visitor arrives. Tell them what's on the ground floor. (2 sentences minimum)
💡 Use: "There is a reception… There are security desks…"
✅ Model: "Welcome! There is a reception right here at the entrance. There are security desks on both sides — please show your ID badge."
Prompt 2 — Visitor needs the meeting room
Direct the visitor to the meeting room. Say which floor and where exactly it is. (2 sentences)
💡 Use: "There are meeting rooms on the… The room is next to / in front of…"
✅ Model: "There are three meeting rooms on the second floor. Meeting Room A is next to the elevator — take a left when you exit."
Prompt 3 — Visitor asks about lunch options
Tell the visitor about food options in the building AND one option nearby. Include one negative.
💡 Use: "There is a cafeteria on… There isn't a restaurant inside, but there is one…"
✅ Model: "There is a cafeteria on the third floor — it's open from noon. There isn't a restaurant inside the building, but there is one just around the corner."
Prompt 4 — Visitor asks about parking
Explain the parking situation. Where is it? Is it free? Are there any other options nearby?
💡 Use: "There is a parking lot behind… There aren't any spaces in front of…"
✅ Model: "There is a parking lot behind the building — it's free for visitors today. There aren't any spaces in front of the entrance, sorry."
Prompt 5 — Full tour closing
Close the tour! Summarize what's in the building (3 things) and wish the visitor a great meeting.
💡 Use: "So, in our building there is/are… Enjoy your visit!"
✅ Model: "So — there is a reception downstairs, there are meeting rooms on the second floor, and there is a cafeteria on the third. Enjoy your visit and let us know if you need anything!"
💡 How to run the relay

Each student (or pair) takes ONE prompt and has 45 seconds to respond. The class builds the full visitor tour together — each response picks up from the last. Click the prompt after each response to show the model answer. Badge 🎤 earned when the full 5-prompt relay is completed.

Solo Challenge — 2 Minutes
Design & describe your dream workplace — and convince us to work there.
  • Describe at least 5 spaces using "There is / There are"

  • Use at least 2 floor numbers (e.g. "on the first floor", "on the top floor")

§6
Source Analysis — Real Workplace English
4 authentic-style sources · Spot There is/are in context · Metacognitive question each
⏱ 8 MIN
📱 WhatsApp Message
"Hey! There's a visitor waiting for you at reception — he says there are two boxes with him. Are there any meeting rooms free right now?"
How many "there is/are" structures can you find? What are they referring to?
📧 Company Email
"Dear team, there is a new break room on the 4th floor. There are fresh coffee machines and a microwave. There isn't a sink yet — it will be installed next week."
What is the email announcing? Find the positive AND the negative structure.
🏢 Building Directory Sign
"Floor 1: Reception · Security · IT Help Desk. Floor 2: Meeting Rooms A–F. Floor 3: Cafeteria · Break Room. Floor 4: Management Offices. There is no gym. There are restrooms on every floor."
Say each floor in a sentence using "There is/are on the ___ floor." Do all 4 floors.
💼 LinkedIn Job Posting
"Our Celaya plant has modern facilities: there are 3 cafeterias, there is a gym, and there are green spaces outside. There isn't a remote work option, but there is a flexible schedule."
Would YOU apply for this job based on the facilities? Use "There is/are" to explain your answer.
📌 Source Analysis Note

Sources 1–3 are comprehension-focused — students find and label structures. Source 4 is production — students must create their own "there is/are" response. Give 5 minutes total, then elicit 1 answer per source from different students. Correct publicly and warmly.

3 Phrases — Use These at Work This Week
Starting tomorrow — in Teams, WhatsApp, or face-to-face
Phrase 1
"There are meeting rooms on the second floor — take the elevator."
Use this when guiding any visitor or new colleague. Precise, professional, and completely natural. Much better than just pointing!
Phrase 2
"Is there a [space] near your office?"
Use this in Teams or Zoom when coordinating a meeting with someone in another location. Simple, direct, and shows you're thinking about their context.
Phrase 3
"There isn't a [X], but there is a [Y] if you need it."
The "negative + positive" structure. Use it when a visitor asks for something you don't have but you can offer an alternative. Warm, helpful, and grammatically perfect.
