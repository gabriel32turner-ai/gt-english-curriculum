§ 04
Grammar Check — Error Correction Only
No re-teaching · Spot the error · Produce the corrected version from your real context
6 min
⚠️ S4B — Fix These 4 PPC Errors
❌ 1. "I been pushing back on this for weeks."→✅ "I've been pushing back on this for weeks." — need have
❌ 2. "She has been challenge the decision."→✅ "She has been challenging the decision." — need -ing
❌ 3. "How long he has been ignoring this?"→✅ "How long has he been ignoring this?" — auxiliary before subject
❌ 4. "The team has been deal with this since months."→✅ "The team has been dealing with this for months." — -ing + for
🔍 Spot the Error
Each sentence has one PPC error. Choose the correct fix.
"My boss has been ignore the team's feedback since the new policy was announced."
"I've been raise this issue since January — nobody has responded."
"How long the team has been dealing with the consequences of that decision?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • 3 items, 6 minutes. After each correct answer: "Produce the corrected sentence about your real situation at work." Transfer is the goal — not identification.
  • If student gets all 3 correctly in under 3 minutes: skip to §5. Spend the saved time on oral production.

1:1 Revisited — Map the Template · Second Listen
Same audio as S4A · Focus: structure analysis · Use as model for §6

🔈 Same audio as S4A. Replace with Google Drive embed.
ElevenLabs: Sarah + Michael · Rate: 0.87

▶ Show Script — Structure Map

[Opener] Employee: I appreciate you giving me a few minutes. I've been wanting to raise something with you for a while, and I want to do it the right way.

Manager: Of course. What's going on?

[Issue + PPC evidence] Employee: I've been noticing that the deadline for the product launch has been moving forward — but the team's capacity hasn't changed. We've been working at full capacity for six weeks already. I think if we don't reconsider the timeline, we're going to see quality issues that will affect the client relationship.

[Boss pushes back] Manager: I hear you. But the client has been pushing for this date since March. We've been committed to it.

[Suggestion] Employee: I understand — and I'm not suggesting we go back on the commitment. I've been thinking about this, and I'd like to suggest a phased approach. We deliver the core features on the agreed date, and we complete the secondary features two weeks later.

[Manager responds + question] Manager: That's actually an interesting way to frame it. How long have you been thinking about this?

[Honest + colloquial] Employee: Honestly though — about three weeks. I wasn't sure how to bring it up. But the team has been struggling, and I felt I had to say something.

Manager: I'm glad you did. Let's put this on the agenda for tomorrow's project meeting. Can you prepare two slides?

Employee: Absolutely. I'll have them ready by 9 AM.

✅ Structure: Opener → PPC evidence → Suggestion → Honest close. PPC: 8 instances. Colloquial: "Honestly though." Result: meeting on agenda, clear next step.

✏️ Second Listen — Structure Focus
This time, map the conversation structure. Focus on HOW, not just what.
The employee starts with "I want to do it the right way." What does this signal to the manager?
The employee uses PPC throughout the issue description — this makes the argument about an observable pattern, not a personal opinion.
When the manager pushes back, the employee abandons the suggestion and agrees with the deadline.
"Honestly though" signals the employee is about to share something genuine — using a colloquial phrase to soften a real admission.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Second listen goal: recognition of structure → replication in §6. After T2: "The employee doesn't give up when the boss pushes back — that's the key skill. They acknowledge the pushback and pivot to their suggestion. Practice that pivot now: 'I understand — AND I'd like to suggest…'"
  • Key pattern for §6: Opener → PPC evidence → Boss resists → Acknowledge + Suggest → Question. This is the 5-step structure students must reproduce.
§ 06
Role-Play — Your Pushback 1:1
You play the employee · Your real situation · Mariana's template · 4+ PPC · No script
18 min
The 1:1 starts now. I am your boss. You are the employee. This is a real conversation — not a grammar exercise.

I'm going to open: "I've got a few minutes. What did you want to talk about?"

Your goal: use Mariana's template. Come with evidence (PPC). Come with a suggestion. End with a question. Use at least 3 vocabulary words from S4A and 1 colloquial phrase. Be specific. Be professional.

🎭 Choose your scenario — the conversation begins immediately.

Scenario A
The Deadline Push
Your boss has been moving the deadline forward every week without consulting the team. The team has been working at full capacity for two months.
Goal: raise the issue + suggest a phased approach. PPC as evidence throughout.
Scenario B
The Unfair Assignment
Your boss has been assigning the best projects to a colleague who has a personal connection — not based on performance. You've been watching this for three months.
Goal: raise the pattern with data. Suggest a merit-based review process. No personal attack — PPC evidence only.
Scenario C · Hard Mode
The Ignored Flag
Six weeks ago you flagged a process problem. Your boss hasn't acted on it. A client has now complained. You need to raise it again — with the client evidence.
Goal: revisit the issue professionally without "I told you so." Come with data, consequence, and a specific solution.
Scenario D · Expert Mode
Your Real Situation
Use your actual situation from §1 — the one you described in the warm-up. Real content only. Mariana's template applied to YOUR reality.
Goal: 5+ PPC · 3 vocabulary words · 1 colloquial phrase · end with a specific question. No invented scenarios.
Teacher Boss Prompts — Use mid role-play to create pressure
"I hear you — but we've been committed to this since March. The client won't wait."
"Are you saying my decision was wrong? I'd like to understand what you're saying exactly."
"You raised this before. What's different now? Why should I reconsider?"
"How long has this been affecting the team? Why am I only hearing about this now?"
"Fair enough — but what's your actual suggestion? What specifically do you want me to do differently?"
"I appreciate you bringing this — but I need you to send me data. Can you have two slides ready by tomorrow?"
Self-Evaluation — Después del role-play
  • I used Mariana's template: "I've been noticing [pattern] that has been affecting [outcome]"
  • I used PPC at least 4 times correctly (has/have + been + -ing)
  • I used at least 3 vocabulary words from S4A (authority / consequence / push back / challenge / suggest / raise / outcome)
  • I came with evidence — not just an opinion or a feeling
  • I offered a specific suggestion — not just a complaint
  • I ended with a question — not a demand
  • When my boss pushed back, I acknowledged it and stayed professional — I didn't give up or escalate
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • NO interrumpas durante el role-play. Mantén el personaje del boss — incluyendo la resistencia. El alumno necesita experimentar el pushback del boss para practicar la pivot. Toma nota de PPC correctos, vocabulario usado, colloquial phrases, y si mantuvo el template.
  • La clave de B1.2: el alumno no solo habla — estructura. Opener → evidence → suggestion → question. Si no hay pregunta al final: "¿Cómo cerraste? The employee in S4A ended with a question. What was yours?"
  • Scenario D (Real Situation) es el más valioso. Dale tiempo extra — puede ir a 120 segundos.
  • Segunda ronda: student plays the boss. "Now you run the conversation — I'm the employee coming to you with a complaint. How do you handle it?" Role reversal reveals different vocabulary and comprehension depth.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "You just had a professional disagreement in English — structured, evidence-based, with a suggestion and a question. That's not a language exercise. That's a workplace skill."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "S5A — Imposter Syndrome — uses the same PPC in a completely different context: describing self-doubt, achievement, and professional identity. Same grammar, very personal topic. Today was the professional preparation."

🗣 Storytelling Challenge — Take 2 · ⏱ 2 minutes
Same prompt · Real situation · 4 parts · No notes · Better than S4A
"Tell me about a time your boss made a decision you disagreed with — and what you did about it."

4 parts: (1) Context — what had been happening + PPC · (2) The decision — what your boss decided + vocabulary · (3) What you did — which strategy + honest outcome · (4) Lesson — what has it been changing. Target: 3+ PPC · 3 vocabulary words · 1 colloquial phrase.

2:00
⏱ 2-Minute Story Timer
Real story. No hypotheticals. Press START when ready.
▶ Show B1.2 Model Story (reveal AFTER student speaks)
"I'm not gonna lie — this situation has been on my mind for a while. About six months ago, my manager decided to change our entire client reporting format — without asking the team. The new format was taking twice as long to complete and the clients had been confused by it for weeks. I disagreed with it, but I stayed silent at first. I thought: who am I to challenge this? But after two months of watching the team spend hours on something that wasn't working, I asked for a 1:1. I said: 'I've been noticing that the new format has been taking significantly more time than the previous one — and honestly though, I've been getting feedback from clients that they're confused by it. Could I suggest we test both formats and compare the results?' He agreed. We ran the test. The original format won. He never said 'you were right' — fair enough, that's not his style. But the team went back to the original format the following month. What I've learned: you need data, you need a suggestion, and you need to frame it as a question — not a complaint."
