§ 01
Warm-Up — Picture Prompt: What Are the Rules Here?
5–7 min
Choose one photo. Look at the workplace environment. Without preparation, describe at least 3 rules that probably exist in that place — using must, mustn't, have to, and don't have to. No notes. Speak directly.

The Control Room

The Corporate Office

The Remote Meeting

Follow-up questions from teacher: "Do the people in this photo have to wear special clothing?" / "What mustn't they do in this environment?" / "Is anything optional for them?"
Layer 1 — Diagnostic Purpose
  • This is a cold-start diagnostic. You can see immediately which modal is weakest for the student today. If they default to must/mustn't but avoid don't have to — that's your focus for §4 and §6.
  • 5–6 min max. Don't over-coach here — the student needs to produce with what they have. Correction comes after, not during.
  • If they are fluent and confident: push with the follow-up questions immediately. If hesitant: let them pick Photo 2 (office) — easiest for most corporate students.
Layer 2 — Opening Energy
  • "No preparation. No grammar review first. You just talked about workplace rules in English. That's the B session — you work from what you already have."
  • Any modal sentence, however simple, deserves acknowledgment: "That's a real sentence. We're already working."
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • B sessions start harder than A sessions by design — the student doesn't get a vocabulary warm-up or grammar review before speaking. This is intentional: mastery means producing without scaffolding.
  • "In a real job conversation, nobody tells you 'remember: use must for strong obligation.' You just have to do it. That's what this session trains."
§ 02
Recap — Modal Flip Cards
6–8 min
Flip each card to reveal the answer. Use as a rapid-fire oral drill — student reads the front, says the answer, then flips to confirm. No writing. Pace: ~20 seconds per card.
01
Forbidden. Do NOT do this.
Which modal?
MUSTN'T
"You mustn't share the client password."
02
Not required. Your choice.
Which modal?
DON'T HAVE TO
"You don't have to wear a suit on Fridays."
03
The law requires this. External obligation.
Which modal?
HAVE TO
"We have to submit the tax report by law."
04
The company policy requires this. Internal rule.
Which modal?
MUST
"Employees must wear their badge at all times."
05
Asking about an obligation. Question form.
How?
DO YOU HAVE TO…?
"Do I have to attend all meetings? — No, you don't."
Key contrast to confirm before moving on: Ask the student: "What's the difference between 'You mustn't go' and 'You don't have to go'?" — They must produce this distinction verbally before you continue. If they can't, spend 90 extra seconds here. This is the load-bearing concept of M2.
Layer 1 — Recap Strategy
  • Keep this fast: 5–6 min total. Flip all 5 cards. Student says the answer before flipping — then flips to confirm.
  • Card 05 (question form) is often the weakest in production. If they hesitate: model it once, then have them repeat. That's enough.
  • Do the contrast check out loud — don't skip it. It's the gateway to the role play in §4.
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "In S2A you built these tools. Right now you're showing me they stayed with you. That's retention — that's real learning."
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • The B session recap is deliberately shorter than the A session grammar section. The goal is to activate memory, not re-teach. If the student is strong here, go faster and invest that time in the writing task or info gap.

3–4 minutes. Fast-paced, competitive. No explanation — just play. The game reinforces the mustn't vs don't have to distinction in a low-stakes environment.
Layer 1 — Game Management
  • Count to 5 out loud for each card. If the student can't produce a sentence in 5 seconds, you say it — then they repeat. Repeat = full credit.
  • The 8 cards cover all 4 modals twice (2× must, 2× mustn't, 2× have to, 2× don't have to). If one type is consistently slow, that's the target for §6 (Info Gap).
  • Cards can be played in any order — tap them in the order you want. Don't start with the easy ones.
Layer 2 — Game Energy
  • Keep energy high. Celebrate speed: "That was fast — and it was perfect. That's the goal."
  • If they get a wrong modal: "Almost — but that word changes the meaning completely. Try again." Then model the correct one.
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • The game section should always feel different from the activity sections — more energetic, less analytical. Let the student laugh if they get one wrong. Playful error correction is effective at A2.2.
§ 04
Role Play — You Are the HR Manager
10–12 min
Role Play · HR Onboarding · Info Gap Speaking
Welcome to Nexcore — Your First Day
Setup: The student is the HR Manager at Nexcore Industrial Solutions. The teacher plays Carlos — a new engineer on his first day. Carlos has many questions about company rules. The HR manager (student) must explain what Carlos must do, mustn't do, has to do, and doesn't have to do — using real corporate language.

Student goal: Answer all of Carlos's questions using the four modals. No notes. Speak naturally.
Student — HR Manager
Rule 1: Badge — must wear at all times, including factory floor
Rule 2: Confidentiality — mustn't share project data externally
Rule 3: Remote work — must notify manager 24h in advance
Rule 4: Friday dress — don't have to wear formal attire
Rule 5: VPN — must use when working from home
Bonus: Make up one extra rule from your real company
Teacher — Carlos (New Employee)
"Do I have to wear my badge in the break room too?"
"Can I send project updates through my personal WhatsApp?"
"Do I have to tell my manager if I work from home?"
"I want to wear jeans on Fridays — is that OK?"
"What happens if I use public Wi-Fi without the VPN?"
"Is there anything else I should know on my first day?"
⚡ Role Play Challenge — Round 2
Switch: now the teacher plays a difficult new employee who pushes back on the rules. Student (still HR manager) must explain why each rule exists and defend it professionally. "You mustn't because…" / "It's compulsory because…" — reasons and consequences, not just rules.
⚡ Micro-Speaking — Post Role Play
Without looking at the role play card — what were the 5 rules you just explained? Recall them from memory, using the correct modal for each.
Layer 1 — Role Play Direction
  • Play Carlos as a slightly confused but good-faith new employee — ask follow-up questions that require the student to use different modals. Don't make it adversarial in Round 1.
  • Round 2 (pushback): be mildly resistant. "But why can't I use WhatsApp? It's easier." This forces the student to produce reasoning using because + modal — a big upgrade from isolated sentences.
  • If the student gets stuck: pause the role play, ask "What modal do you need here?" — then resume. Don't just give the answer.
  • The bonus rule (from their real company) is the most authentic moment in the session. Give it full attention and praise the real-world connection.
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "You just explained 5 company rules in English — from the HR manager's seat. That's a real professional function."
  • After the pushback round: "When you can defend a rule and explain why it exists, that's B1 language. You're touching B1 territory right now."
Layer 3 — GT Originals & Upsell
  • 💼 "Any student in HR, safety, project management, or operations can use this exact interaction at work — new employee onboarding, supplier inductions, contractor briefings."
  • 🎯 The writing task in §5 continues from this role play — the student now formalizes the rules they just explained verbally. The connection is intentional and reinforces both skills.
§ 05
Writing Task — Write Your Company Policy
8–10 min
Real-World Output · Written Production
Nexcore Industrial Solutions — Employee Policy Card
You are the HR manager. Write a short policy card for new employees. Use each of the four modals exactly once: must, mustn't, have to, and don't have to. Write real, professional sentences — not examples from class. The rules can come from your real workplace.
Example — How it should look
"All employees must display their access badge visibly at all times on company premises.
Employees mustn't share confidential project information via personal messaging apps.
Engineers have to submit a weekly progress report to their line manager by Friday at 5 PM.
Staff don't have to work on public holidays — these are paid rest days."
📋 Your Policy Card — Write below (spoken or on paper)
MUST
All employees must _________________________________________________
MUSTN'T
Employees mustn't _________________________________________________
HAVE TO
________________________ have to _________________________________
DON'T HAVE TO
________________________ don't have to __________________________
🏆 Extension: Read Your Policy Aloud
After writing (or dictating) your 4 sentences, read the complete policy aloud in one go — as if you are presenting it to a new employee. No pausing, no looking up. Professional, clear, direct. That's the real HR delivery standard.
Quick Check — Policy Errors
A new employee wrote these rules. Find the error and choose the correct version.
1. "You mustn't to bring lunch from home." — What is wrong?
2. A rule says: "The gym on floor 3 is optional." How do you say this with a modal?
3. "Do employees must attend the orientation?" — What is wrong?
Layer 1 — Writing Management
  • The student can write on paper, dictate to you, or speak each rule aloud. All three are valid — what matters is the production, not the medium.
  • The biggest errors to watch for: (a) "must to" — no infinitive; (b) confusing mustn't and don't have to; (c) wrong subject-verb agreement with "have to" (he has to, not he have to).
  • Answer key for quick check: Q1 No "to" after modal · Q2 Employees don't have to use the gym · Q3 Use "Do + have to" not "Do + must"
  • The extension (reading aloud) is the most important moment of this section. A student who can read their policy in one professional delivery is ready for real HR communication in English.
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "You just wrote a real company policy in English. That's a document. Not a classroom exercise — a document you could attach to an email today."
  • After the read-aloud: "That's the professional delivery. That's the standard. You're there."
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • 🏆 This writing task is the pinnacle output of M2. It is also part of the Exit Task for the module. A student who can produce 4 correct modal sentences in a policy card has completed the core objective of Rules & Obligations.
  • 💼 "This policy card is LinkedIn-ready. You can post it as a professional milestone: 'Today I wrote company policy in English.' That's a real credential."
§ 06
Speaking — Info Gap: Two Companies, One Conversation
8–10 min
Info Gap · Speaking Format · S2B Rotation
Company A vs Company B — What Are Their Rules?
The student has information about Company A. The teacher has information about Company B. Neither person can see the other's card. Ask and answer questions to complete both profiles. Use "Do you have to…?", "Do they have to…?", "Can they…?", "Is it compulsory to…?" — and answer using must, mustn't, have to, don't have to.
Student's Card — Company A: Nexcore Industrial
🪪Badge: must wear at all times — factory and offices
👔Dress code: ??? — ask the teacher
🏠Remote work: must notify manager 24h in advance
📱Phone in meetings: ??? — ask the teacher
🔒VPN: must use from home — compulsory
📊Reports: ??? — ask the teacher
Teacher's Card — Company B: Databridge Mexico
🪪Badge: don't have to wear — honor system
👔Dress code: don't have to wear formal attire — casual always
🏠Remote work: don't have to notify — fully flexible
📱Phone in meetings: mustn't use phone during client calls — strict rule
🔒VPN: must use always — even in office on public Wi-Fi
📊Reports: have to submit a brief daily update — 3 lines maximum
💡 Teacher: only reveal your card info when the student asks the correct question. Don't volunteer information. Force the question form production.
💬 Useful Question Starters
"At Company B, do employees have to wear a badge?"
"Is it compulsory to notify the manager before working remotely?"
"What about phones in meetings — are they permitted?"
"Do they have to submit reports? How often?"
Post Info Gap — Comparison
After completing both profiles, answer these questions about the two companies.
4. Which company has stricter rules about technology (phones, VPN)?
5. At which company do employees have more flexibility about remote work?
⚡ Micro-Speaking — Compare out loud
Compare the two companies in 4–5 sentences. Use: "At Nexcore, employees must… but at Databridge, they don't have to…"
Which company would you prefer to work for? Explain using must, mustn't, have to, don't have to.
Layer 1 — Info Gap Management
  • The student should ask at least 3 questions with the correct question form: "Do they have to…? / Is it compulsory to…?" Don't answer unless they form the question correctly — gently redirect: "Ask me the question."
  • Answer key: Q4 Databridge Mexico (phones in meetings + always VPN, even in office) · Q5 Databridge — fully flexible, no notice needed.
  • The comparison micro-speaking is where the four modals get used simultaneously in connected speech. This is the highest-level production task of M2.
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "You just ran a comparative analysis of two companies' HR policies — in English. That's a board meeting skill."
  • When they compare: "You used all four modals in one paragraph. That's exactly what mastery looks like."
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • The info gap format simulates real situations where students don't have complete information and must ask questions to get it — procurement meetings, supplier calls, client onboarding. The language function is identical.
§ 07
Speaking Practice — Free Production
8–10 min
Speaking · Free Production · No scaffold
Your Rules. Your Company. Your English.
No role play. No cards. No example sentences. The teacher asks, the student answers from their real experience. All four modals active. 8–10 minutes of authentic speaking.
Question 01
Tell me about a time you had to follow a rule at work that you didn't agree with. What did you have to do? What couldn't you do?
Use: "I had to… / I couldn't… / We were required to… because…"
Question 02
If you could change one rule at your company, what would it be? What would employees have to do differently? What would they no longer have to do?
Use: "I would change… / Employees would have to… / They wouldn't have to… anymore."
Question 03
A new international colleague joins your team next week. What are the 3 most important things you must tell them about working at your company?
Use: "First, you must… / Second, you mustn't… / And finally, you don't have to…"
Question 04
Do you think companies should have more rules or fewer rules? Use examples from your experience.
Use: "I think employees must… / On the other hand, they don't have to… / In my opinion, it's compulsory to…"
Question 05
What is the most important rule in your personal life — not at work? Something you must do, or mustn't do, to stay healthy, successful, or happy?
Use: "In my personal life, I must… / I try never to… / I believe I don't have to… to be happy."
🏆 Final Speaking Challenge — 2 min monologue
"You have 2 minutes. Describe your company's 4 most important rules — one must, one mustn't, one have to, one don't have to — and explain why each one exists. Start when you are ready."
No preparation. No cards. No notes. Teacher only stops for serious modal errors. This is the module mastery proof — if the student can do this, they own the M2 grammar system.
Layer 1 — Free Production Strategy
  • Q1 uses past tense implicitly ("I had to…") — this is intentional. A2.2 students know simple past. Let it emerge naturally. Don't correct the past tense here — focus only on modals.
  • Q3 is the most useful for the student professionally — give it 2–3 minutes if time allows. It simulates an actual onboarding conversation they will have in real life.
  • The 2-min monologue is the exit proof of mastery. If the student produces 4 modal sentences with reasons and no prompting — they have completed M2's core objective.
  • Error tracking during free speaking: make a mental note of max 2 errors. Give feedback at the end, not during. One correction per session — the most frequent or most impactful one.
Layer 2 — Closing Motivation
  • "You spoke for nearly 10 minutes about real workplace rules, in English, without grammar notes. That is A2.2 mastery. That's what this module was for."
  • After the monologue: give one specific, genuine piece of positive feedback — not generic praise. Example: "Your use of 'compulsory' was natural. That word tells me you really own this vocabulary now."
Layer 3 — GT Originals & Module Close
  • 💼 "The four modals of M2 (must, mustn't, have to, don't have to) appear in every professional environment — HR documents, contracts, safety briefings, onboarding, compliance. You now have all four."
  • 🎯 Module Exit Task preview: "In S5 (the review session) you'll do a final 5-minute speaking task: describe 4 real workplace rules + write a 4-line policy statement. You already did both today. The review is the polish, not the learning."
  • Testimonial trigger: "Students who complete M2 tell me they start noticing modal verbs everywhere — in English signs, contracts, emails from international clients. When you notice the grammar in the wild, that's when it becomes yours."
