💰 When you think about money, do you mostly think about right now (bills, salary) or about the future (savings, goals)?

→ Many students have informal goals but never say them in English — this is the session's core need

→ Links personal context to vocabulary: mortgage, pension, emergency fund, apartment
📅 In 5 years, where do you want to be financially? What needs to happen for that to be possible? What's the biggest obstacle?
→ This is the session's speaking topic — collect answers to revisit in §7 Production

Quick Win — 20 seconds
Say this out loud right now: "If I manage to save more this year, I'll…" — finish the sentence with something real from your life. It doesn't need to be perfect.
📘 Pedagogical S4 speaks directly to a core anxiety for adult learners in the Bajío: financial security and planning. Many engineers and coordinators earn good salaries but have no explicit financial plan — this session gives them the language to talk about it. The First Conditional is their tool for expressing real conditions and realistic goals. Expect high engagement.
💡 Engagement Don't rush the warm-up. The goal for Q4 ("where do you want to be in 5 years?") is the emotional anchor of the session. Write student answers on the board. Return to them in §7 Production and ask students to convert their own answers into First Conditional sentences.
§2 Speaking Topic — Financial Goals Rodrigo Vega shares his 5-year plan · Model B1.1 answers ⏱ 8 MIN
Session Topic
Do you have specific financial goals for the next 5 years?
Round 1 — Personal
Do you have an emergency fund? If not, what would make you start one? If yes, is it enough?
"I don't have a real emergency fund yet. If I cut back on eating out, I'll be able to save about $2,000 pesos a month. I think three months of salary would make me feel secure. It's my number one goal right now."
Round 2 — Work & Salary
If you get a raise or a bonus this year, what will you do with the extra money? Save it, invest it, or spend it?
"If I get any extra income this year, I'll split it — half to savings, half to pay off a debt. I've made the mistake of spending bonuses before and regretting it. If I build a habit of saving first, I'll feel more in control of my finances."
Round 3 — Big Picture
What's your most important financial goal for the next 5 years? What will change in your life if you achieve it?
"My main goal is to buy an apartment. If I save consistently for three more years, I'll have enough for the down payment. If I achieve that, I'll stop paying rent — which is basically money I'll never see again. It will completely change my financial situation."
📘 Context — Rodrigo Vega Rodrigo (B1.1 character) is at a mid-career inflection point — good salary, increasing expenses, thinking about stability. His financial narrative runs through M3: he saved in S1, shopped more consciously in S2–3, and now in S4 he's articulating his first real financial goals. Students at this level identify strongly with his situation.

§4 Colloquial English — Money Talk 4 phrases for natural financial conversation · Used by native speakers every day ⏱ 5 MIN
"I'm saving for…"
Used to explain what financial goal you're working toward. More personal and natural than "My financial goal is to…"
"I'm saving for a car." / "I'm saving for a house down payment." / "I'm saving for my kids' education."
"I'm trying to spend less."
A natural way to say you're cutting expenses. "Trying to" shows effort and intention — very honest and relatable. Native speakers use this constantly.
"I'm trying to spend less on food." / "I'm trying to spend less on things I don't need."
"I need to be more organized."
Extremely common self-reflection phrase about money management. Shows self-awareness without being self-critical. Very useful in professional contexts too.
"With my money, I need to be more organized." / "I track expenses but I need to be more organized about it."
"It's easy to lose track."
"Lose track of something" = stop monitoring it closely. Native speakers say this about money, time, and progress. Pairs perfectly with the verb "track" from §3.
"With online shopping, it's easy to lose track of what you're spending." / "I lose track of my subscriptions."
💡 Drill Note Run a 2-minute drill: Teacher gives a context ("You just bought something you didn't need") → student responds with one of the 4 phrases. Then flip it: student gives the context → partner responds. Goal is to hear all 4 phrases used naturally at least twice each before moving to §5.
§5 Grammar — First Conditional + Financial Goals Financial Goals Ladder · Referential approach · 2 interactive activities ⏱ 10 MIN
⚡ 30-Second Poll — Grammar Gut Check
Which sentence sounds more natural and correct to you?
❌ Close — but "will" never goes in the if-clause of First Conditional. The if-clause uses Present Simple, even though we're talking about the future. "If I will save" is the #1 error at B1.1.
✅ Correct! "If I save enough" uses Present Simple in the if-clause — exactly right. The future idea is expressed by "I'll buy" in the result clause. This is First Conditional: real, possible, future condition → likely result.
❌ "If I saved" is Second Conditional — that's for unreal/hypothetical situations. First Conditional uses Present Simple in the if-clause for real, possible future conditions. S5 of this module covers Second Conditional.

First Conditional Structure
Click any step to see how First Conditional describes a real financial goal…
PartStructureFinancial example
If-clauseIf + Present SimpleIf I save $5,000 this year…
Result clausewill + base verb…I'll have the down payment.
Negative ifIf + don't/doesn't + base verbIf I don't track my spending, I'll never save enough.
Negative resultwon't + base verbIf I invest early, I won't have to worry about my pension.
Reversed orderResult first, then if-clauseI'll buy the apartment if I save enough by 2027.
💡 Rule to drill: The if-clause NEVER uses "will." Even though you're talking about the future, use Present Simple: "If I save…" NOT "If I will save…" — this is the #1 error at B1.1.
⚠️ Top errors — correct immediately:
✗ "If I will get a raise, I'll invest." → ✅ "If I get a raise, I'll invest." — no will in the if-clause
✗ "If I save enough, I would buy." → ✅ "If I save enough, I will buy." — would = Second Conditional
✗ "I'll buy an apartment if I'll have money." → ✅ "…if I have enough money." — same rule applies in reversed order

✏️ Activity B — Grammar Rules: True or False?
In First Conditional, the if-clause uses Present Simple, not will + verb.
"If I would save more, I'll invest" is a grammatically correct First Conditional sentence.
The result clause can also come before the if-clause: "I'll buy an apartment if I save enough."
First Conditional is only used for things that are impossible or hypothetical.
§6 Listening — Rodrigo's 5-Year Plan Rodrigo Vega shares his financial goals in a podcast interview · First Conditional throughout ⏱ 8 MIN
Rodrigo Vega
Senior Analyst · Celaya · B1.1 Character
Context: Personal finance podcast
Format: Interview, informal
Rodrigo Vega — My 5-Year Financial Plan
B1.1 · M3 · S4 · Voice: Marcus (American English) · Speed: 0.85
Interviewer: Rodrigo, thanks for joining us. Let's start simple — do you have a financial plan for the next five years?

Rodrigo: Honestly? I didn't have one until about a year ago. I was earning well, but I was losing track of everything. If I hadn't started tracking my expenses last year, I wouldn't know where my money was going. But we're talking about First Conditional today, so let me focus on what I'm planning now.

Interviewer: Perfect. What's your first goal?

Rodrigo: An emergency fund. I'm almost there. If I save two more months at my current rate, I'll complete my three-month fund by March. That's been my foundation. If I have that security, I'll feel much more confident making bigger decisions.

Interviewer: Like what?

Rodrigo: Like investing. If I finish the emergency fund, I'll start putting money into CETES — they're low risk and the returns are decent right now. If the interest rates stay good, I'll put in about fifteen percent of my salary every month. I calculated it — if I do that consistently, I'll have a significant amount in ten years.

Interviewer: And property? Is that in the plan?

Rodrigo: Yes, but it's step four, not step one. If I invest consistently for three years, I'll have enough for a down payment on a small apartment in Celaya. I won't buy anything if the mortgage payments are more than thirty percent of my salary — that's my rule. If I stick to that rule, I'll manage the payments without stress.

Interviewer: What happens if you don't achieve all of this?

Rodrigo: The key thing is I've changed how I think. If I stay organized and keep tracking, I'll make progress — even if it's slower than planned. The worst outcome would be if I stop paying attention. If I lose track again, I'll be back to square one. And I really don't want that.

§7 Production — Your Financial Plan Real goals · First Conditional in authentic context · Accuracy + fluency combined ⏱ 12 MIN
📊 Class Opinion Meter — Agree or Disagree?
"If I had a clear financial plan, I would feel much less stressed about money."
55% of the class agrees
💬 How to run this: Students answer using First Conditional wherever possible. Partner marks: (1) any "will" in the if-clause, (2) any "would" where "will" is needed (Second Conditional confusion). Correct immediately. The goal is confident, accurate First Conditional in a real context.
Question 1 — Your Number One Goal
What is your most important financial goal right now? What will change in your life if you achieve it? How long will it take?
💡 Use: "My main goal is to [verb]… If I [present simple], I'll [result]… I think it will take [time]."
Question 2 — The Conditions
What needs to happen for your goal to be possible? Give 3 specific conditions using "If…, I'll…" For example: a raise, a reduction in expenses, a specific decision. Be honest and specific.
💡 Use: "If [condition 1], I'll… / If [condition 2], I'll… / But if [obstacle], I won't be able to…"
Question 3 — The Obstacles
What's the biggest thing that could stop you from reaching your financial goal? What will you do if that obstacle appears? Plan B — use First Conditional.
💡 Use: "The main risk is [problem]. If [problem] happens, I'll [Plan B]. I won't give up if…"
Question 4 — Advice to a Friend
Your best friend has never saved a single peso — they spend everything they earn. What advice will you give them? Use First Conditional to show them what will happen if they change their habits.
💡 Use: "If you [action], you'll [positive result]. / If you don't [action], you won't [good outcome]. / The first step is…"

You are Rodrigo Vega. Explain your complete 5-year financial plan to a friend who asks: "What's your plan?" — Use at least 4 First Conditional sentences. No notes.
🎯 This week at work — use these 3 phrases
3 Phrases Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"I'm saving for [something specific]."
Use this the next time a colleague asks what you're doing with your money, or when you want to explain why you're cutting expenses. More natural than "My financial goal is…" — this is how native speakers talk.
PHRASE 2
"If I [plan/save/track], I'll [result]."
Use this in any planning conversation — at work or at home. It works for financial goals, project planning, any situation with a realistic condition and a result. This is First Conditional in real life.
PHRASE 3
"I need to be more organized about [topic]."
Use this in professional conversations when you want to show self-awareness without being self-critical. Works for money, time management, email, projects — anywhere. It signals maturity and a growth mindset.
