§3 Grammar · Problem → Tool
Should, Shouldn't, Could
15–18 MIN
🚨 Real Situation — Career Dilemma
Your colleague Carla tells you: "I'm nervous about my job interview tomorrow. I don't know what to wear. I haven't prepared examples. And I'm very tired."

You want to help. You need to give advice — but how do you say this naturally in English?
❓ How do you sound professional? How do you sound friendly but not too strong? What's the difference between "You should do this" and "You could try this"?
✅ The Tool: Should · Shouldn't · Could
You should prepare examples because they help you stand out. (= recommendation, good idea)
You shouldn't stay up late — you need sleep before the interview. (= warning, not a good idea)
You could wear formal business clothes or smart casual. (= suggestion, gentle option)

Rule: Should = "I think this is good for you" · Shouldn't = "I think this is bad for you" · Could = "This is one possible option, you could try it"
📊 Grammar Reference Table
FormStructureExample
AffirmativeSubject + should + base verbYou should prepare examples.
NegativeSubject + shouldn't + base verbYou shouldn't be late to the interview.
QuestionShould + subject + base verb?Should I call or email?
Short answerYes, you should. / No, you shouldn't."Should we wait?" — "Yes, we should."
Could (suggestion)Subject + could + base verbYou could try a different approach.
Why don't you/weWhy don't + subject + base verb?Why don't you research the company first?
⚠️ Critical error to correct immediately:
✗ "You should to prepare" → ✅ You should prepare (base verb — NO "to")
✗ "Should you to call?" → ✅ Should you call? (same rule)
✗ "You could to try" → ✅ You could try (same rule — all modals)
✗ "You should do it because to be professional" → ✅ You should do it because it's professional (use adjective, not infinitive)

✓ SHOULD: prepare examples from your past work
✓ SHOULD: research the company before the interview
✓ SHOULDN'T: stay up late tonight

§4 Reading
Andrea's Email — Career Advice
8–10 MIN
Andrea Soto
Career Coach & Career Changer · Mexico City
Andrea worked as an operations manager for 8 years, then decided to become a career coach. She gives advice to professionals on interviews, career changes, and professional development. She knows both sides — the stress of traditional corporate work AND the freedom of building your own path.
📌 Watch for: should / shouldn't · could · advice giving · professional email tone · why don't you / we
From:
Andrea Soto (Career Coach)
Subject:
Interview Preparation Guide
Hi there,

I'm so glad you're preparing for your interview. Here's my advice:

Before the interview:
You should prepare 3–4 examples from your past work. You should be able to describe a difficult situation, how you handled it, and what the result was. Why don't you write these examples down and practice saying them?

You should research the company. Look at their website, their products, and their recent news. You could also connect with someone who already works there — they might give you insider tips.

On the day:
You shouldn't">shouldn't stay up late the night before. You need sleep. You could review your notes quickly in the morning, but don't stress yourself.

Wear something professional. You could wear a formal suit or smart casual — it depends on the company culture. But you shouldn't">shouldn't wear jeans or very casual clothes.

During the interview:
Listen carefully. You should ask questions about the role and the team. Why don't you ask: "What does a typical day look like?" or "What are the biggest challenges in this role?"

After the interview:
You should send a short thank you email within 24 hours. You could mention something specific from your conversation.

You've got this! Any questions?

Best,
Andrea

§5 Listening
Manager's Advice — Real Conversation
10–12 MIN
A manager gives advice to a stressed employee about workload and career decisions
One-on-One: Career Advice & Workload
A2.2 · M2 · S3A · Voice: Mark (British English, manager) · Speed 0.82
📜 Full script (for teacher use — show during activity if needed)
"Hi, how are you doing? I wanted to check in — you look stressed."
"I'm... I'm okay, but I have so much work. I'm not sleeping well."
"I understand. You should talk to me about this. How many projects are you handling right now?"
"Three big ones. And emails all day. I don't have time to finish anything."
"That's too much. You shouldn't accept more work until you finish these three. Why don't you prioritize? Pick the most important one and focus on that first."
"But what if the client gets angry?"
"You could send them an email explaining your timeline. Be honest. Good clients understand. You should also take a day off soon to rest. That's important."
"A day off? I have too much to do."
"Exactly. You need rest to work well. You could also think about whether you want to continue in this role. Some people are happier in less demanding positions. What do you think?"
"Maybe... I don't know. I like the work, but the pressure is too much."
"Here's my advice: you should prioritize this week, you should talk to the clients, and you could consider talking to HR about your workload. Don't accept more. And take that day off. Okay?"
"Okay. Thank you."
✏️ Listening — Multiple Choice
1. How many projects is the employee handling?
2. What does the manager say the employee should NOT do?
3. What could the employee do to help with workload?
§ Speaking Practice
Give Real Advice — Pair Work
10–12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Students speak in pairs. One student gives a real problem; the other gives advice using should/shouldn't and could. Listen for grammar accuracy — correct "should to" errors immediately. Switch roles after Q3.
Question 1 — Real Work Problem
Tell your partner a real problem from your work right now: too many meetings, a difficult client, not enough sleep, too much email, stressful deadline. Your partner gives you advice using "You should..." and "You could..."
💡 Model: "You should talk to your manager because… / You could try… / Why don't you…?"
Question 2 — Interview Advice
Someone tells you: "I have a job interview next week and I'm very nervous." Give 4 pieces of advice: 2 with "should," 1 with "shouldn't," and 1 with "could."
💡 Start with: "You should definitely… / You should also… / You shouldn't… / You could try…"
Question 3 — Career Decision
Your colleague says: "I have an offer for a new job, but I'm not sure if I should accept it. The salary is better, but the company is in another city." Give advice about what they should consider.
💡 Use: "You should think about… / You could ask the company about… / Why don't you talk to…?"
Question 4 — Daily Life Advice
Your friend says: "I'm very tired. I work too much, I don't exercise, and I eat badly." Give them 3 suggestions using should, shouldn't, and could for better work-life balance.
💡 Ideas: sleep, exercise, time management, delegating, saying no to extra work, hobbies
Question 5 — Advice You Give Often
What advice do you give to people regularly — at work, at home, or to friends? Give 3 pieces of advice you always say, using should/shouldn't/could. Example: "I always tell my team they should communicate early, they shouldn't wait for problems..."
💡 Think of your natural advice-giver role — family, work, culture, experience

🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"You should [action] because it's [reason]."
Use this when giving professional advice to a colleague or friend. The "because" makes it sound thoughtful, not bossy. Examples: "You should update your CV because it will help you get interviews" or "You should communicate early because clients appreciate transparency."
PHRASE 2
"You could try [option] instead."
This is gentler than "should." Use it when offering an alternative or when the person might not like your first suggestion. Sounds friendly and open to other ideas. Example: "The email approach is good, but you could try calling — it might be faster."
PHRASE 3
"Why don't you [action]?"
This is a natural, conversational way to suggest something. Sounds less formal than "should" and more like a friend. Example: "Why don't you ask for more time?" or "Why don't we schedule a quick meeting to discuss this?"

Session Checklist
5–8 MIN
01
Should = recommendation. Sounds professional. Use it when you think something is good for the person.
02

03
