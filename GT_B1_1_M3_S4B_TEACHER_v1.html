📘 How to run Hot Take Students get 30 seconds to think, then 60 seconds to speak — no notes. Key requirement: at least 2 First Conditional sentences in the answer. Partner writes down every "will" they hear in an if-clause (error). Teacher collects errors on the board — use them in the Speed Round. This is a cold production task: no warm-up, straight to output.
§2 Pitch Round — Saver vs Spender Two sides · First Conditional starters · 3 minutes per side ⏱ 10 MIN
💬 Motion: "If you save and invest from an early age, you will have a much better life than if you spend freely now." — Argue for your assigned side using First Conditional. Each side has 3 minutes. Aim for at least 4 First Conditional sentences.
Side A — The Saver
"Save & Invest Now"
  • "If you invest even 5% of your salary now, you'll be surprised how much you have in ten years."
  • "If you build an emergency fund first, you'll never have to use a credit card in a crisis."
  • "If people don't plan their retirement, they'll depend on family or the government."
  • "If you track your expenses for just one month, you'll find money you didn't know you had."
Side B — The Spender
"Live in the Present"
  • "If you sacrifice everything for the future, you'll look back and realize you didn't enjoy the journey."
  • "If you spend on experiences now, you'll have memories that money can't buy later."
  • "If the economy changes, your savings plan won't protect you — adaptability will."
  • "If people are too focused on financial goals, they'll miss opportunities to live fully."
💡 Engagement After both sides present, ask the class to vote: who made the stronger case — and more importantly, which argument used First Conditional more effectively? This metacognitive step (grammar as a tool for persuasion) is the B1.1 leap. Students notice that "If you invest now, you'll…" is inherently more persuasive than a simple opinion statement.
§3 Relay — The If-Chain 5 transformations · Each result becomes the next condition · First Conditional in sequence ⏱ 8 MIN
🔗 How the If-Chain works: Each step gives you the condition for the next sentence. The result of sentence 1 becomes the "if" of sentence 2. Read the example in each step, then write your own version in the text box. Click "Show Model" to compare. The chain tells Rodrigo's complete financial journey — one condition at a time.
🔗 Rodrigo's Financial If-Chain
1
START HERE — The first condition:
If Rodrigo tracks his expenses this month, he will see where his money goes.
✍️ Now write your own version — use a different verb or situation:
"If I track my spending with an app, I'll find at least 2,000 pesos I didn't know I was wasting."
2
↳ Result of Step 1 becomes the condition:
If he sees where his money goes, he will start saving automatically.
✍️ Continue the chain — what happens when you start saving?
"If I start saving consistently, I'll build my emergency fund within six months."
3
↳ Result of Step 2 becomes the condition:
If he builds his emergency fund, he will feel financially secure for the first time.
✍️ What changes when you feel financially secure?
"If I feel financially secure, I'll be able to make bigger decisions — like investing or changing jobs."
4
↳ Result of Step 3 becomes the condition:
If he starts investing, his money will grow while he sleeps.
✍️ What becomes possible when your money is growing?
"If my money grows consistently, I'll have enough for a down payment on an apartment in three years."
5
↳ The final result — Rodrigo's 5-year goal:
If he buys his apartment, he will stop paying rent — and start building real wealth.
✍️ Write YOUR final goal — what will your financial chain end with?
"If I buy my own place, I'll stop giving money to someone else every month and start building something that's truly mine."
🗣️ Spoken Chain Challenge
Now do it WITHOUT reading. Partner A says Step 1. Partner B says Step 2 (using Part A's result as their "if"). Continue until you reach Step 5. No notes — just the chain. Time limit: 90 seconds total.

§5 Solo Challenge — Your 5-Year Financial Plan 120 seconds · Personal · First Conditional throughout · No notes ⏱ 8 MIN
⭐ Solo Challenge — 2 Minutes
Give your complete 5-year financial plan. Speak as if you're explaining it to a colleague who asks: "What's your plan for the next five years — financially?" No notes. Real goals only.
01
Start with your current situation — what step of the Financial Goals Ladder are you on?
02
Use at least 4 First Conditional sentences: "If I [present simple], I'll [result]."
03
Include at least 1 negative: "If I don't [action], I won't [consequence]."
04
End with your most important long-term goal and what it will change in your life.
05
No "will" in the if-clause. Partner marks every violation — lose 1 point per error.
📘 How to assess Partner rates: (1) Grammar accuracy — no "will" in if-clause (0–3 pts), (2) Vocabulary from S4A — used at least 4 financial words (0–3 pts), (3) Coherence and flow — does the plan make sense and sound natural? (0–4 pts). Max 10 points. Enter scores into the Leaderboard in §7.
§6 Source Analysis — Financial Advice in the Wild 4 real-world formats · First Conditional in use · Critical reading + metacognition ⏱ 8 MIN
🐦 Tweet / X Post
"Hot take: If you still don't have an emergency fund in your 30s, you're going to have a very bad time. Stop buying coffee and start a fund. TODAY. If you start this month, you'll have 3 months saved by summer. No excuses."
1. Find 2 First Conditional sentences in this tweet. Are they grammatically correct?
2. Does the author's tone make the advice more or less persuasive to you?
🧠 Metacognitive: Why does First Conditional make financial advice sound more convincing than simple commands like "Save money!"?
💬 WhatsApp — Group Chat
Carlos: "Oye, if you don't start saving your Afore contributions now, you will retire with almost nothing. I calculated it — if you put in an extra 2% per month, you'll add like 40% to your total pension. My brother worked this out with an advisor."
1. Is Carlos's First Conditional correct? Find any errors.
2. Is "you will retire" or "you'll retire" more natural in this context? Why?
🧠 Metacognitive: Native speakers often use "you will" (not contracted) for emphasis or warnings. When is this more effective than "you'll"?
📧 Email Newsletter — Finance Blog
"Dear Rodrigo, if you invest $2,000 pesos per month in CETES starting now, you will accumulate approximately $280,000 in ten years at current rates. If you wait five years to start, you will only accumulate $120,000. The difference is $160,000 — that's the cost of waiting."
1. What is the writer's strategy for using First Conditional twice? Is it effective?
2. Could you rewrite "you will accumulate" as "you'll accumulate"? Does it change the tone?
🧠 Metacognitive: The writer uses data inside the First Conditional. How does this change the impact of the grammatical structure?
💼 LinkedIn Post
"5 things I wish I knew at 25 about money: 1. If you start tracking expenses, you will find 15–20% waste immediately. 2. If you build a 3-month emergency fund, you will sleep better every night. 3. If you invest early, compound interest will do the heavy work. The best time to start was yesterday. The second best time is today."
1. The author uses First Conditional in a list format. Is this effective? Does it feel natural?
2. Which of the 3 conditional sentences is most persuasive to you — and why?
🧠 Metacognitive: This LinkedIn post uses generic "you" not a specific person. How does First Conditional work differently when addressing everyone vs one person?
⭐ Discussion anchor After pairs discuss, bring the class together for one question: "Which of these 4 sources would actually change your financial behavior — and why?" This connects the grammar to real persuasion and rhetoric — a B1.1 critical thinking skill that differentiates this program.

🎯 This week at work — use these 3 phrases
3 Phrases Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"If you [action], you'll [result]."
Use this when giving advice at work or to a friend. First Conditional makes advice sound evidence-based, not just opinion. "If you track your time, you'll be surprised how much you waste in meetings" works in any professional context.
PHRASE 2
"If I don't [action], I won't [result]."
The negative form of First Conditional — use it to explain consequences honestly. "If I don't prepare, I won't present well." More direct and professional than "maybe I should prepare." Shows clear thinking.
PHRASE 3
"My goal is to [verb] by [time]."
Clear, professional, and immediately understandable. Use it in work conversations about projects, in personal conversations about money, or in performance reviews. "My goal is to finish the report by Friday" sounds like a B2 speaker.
