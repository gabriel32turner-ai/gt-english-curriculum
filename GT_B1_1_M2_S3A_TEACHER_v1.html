Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Diagnostic function: What does the student already know about salary/benefits vocabulary? Don't intervene yet. Note: do they say "bonus", "healthcare", "pension", "perks"? All of that gets activated in §2.
  • Key observation: Do they justify ("because I value…") or just describe? B1.1 target is giving reasons. If they just say "I choose A" — push: "Why? What's your reason?"
  • Error to note (fix in §3): "I haven't never had good benefits" → "I've never had good benefits" — double negative, very common with Spanish L1 speakers.
Capa 2 · Motivation
  • Script: "Today we're talking about something that directly affects your professional life — what you actually earn, beyond the number on your paycheck. The benefits package can be worth thousands of pesos you never see. Let's start: which offer do you choose?"
  • Micro-win: If the student produces a complete opinion: "Perfect — that's exactly the kind of sentence we're going to build on. More of that." Award Badge 1 (🎙 First Speaker) after they speak.
Capa 3 · Context (Bajío)
  • In manufacturing companies in Guanajuato and SLP, benefits packages (IMSS, Infonavit, savings funds, food vouchers = "vales de despensa", bonuses = "aguinaldo") are a huge part of total compensation. This topic is highly personal and motivating.
  • Key cultural note: Many Mexican corporate students undervalue their benefits because they don't calculate total comp. This session gives them the vocabulary to discuss it — and eventually negotiate it in English.

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Don't read each definition aloud. Click ▶ together. Student repeats once. Then ask for an immediate example from their real job: "Does your company provide any of these?"
  • Key distinction to drill: "salary" (fixed, monthly) vs "compensation" (total package). Most students say "salary" when they mean total comp. Correct this early — it's a $$ conversation gap.
  • Pronunciation trap: "benefit" /ˈbenɪfɪt/ — students say "be-ne-FI-cio" (Spanish transfer). Stress on first syllable. "BENnefit". "BENefits". Model it 3 times.
  • Award Badge 2 (📚 Vocab Master) when flashcard wall reaches 12/16 or higher.
Capa 2 · Activation
  • Quick oral activation: "Tell me one benefit you've already received at this company. One you haven't received yet. One you've never had at any job." This forces immediate grammar + vocab integration before §3.
§ 03
⚡ Grammar — PP Adverbs in Context
just · already · yet · never · ever — precision tools for compensation conversations
03
8 min
⚡ Present Perfect Precision Adverbs — Benefits & Work Context
just
Very recently happened · Immediate past
"We've just updated the benefits package." · "I've just received a job offer."
already
Before expected / sooner than expected
"I've already negotiated my salary once this year." · "They've already offered me healthcare."
yet
Until now — questions & negatives only
"Have you received your bonus yet?" · "They haven't updated the pension plan yet."
never / ever
In your whole life · All experiences
"I've never worked somewhere with private healthcare." · "Have you ever negotiated a benefits package?"
⚠️ Top errors to correct immediately:
✗ "I haven't never received a bonus" → ✅ I've never received a bonus (no double negative)
✗ "Have you received already your bonus?" → ✅ Have you already received your bonus? (position matters)
✗ "I haven't received my bonus still" → ✅ I haven't received my bonus yet ("still" is not used this way)
✏️ Quick Poll — Choose the Correct Adverb
1. "We _____ implemented the new healthcare policy — it started yesterday."
2. "Has the company increased the food allowance _____ ?" (We expected it last month)
3. "I've _____ worked at a company that offered private healthcare — never experienced it."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.1 grammar is referential — clear explanation + 2 activities, no drilling. The Quick Poll is the ONLY grammar exercise. After that, grammar appears naturally in speaking (§4–§SP).
  • The key insight: these adverbs add timeline precision. Without them, PP is vague. With them, it sounds professional. Example contrast: "I received a bonus" vs "I've just received my Q3 bonus." The second is B1.1.
  • Position rule: just/already go BETWEEN have and past participle. Yet goes AT THE END. Never goes between have and past participle. Award Badge 3 (⚡ Grammar Pro) when all 3 poll answers are correct.
Capa 3 · Real Application
  • "You will use ALL of these adverbs today in real conversations about your own job and benefits. The grammar is not the goal — the conversation is."

§ 05
🃏 Question Cards — Reveal & Respond
4 questions · Graded difficulty · Click to reveal model B1.1 answer
05
10 min
1
BasicPersonal Experience
What benefits has your current company already provided? Have you received any that surprised you?
Use: have already provided · have received · has included · just / already / never
Model Answer — B1.1 Target
"My company has already provided several benefits — healthcare, a food allowance, and flexible Fridays. I was actually surprised when they offered the food allowance — I hadn't expected that. The one benefit I haven't received yet is a performance bonus, but I understand it's coming in Q4. I've never worked somewhere that provided private healthcare before this job."
Note PP adverbs in action: already · haven't received yet · never. This is natural B1.1 — the adverbs add timeline and precision, not just grammar exercise.
2
MediumOpinion + Justification
Would you take a lower salary for better benefits? Why — or why not? Has your opinion on this changed over the years?
This is the core question of today. Don't think too long — just speak.
Model Answer — B1.1 Target
"Honestly, yes — I would. I've already been in situations where I chose a higher salary and ended up stressed with no healthcare or flexibility. Now I think about total compensation, not just the number. When I calculate the value of benefits — pension, healthcare, allowances — it can represent 20 or 30 percent of your actual income. I've never regretted taking a job with fewer perks, but I have regretted taking one with no healthcare. That's a deal-breaker for me now."
This answer uses: already, never + PP · compensation vocabulary · real justification with numbers. This is the B1.1 opinion standard.
3
MediumWorkplace Reality
Do companies in Mexico offer competitive benefits packages? What have you noticed — or what haven't they done yet?
Use: haven't offered yet · have already changed · have never included
Model Answer — B1.1 Target
"It depends a lot on the company. Large multinationals — automotive, aerospace — have already invested heavily in benefits to attract and retain talent. But smaller companies haven't caught up yet. I've never worked at a small company that offered private healthcare or a real pension plan. The law requires the basics — IMSS, Infonavit — but 'competitive' compensation means much more. I think the market is changing, but slowly."
Mexico-specific context: IMSS + Infonavit = legal minimums. Companies that go beyond that retain talent. This is highly personal and discussable for Bajío students.
4
AdvancedNegotiation Scenario
You've just received a job offer. The salary is 10% lower than your current one, but the benefits are significantly better. How do you negotiate? What do you say?
Roleplay scenario · Use negotiation verbs + PP adverbs in your response
Model Answer — B1.1 Target
"Thank you for the offer — I've already reviewed the compensation package in detail, and I'm genuinely interested. I do want to negotiate the base salary slightly. I've just completed a major project at my current company, and the market rate for my role has already increased this year. Would it be possible to discuss a salary closer to my current level? The benefits package is excellent — the healthcare, pension, and flexible work policy are genuinely valuable — and I haven't yet seen a package this complete in my sector. I'd love to make this work."
This is real business English negotiation at B1.1. The student should adapt this to their actual industry. "I'd love to make this work" = professional closing phrase.

§ SP
🗣 Speaking Practice — It's Not Just About the Money
6 questions · Progressive depth · Click to reveal model B1.1 answers
SP
10 min
How this works: Student answers first — then reveal the model answer. The model is your benchmark, not your script. Aim to be as complete and natural as the model.
1
What's the most valuable benefit you've ever received from a job — and has it changed your opinion of that company?
Use: have ever received · has changed · I've always believed…
Personal experience 60 sec B1.1
Model B1.1 Answer
"The most valuable benefit I've ever received was flexible working hours. It sounds simple, but it changed everything — I could drop my children at school, manage my energy, and work when I was actually productive. It completely changed how I felt about the company. I've never been as loyal to an employer as I was at that job. I think flexibility is worth more than any bonus to me personally."
Note: this answer uses "ever", "never", "completely changed" — natural Present Perfect with clear impact on the present.
2
Have you ever negotiated your salary or benefits package? What happened — and what would you do differently now?
Use: I've already / I've never / I hadn't yet / I would now…
Negotiation 90 sec B1.1
Model B1.1 Answer
"I've only negotiated my salary once — and honestly, I wasn't well prepared. I hadn't researched market rates, I didn't have data, and I had never had a real negotiation conversation in English. I received a small increase, but I think I could have gotten more. Now, I've already started tracking the market and understanding my total compensation. Next time, I'll negotiate both salary AND benefits. I know the vocabulary now."
The last sentence "I know the vocabulary now" = meta-awareness of the session. It's subtle but powerful — the student connects learning to real application.
3
What's one benefit your current company hasn't offered yet — but that you think would make a real difference to employees? Why?
Use: hasn't offered yet · would attract · would retain · I believe…
Opinion 90 sec B1.1
Model B1.1 Answer
"My company hasn't offered a remote work option yet, and I think that's a real missed opportunity. We work in a manufacturing context, so it's not always possible — but for administrative and engineering roles, it absolutely is. I've already seen colleagues leave for companies that provide more flexibility. I believe that if you offer remote work, you attract better talent AND retain the people you have. It doesn't cost money — it costs trust."
Strong closing: "It doesn't cost money — it costs trust." This is the kind of opinion sentence that distinguishes B1.1 from B1.2. Encourage the student to find their own version.
4
It's not just about the money — do you agree or disagree? Give examples from your own experience to support your position.
This is the session's central debate. Surprise the teacher with something they haven't heard yet.
Debate 2 min B1.1 ⭐
Model B1.1 Answer
"I agree — and disagree. I think it IS about money up to a certain point. If you haven't covered your basic needs — rent, food, security — then salary is everything. But once you've already crossed that threshold, the other things start to matter more: culture, flexibility, healthcare, growth. I've worked in places that paid well but were toxic, and I've worked in places that didn't pay much but I never wanted to leave. The money was better at the first job. The life was better at the second. I know which one I'd choose now."
This answer has nuance — agree AND disagree, with a reason. That's the B1.1 opinion target. It also uses "crossed a threshold" — teach this phrase, it's very natural in English.
