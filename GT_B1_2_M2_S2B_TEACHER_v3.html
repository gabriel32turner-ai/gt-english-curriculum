⚡ Rapid RS Questions drill: Maestro da una pregunta directa → alumno la reporta inmediatamente. Sin pausa.
"Are you planning to negotiate?" → She asked me if I was planning to negotiate.  ·  "Why do you think you deserve more?" → He asked me why I thought I deserved more.  ·  "Don't share this." → She told me not to share it. · 4 rondas. Velocidad creciente.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • RS Questions drill: fastest consolidation tool. If "asked what did I earn" appears → stop, draw the contrast visually: cross out "did", rewrite in statement order. One correction, immediate retry. Never let the error pass twice.
  • 5 minutes max. If the drill goes well → one bonus command round: "Don't discuss this with anyone." → "She told me not to discuss it." Commands are the weakest for most students.

Game 2 — 5–6 min · Salary Buzz

Toca la carta → respuesta completa. B1.2 standard: full sentence. RS bonus: incluir una RS question en cualquier respuesta.

DEFINE + USE
pay gap
Difference in pay between groups. "HR asked me if I thought the pay gap in our team was significant. I'm not gonna lie — it is."
RS DRILL
"Why do you think you deserve a raise?"
→ "My manager asked me why I thought I deserved a raise." (Wh- reported → statement order, no auxiliary did)
COLLOQUIAL
HR hides salary info as policy. Your reaction?
"That's such a mood." — "Asking for transparency and being told 'it's company policy'? That's such a mood — everyone knows what it hides."
DEFINE + USE
resentment
Bitter feeling of being treated unfairly. "She told me her resentment had been building for months — ever since she found out about the pay gap."
RS DRILL
"Are you open to discussing salary expectations?"
→ "The recruiter asked me if I was open to discussing my salary expectations." (if + statement order, are → was)
COLLOQUIAL
HR asks you to justify your request without any reference data.
"No way." — "No way can I justify my request if they won't tell me the salary band. The information asymmetry is the whole problem."
DEFINE + USE
disclosure
Making information known. "My manager asked me not to disclose the terms of my contract — that's salary secrecy culture at its most visible."
RS DRILL
"Don't talk to HR before talking to me."
→ "She told me not to talk to HR before talking to her." (negative command: told me + not to + base verb)
COLLOQUIAL
5% salary difference per year, 20-year career. One phrase?
"It adds up." — "5% per year sounds small. In pension and lifetime earnings? It adds up to hundreds of thousands of pesos."
DEFINE + USE
negotiate
To discuss terms to reach an agreement. "She asked me whether I had ever negotiated a salary offer — or just accepted the first number. I had never pushed back."
COLLOQUIAL
Someone makes a fair point about secrecy you hadn't considered.
"Fair enough." — "He said companies need secrecy to prevent internal politics. Fair enough — but that same secrecy enables discrimination. They're not the same problem."
CHALLENGE ⭐
Use "asked whether" + "equity" + colloquial phrase in one sentence.
E.g.: "She asked whether we believed the structure was based on equity — fair enough question. I'm not gonna lie: it isn't." Accept any natural combination.

§ 04
News — Read & React: The EU Salary Transparency Directive
8–10 min
Adapted News Article · B1.2 Reading · Reported Speech in the Wild
EU Companies Must Now Publish Salary Ranges — What Does This Mean for Workers?
The European Union's Salary Transparency Directive came into force in 2023, requiring companies with more than 100 employees to disclose the salary range for any advertised job. Workers will also have the right to request information about the average pay of colleagues doing the same work, broken down by gender.

Supporters of the law argue it is the most effective tool ever created to close the pay gap. "You cannot have equity without information," one Brussels official was quoted as saying. Unions mentioned that the directive would end decades of resentment caused by hidden salary disparities.

Critics, however, warn of unintended consequences. Several HR directors told journalists they were concerned the law would create internal tension. One manager said her team had asked her whether the directive meant all salaries would be published individually — they do not. Another executive asked whether smaller companies would be able to justify the administrative cost.

Mexican companies, while not subject to the directive, are watching closely. In a country where salary remains one of the most taboo topics in professional culture, transparency at this scale would represent a cultural revolution. HR professionals here have already begun asking: could something similar work in Mexico?
Your tasks:
1. Report 2 things from the article — what the official said, what HR directors told journalists, what the manager's team asked. Use: said / told / asked whether / asked if.
2. React: Do you think this law would work in Mexico? Why or why not? Use: disclosure / equity / resentment / pay gap + 1 colloquial phrase.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The RS structures in the article are highlighted in cyan — "was quoted as saying / mentioned / told journalists / said her team had asked her whether / asked whether." Ask the student to find them before discussing content. That's the RS reading skill.
  • Task 1: report from the article. Task 2: opinion reaction. 4 minutes to read, 4 minutes to react. The Mexico paragraph is the emotional hook and the direct bridge to §6 debate.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "This is a real law affecting real companies right now. HR directors in Europe are having this conversation. You're going to debate whether it makes sense for Mexico — in English."
§ 05
Role Play — The Salary Review
12–14 min
Role Play · No scaffolding
You found out your colleague earns 15% more than you.
You've requested a salary review meeting. Your manager agreed but confirmed nothing. This is a real negotiation. Your objective: get a clear answer, a commitment, or an honest reason. The teacher plays HR / your manager. No scripts — just use everything you have.
YOUR ROLE — Employee
Objective: Ask why the gap exists. Request a raise or a timeline. Report the conversation after.
Available vocab: negotiation / equity / fairness / comparison / resentment / disclose / justify / close the gap
RS question starters: "I'd like to ask whether… / Could you tell me if… / I want to understand why…"
Colloquial available: "I'm not gonna lie…" / "Fair enough, but…" / "It adds up…"
Pushback: If HR says budget is limited → ask when. If HR asks if you have other offers → counter-question.
TEACHER ROLE — HR Manager
Opening: "Thanks for coming in. I understand you wanted to discuss your compensation."
RS trigger questions to ask:
→ "Are you satisfied with your current salary?"
→ "Do you have any offers from other companies?"
→ "Why do you feel the comparison is unfair?"
→ "What salary range are you expecting?"
Resistance: "Budget is limited." / "I can't disclose individual salaries." / Push back twice before conceding.
🎯 Post-RP Challenge — Report the Conversation
Immediately after: "Report the meeting in 5–6 sentences. What did HR ask you? What did they tell you to do? What did you ask? Use: asked whether / asked if / asked why / told me to / told me not to." — The conversation just happened. The RS is real. This is the most authentic RS question practice of the session.
B1.2 RP standard: No scaffolding during the RP itself. After: identify 1 RS error + 1 vocabulary gap. Model a correct sentence. "Now say that sentence correctly." One retry. Done.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The most valuable activity of S2B. Each HR question you ask as the teacher is a future RS sentence for the student. They report it immediately after — that's real Reported Speech, not textbook exercise.
  • Push back at least twice as HR. "Budget is limited" is resistance #1. "I can't disclose other salaries" is resistance #2. Make the student work for every concession. Negotiation without pushback is just conversation.
  • Post-RP report: 60 seconds, 4+ RS instances = B1.2 mastery confirmed. "She asked me whether I was satisfied. I told her I wasn't. She mentioned the budget. I asked why the comparison showed a gap." — if they hit that, celebrate it specifically.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "You just had a salary negotiation in English. The language was yours — no grammar on the board, no hints. That's what B1.2 feels like."
Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
  • "If you ever negotiate a salary in English — with a multinational HR team or in a job interview — this is the language you just practised. The ROI on today's session is directly measurable."
§ 06
Debate — Should Mexico Require Salary Transparency by Law?
10–12 min
⚖️
B1.2 Debate · Full Production · No Scaffolding
Mexico's companies: legally required to publish salary bands?
"The EU has done it. Some US states have done it. Should Mexico follow — or is the cultural and business context too different? Take a position. Defend it. Use the vocabulary from S2A and report what different experts have said."
✅ PRO — Transparency
Salary secrecy enables pay gaps you can't fix what you can't measure
Employees have a right to know if they're paid fairly relative to contribution
Transparency improves retention — people who feel fairly paid are more loyal
Mexico has labor laws — salary transparency is a natural next step
International companies in Mexico already comply in their home countries
VS
❌ ANTI — Privacy
Disclosure creates resentment and internal politics that damage team cohesion
Mexican corporate culture is not ready — the taboo is cultural, not just corporate
Salary depends on loyalty, relationships, negotiation — numbers without context mislead
Small businesses cannot absorb the administrative cost of compliance
Without salary bands by role, numbers alone create misunderstanding
💬 B1.2 target language: "I'd argue that…" / "The evidence suggests…" / "My colleague pointed out that…" (RS!) / "Fair enough, but…" / "I'm not gonna lie — the data shows…" / "It adds up to a systemic problem." / "There's no way companies would voluntarily disclose this without legal pressure." / Reference §4 article: "The EU official mentioned that equity requires information — and I think…"
Debate structure: 2 min pro · 2 min anti · 2 min free exchange (student can switch sides). B1.2 RS integration: Any reference to §4 news → must be reported: "The Brussels official said that equity requires information." / "The HR director mentioned transparency creates tension." — RS in a political argument = natural mastery.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The visual debate arena (images left and right) creates a physical "pro vs anti" frame. Point to the image on the student's side when they're arguing. Makes position-switching more dramatic and memorable.
  • RS integration instruction: when students reference the article → they must report it. This is spontaneous RS mastery. Mark it explicitly: "You just used Reported Speech in a political argument. That's B1.2 in the wild."
  • If student stays comfortable → challenge: "Now argue the opposite. Same vocabulary, opposite position." Switching forces more nuanced language.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "This topic is current, it's real, and it's coming to Mexico. The conversation you just had in English — you'll have it again, in real life, sooner than you think."
Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
  • "S3A continues with Side Hustles — same grammar (RS), new topic. By S4 you'll be using RS to talk about luxury brands and status. By S5 you'll be doing a capstone about financial freedom. The language builds."
