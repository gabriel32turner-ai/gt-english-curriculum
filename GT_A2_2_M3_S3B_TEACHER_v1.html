§1
Hot Take — 60 Seconds, No Notes
Andrea's scenario · Timed production · should/shouldn't/could under pressure
⏱ 5 MIN
🔥 The Situation
Andrea Soto has 24 hours to decide: accept the higher-paying job at her old company, or stay at her current startup. Her current manager just told her there's a promotion available — but it won't be confirmed for 3 months. The old company wants an answer tomorrow morning.
You have 60 seconds. Give Andrea your honest recommendation. Use at least 3 modals: should / shouldn't / could. No notes. The clock starts now.
Hot Take Timer
01
Use should at least once — give a clear recommendation
02
Use shouldn't at least once — warn against something specific
03
Use could at least once — offer a gentle option she might not have considered
📘 Pedagogical Note
Students often shut down when they can't remember the exact grammar. Hot Take trains them to commit and produce under time pressure — the real-world condition. Accept all attempts; correct only after the timer ends. Focus error correction on: missing modals, "should to," and "I recommend you to."
§2
Pitch Round — Debate: Stay or Go?
Two sides · Structured argument with should/could · 3 minutes each side
⏱ 10 MIN
📋 How to run this: Divide the class. Side A argues Andrea should stay. Side B argues Andrea should take the offer. Each side has 3 minutes to build their case using should, shouldn't, and could. Then open a 2-minute class vote. The side with the better grammar wins — not just the better argument.
Side A — Stay
Andrea should stay at the startup
  • "She should wait for the promotion — it's almost confirmed."
  • "She shouldn't leave just for money — growth matters more at her stage."
  • "She could negotiate a raise at her current company using the offer as leverage."
  • "Why don't we think about where she wants to be in 5 years? The startup gives her that path."
  • "If I were her, I wouldn't go back to a company that already let her go."
Side B — Go
Andrea should take the offer
  • "She should take the 20% raise — that's real money, not a promise."
  • "She shouldn't wait 3 months for a promotion that might not happen."
  • "She could always look for new opportunities again once she's more stable financially."
  • "Why don't we be practical? Bills don't wait for corporate timelines."
  • "Have you thought about the fact that job security at a startup is risky?"
⏱ PITCH TIMER
🔥 Engagement Note
Award a bonus point to any student who correctly uses "Why don't we…?" or "Have you thought about…?" — the colloquial forms from S3A. Call them out by name when it happens. Public recognition accelerates uptake.
§3
Relay — Advice Chain
5 transformations · Each student builds on the last sentence · should → could → shouldn't
⏱ 8 MIN
💬 Advice Chain Rules: Each prompt gives a situation + a starting sentence. Student 1 reads the sentence. Student 2 must add a second piece of advice using a different modal. Student 3 adds a third using the remaining modal. Then the class votes: does the chain make sense? Is the advice realistic?
1
Your colleague has too much work and is skipping lunch every day.
Starter: "She should talk to her manager about her workload."
→ Student 2: Add advice with could | → Student 3: Add advice with shouldn't
Chain example: "She could block her lunch hour in the calendar." / "She shouldn't accept more projects right now."
2
Your friend has a job interview tomorrow morning at 8 AM and hasn't prepared anything.
Starter: "He shouldn't go to bed early — he needs to prepare tonight."
→ Student 2: Add advice with should | → Student 3: Add advice with could
Grammar note: "shouldn't go to bed" here means "shouldn't go yet" — check students parse this correctly.
3
A client is always sending angry emails to the team. Your colleague doesn't know how to respond.
Starter: "He could schedule a call instead of replying by email — it's less confrontational."
→ Student 2: Add advice with should | → Student 3: Add advice with shouldn't
Focus here on professional register — "shouldn't ignore the emails" is a natural continuation.
4
Andrea is thinking about changing careers again — now she wants to study for a master's degree while working full time.
Starter: "She should research the program requirements before applying."
→ Student 2: Add advice with could | → Student 3: Add advice with shouldn't
Challenge: Can students connect the advice logically? "She could start part-time / She shouldn't underestimate the time commitment."
5
A friend works from home and never exercises. He says he feels tired all the time but has no motivation to change.
Starter: "He shouldn't sit at the same desk for 8 hours without moving."
→ Student 2: Add advice with should | → Student 3: Add advice with could
⭐ Challenge: Student 4 (if available) can add a colloquial phrase: "If I were him…" or "Have you thought about…?"

Your CV has a 2-year gap. What should you do before the interview?
✅ "You should prepare an explanation. You could mention any freelance work or personal projects."

A colleague asks: "Should I mention my salary expectations in the first interview?" Advise them.
✅ "You shouldn't mention salary in the first interview — wait until they bring it up."
Modal: shouldn't
You sent an email to the wrong client with confidential information. What should you do?
✅ "You should contact your manager immediately. You could also send a follow-up email to explain the mistake."
Modal: should + could
Your colleague is always 10–15 minutes late to team meetings. What should you tell them?
✅ "You should talk to them privately. You shouldn't criticize them in front of the team."
Modal: should + shouldn't
A friend works overtime every week but never gets paid for it. Advise them.
✅ "They should talk to HR about the policy. They shouldn't keep working for free without saying anything."
Modal: should + shouldn't
Your team member uses their phone for personal social media during work hours. What do you do?
✅ "You could mention it casually first. If it continues, you should have a direct conversation about focus."
Modal: could → should (escalation)
A colleague hates their manager but is afraid to say anything. What should they do?
✅ "They should document specific examples. They could talk to HR if it's a serious issue. They shouldn't quit without trying to fix it first."
Modal: should + could + shouldn't
Your friend wants a promotion but has never asked for one. What would you advise?
✅ "They should request a meeting with their manager. They could prepare a list of recent achievements to show their value."
Modal: should + could
Two colleagues in your team don't speak to each other after an argument last month. What should happen?
✅ "They should have a mediated meeting. The manager shouldn't ignore the conflict — it affects the whole team."
Modal: should + shouldn't
You missed an important deadline. Your client is angry. What do you do right now?
✅ "You should call them — not email — and apologize. You should give a new deadline immediately. You shouldn't make excuses."
Modal: should + shouldn't
§5
Solo Challenge — 2 Minutes · No Help
Andrea's full career scenario · Complete advice monologue · All modals required
⏱ 8 MIN
🎯 Solo Challenge — Andrea's Final Question
Andrea calls you one last time. She says: "I've thought about everything. I still don't know what to do. I need one final, honest recommendation from you. Tell me what I should do — and be specific. I'm listening."
  • Give at least 2 sentences with "should" — clear, direct recommendations
  • Give at least 1 sentence with "shouldn't" — something to avoid
  • Give at least 1 sentence with "could" — a creative option she hasn't considered
  • Add a reason to at least 2 of your pieces of advice: "…because…"
  • End with a colloquial phrase from S3A: "If I were you…" / "It's worth trying." / "My advice would be…"
Solo Challenge Timer
💰 Retention & Upsell Note
After the Solo Challenge, acknowledge the best attempt publicly: "That was B1-level advice delivery. That's exactly the fluency we build in B1.1." This plants the seed for the next enrollment conversation. Students who feel progression are students who renew.

Source Analysis — Real Advice in the Wild
4 sources · Identify should/shouldn't/could · Metacognitive question on each
⏱ 8 MIN
💬 How to run this: Students read each source and answer the metacognitive question below it. Key question: is the advice effective? Does the modal choice match the situation?
🐦 Tweet / X Post
"Hot take: You shouldn't accept a counteroffer from your current company if you're already looking. They should have valued you before you had one foot out the door."
Q: Do you agree with this advice? Is "shouldn't" the right strength here, or is it too strong? What could the writer have said instead to sound less extreme?
💬 WhatsApp (Friend to Friend)
"ok tbh you should just take the money?? like you could always find another startup but 20% more is real money. you shouldn't overthink this"
Q: This is informal advice between friends. Is the grammar correct? Does the register (casual style) match the modals used? Would you give this advice the same way in a professional context?
📧 Email — Career Coach
"Dear Andrea, based on what you've shared, I believe you should request a meeting with your current manager this week. You could use the offer as a starting point for that conversation. However, you shouldn't disclose the exact salary figure if you're not comfortable. My advice would be to prioritize the conversation before making any decision."
Q: The coach uses three different modals. Which one is the strongest advice? Which is the gentlest? Does the ending phrase match the overall tone of the email?
💼 LinkedIn Post
"Career advice nobody talks about: You shouldn't make salary decisions based on one number alone. You should look at the total package: growth, culture, flexibility, and title. Companies could offer a lower base but much more long-term value. Think before you sign."
Q: This advice is written for a large audience — not one person. Does using "you" feel personal or distant? How would you rewrite the first sentence using "could" instead of "shouldn't"? Does it change the tone?

🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases — Use Them This Week
PHRASE 1
"You shouldn't make this decision quickly — give yourself a few days."
Use this whenever someone is about to make a major decision under pressure. "Shouldn't" here protects, not criticizes. It sounds experienced, not bossy.
PHRASE 2
"You could use the offer as leverage — have you thought about that?"
Two colloquial phrases in one sentence. "Could" makes the suggestion feel low-risk. "Have you thought about…?" invites them to consider it rather than commanding. Highly professional.
PHRASE 3
"My advice would be to wait and see what your manager says first."
The most formal and professional advice phrase in English. Use this when you want your recommendation to sound considered and calm — not reactive. Perfect for written advice too.
