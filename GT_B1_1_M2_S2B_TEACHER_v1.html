Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • S2B opens with speed. Unlike S2A where we build up slowly, S2B expects the student to jump immediately into opinion production. If they still need vocabulary scaffolding after S2A, dedicate 3 minutes here to review the Group A opinion openers before moving on.

02
8 min
⬆️
Upgrade Your Language — Not Just What You Say, But How
Click each card to hear the upgrade · Mark it when you use it in §3 or §4
6 Upgrades
I don't agree
"I'm afraid I don't share that view."
Polite but firm. Use in professional disagreements — sounds confident, not aggressive.
You're wrong
"I see it differently — here's why."
Challenges without attacking. Creates space for your argument without closing the conversation.
OK, but…
"That's a fair point, however…"
Acknowledges the other person before countering. This is what B1.1 concession sounds like.
I think so
"I feel strongly that this is the case."
Elevates from uncertain to confident. Use when you have evidence or real experience behind the claim.
Maybe you're right
"I can see where you're coming from, but…"
Shows empathy and then maintains your stance. The "but" is the key — don't leave it out.
I want to add something
"If I could just add one more point…"
Signals you're not done. Essential for extending your argument without being interrupted mid-thought.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The "Mark Used ✓" mechanic creates accountability — if the student hasn't marked a phrase by §4, ask them to use it once before §SP. The goal is that all 6 phrases are activated in context, not just read.
  • Common error with "That's a fair point, however…": students often stop at "however" and can't continue. Drill the full turn: "That's a fair point, however… [reason] + [example]." The structure is point → however → counter-reason → example.
  • "I'm afraid I don't share that view" — "I'm afraid" here is softening language, NOT literal fear. Explain: in American English this is rare; more common is "I'm afraid" in British professional contexts. But it reads as sophisticated in any formal register.
  • Speed drill: say the original version, student upgrades it in 3 seconds. "I don't agree." → pause → student: "I'm afraid I don't share that view." Run all 6 in under 2 minutes.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Script after speed drill: "From now on, for the rest of this session, every time you want to disagree with me, you use one of these phrases. Plain 'no' or 'I don't agree' costs you a restart. Ready?"
  • High-energy moment: run a mock disagreement using the phrases. Teacher says something provocative ("Open offices are always better for productivity") — student must respond with one of the 6 upgrades immediately. Keep it playful.

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Run 2 scenarios minimum, 3 if time allows. After each scenario, count: how many §2 phrases did the student use? If fewer than 2, make them redo the last turn with the upgraded language before moving to the next scenario.
  • Escalation pattern: start with soft counterarguments, then increase pressure. First exchange: present both sides logically. Second exchange: argue more aggressively. Third: bring in a hypothetical ("But what if your company required it? How would you negotiate?").
  • Key B1.1 error to watch: students who concede too easily ("Yes, you're right.") without countering. Prompt: "You conceded. Now counter. What do you say next?" — force them to maintain the position.
  • Target output per student turn: 3 sentences minimum. Opener (phrase upgrade) + position + reason. If they produce only 1 sentence, ask: "Can you add a reason? And an example from your own experience?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • After the student defends well: "That's a B1.1-level argument. You used [phrase], gave a reason, and added an example. That's the formula. Now let's make it harder."
  • Personal stakes: tie the scenario to their real situation. "At your company, which of these 4 scenarios are most relevant right now? Let's debate that one."
Capa 3 · Upsell
  • "The skill you just used — holding a position under pressure while using precise language — is exactly what shows up in English performance reviews, project negotiations, and international team meetings. This is the gap between B1 and B2 communication."
§ 04
🤝 Concede & Counter — The Art of Partial Agreement
8 click-to-reveal patterns · The most powerful B1.1 debate skill
04
10 min
🤝
Concede Intelligently — Then Counter Precisely
Click each pattern to expand · Say it out loud with your own example
8 Patterns
Why Conceding Wins
The strongest debaters agree first — then counter
Conceding shows intellectual honesty — people trust you more
It disarms the opponent — they expect resistance, not agreement
The counter after the concession hits twice as hard
It is the signature structure of B1.1 → B2 discourse
Pattern 01
"You raise a valid point, but…"
Full turn: "You raise a valid point about office collaboration. But from my perspective, the data on remote productivity tells a different story — and my own experience confirms it."

⏱ Practice: use with Scenario 01 from §3.
Pattern 02
"I can see the logic, however…"
Full turn: "I can see the logic behind fixed hours — structure does help some people. However, I feel strongly that this benefit only applies to certain personality types and job functions."

⏱ Practice: use with Scenario 02 from §3.
Pattern 03
"That may be true in some cases, but…"
Full turn: "That may be true in some cases — startups do have higher failure rates. But the creative freedom and speed of iteration they offer is something most corporations simply cannot replicate."

⏱ Practice: use with Scenario 03 from §3.
Pattern 04
"I understand the concern, but I still think…"
Full turn: "I understand the concern about accountability in team projects. But I still think that shared ownership, when done right, produces better outcomes than any individual effort alone."

⏱ Practice: use with Scenario 04 from §3.
Pattern 05
"To some extent, yes — but overall…"
Full turn: "To some extent, yes — structure does reduce decision fatigue. But overall, the cost to creativity and morale in rigidly structured environments is far too high to justify."

🎯 This pattern is for when the counter-argument is partly correct.
Pattern 06
"That's a good point, although…"
Full turn: "That's a good point — team collaboration does reduce individual errors. Although, in my experience, it also multiplies decision time and dilutes accountability. There's a real trade-off."

🎯 "Although" mid-sentence is a B1.1 marker — very natural in speech.
Pattern 07
"I partially agree — what I'd push back on is…"
Full turn: "I partially agree that flexibility can create scheduling chaos. What I'd push back on is the assumption that people can't manage themselves. In my experience, most professionals thrive when given autonomy."

🎯 Advanced B1.1 — very precise signaling of partial agreement.
Pattern 08
"Fair enough — and yet…"
Full turn: "Fair enough — the numbers on in-office collaboration do show some benefits. And yet, when you look at long-term retention data, flexible companies consistently outperform rigid ones by a wide margin."

🎯 "And yet" is a powerful rhetorical move — concedes cleanly then redirects forcefully.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Drill Pattern 03 ("That may be true, but…") and Pattern 06 ("That's a good point, although…") first — these are the most versatile and appear most naturally in conversation. Patterns 07 and 08 are B1.1→B1.2 bridges: introduce them but don't require mastery today.
  • Production drill: teacher makes a statement, student must select a pattern and complete the full turn within 20 seconds. If they freeze: "Which pattern fits? Say the first half of it." — the opening phrase often unlocks the rest.
  • Common error: students concede but forget the counter. "That's a good point." — full stop. The counter is non-negotiable. Write on the board: CONCEDE → "That's a good point" + COUNTER → "although / however / but + [your argument]".
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Game variant: teacher makes 4 rapid statements about work environments. Student must use a different concede-counter pattern for each one. Track which patterns they use — if they repeat, prompt: "Try Pattern 5 this time."

The Boardroom Minute: Defend Your Position to Leadership
Imagine you have exactly 60 seconds to address your company's leadership about one change you would make to your work environment. No notes. No preparation. Real English. Real conviction.
1
Open with your strongest opinion statement: "I feel strongly that…"
2
Give 2 specific reasons with evidence or experience — no generic claims.
3
Anticipate resistance: "You may argue that… and that's a fair point. However…"
4
Close with a proposal, not just an opinion: "What I'd propose is…"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • §SP in S2B is the culmination of the entire M2 pair. The student should produce 3–4 connected sentences per turn with NO vocabulary prompting from you. If they need prompts, they are not at mastery — return to §2 or §4 for one more round.
  • For SP Card 4 (3-turn debate): you as teacher should escalate each turn. Turn 1: soft counterargument. Turn 2: bring in a "statistic" or "expert." Turn 3: a personal anecdote (real or invented) that challenges their position. They must hold through all three.
  • For SP Card 5 (2-minute free): time it with your phone. After 2 minutes, give immediate feedback on: (a) vocabulary range — how many S2A words appeared? (b) concession language — did they concede at least once? (c) argument structure — was there a clear opener, body, close?
  • Boardroom Minute: this is a high-stakes performance task. Set the tone before starting: "Imagine I'm your VP. You have one minute. I'm taking notes." The formality shift changes the student's English register visibly.
Capa 2 · Motivation
  • After the Boardroom Minute: "That was your most complex English output since we started working together. You argued a position, conceded intelligently, and proposed a solution — all in English, without support. That's what B1.1 looks like at its ceiling."
  • If the student was exceptional: "That's B1.2 territory. In S3A and S3B we'll take this further — longer output, more complex arguments, and more nuanced vocabulary."
Capa 3 · Upsell & Retention
  • "The Boardroom Minute you just did? That's a real skill in real workplaces. 'I feel strongly that… although I understand the concern… what I'd propose is…' — that three-part structure is what differentiates B1 professionals from B2 leaders in international business contexts."
  • Testimonial trigger: "On a scale of 1 to 10, how different does your English feel about work topics compared to when we started M2? What changed most?"
