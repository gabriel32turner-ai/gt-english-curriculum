Colloquial ‚ÄĒ Uncertainty in Real English
5 phrases professionals use when things aren't confirmed yet
‚ŹĪ 5 MIN
"We'll see."
The most common English response when you're genuinely unsure. NOT negative ‚ÄĒ it means "the situation is open." Extremely common in business emails and calls when a decision hasn't been made yet.
"Will the client confirm this week?" ‚ÄĒ "We'll see ‚ÄĒ I sent the email this morning."
"It depends."
The professional way to say "there are factors I can't control." Always follow with what it depends on: "It depends on the client / the budget / the approval." Standalone "it depends" sounds evasive ‚ÄĒ add context.
"Are you attending the conference?" ‚ÄĒ "It depends on the budget ‚ÄĒ I might not go."
"I'll keep you posted."
Signals that you're aware of the uncertainty AND you'll update them as things develop. This is a promise of communication ‚ÄĒ very professional. Use after "might" or "may" statements to close the loop.
"The supplier might delay ‚ÄĒ I'll keep you posted as soon as I hear back."
"TBD." / "It's TBD."
To Be Determined ‚ÄĒ used in agendas, emails, and schedules when a detail isn't confirmed yet. Extremely common in corporate M√©xico and international contexts. Can also say "TBA" (To Be Announced) for public events.
