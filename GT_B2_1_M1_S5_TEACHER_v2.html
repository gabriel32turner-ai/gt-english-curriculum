§1 · Warm-Up

Rapid Fire

10 questions · 15 seconds each · leadership instincts · no preparation

⏱ 5 min
Rapid Fire: Leadership Edition
First instinct only — 15 seconds per question. These questions surface how students think about leadership before any vocabulary or framing. That raw answer is the most honest version. Note the responses — some will reappear in §5.
5:00
Warm-Up
Q 01
Are leaders born or made?
Q 02
What's the difference between a boss and a leader?
Q 03
Can you lead without authority?
Q 04
Is it better to be liked or respected as a leader?
Q 05
What's the biggest mistake a leader can make?
Q 06
Does your company reward good leadership — or just results?
Q 07
Can someone be a great leader and a bad manager at the same time?
Q 08
Is adapting your style to your team a strength — or a sign of inconsistency?
Q 09
What makes someone lose credibility as a leader?
Q 10
Would you rather lead a team that needs direction — or one that runs itself?
🟢 Facilitation
🌹 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • S5 is the Module 1 exit — students have 4 sessions of vocabulary and structure behind them. Rapid Fire is faster today because fluency has increased. If a student goes over 15 seconds, cut at 20 — they're more confident now.
  • Q 01 always generates strong conviction — note the answer. It predicts how the student will frame their Exit Presentation in §5.
  • Q 06 carries the most cultural load for Mexican corporate learners. Don't linger — but note the answer. Use it as a push question in §3 Q&A.
  • Bridge out: "All of those questions point to the same thing — your style. And that's exactly what we're going to name and present today."
  • Q 02 — student says they're the same: Push with one word — "Really? Same?" — and wait. The pause usually produces the distinction on its own.
  • Q 08 produces uncertainty: Good. That tension between adaptability and consistency is the core of the Exit Presentation. File it — don't resolve it here.
  • Q 10 is split into two parts: If the student reads it as either/or and can't decide — prompt: "Which have you actually experienced? What happened?"
  • After the round, ask the student to pick the one question that best describes their current leadership situation. That question becomes their §5 Exit Presentation anchor — tell them to remember it.

§2 · Vocabulary

20 Words

10 nouns + 10 verbs · leadership lexical set · click to reveal · required in §5

⏱ 10 min
Vocab: Leadership Set
Click any word to reveal its definition. Teacher-paced. In Student Mode, definitions are hidden. Minimum 4 of these words must appear in each Exit Presentation in §5. Tell students now — not at §5.
10:00
Vocabulary
Nouns (10)
leadershipThe ability to guide, inspire, and influence others toward a shared goal or direction
visionA clear picture of where a team or organization is heading — and why it matters
authorityThe formal power or right to make decisions, give direction, and expect compliance
initiativeThe ability to act independently and proactively — without waiting to be told
credibilityThe quality of being trusted and believed in — earned through consistency and competence
delegationThe act of assigning responsibility and authority to others while retaining overall accountability
influenceThe ability to shape others' thinking, behavior, or decisions — without necessarily having authority
decisionA choice made — especially one that commits a team or direction to a particular path
styleThe characteristic way a person leads — shaped by personality, values, and experience
pressureThe demands, expectations, or constraints that test how a leader performs under stress
Verbs (10)
inspireTo motivate others by appealing to their values, aspirations, and sense of purpose
empowerTo give someone the confidence, tools, and authority to act independently and effectively
challengeTo push someone — or a situation — beyond their current limits in a constructive way
motivateTo give someone a reason to act — by connecting tasks to their needs or goals
adaptTo change your approach deliberately in response to new people, contexts, or demands
communicateTo convey information, expectations, and direction clearly — and ensure they are understood
delegateTo transfer a task or decision to someone else — while keeping the responsibility for the outcome
leadTo take people from where they are to where they need to be — through direction or example
take chargeTo assume control or leadership — especially in a situation where no one else has stepped up
step backTo deliberately reduce your involvement — allowing others to lead, decide, or grow
Activation Activities
Activity A — Leader vs. Manager Divide (4 min)

Students sort today's 20 words into two informal categories: words they associate more with leading vs. words they associate more with managing. There are no correct answers — the conversation the sorting generates is the activity.

Discussion Trigger

"Can you inspire without authority? Can you have credibility without a formal role? Can you step back and still lead?" — let the student work through these questions with the word list in front of them.

Activity B — Self-Portrait (3 min)

Each student picks 3 words that describe how they currently lead — and 2 words that describe how they wish they led. One sentence per word. This feeds directly into §5 Exit Presentation.

Target Output — B2.1

"Right now I communicate clearly but I struggle to empower my team — I tend to take charge instead of stepping back and letting people develop."

Activity C — Verb Pairs in Context (3 min)

Quick contrasts — one sentence per pair. Teacher gives the situation; student uses both words.

Pairs
  • Inspire vs. motivate — what's the emotional difference? Which lasts longer?
  • Take charge vs. step back — when does a good leader do each? Can they do both in the same week?
  • Delegate vs. empower — is it possible to delegate without empowering? What does that look like?
🟢 Facilitation
🌹 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • This is the last vocabulary set of Module 1. Frame it explicitly: "These are the words you need to talk about leadership in English. After today, you'll have 80 words across 4 topic clusters. That's a working vocabulary — not a list."
  • Activity B is the most important. The self-portrait is direct preparation for §5. Don't rush it — the sentences students produce here are the ones they'll build their presentations from.
  • Priority words for §5: style, credibility, adapt, empower, step back, influence. Flag these explicitly: "These six should definitely appear in your presentation."
  • Take charge and step back are phrasal verbs — two words each. Write them on the board as phrases. Common error: "take the charge" / "step-back."
  • "Inspire" vs. "motivate" feels too subtle: Ground it — "Motivation answers 'what's in it for me?' Inspiration answers 'what could we become?' One is transactional. The other is transformational."
  • "Credibility" feels passive: It is — it's earned, not claimed. Contrast: "You can assert authority. You can't assert credibility. Someone else has to give it to you based on what you do."
  • Activity A sorting produces conflict: Expected. Let it run — the disagreement is the lesson. There is no correct answer, and that instability is linguistically productive.
  • Connect to Module 1 vocabulary arc: "In S1 you learned feedback language. S2 — team roles. S3 — team dynamics. S4 — conflict. Today — leadership. These aren't separate topics. They're one conversation about how professionals work together." This synthesis is important for Module 1 closure.

§3 · Q&A · Three Dynamics

Q&A

Teacher → Student · Student → Teacher · Student → Student · leadership in real context

⏱ 10 min
Guided Q&A: Leadership & Style
Seven questions — three dynamics. Same format as S4. Each question is marked. The conversation stays real — professional experience only, no hypotheticals unless the student chooses them.
10:00
Q&A
🟢 Teacher → Student
⭐ Student → Teacher
🟡 Student → Student
Teacher → Student
Q1
How would you describe your leadership style right now — in one sentence? Not the style you aspire to — the one you actually use?
Push: Is that style deliberate — or just what happens under pressure? Have you ever tried to adapt it and failed?
Teacher → Student
Q2
Think of the best leader you've worked under. What specifically did they do that built their credibility with the team?
Push: Was it their authority — or their influence? Did they inspire or motivate? What's the difference in how it felt?
Student → Student
Q3
Ask your partner: "What's one decision you made as a leader — or as part of a team — that you'd make differently today? Why?"
Teacher note: circulate. This question will naturally produce Third Conditional language — harvest any sentences you hear. They become reference points for §4.
Student → Teacher
Q4
Ask me something about leadership — a real question you have. It can be about language, about experience, or about how to handle a specific type of situation.
Teacher note: answer authentically. Use today's vocabulary naturally. If the student asks a language question — answer it in context, not as a grammar lesson. Model the register they should aim for in §5.
Teacher → Student
Q5
Does your company culture support strong leadership — or does it limit it? What gets rewarded: initiative or compliance?
Push: If you had more authority, would you lead differently? What would you do first?
Student → Student
Q6
Ask your partner: "What's one thing a leader did — or didn't do — that changed how you work today?"
Teacher note: after 90 seconds, ask one student to share their partner's answer in third person. This requires active listening and reformulation — a production skill often underdeveloped at B2.1.
Student → Teacher
Q7
One more — ask me anything about leadership style, pressure, or how to communicate vision in English. Something practical.
Teacher note: S5 is the exit session — students should feel comfortable enough to ask substantive questions. If Q7 produces silence, prompt: "Ask me something you'd actually want to know in a real meeting with a leader whose English is stronger than yours."
🟢 Facilitation
🌹 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • By S5, students should be initiating Student→Teacher questions with more confidence than in S4. If they're not — the Q&A format is working; it just needs more time. Don't rush Q4 and Q7.
  • Q3 is the natural TC harvesting moment — listen carefully during the Student→Student exchange. Any sentence containing "would have," "hadn't," or "if I had" gets written on the board. These sentences will be referenced in §4.
  • Q6 reformulation: pushing the student to report their partner's answer in third person is a discrete grammar and discourse skill. Do it once per session — not every time.
  • Time per question: 80–90 seconds. Q4 and Q7 may run to 2 minutes — allow it.
  • Q1 — student describes the style they aspire to, not the one they use: Redirect — "That's what you want to be. What are you actually like under pressure? When something goes wrong — what do you do?"
  • Q5 produces deflection ("My company is fine"): Push gently — "Specifically — in your last project, was the person with the most authority also the person with the most influence? Were they the same person?"
  • Q7 produces small questions: Validate them — a small specific question is better than a vague large one. Answer precisely and move on.
  • For advanced students: in Q6, ask the student to summarize their partner's answer AND then challenge it — one sentence of pushback. This produces adversarial reasoning at B2.1, which is significantly harder than agreement.

§4 · TC Full Integration

TC Round

Every answer requires Third Conditional — no explanation, no drilling — just conversation

⏱ 10 min
Third Conditional: Full Integration
Six questions about leadership decisions in the past. Each one is designed so that an honest answer requires Third Conditional. The teacher does not explain the structure — the questions do the work. If TC appears naturally, note it. If it doesn't, prompt once.
10:00
TC Round
Teacher Note — How this section works

Ask each question. Wait for the response. If the student naturally produces a Third Conditional sentence — harvest it, write it on the board, continue. If they don't — ask the embedded prompt in italics below each question. One prompt maximum per question. Do not explain the grammar. The structure must come from the meaning of the question, not from grammar instruction.

TC 1
Think of a leadership decision you made that didn't go the way you expected. What happened — and what would the outcome have been if you had made a different call?
If they don't produce TC: "Say it this way: 'If I hadn't [done that], the result would have been…'"
Target: If I hadn't… / the outcome wouldn't have…
TC 2
Was there a moment in your career when you didn't step up — and someone else had to take charge instead? What would have been different if you had taken the initiative?
If they don't produce TC: "Would the team dynamic have changed if you had taken charge at that moment?"
Target: Would [X] have happened if…? / If I had stepped up…
TC 3
Think of a time you chose not to communicate something important to your team. What would have changed if you had been more transparent?
If they don't produce TC: "If you hadn't withheld that information, how would the team have responded?"
Target: If I hadn't withheld… / they wouldn't have…
TC 4
Has someone ever lost credibility with you as a leader — because of a single decision or moment? What would have been different if they had handled it another way?
If they don't produce TC: "If they had acknowledged the mistake at the time, would you have responded differently?"
Target: Would you have… if they had…? / If they hadn't…
TC 5
Is there a team you led — or were part of — that underperformed? If the leadership style had been different from the start, what would have been different about the outcome?
If they don't produce TC: "If the leader had empowered the team instead of controlling it, what would have changed?"
Target: If the leadership had been… / the team would have…
TC 6
Looking at Module 1 as a whole — four sessions of conversations, vocabulary, and skills — what would have been different about your English in the last year if you had had these tools earlier?
If they don't produce TC: "If you had had this vocabulary six months ago, what conversation would you have had differently?"
Target: If I had had… / I would have been able to… — Module 1 closing TC
🟢 Facilitation
🌹 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • TC 6 is intentionally reflexive — it closes the module. It also produces one of the most emotionally resonant Third Conditional sentences students will produce across M1. Give it space. Don't cut it short.
  • Write every TC sentence produced on the board — verbatim. At the end of the round, read them back: "This is Third Conditional at B2.1 in real production. You just did this." No grammar label. Just affirmation.
  • If the student produces the TC structure without prompting on TC 1 — note it. By TC 4 or 5, the structure should be automatic. If it's still requiring prompts by TC 5, that's a §5 focus area.
  • TC 6 also functions as a module-level self-assessment. The student's answer tells you exactly how much language growth they've perceived — valuable data for M2 planning.
  • Student uses affirmative TC instead of negative: Both are valid. If they only use the affirmative form, prompt once for the negative: "Now say it as 'If I hadn't…'" — one example is enough.
  • TC 2 produces no specific memory: Push — "Think of any project, any moment — not necessarily leadership. A moment where you didn't speak up when you could have. What happened?"
  • TC 6 feels too meta: Some students resist reflecting on their own learning. In that case, redirect: "Think of one specific work conversation from the last six months. Which word or phrase from this module would have helped you most?"
  • For advanced students: after TC 6, ask them to produce one sentence using the inverted form (Form C from S2): "Hadn't I had this vocabulary, the conversation would have gone very differently." This tests passive recognition of the formal structure introduced earlier in the module.
  • TC sentences produced in this section can be written by the student and used verbatim in their §5 Exit Presentation — explicitly tell them this. It reduces anxiety and rewards production.

Presenta

⏱ 20 min
Exit Presentation: My Leadership Style
Each student presents for exactly 2 minutes. No slides. No written notes consulted during the presentation. Framework: Describe → Reflect → Project. This is the Module 1 deliverable.
20:00
Presentation
🧭 Leadership Style Compass — 4 Styles
▼ Expand — use this before §5 begins

Present this before students prepare their Exit Presentation. Students identify which style describes them most — then which describes them under pressure. The two answers are often different, and that gap is the most interesting thing to present.

Directive
Sets clear expectations, makes decisions quickly, leads through authority and structure. High control, fast results.
Strength
Effective in crises and with inexperienced teams
Risk
Can suppress initiative and reduce team autonomy over time
Coaching
Develops people through questions, feedback, and long-term investment in individual growth. Relationship-first.
Strength
Builds loyalty, credibility, and sustainable team performance
Risk
Slow in fast-moving environments; requires patience and trust
Democratic
Shares decision-making, values input from the team, builds consensus before acting. Inclusion as a method.
Strength
High alignment and buy-in; team feels ownership of decisions
Risk
Slow decisions; can create confusion about who actually leads
Pace-setting
Sets high standards through personal example. Expects others to perform at the same level — without always explaining how.
Strength
Drives excellence and results in high-performing teams
Risk
Can burn out the team; works poorly when team needs development
Before §5: ask each student — "Which style describes how you normally lead?" Then — "Which style do you default to when under pressure?" The gap between those two answers is the most interesting thing they can present.
Presentation Framework: Describe → Reflect → Project
1
Describe
"What is your leadership style — and what shaped it?"
Name a style from the Compass. Describe 2–3 things you actually do as a leader. Ground it in a real team or role. Vocabulary from today must appear here.
⏱ ~40 seconds
2
Reflect
"What would have been different if you had led differently?"
One specific situation from the past. Use Third Conditional — mandatory. What decision, what gap, what consequence? Be honest — not theoretical.
⏱ ~45 seconds · TC required
3
Project
"How are you going to lead differently going forward?"
One concrete change — not general improvement. What specifically will you do differently next week, next project, next difficult conversation? Use a colloquial phrase if it fits naturally.
⏱ ~35 seconds
Teacher Observation Rubric — Exit Presentation
Vocabulary
Minimum 4 words from today's list used naturally — not forced. Note which ones. Flag any that feel inserted rather than integrated.
Third Conditional
At least one TC sentence in the Reflect phase — mandatory. Note the exact structure: negative form? question form? inverted? Mark the quality: spontaneous production or prompted?
Fluency
Does the student reach 2 full minutes without stopping? Do they fill silence or cut early? Fluency at this level means sustained output, not absence of errors.
Specificity
Is the presentation about a real situation? Generic leadership statements ("I try to be a good leader") are insufficient. Specific memories, names, decisions — that's B2.1 production.
Register
Is the register appropriate and consistent? B2.1 should demonstrate awareness of when to shift between formal and natural professional language. Listen for colloquial phrases from S4–S5.

🟢 Facilitation
🌹 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • Give students 2 minutes of silent preparation before the first presentation — Compass identification + framework review only. No written notes may be used during the presentation itself.
  • The Leadership Style Compass is shown before preparation, not before the Q&A. Timing is deliberate: the Q&A produces authentic instinct; the Compass provides vocabulary for the presentation.
  • Do not interrupt during the 2-minute presentation. Take notes on the rubric — give feedback only after the timer ends. One piece of specific positive feedback + one development point per student.
  • If a student stops before 2 minutes: don't fill the silence for them. After 5 seconds say — "Keep going. Tell me what you're going to do differently." That prompt leads into the Project phase.
  • TC in Reflect: if a student presents without producing TC, ask one question after their timer ends — "What would have been different if you'd led differently in that situation?" That one sentence closes the module requirement.
  • Student identifies two different styles in the Compass (normal vs. under pressure): Excellent — tell them explicitly: "That gap is the most interesting thing you can present. Lead with it."
  • Describe phase stays generic: Push before they start — "Name a team. Name a project. Name a moment. Generic leadership descriptions don't tell me anything about you specifically."
  • Reflect phase uses past simple instead of TC: After the presentation, model the TC version of their sentence and ask them to repeat it once — not as correction, as expansion.
  • Student finishes in 90 seconds: Ask one follow-up question — "You mentioned [specific word/idea]. Can you say more about that?" This produces 20–30 more seconds of natural output.
  • For strong students: add a constraint — the Project phase must use language from two different sessions in Module 1. For example: a feedback verb from S2 + a conflict phrase from S4. Tests cross-session integration.
  • Optional peer feedback: after each presentation, ask one other student to name one specific thing the presenter did well in English — not "good" or "clear" — one specific observation. Builds evaluative listening.

Feedback · Team Roles · Difficult Conversations · Conflict · Leadership. And through all of it — one grammar structure used in real situations, real decisions, real language. That's Module 1.
Module 1 — Global Feedback Script

Deliver this after the last Exit Presentation — under 90 seconds total:

"Across Module 1, this group [SPECIFIC STRENGTH — name a vocabulary word, a structure, or a conversation moment from S1–S5]. The most consistent development area has been [GAP — specific and constructive]. Going into Module 2, the focus is [PREVIEW — what they'll do more of]."

This is a module-level observation — not a session note. It should feel like a real assessment, not a compliment. Be specific. Reference something from S1, something from S5, and the arc between them.

🌹 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • S5 is a milestone session — the Module Close should feel like one. Don't rush §6. Even 3 minutes of genuine reflection on the module arc is more valuable than a fast preview of M2.
  • The Module 1 Global Feedback Note is the most important teacher output of the entire module. Prepare it mentally during §5 presentations — don't improvise it after. It should reference something specific from S1 and something specific from today.
  • Homework: assign based on what the presentations revealed. If TC production was weak — HW1 (TC intensive). If vocabulary range was narrow — HW3. If situational awareness needs development — HW2.
  • Bridge framing for M2: "Module 1 was about your relationships at work — feedback, conflict, leadership. Module 2 is about your impact — how you persuade, influence, and move people. Same person. Bigger stage."
  • Not enough time for §6 after presentations: If §5 ran long, compress §6 to: Global Feedback Note (60 seconds) + HW assignment (30 seconds) + M2 preview (30 seconds). The Module Close card can be shown silently. Don't skip the Global Feedback — it's the most pedagogically significant moment of S5.
  • Students ask about their overall level assessment: Be direct but calibrated — "You're solidly operating at B2.1. Your vocabulary range has expanded noticeably since S1. The area to develop before B2.2 is [specific gap]." Don't inflate. Don't deflate. Be honest.
  • For groups: after all presentations, ask each student to name one word from another student's presentation that they want to add to their own active vocabulary. Creates cross-student lexical exchange and peer modeling.
  • Optional M2 preview activity: ask students — "Think of a time you tried to persuade someone at work and failed. What happened?" Don't discuss it now — plant the seed. That experience becomes M2 S1 raw material.
