Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The rapid fire format removes the perfectionism filter — students say what they actually think. This generates authentic material for §7 (Presentation). Tell them at the start: "Your answers today are the raw material for your presentation at the end."
  • When a student hesitates beyond 5 seconds: "First word that comes to mind. Go." Hesitation is data — note which questions cause the most delay. Those are the most interesting ones to revisit.
  • PPC hook: after Q3 or Q4, write one PPC sentence from their answer on the board. Don't explain — just leave it there. They will notice the pattern before §4.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "I'm going to give you 8 questions. Your only job is to answer fast. Wrong answers don't exist here — only slow ones."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "These 8 questions are what a good manager asks in a 1-to-1 when they actually want to understand how you work. You'll need this vocabulary in performance reviews, team feedback sessions, and job interviews."

GT Originals — The Workplace Has Changed
In 2025, the introvert/extrovert binary is less useful than it was. What matters more is self-awareness — knowing what drains you, what recharges you, and how to communicate that to your team. The most valuable B1.2 skill from today: being able to say in English what you need to do your best work. Not many professionals can do that clearly, even in their own language.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Energy Map: the most important question after they place themselves — "How long have you been in this quadrant? Is it recent or has it always been that way?" → PPC in context. If they placed a team member far from center: "What's been happening with them? Have you noticed this recently?"
  • Work Style RF: "DEPENDS" is the most honest answer for most situations. Don't let students use it as an escape — after choosing DEPENDS, ask "Depends on WHAT exactly? Give me one specific condition." This builds nuance.
  • After 5 situations: "What pattern are you noticing? Is there one type of situation where introverts consistently have the advantage? Extroverts?" → This is the content of the §7 presentation.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Your map is private — I'm not asking you to show it. But I am going to ask you to describe one person on your team using PPC. Without saying their name."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • S4 next session — Your Boss Is Wrong. "If you understand your own work style and your boss's — this session gives you the language to navigate that conversation without damaging the relationship."
§ 04
Grammar — PPC for Work Style Patterns
Describing how someone works — and how it has been changing
04
12–14 min
✅
✅ Describing a work pattern
"She's been working from home every Friday since January."
"He's been doing his best work in the mornings — alone."
"I've been feeling much better since I stopped taking calls before 9."
❌
❌ What hasn't been working
"He hasn't been adapting well to the open office."
"She hasn't been engaging in meetings — she looks exhausted."
"I haven't been recharging properly — too many back-to-back calls."
❓
❓ Asking about work style
"How long have you been working like this?"
"Has she been feeling better since she joined the small team?"
"What have you been doing differently this month?"
💡
💡 PPC = still true now
"He worked from home last week." → past, finished.
"He's been working from home since March." → still now, ongoing.
Use PPC when the pattern is still part of the present situation.
💡 The key question to ask yourself: Is this pattern still happening right now?
"She adapted to remote work." → She did it. It's done.
"She's been adapting to remote work." → The process is still going — she's not fully there yet.

PPC shows a situation that started in the past and is still the current reality.
⚠️ Common Errors — S3 Context
❌ "She is thriving since the promotion."→✅ "She has been thriving since the promotion." (PPC, not present simple)
❌ "He has been work alone all morning."→✅ "He has been working alone all morning." (need -ing)
❌ "How long she has been feeling this way?"→✅ "How long has she been feeling this way?" (auxiliary before subject)
❌ "I've been recharge after the holiday."→✅ "I've been recharging since the holiday." (verb-ing + since for starting point)
✏️ Activity 1 — Choose the Correct Form
Select the option that best describes an ongoing work style pattern.
She ___ so much better since she moved to a smaller team.
He ___ with the remote team for the past three months and it's going well.
"How long ___ you ___ like this — doing everything alone?"
I ___ better since I started taking a proper lunch break away from my desk.
✏️ Activity 2 — True or False
Decide if the sentence is correct and natural.
"He's been working alone since Monday" — this PPC sentence is correct. SINCE marks the starting point.
"She's been thrive in the new role" is correct — she is thriving right now, which is an ongoing situation.
"He adapted well" and "He's been adapting well" can mean different things — one is finished, the other is still ongoing.
"I've been observing her work style" is more natural than "I've been observe her work style."
✏️ Activity 3 — Reorder
Drag the pieces into the correct PPC sentence.
  1. ⠿from home
  2. ⠿has been
  3. ⠿she joined the project team.
  4. ⠿She
  5. ⠿working
  6. ⠿since
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q3: "have / been working" — this is the full PPC question form. Drill until automatic: "How long have you been working like this?" Students must produce this question independently by §7.
  • T2: "been thrive" is the most common error at B1.2 — students know it's PPC but forget the -ing. Ask: "Is 'thrive' the base verb or the -ing form?" → "thriving." Then: "So what's the correct form?" Guide, don't give.
  • Reorder: randomize the order physically if teaching in-person — point to chunks and ask "which comes first?" before letting them drag.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Quick round: I name a person — you give me one PPC sentence about how they work. Your boss. Your best colleague. Your most difficult colleague." Keep it fast.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In S4, we use the same PPC but in a more uncomfortable context: 'My boss has been making decisions without asking the team.' That's a harder sentence to say — but the grammar is identical."
§ 05
Reading — The Wrong Hire
A team leader reflects on hiring the wrong person for the wrong environment · Work style mismatch
05
10–12 min
Laura Torres
Team Lead · Grupo Avance · Guadalajara
Leadership Blog
Watch for: PPC (green) · introvert/extrovert vocabulary · prefer · recharge · thrive · drain · collaborate · adapt

When we hired Rodrigo, I thought he was exactly what the team needed. He was confident, loud in interviews, full of ideas. He answered every question before anyone else finished asking. I thought: perfect. Our team has been struggling to get attention in leadership meetings. He would change that.

Six months later, I understand the problem.

Rodrigo thrives when he can talk. But our team has been working in a deep-focus environment for years. We use async tools, written updates, and quiet blocks. We collaborate in writing, not in meetings. Rodrigo has been struggling with this since day one. He's been arriving at meetings with energy that nobody in the room is ready to match. He's been draining people without knowing it.

The mistake wasn't hiring someone different. Different is good. The mistake was that I never asked the right questions in the interview: How do you work best? What recharges you? What drains you? I hired for personality. I should have hired for output and preference.

Rodrigo has been adapting — slowly, but genuinely. He now has one "open" day per week where he can run brainstorms and calls. The rest of the week, he works the team's way. His output has been improving since we made that change. Not because he changed who he is. Because we finally gave him the right balance.

I've learned something I didn't expect: the best teams aren't made of people who work the same way. They're made of people who understand how everyone works — and adapt.

✏️ Comprehension
Answer based on the text.
Why was Rodrigo the wrong hire for this team — at first?
What did Laura change that helped Rodrigo improve?
Laura says the mistake was hiring someone different from the team — different styles cause problems.
"His output has been improving since we made that change" — this PPC shows the improvement is still ongoing now.
The lesson Laura learned is that the best teams have people who all work the same way.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Key phrase: "I hired for personality. I should have hired for output and preference." Ask the student: "What does this mean in practice? What interview question would you have asked?" → This generates B1.2 functional language: "How do you work best? What drains your energy? What helps you focus?"
  • Pre-presentation setup: "Is there a Rodrigo on your team? Or are YOU the Rodrigo — the person whose work style doesn't fit the culture?" This is the seed for the §7 presentation.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "If Laura had asked you to interview Rodrigo — what three questions would you have asked him about how he works?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In your next job interview or performance review — can you answer these questions in English? 'I've been working best in environments where I can…' — that sentence directly comes from today."

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

Manager: So, how has the week been? I've been noticing you seem a bit tired lately.

Employee: Yeah, honestly — I've been struggling a bit. The new project has a lot of group calls, and I've been finding it hard to focus afterwards. I need quiet time to process things, and I haven't been getting that.

Manager: I didn't know that about you. How long has this been affecting you?

Employee: Since the project started — so about six weeks. I've been doing my best work early in the morning before anyone else is online. That's when I've been getting the most done. But the afternoon calls have been draining me.

Manager: That makes sense. Have you been able to tell the team you prefer to work that way?

Employee: Not really. I didn't want to seem difficult. But I've been thinking about it more lately — I think I need to communicate it.

Manager: You should. The team has been working well together, but we've all been assuming everyone works the same way. Clearly we don't. What do you need to recharge between calls?

Employee: Even thirty minutes of quiet would help. No messages, no questions — just time to think.

Manager: Done. I'll block it in your calendar. And honestly though — I'm glad you told me. I've been missing this kind of conversation.

⏱ Target: 85–100 sec · PPC: I've been noticing / I've been struggling / I've been finding / I haven't been getting / I've been doing / I've been getting / have been draining / have you been able / I've been thinking / has been working / we've been assuming / I've been missing · Rate: 0.87

✏️ Listening Activities
Listen and answer.
What has been draining the employee's energy?
What does the employee need to recharge between calls?
The employee has been telling the team about their work style preferences for several weeks.
"We've been assuming everyone works the same way" — the manager uses PPC to show this is an ongoing team habit, not a one-time mistake.
The manager ends the conversation badly — she doesn't offer any practical help.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Count PPC instances before answering: 12+ in ~90 seconds. That density is the point. After activities: "Could you have this conversation with your manager in English? What would you say?" → This is the §7 presentation in embryo.
  • T2: "we've been assuming" — this is the PPC of a collective, unconscious habit. Ask: "Has your team been making any assumptions about how you work? What has been the effect?" High-value reflection moment.
  • Post-listening roleplay: you play the manager, student plays the employee — but talking about THEIR real work situation. 90 seconds. No preparation.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "The employee says 'I didn't want to seem difficult.' Have you ever felt that? What stopped you from saying what you needed at work — in Spanish, let alone in English?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "This conversation — in English — is the kind of conversation that changes careers. Most people don't have it, because they don't have the language. Today you do."
