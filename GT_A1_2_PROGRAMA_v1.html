Who is this program for?

  • Students who completed A1.1 at Gabriel Turner English Training
  • Professionals with basic English who need to communicate at work
  • Corporate employees with prior exposure to English
  • Adults who want to close their full A1 level with a business focus

By the end of A1.2, students will be able to:

  • Give and follow instructions at work using imperatives
  • Describe their company, products and quantities
  • Receive clients and handle basic business travel situations
  • Report absences and communicate about workplace health
  • Write and read basic business emails with full structure
  • Hold a complete A1 conversation with confidence

Functional English
  • What do you do in your free time? — I usually read and go to the gym.
  • How often do you have meetings? — About three times a week.
  • The next deadline is on March 20th.
  • The conference is in June. The training is on the 5th of April.
  • Please open the document. / Don't forget to save the file.
  • Turn right at the entrance. / Don't use your phone during the presentation.
  • How often do you travel for work? — About once a month.
Everyday English
  • "Not bad!" — neutral positive response
  • "Same here."
  • "See you Thursday!"
  • "Good luck!" / "Good job!"
  • "No problem."
Survival Phrases
  • "Sorry, can you repeat that?"
  • "How do you spell that?"
  • "What does that mean?"
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 📅 Date formats: US (March 15) vs UK (15 March) — critical error in international emails, worth drilling early
  • 🌍 Weather small talk: real call opener — "How's the weather over there?" — immediate impact for corporate students
  • 🔴 False Friend: "actually" = in reality / NOT "actualmente" — one of the most common Mexican errors at A1
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "February" /ˈfebrueri/ / "Wednesday" /ˈwenzdeɪ/ — silent letters, high-frequency errors
  • ⚠️ Common error: "In Monday" → On Monday / "On March" → In March — preposition drilling essential
Exit Task M1

Company & Products
Company overview · Products · Corporate orders · Quantities
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Describing what the company does and what it offers
  • Talking about products and services with quantities
  • Ordering office supplies and making simple purchase requests
  • Talking about stock, inventory and basic budgets
  • Describing the company's structure: plant, warehouse, branch
  • Large numbers: prices, units and budget figures
Grammar
Countable / Uncountable nouns — full system some / any (aff. / neg. / ?): Is there any stock left? How much (price + uncountable) / How many (countable) A lot of / much / many — quantity in corporate context This / that / these / those — consolidation with products Numbers 100–999,999 — prices, units, budgets

Functional English
  • What does your company do? — We produce / sell / provide [product/service].
  • How many employees does your company have? — About 200.
  • We need some paper and a lot of folders for the office.
  • Is there any stock left? — No, there isn't. We need to order more.
  • How much is the total? — It's $4,500.
  • We don't have much time — let's decide now.
  • I'd like to order 50 units of [product], please.
  • There isn't enough equipment in the meeting room.
Everyday English
  • "We're out of stock."
  • "How many do you need?"
  • "Let me check."
  • "That works."
  • "One moment, please."
Order Email (Chunks)
  • "I'd like to order…"
  • "Please find the details below."
  • "Please confirm the delivery date."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 📧 Purchase request email A1.2: "I'd like to order 50 units of... Please find the details below." — real template ready to use in class
  • 🔴 False Friend: "fabric" = cloth/textile / NOT "fábrica" (= factory / plant) — critical error in manufacturing industry
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "invoice" /ˈɪnvɔɪs/ / "equipment" /ɪˈkwɪpmənt/ — high-frequency corporate vocabulary
  • ⚠️ Common error: "informations" → information / "equipments" → equipment — uncountable nouns, no plural
  • 💼 Corporate vocab: large / medium / small enterprise — essential language for company presentations
Exit Task M2
Describe your company: what it does, how many employees it has, what products or services it offers. Then request office supplies using some/any/how many in a short conversation or mini-email. (Speaking + mini-email · 5 min)

Getting Around for Business
Receiving clients · Business travel · Hotel check-in · Airport navigation
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Receiving a client or supplier at reception and escorting them
  • Giving travel directions to a visitor from another city
  • Checking into a business hotel and confirming a reservation
  • Navigating the airport on a business trip
  • Confirming arrival details: flight, time and terminal
  • Using object pronouns to refer to people and documents
Grammar
Object pronouns: me/you/him/her/it/us/them — full system Can + object pronoun: Can you help me? / Call her. / Send it to us. Prepositions of movement: to/from/through/past/along/across How long does it take? / How far is it? Imperatives for directions: Turn left / Go straight / Take the first right Simple Present with travel routines (consolidation)

Functional English
  • Welcome! This way, please. The meeting room is on the 3rd floor.
  • How do I get to your office from the airport? — Take a taxi. It's about 20 minutes.
  • Turn right at the traffic lights, then go straight for two blocks.
  • I have a reservation. My name is García, from [company].
  • I'd like a double room for two nights, please.
  • How long does the flight take? — About 2 hours.
  • Please send her the address. / Call him when you arrive at the hotel.
  • Can you help me? I don't have my visitor badge.
Everyday English
  • "Welcome!" / "This way, please."
  • "Right this way."
  • "Do you need anything?"
  • "It's not far."
  • "Have a good trip!"
Business Travel (Chunks)
  • "I'm here for a client visit."
  • "I'm here for a conference."
  • "My flight is at [time]."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🧳 Business travel opener: "I'm here for a client visit / a conference." — standard arrival phrase at airports and hotels worldwide
  • 🌍 Cultural note: receiving a foreign client — key differences US/UK vs Mexico (personal space, punctuality, use of titles)
  • ⚠️ Object pronoun error: "Call to me" → Call me / "Send to her" → Send it to her — drill with real work scenarios
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "aisle" /aɪl/ — silent S (on a plane) / "receipt" /rɪˈsiːt/ — silent P
  • 📱 Arrival confirmation text: "I'm at the hotel. See you tomorrow at 9." — immediate real-world use for corporate students
Exit Task M3
Receive an imaginary client at reception. Give directions from the airport to your office. Confirm the hotel reservation. Use at least 4 object pronouns in context. (Role play · 4 min)

Health & Work
Workplace health · Absences · Symptoms · HR communication
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Reporting illness and requesting a sick day
  • Describing symptoms related to office work
  • Communicating with HR about absences and medical certificates
  • Talking about physical conditions in the work environment
  • Using possessive pronouns to clarify ownership of documents
  • Giving and receiving simple workplace wellness advice
Grammar
Possessive pronouns: mine/yours/his/hers/ours/theirs — full system have/has as main verb: I have a headache / She has a fever My + body part + hurts/aches: My back hurts. / My eyes ache. Imperatives for health advice: Take a painkiller / Rest today / Drink water "Should" as a fixed chunk: You should rest. / You shouldn't work today. Simple Present with health routines (consolidation)

Functional English
  • I'm not feeling well. I have a bad headache and a sore throat.
  • I need to report a sick day. I won't be able to come in today.
  • Can you send my documents to HR? / The certificate is in her bag.
  • My back hurts. — You should rest and take a painkiller.
  • Is this report yours? — No, it's his. / That laptop is mine, not hers.
  • Are you allergic to anything? — Yes, I'm allergic to penicillin.
  • Our team has a lot of work this week. She doesn't have time to rest.
Everyday English
  • "I'm not feeling well."
  • "I need a sick day."
  • "Get well soon!"
  • "Take care."
  • "Rest today."

GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 📧 Sick day email A1.2: "I'm not feeling well today and I won't be able to come in. I'll send my medical certificate to HR." — ready-to-use template
  • 💼 HR vocabulary in context: sick leave request / absence notification / return-to-work confirmation — real corporate flow
  • 🌍 Cultural note: what to share (and not share) about your health with your manager — US/UK vs Mexico differences
  • ⚠️ Possessive pronoun error: "It's my" → It's mine / "Is your?" → Is it yours? — drill with office objects
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "ache" /eɪk/ — NOT "atch" / "certificate" /səˈtɪfɪkət/ — stress on second syllable
Exit Task M4

Business Communication
Full basic email · Corporate phone · Connectors · Wh-questions
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Writing complete A1.2 business emails with full structure
  • Confirming, requesting information and apologising in writing
  • Corporate phone: receiving, transferring and leaving a message
  • Using connectors to give coherence to written messages
  • Asking complete questions with all Wh-words
  • Professional subject lines and sign-offs
Grammar
Wh-questions — full consolidation: What/Where/When/Who/How/How much/many/long/far/often Imperatives in emails: Please send / Don't hesitate to contact us Object pronouns in writing: Please send it to me / I'll forward it to her. Connectors: and/but/because/so/also/first/then/finally "Could you…?" as a fixed polite request chunk Simple Present for standard written instructions

Functional English
  • Dear [name], / Hi [name], — when to use each and why
  • I'm writing to confirm our meeting on Thursday, March 20th at 10 AM.
  • Please find the report attached. Let me know if you have any questions.
  • Could you send me the agenda? I need it before the meeting.
  • Sorry for the late reply. / Thank you for your email.
  • I'm afraid I can't attend on Friday, but I'm available on Monday.
  • Can I leave a message? — Of course, go ahead.
  • First, open the file. Then, check the data. Finally, send it to me.
Everyday English

  • "On it." — I'll handle it
  • "Sounds good."
  • "Please confirm."
  • "Thanks a lot."
