§1 · Warm-Up

Rapid Fire

10 questions · 15 seconds each · conflict & conversation instincts

⏱ 5 min
Rapid Fire: Conflict Edition
First instinct only. 15 seconds per question — no elaboration, no correction. Read the question, student answers, you move on. Speed is the method.
5:00
Warm-Up
Q 01
When someone upsets you at work — do you say something or let it go?
Q 02
Is avoiding a difficult conversation ever the right choice?
Q 03
What's harder — starting the conversation or finishing it?
Q 04
Can you resolve a conflict without one person apologizing?
Q 05
Is it professional to show emotion during a difficult conversation at work?
Q 06
Who should initiate — the person who caused the problem or the one affected?
Q 07
What's worse: conflict that explodes — or tension that never gets addressed?
Q 08
Do you think most conflicts at work are about the real issue — or something else?
Q 09
Can you be honest in a difficult conversation without damaging the relationship?
Q 10
Is it ever too late to have a conversation that should have happened months ago?
🟣 Facilitation
🟠 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • These questions are designed to surface instinct — not produce correct grammar. Fluency activation only. No error correction at this stage.
  • Q 07, Q 08, Q 10 will generate the most authentic content. Note answers verbatim — they become real discussion fuel in §4.
  • Bridge out of warm-up: "Every single one of these questions came up in a real conversation. We're going to hear one of those conversations right now." — leads directly into §3 listening.
  • Students over-explain: Cut at 15 seconds, no exceptions. The discomfort of being cut off is the point — it teaches commitment to a position before it's fully formed.
  • Q 04 produces long silence: Fine. After 5 seconds say — "First word. Can you or can't you?" The binary unlocks them.
  • Q 08 gets a philosophical answer: Good data. Write the answer down — use it in §4 Q&A Q3 as a reference point.
  • After all 10: ask the student to pick the one question they'd most like to discuss for a full 5 minutes. That becomes their §5 anchor question.

§2 · Vocabulary

20 Words

10 nouns + 10 verbs · conflict & conversation lexical set · click to reveal

⏱ 10 min
Vocab: Conflict Resolution Set
Click any word to reveal its contextual definition. Teacher-paced. In Student Mode, definitions are hidden. Target: these words must appear in §4 Q&A and §5 Speaking — track production actively.
10:00
Vocabulary
Nouns (10)
confrontationA direct, often tense face-to-face encounter where disagreement is addressed openly
misunderstandingA failure to correctly interpret what someone said, meant, or intended
boundaryA limit a person sets on what they will accept in terms of behavior or treatment
escalationThe process by which a conflict grows more intense, serious, or unmanageable over time
resolutionA concrete outcome that ends or meaningfully reduces a conflict — agreed upon by both parties
grievanceA formal or informal complaint about something perceived as unfair or wrong
apologyAn explicit acknowledgment that one's actions caused harm — genuine, not tactical
mediationThe involvement of a neutral third party to help two sides reach an agreement
toneThe emotional quality carried in the voice or language — what a message feels like, beyond its words
accountabilityAccepting responsibility for one's actions and their consequences — without deflection or excuse
Verbs (10)
confrontTo face a person or issue directly, even when doing so is uncomfortable
clarifyTo make something clearer by asking for or giving more specific information
de-escalateTo actively reduce the intensity or tension of a conflict before it gets worse
apologizeTo express genuine regret for something you said or did that caused harm
mediateTo act as a neutral third party and help two sides in a conflict communicate and reach agreement
assertTo state one's position, needs, or limits clearly and confidently without aggression
escalateTo intensify a conflict — either deliberately or through inaction and avoidance
resolveTo find a concrete solution that both parties can accept and move forward from
miscommunicateTo fail to convey a message clearly — resulting in confusion, misunderstanding, or conflict
acknowledgeTo recognize and accept what someone has experienced or expressed — without necessarily agreeing
Activation Activities
Activity A — Pair Contrasts (4 min)

Students discuss the difference between these pairs — one sentence per pair is enough. The distinction is the point, not the definition.

Contrast Pairs

Confront vs. assert — one implies aggression; the other implies confidence. Which is which?

Escalate vs. de-escalate — can you do both in the same conversation? How?

Acknowledge vs. apologize — is it possible to acknowledge without apologizing? When would you do that?

Resolution vs. mediation — can you have one without the other?

Activity B — Real Situation (3 min)

Each student picks any 3 words from today's list and uses them to describe a real conflict situation they've witnessed — or been part of — at work. One sentence per word. No invented scenarios.

Target Output — B2.1

"There was a misunderstanding about ownership — neither side wanted to confront the other, which caused the situation to escalate for three weeks."

Activity C — Word in Context (3 min)

You say a situation. Student must respond using one of today's vocabulary words — without choosing in advance. Keeps the retrieval fast and contextualized.

Sample Situations
  • "Your colleague takes credit for your work in a meeting." → student responds using one word from the list
  • "A team member hasn't replied to your messages in two weeks." → one word
  • "Your manager changed the brief again without telling you." → one word
  • "Two people on your team stop talking to each other." → one word
🟣 Facilitation
🟠 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • Priority words for production tracking: de-escalate, assert, accountability, acknowledge, escalate — these are the highest-frequency words in §5 Speaking and the Conversation Repair Kit.
  • Activity A is the highest value per minute in this section. Spend 4 full minutes here. The confront/assert distinction is one of the most transferable lessons of the session.
  • Time split: A = 4 min / B = 3 min / C = 3 min. If running behind, cut C — not B.
  • Remind students at the start of B: "Real situations only. Not invented. The vocabulary works better in real memory."
  • "Grievance" sounds too legal: Clarify — "In everyday use, a grievance is just a complaint you've been holding onto — often unspoken. You don't need HR for a grievance."
  • "De-escalate" is hard to pronounce: Model it slowly — dee-ES-kuh-layt. The prefix "de-" is key. Have the student repeat once before moving on.
  • Confront vs. assert produces real disagreement: Good. Let it run 20 extra seconds. If they can't resolve it — that itself is a demonstration of the difficulty of confrontation.
  • Activity C goes too slowly: Increase pressure — "You have 4 seconds. Go." The time constraint is the activation mechanism.
  • For strong students: ask them to produce one sentence that contains three words from today's list in a way that sounds natural — not a list. Tests integration, not just recognition.
  • Connect to S2 vocabulary (feedback set): "Last session you used 'address' and 'redirect.' How do those relate to 'confront' and 'de-escalate'? Are they the same? More formal? Less direct?"

§3 · Listening

Elena Voss

First-person monologue · a conversation that almost didn't happen · natural pace

⏱ 10 min
"The Rodrigo Conversation" — Audio Monologue
Elena Voss describes a conflict with Rodrigo Peña that she avoided for weeks — and what finally forced her to address it. Natural pace, first person, emotionally specific. Web Speech API — Chrome recommended.
10:00
Listening
Pre-Listening — 1 min, no preparation

"Have you ever avoided a conversation at work for so long that the situation got worse? What finally made you have it?" Take 2 answers maximum. Do not preview the content of the audio. Let the monologue land on its own.

Script · "The Rodrigo Conversation"

I avoided it for six weeks. Six weeks of reading his emails with a knot in my stomach, six weeks of perfectly reasonable project updates that somehow felt like — passive escalation. And I told myself it was about timing. That I'd wait until after the client delivery. Then I'd wait until after the quarterly review. Then there was always something.

The truth is I didn't know how to start the conversation without it turning into a confrontation. Rodrigo is good at what he does — genuinely — but somewhere in the last quarter, the boundary between his role and mine had completely dissolved. He was making commitments to clients that affected my team's timeline — without telling me. And every time I thought about raising it, I heard myself saying: but he probably didn't mean it that way. That's how we miscommunicate without ever saying a word.

Eventually it was Marco who said something. He came to me and said — "Elena, this can't go on." And that's when I realized: the fact that someone else had to name it meant I had already failed to assert myself. So I asked Rodrigo for fifteen minutes. I told him what I'd observed. I didn't escalate, I didn't make it personal. I just tried to clarify what I needed from him going forward. He listened. He actually acknowledged it. And we got to something that looked like a resolution — not perfect, but real. What I wish I had done differently? I wish I hadn't waited for the knot in my stomach to become a crisis.

Speed: 0.84×
Comprehension Tasks
Task 1 — Gist (1 min · after first listen)
Question

"Why did Elena wait so long to have the conversation with Rodrigo?"

Target: She feared the conversation would become a confrontation. She kept finding reasons to delay — timing, delivery, the quarterly review. She was avoiding the escalation by doing nothing, which was itself a form of escalation.

Task 2 — Detail (3 min · after second listen at 0.78×)
Four Questions
  • What specific thing was Rodrigo doing that affected Elena's team?
  • What phrase did Elena use to justify not acting? (Quote as closely as possible.)
  • Who finally said something — and what did they say?
  • What does Elena say she wishes she had done differently?
Answer Key
  • Making commitments to clients that affected Elena's team's timeline — without informing her.
  • "But he probably didn't mean it that way."
  • Marco. He told Elena: "This can't go on."
  • She wishes she hadn't waited for the situation to become a crisis before asserting herself.
Task 3 — Speaking Response (3 min)
Discussion Prompt

"Elena says: 'That's how we miscommunicate without ever saying a word.' What does she mean? Has that happened in your team?"

Each student must use at least 2 words from today's list in their response. This is the natural bridge into §4. When students start producing sentences about their own experience — that's the entry point for the Q&A.

🟣 Facilitation
🟠 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • Rate target: 0.84× first listen / 0.78× second listen. Never 1.0× — the synthetic voice is unnatural at full rate.
  • The script contains 9 vocabulary words from §2 highlighted in gold. After the second listen, point to each one: "Did you hear that word? That's exactly what we practiced." Recognition reinforces acquisition.
  • Elena's final line — "I wish I hadn't waited for the knot in my stomach to become a crisis" — is a natural Third Conditional seed. When students respond to Task 3, listen for it. If no one produces it spontaneously, model it once and move to §4.
  • Task 3 is the entry into §4. Do not let Task 3 become a full discussion — keep it to 3 minutes and redirect into the Q&A structure.
  • Task 2, Q2 — students can't recall the quote: Play the second paragraph again specifically — start at "And every time I thought about raising it…"
  • "Passive escalation" in the script: Students may ask what this means. It's a non-taught compound — explain once: "You're not actively making things worse. But by doing nothing, you're allowing the escalation to happen. That's passive escalation."
  • Task 3 response stays surface-level: Push — "Give me a moment from your own team. Not a general comment — a specific situation."
  • For advanced students: ask them to reconstruct Elena's strategy — what did she do right in the actual conversation? (She observed, didn't personalize, clarified what she needed, didn't escalate.) Then ask: would they have done it differently?
  • Third Conditional bridge — ask directly after Task 3: "If Elena hadn't waited six weeks, what would have been different for the team?" Harvest the sentence — it reappears as a §5 anchor.

§4 · Q&A · Three Dynamics

Q&A

Teacher → Student · Student → Teacher · Student → Student · guided dialogue on conflict

⏱ 10 min
Guided Q&A: Difficult Conversations
Seven questions — three dynamics. Each question is marked with its dynamic. The format rotates naturally: you ask, the student asks, students ask each other. No roles, no mechanics — just conversation with structure.
10:00
Q&A
🟣 Teacher → Student
⭐ Student → Teacher
🟢 Student → Student
Teacher → Student
Q1
Think of a conflict at work you didn't address immediately. What was the consequence of waiting?
Push: Was there a moment when it escalated from a misunderstanding into something bigger? What triggered that shift?
↳ Third Conditional: "If I had addressed it earlier…"
Teacher → Student
Q2
What's the difference between asserting yourself and being aggressive in a difficult conversation? Where is the line?
Push: Can you give me an example — real or observed — where someone crossed that line? What happened to the resolution?
Student → Student
Q3
Ask your partner: "Have you ever had to de-escalate a conflict that wasn't yours? How did you do it without making it worse?"
Teacher note: circulate and listen. Note any natural use of today's vocabulary. After 90 seconds, bring the group back and ask one student to summarize their partner's answer.
Student → Teacher
Q4
Now you ask me something — about a difficult conversation, conflict, or accountability at work. Any question you actually want to know the answer to.
Teacher note: answer authentically. This is not a test of your language — it's a model of how to respond in real conversation. Use vocabulary from §2 naturally. Students will mirror your register.
Teacher → Student
Q5
Elena says she "miscommunicated without ever saying a word." In your company — does that happen? What does it look like?
Push: Is it cultural? Is it the hierarchy? Or is it that people simply don't have the language to assert a boundary without it feeling like a confrontation?
↳ Third Conditional: "If the culture had been different…"
Student → Student
Q6
Ask your partner: "If you had to apologize to someone at work right now — someone you've had tension with — what would you actually say?"
Teacher note: this question is deliberately uncomfortable. Let it be. The discomfort generates authentic language. If a student says "I have no one to apologize to" — push gently: "Are you sure?"
Student → Teacher
Q7
One more — ask me anything about how to handle a conflict situation. Something you actually find difficult in English or at work.
Teacher note: if the student asks a grammar or vocabulary question — answer it directly in context, not as a lesson. If they ask a situational question — give a real answer, then ask them to reflect on their own equivalent situation.
🟣 Facilitation
🟠 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • The three dynamics are explicit — students see which format is active. This reduces cognitive load: they know whether to prepare a question or a response.
  • Student → Teacher (Q4, Q7): This is high-value production. The student must generate a question — that requires active grammar and lexical choice. Do not rush through it. Give the student 10 full seconds to formulate before prompting.
  • Student → Student (Q3, Q6): Circulate. You are not the center of the conversation during these. Your role is to listen for vocabulary production and harvest Third Conditional sentences.
  • If only one student: adapt Q3 and Q6 — teacher takes the partner role, but the student initiates the question. The dynamic shifts but the production stays the same.
  • Time per question: approximately 80–90 seconds. Do not rush Q4 and Q7 — they produce the highest-quality language in this section.
  • Q4 or Q7: student can't formulate a question: Scaffold — "Think of the one thing about conflict at work that you genuinely don't know how to handle in English. Start there."
  • Q6 produces resistance: Completely expected. Don't force it. If the student deflects — accept it, then ask: "What would you say to someone who needed to apologize to you? Same thing?" Redirects without pressure.
  • Q5 goes long: Cultural discussions can run deep. Cut at 90 seconds even if the conversation is rich — save energy for §5.
  • Third Conditional doesn't appear organically: Model one sentence yourself in Q1 or Q5, then move on. Don't make it explicit — just plant it.
  • For advanced students: after Q7, ask them to reformulate their question using more formal language — then more informal. This builds register awareness without the Register Switch mechanic from S2.
  • If the session is running with a group: run Q3 and Q6 simultaneously across pairs, then debrief by asking one pair to share the most interesting thing they heard. Maintains pace without losing depth.

§5 · Speaking + Repair Kit

Speaking

Guided conversation on conflict · 5 colloquial phrases · Conversation Repair Kit

⏱ 15 min
Guided Conversation: Difficult Conversations
Six open questions — extended responses expected. Students develop their answers beyond one sentence. Teacher prompts with follow-up when the answer is thin. Third Conditional must appear at least once per student before the section ends.
15:00
Speaking
SP 1
Describe a difficult conversation you had — or should have had — at work in the last year. What made it difficult? What was at stake?
Expect 4–6 sentences minimum. Push for specificity: who, what context, what the tension was about. If the answer is vague, ask: "Tell me the moment — not the general situation."
SP 2
What's the hardest part of a difficult conversation for you personally — starting it, staying calm in it, or reaching a resolution?
This is personal — give the student space. Follow up: "Why that stage specifically? What happens to you in that moment?" Vocabulary target: assert, de-escalate, tone.
SP 3
Think of a situation where a conflict was resolved well — at your company or somewhere else. What made the resolution real, not just superficial?
Push for the distinction between a real resolution and a temporary truce. "Did someone take accountability? Did anyone apologize — and mean it?"
SP 4
In your experience — does your company handle conflict openly, or does it tend to suppress it? What are the consequences of that culture?
Cultural question — high-yield for B2.1 Mexican corporate context. Vocabulary target: escalation, grievance, mediation. Third Conditional entry: "If the culture had encouraged accountability earlier…"
SP 5
What would you do differently — in a specific past situation — if you could have that conversation again?
Direct Third Conditional trigger. Target: "If I hadn't avoided that conversation, the escalation wouldn't have happened." If the student produces an affirmative conditional, push: "Now say it in the negative — if you hadn't…"
SP 6
What's one thing you're going to do differently — at work — in the next difficult conversation you face?
Forward-looking close. No conditional needed here — future intention. But listen for vocabulary and colloquial phrases. This answer feeds directly into §6 Speaking Challenge.
Colloquial Phrases: Conflict Register
"Let's clear the air."
Neutral–InformalOpening a difficult conversation
Used to propose an honest conversation after tension has built up — implies both parties know something needs to be addressed.
Context: "I know things have been tense between us since last month. Can we find 15 minutes this week — just to clear the air?"
"I hear what you're saying, but…"
NeutralAcknowledging without agreeing
A way to validate the other person's point before introducing a counter-position. The "but" signals disagreement without dismissal — critical for de-escalation.
Context: "I hear what you're saying, but from my perspective, the timeline shifted because of decisions made above both of us."
"That's on me."
Informal–DirectTaking accountability
A direct, zero-excuse admission of responsibility. More powerful than a formal apology because it's immediate and unqualified. High trust signal.
Context: "The brief changed without notice and the team didn't know. That's on me — I should have communicated it the moment the decision was made."
"Let's not make this bigger than it is."
InformalDe-escalating in real time
Used to prevent a conflict from escalating further — signals a desire to resolve without drama. Can be used by either party, but is most effective from the person with more authority.
Context: "I understand you're frustrated — and I get it. But let's not make this bigger than it is. What do we actually need to fix?"
"Where do we go from here?"
NeutralMoving toward resolution
A closing move — shifts the conversation from the problem to the solution. Signals that the speaker is ready to move past accountability and into action.
Context: "OK — we've talked about what happened and why. That's done. Where do we go from here? What does a resolution actually look like for both of us?"
🛠 Conversation Repair Kit — 5 Moves
▼ Expand

Five sequential moves for repairing a difficult conversation. Use this after SP 3–5 — show it live and ask students which move Elena used in her conversation with Rodrigo, and in what order.

1 · Acknowledge → 2 · Clarify → 3 · De-escalate → 4 · Propose → 5 · Close
1
Acknowledge
Formal:"I understand this situation has created tension — and I want to address it directly."
Informal:"Look — I know things haven't been right between us. I get it."
2
Clarify
Formal:"I want to make sure I understand what happened from your perspective before I share mine."
Informal:"Help me understand — what did you think was happening? Because I may have read it wrong."
3
De-escalate
Formal:"I don't want this conversation to become a confrontation — I'm here to resolve it, not escalate it."
Informal:"Let's not make this bigger than it needs to be. We're on the same team."
4
Propose
Formal:"Going forward, I'd like to propose a clearer boundary around how we coordinate decisions that affect both teams."
Informal:"Here's what I think would work — you loop me in before any client commitments that touch my timeline. Does that work for you?"
5
Close
Formal:"I appreciate you engaging with this. I think we've reached a point we can both move forward from."
Informal:"Good. So we're clear. Where do we go from here?"
🟣 Facilitation
🟠 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • SP 1–6 are conversation starters — not comprehension checks. The right response is always a developed personal answer, not a summary of the topic. If the student gives a definition instead of an experience — redirect: "Tell me about you, not about the concept."
  • Colloquial phrases: present one at a time, 30 seconds each. Ask student to use it in a sentence immediately before moving to the next. Don't batch-present all 5 then drill — the production comes in real time.
  • Conversation Repair Kit: expand it live after SP 3. Ask students to map Elena's conversation to the 5 moves — which did she use? In what order? Did she skip any? This is discussion, not a quiz.
  • SP 5 is the mandatory Third Conditional trigger. If the student doesn't produce it naturally, ask directly after their answer: "Now say that using 'If I hadn't…'" One sentence is enough.
  • SP 6 is the bridge to §6. Note the student's answer — it feeds directly into the Speaking Challenge prompt.
  • SP 1 — student says they've never had a difficult conversation: Almost never true. Push — "A difficult email? A meeting where you said nothing when you wanted to say something? That counts."
  • Colloquial phrases feel forced: Don't drill them in sequence. Present one, use it once in your own sentence, then ask the student to produce theirs. Move on. Return to the list at the end of SP 6 if time remains.
  • Repair Kit: students say Elena used all 5 moves: Push for order and evidence — "Show me where in the script she de-escalates. What did she actually say?"
  • Advanced: ask students to produce their own Repair Kit sentence for each of the 5 moves — applied to a real situation from their workplace. This creates a personalised language bank they can actually use.
  • For groups: after SP 4, assign each student one of the 5 Repair Kit moves and ask them to produce both the formal and informal version for a given situation. Debrief in 2 minutes.

Global Feedback Note — Teacher Script

After the challenge round, one group-level observation — under 45 seconds:

"Today the group [STRENGTH — specific: name the vocabulary or structure produced naturally]. One area to develop is [GAP — forward-looking, not critical]. In S5 we move into [PREVIEW]."

Homework — choose 1, or assign based on the session
HW1
Conversation Audit: Identify one difficult conversation you're avoiding right now at work. Write 4–6 sentences: what the conflict is about, why you haven't had the conversation, and what would happen if you did. Use at least 4 vocabulary words from today. Bring it to S5.
HW2

HW3
Third Conditional Reflection: Three sentences about a conflict situation that could have gone differently. Use the negative form (If I hadn't…) for one, the question form (Would it have resolved…?) for one, and your choice for the third. All three must be about the same situation.

🟣 Facilitation
🟠 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • The Speaking Challenge prompt is intentionally personal — it asks about a real unresolved situation. This is by design. The discomfort of the topic produces more authentic language than any hypothetical scenario.
  • If a student doesn't reach 60 seconds: don't let silence sit — "What would happen to the relationship if you had that conversation today? Add that." Each follow-up question adds 10–15 seconds of production.
  • Third Conditional is mandatory in this challenge. If the student finishes without producing one, pause before moving to the next student: "One more thing — tell me what would have been different if you'd had that conversation six months ago."
  • Homework assignment: base it on what was weakest in the session. Third Conditional gaps → HW3. Vocabulary gaps → HW1. Language production gaps → HW2.
  • Student says they have no unresolved conflict: Fine. Ask them to describe a past one that did resolve — and what they would change about how they handled it. Same production, different frame.
  • Third Conditional in the challenge is forced: That's acceptable at this stage. Forced production still builds the structure. Natural production will follow with repetition across sessions.
  • Global Feedback Note feels generic: Force specificity on yourself — name one actual word or sentence the group produced today. "The group used 'de-escalate' three times in natural production — that's a significant leap from passive recognition."
  • For strong students: add a constraint to the Speaking Challenge — they must apply all 5 Repair Kit moves within their 60-second answer. Tests sequencing and production under pressure simultaneously.
