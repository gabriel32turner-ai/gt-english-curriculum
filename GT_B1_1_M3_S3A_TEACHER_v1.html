→ "Try on" is key vocabulary for today — note it on the board

→ Push for specific examples: "The assistant helped me…" / "I hate the checkout line…"

→ Connects to vocabulary: return, receipt, refund, delivery — all in today's list
⚡
Quick Win — 20 seconds
Say this out loud right now: "If I buy clothes online, I'll probably have to return them — I need to try them on first." First Conditional. Real. Yours.
📘 Pedagogical S3 sits between S2 (Online Shopping Behavior) and S4 (Financial Goals). It's the most concrete and immediately relatable session of M3 — everyone has an opinion on in-store vs online. The First Conditional is the natural grammar for expressing purchase decisions: "If the price is lower online, I'll order it" / "If I can't try it on, I won't buy it." Push for real examples throughout.
💡 Engagement Ask the class: "Is anyone here a 100% online shopper? Raise your hand. Is anyone a 100% in-store shopper?" — almost no one will be either extreme. That's the discussion: it depends on the product. Today's grammar — First Conditional — is perfect for conditional preferences: "If it's clothing, I'll always go to the store. If it's electronics, I'll buy online."
§2 Speaking Topic — In-Store vs Online Rodrigo Vega's shopping habits · 3 rounds · Model B1.1 answers ⏱ 8 MIN
Session Topic
For what products do you still prefer a physical store?
Round 1 — Clothing
Do you buy clothes online? What happens if the size is wrong? When do you absolutely need to go to a physical store?
"I buy basic things online — t-shirts, socks — but if it's anything more formal, I'll always go to a store. If I can't try it on, I'll probably return it. I've returned too many things. It's just not worth the hassle for clothing."
Round 2 — Electronics
Do you test electronics in a store before buying them online cheaper? Is that fair to the store? Would you buy a phone without touching it first?
"Honestly? Yes — I've done that. I go to the store, I touch the laptop, I compare the screens, and then I buy it 20% cheaper online. I don't feel too bad about it. If the store offers better service and the same price, I'll buy there. But if the price difference is big, I'll always choose online."
Round 3 — Furniture / Big Items
Would you buy a sofa online? What about a mattress? Is there a category where you absolutely need to physically experience the product before buying?
"Furniture? No way online. If I can't sit on the sofa, I won't buy it. Same with mattresses — that's years of sleeping. For things I'll use every day, if I can't touch and test it in person, I'll feel nervous about the purchase. That's where the store still wins, absolutely."
📘 Context — Rodrigo Vega Rodrigo (B1.1 character) is a pragmatic mid-career professional. He shops both online and in-store depending on product type. His perspective: online for repeatables (books, supplements, basic clothing), in-store for high-involvement purchases (clothes he'll wear to work, furniture, shoes). Students who work in manufacturing tend to agree — they value durability and fit over convenience.

§4 Colloquial English — Shopping Talk 3 natural phrases · In-store context · Used by native speakers every day ⏱ 5 MIN
"I like to see it before I buy it."
The most natural way to say you prefer physical stores for visual or tactile products. Common in any buying conversation — even for cars, houses, or appliances.
"I'm not an online furniture buyer. I like to see it before I buy it. If I can't sit on the sofa, I won't spend that money."
"I need to try it on first."
Specifically for clothing and shoes. "Try it on" = to put it on your body to check the fit. One of the most common in-store shopping phrases in English.
"I need to try it on first — if I order online and it doesn't fit, I'll have to deal with the return process, and that's a waste of time."
"I just want to be quick."
Explains why you prefer efficiency over experience — for routine purchases. "Just" softens the statement. Pairs naturally with "I'll order online if I don't have time."
"I just want to be quick — if it's something I buy every week, I'll do it online. I don't want to spend 40 minutes in a supermarket for 5 items."
💡 Drill Note Each phrase pairs naturally with First Conditional. After students say each phrase, push them to finish with a condition: "I like to see it before I buy it — IF the store is nearby, I'll go. If not, I'll take the risk online." Run 3 quick rounds: Teacher says the context → student responds with the phrase + a First Conditional sentence.
§5 Grammar — First Conditional in Shopping Decisions In-Store vs Online Showdown · Referential approach · 2 activities ⏱ 10 MIN
⚡ 30-Second Poll — Grammar Gut Check
Which sentence correctly uses First Conditional for a shopping decision?
❌ "Will" never goes in the if-clause — not even when the situation is about the future. The condition is expressed with Present Simple: "If I find…" The future idea lives in the result: "I'll buy it there."
✅ Correct! "If I find" — Present Simple in the if-clause. "I'll buy it" — will + base verb in the result. Real condition, likely outcome. This is exactly how native speakers express conditional shopping decisions.
❌ "If I found… I would buy" is Second Conditional — for hypothetical or unlikely situations. If you're genuinely considering buying online, First Conditional is the right choice. Second Conditional suggests it's unlikely to happen.

If-clauseIf + Present SimpleIf the price is better online…

💡 The rule that matters most: No "will" in the if-clause. "If I find a deal…" not "if I will find…" — even though you're talking about the future. This is the number one error at B1.1.
⚠️ Correct immediately:
✗ "If I will go to the store, I'll try it on." → ✅ "If I go to the store, I'll try it on."
✗ "If I can't find it, I would order online." → ✅ "If I can't find it, I will order online."
✗ "I'll buy it if it will fit." → ✅ "I'll buy it if it fits."

✏️ Activity B — Grammar Rules: True or False?
In First Conditional, the if-clause uses Present Simple — not will + verb.
"If I won't find my size, I'll order online" is a grammatically correct First Conditional.
"I'll buy it online if the price is lower" is a correct First Conditional — even though the result comes first.
First Conditional is only used for real, possible future situations — not hypothetical ones.
§6 Listening — Rodrigo Goes Shopping A Saturday in Celaya · In-store vs online decisions · First Conditional throughout ⏱ 8 MIN
Rodrigo Vega

Context: Weekend shopping trip
Format: Internal monologue podcast
Rodrigo's Saturday — A Day of Decisions
B1.1 · M3 · S3 · Voice: Marcus (American English) · Speed: 0.85
Saturday. My day off. And I have a list of five things to buy. The question is always the same — store or online?

First: a new work shirt. My manager said we have a client visit next Friday. If I order online, it might not arrive in time — and I've been burned by that before. If I go to the store today, I'll be able to try it on, choose the right fit, and have it ready. So the store it is.

Second: a new laptop charger. Mine stopped working yesterday. If I go to the electronics store downtown, I'll find one today. But the price will probably be higher than online. If I order online, I'll save maybe 30 percent, but I'll wait three days without being able to work properly. I'll go to the store — I can't wait three days.

Third: a book I want to read. That's an easy one — if it's available online for less than 200 pesos, I'll order it. If not, I'll check the bookstore near my apartment. I prefer physical books. If I read on a screen, my eyes get tired.

Fourth: a new sofa for my apartment. I've been putting this off for months. If I buy it without sitting on it first, I'll regret it. I'll go to the furniture store this afternoon and test three or four before I decide.

Fifth: groceries. I need things for the week. If I order delivery, I'll spend 80 more pesos in fees and the vegetables are never as fresh. I'll go to the market. Some things just work better in person.

Five decisions. Five different answers. It's never one or the other — it depends.

§7 Production — Your Shopping Rules Real decisions · First Conditional in authentic context · 6 speaking prompts ⏱ 12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Students answer using First Conditional. Partner marks: (1) any "will" in the if-clause, (2) any "would" where "will" is needed. Correct after each response — don't let errors accumulate. Progress: Q1–Q2 = warm, Q3–Q4 = real, Q5–Q6 = challenge.
Question 1 — Your Shopping Rules
What are your personal rules for in-store vs online shopping? Make at least 3 rules using First Conditional — for different product categories.
💡 "If it's clothing, I'll… / If I need it urgently, I'll… / If the price difference is more than [X]%, I'll…"
Question 2 — The Return Story
Have you ever bought something online that you had to return? What happened? What will you do differently next time? Use First Conditional for the "next time" part.
💡 "I bought [item] online and… / If I buy [item] online again, I'll… / I won't buy [item] online if…"
Question 3 — The Future of Physical Stores
In 10 years, will physical stores still exist? Which ones will survive? Which will disappear? Give your predictions using First Conditional: "If [trend continues], [store type] will…"
💡 "If online shopping keeps growing, small bookstores will… / If supermarkets can't compete on price, they'll… / If the experience is good enough, people will always prefer…"
Question 4 — Luxury vs Necessity
Does it make a difference if something is expensive vs cheap? Are your in-store vs online rules different for a $500 item vs a $5,000 item? Explain using conditions.
💡 "If the price is under [X], I'll take the risk online. If it's over [X], I'll always go to a store."
Question 5 — Convince Me
You are a physical store owner. Your best customer just told you they're switching entirely to online shopping. What will you say to convince them to come back? Use 3 First Conditional arguments.
💡 "If you come to our store, you'll…" / "If you try it online, you'll miss…" / "If we don't have it, we'll order it for you…"
⭐ Speaking Challenge — 90 Seconds
You have $10,000 pesos to spend on: clothing, a phone, a piece of furniture, and groceries. Explain your shopping plan using First Conditional for each item. Where will you go? Why? No notes.

3 Phrases Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"I like to see it before I buy it."
Use this when explaining why you prefer a physical store for a specific product. It's honest, natural, and immediately understood. Use it this week when someone recommends an online purchase and you disagree.
PHRASE 2
"If I can't try it on, I won't buy it."
First Conditional for a real personal rule. This phrase is practical and immediately shows that you know how to use conditional grammar naturally. Use it the next time someone asks if you buy clothes online.
PHRASE 3
"It depends — if [condition], I'll [decision]."
The most versatile phrase in English for expressing conditional preference. "It depends" sounds mature and thoughtful. Adding the First Conditional makes your answer specific and grammatically precise. Use it anywhere this week.

Session Badges Earned

01
Present Simple in the if-clause. "If I go to the store…" — never "if I will go." The condition is a real possibility, expressed in Present Simple even though the meaning is future.
02
It depends on the product. In-store vs online isn't a binary choice. First Conditional lets you express that nuance: "If it's clothing, I'll go to the store. If it's books, I'll order online."
03
Touch, try on, compare. These three verbs are the heart of in-store shopping English. If you can say "If I can't touch it / try it on / compare it in person — I'll need to think twice," you sound like a B1+ speaker.
