§ 01
Warm-Up — Free Discussion
Open question · No right answer · Real experience only
01
6–8 min
Free Discussion
Real situations only. No hypotheticals.
B1.2 · High stakes
💬 Think of a time your boss made a decision you completely disagreed with. What was the decision? What did you do — and what do you wish you had done differently?
🔥 Has there ever been a situation at work where you knew your boss was wrong — but you stayed silent? What was the consequence? Has that been affecting you since?
⚡ Quick ranking — which of these feels most natural for you right now? Stay silent / Speak up diplomatically / Push back directly / Go above their head. Which one have you been using most? Which one do you wish you used more?
🎯 Teacher: This topic generates the most authentic, unscripted speaking of the entire module. Let it run — do not cut it short. Log their answers: you will use them as material for §7 (Storytelling). PPC hook: after their answer, ask "Has this been affecting how you work? How long has this been going on?" Write the PPC sentence they produce on the board.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • This is the highest-engagement topic in M1. Students have always experienced this — some have strong emotional reactions. Create a safe space: "I'm not asking you to name anyone. I'm asking you to describe the situation and what you did."
  • Ranking exercise: after student ranks, ask "Why?" and then "What's been holding you back from using the option you prefer?" — this generates reflective B1.2 language and PPC production.
  • Grammar hook: every answer can be extended with PPC. "I stayed silent" → "How long have you been staying silent about this?" "I pushed back" → "Has that been working? How long has the situation been unresolved?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "I'm going to tell you about a boss decision I thought was completely wrong — and what I did about it. Then you tell me yours." (Share something real and brief — it signals psychological safety.)
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "By the end of this session, you'll have specific phrases — in English — to handle this exact conversation without damaging your professional relationship. That's a skill most people never develop, even in their own language."

GT Originals — The Cost of Silence
Mexican professional culture traditionally values harmony and respect for hierarchy — which is a genuine strength. But it can also create a pattern where employees stay silent when they disagree, absorb bad decisions, and develop resentment. The goal today is not to teach students to be confrontational — it is to give them the language to be assertive, professional, and respectful at the same time. That combination is rare, and it makes them more valuable in any organization.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Boss Analyzer: after each reveal, ask "Does this match what you actually did in a real situation? Which strategy have you been using most?" The goal is reflection + PPC production, not just reading the outcomes.
  • Response Builder: after student speaks the built phrase, ask: "Now make it more specific — add a real consequence you've been observing." This bridges the tool to their real professional situation.
  • Challenge for B1.2 level: after building one response, clear it and rebuild it from scratch — faster, without reading. Goal: the phrase should feel natural enough to use in a real meeting.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "The Response Builder gives you the skeleton. Now you need to make it yours. Change one word in each part to fit a real situation from your office. Then say the whole thing in one breath."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "S5 next class is the Exit Task of M1 — Imposter Syndrome. Everything from S1 to S4 feeds into it. The language you've been building — PPC, vocabulary, response strategies — comes together in one 2-minute story."
§ 04
Grammar — PPC for Ongoing Conflict
Describing tension, disagreement, and unresolved situations at work
04
12–14 min
✅
✅ Describing what has been happening
"The team has been pushing back on this decision for two weeks."
"I've been trying to raise this issue since the last meeting."
"He's been making decisions without consulting anyone since he got promoted."
❌
❌ What hasn't been happening
"Management hasn't been listening to the team's concerns."
"He hasn't been giving us a reason for the change."
"I haven't been getting any feedback since I raised the issue."
❓
❓ Investigating the situation
"How long has this been going on?"
"Has the team been dealing with this since January?"
"What have you been doing to address it?"
💡
💡 PPC makes it sound professional
"My boss is wrong." → Sounds emotional.
"My boss has been making decisions that have been affecting the team." → Evidence-based. Specific. Professional.
PPC shows a pattern, not just a feeling.
💡 The professional upgrade: PPC turns a complaint into an observation.
"He never listens." → Sounds like a personal attack.
"He hasn't been listening to the team's concerns since the project started." → This is evidence. This can go in a report. This can be said in a meeting.

At B1.2, this distinction is the difference between being heard and being dismissed.
⚠️ Common Errors — S4 Context
❌ "I been pushing back on this for weeks."→✅ "I've been pushing back on this for weeks." (need have)
❌ "She has been challenge the decision."→✅ "She has been challenging the decision." (need -ing)
❌ "How long he has been ignoring this?"→✅ "How long has he been ignoring this?" (auxiliary before subject)
❌ "The team has been deal with this since months."→✅ "The team has been dealing with this for months." (-ing + for, not since)
✏️ Activity 1 — Choose the Correct Form
Select the most natural PPC sentence for each workplace situation.
The director ___ the budget cuts without explaining the reason to anyone on the team.
I ___ this issue for three weeks — and nobody has responded yet.
"How long ___ the team ___ with this decision?"
She ___ a different approach for months — but her manager won't reconsider.
✏️ Activity 2 — True or False
Decide if the sentence is correct and natural.
"The team has been pushing back on this decision" suggests the pushback is ongoing — not finished.
"I've been challenge this approach since Monday" is correct PPC — "challenge" is a verb, not a noun.
"He hasn't been listening" is more professional than "He never listens" when describing an ongoing pattern.
"She has been comply with the decision" is correct — comply is a verb and -ing was added.
✏️ Activity 3 — Reorder
Drag into correct PPC sentence order.
  1. ⠿without consulting the team,
  2. ⠿The manager
  3. ⠿has been making
  4. ⠿decisions
  5. ⠿and it has been affecting morale.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Rule box is the key insight for S4: PPC as professional upgrade. After reading it, ask student to take ONE complaint from §1 and rewrite it as a professional PPC observation. They must use a specific time marker (for / since). This is the skill that transfers to real professional English.
  • Q3: "has / been dealing" — this is the B1.2 question form drill. Push until it's automatic. "How long has the team been dealing with this?" — write it, say it, produce it spontaneously.
  • Reorder: the compound PPC ("has been making decisions... and it has been affecting morale") is the target structure for §7 Storytelling. It shows consequence of ongoing action.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Speed round: I describe a bad boss behavior — you give me the PPC sentence in 8 seconds. 'Boss keeps changing the deadline.' Go."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In S5 — Imposter Syndrome — you use ALL of this. PPC to describe the feeling. Vocabulary to name it. The response strategies from today to talk about what you're doing about it. S4 is the foundation."
§ 05
Reading — The Moment I Spoke Up
A senior analyst describes the day she finally pushed back — and what happened
05
10–12 min
Mariana Vega
Senior Analyst · Grupo Ferrán · Monterrey
Personal Essay
Watch for: PPC (green) · authority / challenge / consequence / suggest · how Mariana frames her disagreement — professionally, not emotionally

For eight months, I had been watching the same mistake repeat itself. My director had been assigning projects based on relationships, not skill. The wrong people had been leading client presentations — and we had been losing clients because of it. I knew it. The team knew it. And we had all been staying silent.

The problem was authority. My director had been building his reputation in the company for fifteen years. Who was I to challenge him?

But after the third client cancellation in two months, I decided the consequence of staying silent was worse than the consequence of speaking up.

I didn't go into his office angry. I had been preparing for three days. I wrote down the data. I wrote down the outcomes. I prepared a specific suggestion — not just a complaint. And then I said: "I've been noticing a pattern in our project assignments that I think has been affecting our client retention. Could I share some data with you?"

He listened. He didn't agree immediately — in fact, he pushed back. But two weeks later, he changed the assignment process. He never told me he used my idea. But I could see it in the outcome.

What I learned: speaking up works better when you come with evidence, a suggestion, and a question — not with emotion and certainty. The approach matters as much as the decision to speak at all.

✏️ Comprehension
Answer based on the text.
What made Mariana decide to finally speak up?
What was Mariana's approach when she finally spoke to her director?
Mariana's director immediately agreed and thanked her for the feedback.
"I had been watching the same mistake repeat itself" — this PPC shows the pattern was ongoing for months before she acted.
Mariana's key lesson is that the way you raise an issue matters as much as the decision to raise it at all.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Key sentence for B1.2 analysis: "I've been noticing a pattern in our project assignments that I think has been affecting our client retention." Ask: "Why did she use PPC here? Why not 'I noticed a problem'?" → PPC signals ongoing observation (not one event) and professional precision.
  • Retell task: 90 seconds, no text. Student retells Mariana's story using PPC. Minimum 3 PPC sentences. This is the S7 Storytelling format — use this as a practice run.
  • Personal transfer: "Now — your story from §1. Tell it the way Mariana told hers: what had been happening, what you noticed, what you did." This is the §7 prompt.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Mariana prepared for three days. If you had three days to prepare to challenge a decision your boss has been making — what data would you collect? What would you say first?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Mariana's opening line is a template. 'I've been noticing [pattern] that I think has been affecting [outcome]. Could I share [data/suggestion] with you?' — memorize this. You will use it."

1:1 — Raising a Difficult Issue with a Manager
B1.2 · M1 · S4 · Dialogue · ~100 seconds · Natural pace · PPC + professional register

🔈 Audio placeholder. Replace with Google Drive embed.
ElevenLabs: Sarah + Michael · Rate: 0.87 · Stability: 0.60

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

Employee: I appreciate you giving me a few minutes. I've been wanting to raise something with you for a while, and I want to do it the right way.

Manager: Of course. What's going on?

Employee: I've been noticing that the deadline for the product launch has been moving forward — but the team's capacity hasn't changed. We've been working at full capacity for six weeks already. I think if we don't reconsider the timeline, we're going to see quality issues that will affect the client relationship.

Manager: I hear you. But the client has been pushing for this date since March. We've been committed to it.

Employee: I understand — and I'm not suggesting we go back on the commitment. I've been thinking about this, and I'd like to suggest a phased approach. We deliver the core features on the agreed date, and we complete the secondary features two weeks later. That way we're not breaking the deadline — we're managing the scope.

Manager: That's actually an interesting way to frame it. How long have you been thinking about this?

Employee: Honestly though — about three weeks. I wasn't sure how to bring it up. But the team has been struggling, and I felt I had to say something.

Manager: I'm glad you did. Let's put this on the agenda for tomorrow's project meeting. Can you prepare two slides?

Employee: Absolutely. I'll have them ready by 9 AM.

⏱ Target: 95–110 sec · PPC: I've been wanting / I've been noticing / has been moving / We've been working / has been pushing / We've been committed / I've been thinking / has been struggling · Rate: 0.87

✏️ Listening Activities
Listen and answer.
What is the employee's specific suggestion for solving the problem?
Why had the employee waited three weeks before raising the issue?
The employee comes to the meeting with a complaint only — no data and no suggestion.
"The team has been struggling" — this PPC signals the team's difficulty is ongoing, not a one-time incident.
The manager reacts negatively and dismisses the employee's suggestion.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Count PPC sentences before answering: 8 in ~100 seconds. Point out: the employee uses PPC throughout to describe what has been happening — this is what makes the argument sound professional rather than emotional.
  • Key phrase: "I've been noticing a [pattern] that has been affecting [outcome]" — this is Mariana's structure from §5, applied here. After listening: "Write this structure down. This is your template for speaking up professionally in English."
  • Roleplay: student plays the employee, teacher plays the manager — but using a REAL situation from the student's office. Push for PPC in every key statement.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "The employee says 'I've been wanting to raise this for a while.' Why do people wait? What has been stopping you from raising something important at work?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "This conversation — in English — changes outcomes. The employee left with a meeting on the agenda and a clear next step. That's the power of language used well. Today you have it."

🗣 Storytelling Challenge — ⏱ 2 minutes
Format: First-person story · Real situation · 4 parts · No notes
"Tell me about a time your boss made a decision you disagreed with — and what you did about it."
Context · 25 sec
What had been happening? What was the situation? Use PPC to describe the pattern you had been noticing.
The decision · 25 sec
What did your boss decide? Why did you disagree? Use vocabulary from today: authority, consequence, approach.
What you did · 35 sec
Did you stay silent, speak up, push back, or escalate? What happened? Be honest — even if you wish you had done something differently.
Lesson · 35 sec
What have you learned from this situation? What has it been changing about how you handle disagreement at work?

"I'm not gonna lie — this situation has been on my mind for a while. About six months ago, my manager decided to change our entire client reporting format — without asking the team. The new format was taking twice as long to complete and the clients had been confused by it for weeks. I disagreed with it, but I stayed silent at first. I thought: who am I to challenge this? But after two months of watching the team spend hours on something that wasn't working, I asked for a 1:1. I said: 'I've been noticing that the new format has been taking significantly more time than the previous one — and honestly though, I've been getting feedback from clients that they're confused by it. Could I suggest we test both formats and compare the results?' He agreed. We ran the test. The original format won. He never said 'you were right' — fair enough, that's not his style. But the team went back to the original format the following month. What I've learned: you need data, you need a suggestion, and you need to frame it as a question — not a complaint."
