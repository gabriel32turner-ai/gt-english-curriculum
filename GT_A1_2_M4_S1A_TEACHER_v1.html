⚡ Teacher Note — Running This Section
Students discuss in pairs. Each round has a photo for visual context. The teacher monitors and notes vocabulary gaps. After Round 2, call on 2–3 students to share. The model answer (reveal button) shows the target language level — do NOT show before students attempt.
Round 1 — Context
Imagine it's Monday morning. You wake up and feel terrible. You have a headache, a sore throat, and a slight fever. What do you do? Do you go to work or stay home?
💡 Use: "I think I should… / I have a… / My [body part] hurts"
Round 2 — Communication
You decide to call your manager. What do you say? What information does your manager need? Try to say it in English — what are the key phrases?
💡 Key info: your name, your symptom, that you won't come in, what you'll send (certificate)
Round 3 — Advice
Your colleague tells you: "I have a terrible back pain from sitting all day. My neck also hurts." What advice do you give? What should they do?
💡 Try: "You should rest / drink water / see a doctor / take a painkiller / stretch"
Model — calling your manager:
"Hi Daniel, this is Mónica. I'm not feeling well today. I have a bad headache and a sore throat. My temperature is 38 degrees. I won't be able to come in today. I'll send my medical certificate to HR this afternoon. I'm sorry for the inconvenience. I'll be back tomorrow if I feel better."

Model — giving advice:
"You should take a painkiller. You should also rest for ten minutes every hour. You shouldn't sit in the same position all day — you should stand up and stretch. You should see a doctor if the pain continues."

§4
Colloquial — Real Phrases for Health Situations
Natural everyday English · What people really say when they're not well at work
⏱ 5 MIN
"I'm not feeling well."
The standard way to say you are sick. More polite than "I'm sick" in a professional context.
"Hi Ana, I'm not feeling well today. I won't be in the office."
🕐 When: calling your manager, texting your team, writing to HR
"Get well soon!"
What you say to a colleague who is sick. Warm and professional. Very common in WhatsApp messages.
"Sorry to hear you're sick! Get well soon. We'll cover for you today."
🕐 When: a colleague reports a sick day to the group chat
"Take care."
Short, warm closing phrase. Works as goodbye, sign-off in an email, or as advice to someone unwell.
"I'll see you tomorrow. Take care!"
🕐 When: ending a call, closing a message, saying goodbye to a sick colleague
"Rest today."
Direct, caring imperative. Natural English — no article needed. Very common in informal advice.
"Don't worry about the report. Rest today and come back tomorrow."
🕐 When: giving quick advice to a sick colleague or team member
"I need a sick day."
Direct way to request a day off because you are not well. Used with managers and HR — clear and professional.
"Daniel, I need a sick day today. I have a fever and a cough."
🕐 When: making a request to your manager at the start of the day
💡 Teacher Note — Drill Tip
Drill these as chunks, not word-by-word. "Get well soon" — clap the rhythm: GET well SOON (3 beats). "I'm not feeling well" — 5 syllables with stress on FEEL. Have students repeat after you with the natural rhythm. Then: one student says a symptom, partner responds with one of the 5 phrases.
§5
Grammar — have/has · My [Body Part] Hurts · Should (chunk)
The three tools for talking about health at work · Problem → Tool format
⏱ 12–15 MIN
🚨 Real Situation
It's Monday morning. Your manager calls you: "Are you coming in today?"
You feel terrible. You want to say:
"Tengo un dolor de cabeza muy fuerte. Me duele la espalda. No me siento bien. Deberías dejarme faltar hoy."
❓ How do you say this in English? What structures do you need?
✅ The Tools
Tool 1: I/She/He have/has + [symptom] → "I have a bad headache."
Tool 2: My [body part] hurts / aches → "My back hurts."
Tool 3: You should + base verb → "You should rest today." (fixed chunk at A1.2)

⚠️ Common errors — correct immediately:
✗ "I have headache" → ✅ I have a headache (article needed!)
✗ "She have a fever" → ✅ She has a fever (3rd person = has)
✗ "My back it hurts" → ✅ My back hurts (no "it" — subject is "my back")
✗ "You should to rest" → ✅ You should rest (no "to" after should)

🗣️ Micro-Speaking
Tell your partner: "What's one health problem you have at work? Say the full sentence using 'I have' or 'My [body part] hurts.'"
Model: "I often have eye strain. My eyes ache after long meetings on Teams."
✏️ Activity A — Choose the Correct Form
1. Mónica _____ a terrible headache this morning — she can't focus on the report.
2. I _____ a cold and a sore throat. I can't come to the office today.
3. My _____ hurts from sitting in front of the computer for eight hours.
4. You should _____ today. Don't come to work with a fever.
5. "How's Carlos?" — "He's not well. He _____ a bad cough and a slight fever."
✏️ Activity B — True or False?
We say "I have a headache" with the article "a." You cannot say "I have headache."
"She have a fever" is correct because "fever" is singular.
"My back it hurts" is incorrect — you cannot use "it" when "my back" is the subject.
"You should to rest" is correct — you always need "to" after "should."
"My eyes ache" and "My eyes hurt" are both correct and mean the same thing.

⚡ Teacher Note — Listening
First listen: students listen for the main idea only — Why is Mónica calling? Second listen: students write the 3 symptoms. Third listen (optional): students note what Mónica promises to send. This 3-pass approach builds listening stamina at A1.2 level.
Mónica Reports a Sick Day — Call to Manager
A1.2 · M4 · S1A · Voice: Sofia (Mx English) · Speed: 0.80
[Daniel speaking]: Good morning, Mónica. Are you coming in today?

[Mónica speaking]: Hi Daniel, good morning. I'm sorry, but I'm not feeling well today. I have a bad headache and a sore throat. My back also hurts — I think I have a cold.

[Daniel]: Oh no! How is your temperature?

[Mónica]: It's 38.2. I have a slight fever too. I don't think I should come in today.

[Daniel]: Of course not. You should stay home and rest. Don't worry about your work.

[Mónica]: Thank you. I'll send my medical certificate to HR this afternoon. I have a doctor's appointment at eleven.

[Daniel]: Perfect. Take a painkiller and drink lots of water. Get well soon, Mónica!

[Mónica]: Thank you so much, Daniel. Take care. Bye!

§SP
Speaking Practice — Health Reports at Work
Real situations · Symptoms + advice + sick day communication · Pair activity
⏱ 10–12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Students answer using the target structures. Partner listens and marks: (1) missing article ("have headache" instead of "have a headache"), (2) "she/he have" instead of "has". Teacher corrects after each student, not during. Switch pairs after Q3.
Question 1 — Symptoms
Tell your partner 3 symptoms you have right now — or 3 symptoms you often have at work. Use "I have…" for each one. Add the intensity (slight / bad / terrible).
💡 "I have a slight headache right now. I also have eye strain — I've been on the computer all morning."
Question 2 — Giving Advice
Your partner tells you a health problem. Give them 3 pieces of advice using "You should…" and "You shouldn't…". Be specific — not just "rest" but "You should take a painkiller and drink 2 litres of water."
💡 Use: "You should see a doctor / take a day off / rest / drink water / stretch every hour"
Question 3 — Sick Day Call (Role Play)
Partner A: you are the manager. Partner B: you are the sick employee. B calls A to report a sick day. Include: your symptoms (at least 2), your temperature, what you will send to HR, and when you plan to return. Then switch roles.
💡 Model opening: "Hi [name], this is [your name]. I'm not feeling well today. I have…"
Question 4 — Team Update
You are the team leader. Tell the group: your colleague Carlos is sick today. Describe his 3 symptoms using "He has…" and "His [body part] hurts." Then give 2 pieces of advice you gave him.
💡 "Carlos is not in today. He has a fever and a bad cough. His throat hurts too. I told him he should…"
Question 5 — Workplace Wellness
What is the most common health problem in your department? Why does it happen? What should your company do about it? Give your honest opinion in English.
💡 "In my department, many people have… because… I think the company should…"
Question 6 — 60-Second Challenge ⭐
You are Mónica Herrera. In 60 seconds, give a complete sick day report: 3 symptoms with intensity, what your doctor said, what you'll send to HR, and when you'll return. No notes.
💡 Model opening: "Hi, this is Mónica. I'm not feeling well today. I have a terrible headache and…"

3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
Real phrases for real situations · Use them this week at work
PHRASE 1
"I'm not feeling well. I have a [symptom]."
Use this tomorrow if you feel sick — to your manager, in a WhatsApp message, or in an email to HR. Professional, clear, and 100% correct English. Replace the brackets with your real symptom: headache / fever / sore throat.
PHRASE 2
"You should rest. You shouldn't come in today."
Use this when a colleague tells you they are sick. It sounds caring, natural, and it uses the correct grammar structure. You can swap "rest" for any advice: take a painkiller / see a doctor / drink water.
PHRASE 3
"I'll send my medical certificate to HR."
This is the professional closing phrase for any sick day communication. It shows you know the process and handle things correctly in English. Your HR team and manager will immediately understand — no confusion.

⏱ 5–8 MIN
01
have / has + symptom. "I have a headache." "She has a fever." The article "a" is essential. Third person always takes "has" — never "have."
02
My [body part] hurts / aches. "My back hurts." Never add "it" — "my back" is already the subject. Use hurts or aches — both are correct.
03
You should + base verb. No "to." No -ing. "You should rest." — not "You should to rest." This chunk works for all advice at A1.2.
