§2
Debate — Plan A vs Plan B
Two sides · Two project approaches · Use "going to" to argue your case
⏱ 10 MIN
Scenario: Solaris Manufacturing needs to reduce costs by 15% next quarter. The management team has two competing plans. You are assigned one side — defend it using "going to."
Side A — Automate
"We're going to invest in automation. We're going to reduce manual processes. We're not going to lay off anyone — we're going to retrain the team."
  • "We're going to save money by investing in technology."
  • "The team is going to learn new skills."
  • "In 6 months, we're going to see results."
Side B — Cut Costs
"We're going to reduce the budget in three areas. We're going to renegotiate with suppliers. We're not going to make any big investments right now."
  • "We're going to save 15% without spending more money."
  • "We're going to focus on what we already have."
  • "We're not going to take unnecessary risks."
🎭 Engagement: Assign sides randomly. 3 minutes per side. Encourage students to respond to each other's points: "You said you're going to invest in technology, but we think we're going to save more by..."

💡 Pedagogical: This activity forces the negative form under pressure. Students must transform affirmative "going to" into negative. Watch for: "not going to" vs "isn't/aren't going to" — both are acceptable at A2.1.

§6
Source Analysis — Going To in the Wild
4 real-world sources · Find and analyze "going to" in authentic professional texts
⏱ 8 MIN
📧 Email — Project Update
Hi team, quick update: we're going to move the deadline to March 28th. I'm going to send the revised timeline by end of day. The client isn't going to be happy, but we need more time. — Valeria
🤔 How many "going to" uses can you find? Which one is negative? Why does Valeria say "isn't going to be happy" instead of "won't be happy"?
💬 WhatsApp — Team Chat
Daniel: Are you going to finish the proposal today?
Valeria: Yes, I am. I'm going to send it by 5 PM.
Daniel: Great. I'm not going to be in the office tomorrow, so send it to my email.
Valeria: Got it. We're good to go 👍
🤔 Notice: Daniel uses a short answer "I'm not going to be in the office" — is this a plan or a schedule? How do you know?
📋 Document — Meeting Minutes
Action items from Q1 planning meeting:
• Valeria is going to contact 3 new suppliers by March 10
• Operations team is going to implement new inventory system in April
• Marketing is NOT going to launch the campaign until the budget is approved
🤔 These are action items from a meeting. Why is "going to" the right choice here, and not Simple Present or "will"?
📰 Headline — Industry News
"Solaris Manufacturing is going to expand operations to a new plant in León, Guanajuato. The company is going to hire 200 new employees in Q2."
🤔 This is a news article. The company already decided — that's why it uses "going to." What would change if they used "will" instead? Would the meaning be different?
💡 Pedagogical: The metacognitive questions are the real learning here. Students need to understand WHY "going to" is chosen — because the plan already exists in someone's head. This distinction will be critical in M5 when "will" is introduced.

🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"I'm going to send it by [day]."
The most useful sentence in corporate English. Use in standups, WhatsApp, email. Replaces "I send it tomorrow" with professional future English.
PHRASE 2
"We're not going to postpone this — it's a priority."
Shows decisiveness in English. Use when your team needs to move fast. Combines negative "going to" with a clear reason.
PHRASE 3
"What are you going to do about [the problem]?"
The manager's question. Whether you ask it or answer it, this is the most important "going to" question in business. Practice both sides.
