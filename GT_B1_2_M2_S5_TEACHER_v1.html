Speaking Topic — Financial Freedom
⏱ 12 min · 3 rounds
📋 Logistics

3 rounds, 3–4 min each. This topic touches real financial anxiety in corporate professionals. Handle with energy, not sensitivity — it generates the best speaking at B1.2. Push for RS: "Someone told me…" / "I was told…" throughout.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Round 1: definition + personal meaning → push for specifics, not generalities
  • Round 2: what others say → RS gateway ("People say that… / My parents told me…")
  • Round 3: realistic plan → opinion + colloquial phrases from M2
💡 Improvement: RS Bridge Questions

After each round, teacher asks: "What did your partner say?" to another student → forces RS production without announcing it as grammar. Natural and effective.

💰
Financial Freedom — What Does It Mean to You?
Round 1 — Your Definition
What does financial freedom mean to you personally? Is it about money, time, choices — or something else?
Round 2 — What Others Say (Reported Speech)
What have people around you said about money and freedom? Report their opinions: "My father told me that…" / "A friend said…" / "My boss mentioned…"
Round 3 — Your Plan
Is financial freedom realistic for you? What is your plan — or why don't you have one? Be honest.

For me, financial freedom means not having to worry about next month. It is not about being rich — it is about having enough savings and discipline to make choices without fear.

My father told me that the most important thing was to eliminate debt first. He said he had never felt free until he paid off the house. A friend of mine mentioned she was thinking about investing, but she said it still felt sketchy to her. No way she would put her savings in crypto.

My plan? I budget every month. I track everything. It adds up — small decisions matter. Is it realistic? I think so, but it is not gonna happen overnight. You need discipline and independence from lifestyle pressure.

Colloquial Phrases — M2 Review
⏱ 10 min · 5 key phrases
📋 Logistics

5 phrases from M2 program. Student explains in English. Each example integrates RS and M2 vocabulary. Push for own sentence. These phrases are what separate B1 from B1.2 — natural sounding professional English.

🎯 Improvement: RS + Colloquial Combo

Ask students to chain a colloquial phrase inside RS: "He told me it was sketchy and that it was not gonna happen." This is how natural speakers embed informal language inside reported speech — high-value B1.2 production.

💡 Extension

Role play: Student A makes a financial statement. Student B reports it to Student C using RS + colloquial. Triangle dynamic — maximum practice, zero gamification.

No way.
Expressing shock or disbelief — strong reaction
"He earns that much? No way. He told me he was earning much less. I feel like he wasn't being honest."
That's sketchy.
Something feels dishonest or suspicious
"She mentioned a friend had doubled his money in two weeks. I said that's sketchy — sounds too good to be true."
I'm broke.
Informal for having no money right now
"I can't go to the conference this year. I'm broke. My partner said we had spent too much on the renovation."
It adds up.
Small amounts accumulate into something significant
"One coffee a day seems fine, right? But it adds up. He told me he had saved $15,000 in a year just by tracking small expenses."
Not gonna happen.
Something is impossible or very unlikely
"A raise this quarter? Not gonna happen. My manager said the budget was already closed. She mentioned maybe next year."

Grammar — Reported Speech: Tense Shift
⏱ 10 min · Reference + Poll + RS Transformer + Activity
📋 Logistics

REFERENTIAL. Tense shift table visible. Poll: 3 questions, 10 pts each. RS Transformer: 3 direct→reported conversions (improvement activity). Activity B: choose the correct reporting verb. Badge 🎯 at combined 4+/6.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus

The #1 error: "I am" → he said he was. Students keep the present tense inside RS — this is the persistent B1.2 error. The RS Transformer activity forces them to do the shift actively, not just recognize it.

💡 GT Originals from Program
  • ⚠️ "I am" → he said he WAS — most common RS error globally
  • 🎯 Side hustles topic generates best RS practice (real frustration = real speech)
  • 💰 Salary negotiation RS: "I've already received one offer" → She said she had already received one offer
  • 📱 Crypto opinions: "I would never invest" → He told me he would never invest — natural RS practice

REPORTED SPEECH — TENSE SHIFT REFERENCE

Direct Speech→Reported SpeechExample
"I am thinking…"→she said she was…She said she was thinking about investing.
"I will quit…"→he said he would…He told me he would never go back to a 9-to-5.
"I have done…"→they said they had done…They mentioned they had been working on it for months.
"Are you happy?"→she asked if I was…She asked me if I was happy with my salary.
"I can do it."→he said he could…He said he could manage both jobs at the same time.

Listening — The Money Talk
⏱ 12 min · Audio + MC + T/F + Speaking Response
📋 Logistics

ElevenLabs placeholder — professional female voice, Mexican accent, 85% speed, ~220 words. Scenario: woman describing a conversation she had with a friend about financial decisions. Heavy RS throughout. 2 MC + 3 T/F. Play once gist, twice detail.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Target: recognize RS tense shifts in natural speech (said/told/mentioned/asked)
  • Script has 4 reporting verbs and both statement + question RS
  • Speaking Response: "Report a real money conversation you had this week"
💡 Script Design — Improvement

Script deliberately mixes RS with M2 colloquial phrases ("sketchy," "adds up," "broke"). This models natural integration — B1.2 students hear how informal language works inside reported structures.

Andrea — Financial Planner · Querétaro
Sharing a conversation she had with her friend Laura
"She told me she had never thought about retirement. I said that scared me."

So, I had coffee with my friend Laura last week, and we ended up having this big conversation about money. She told me she was thinking about investing but she said everything online looked sketchy to her.

I asked her if she had a monthly budget. She said no — she mentioned she had never really tracked her expenses. I told her it adds up — even small things like subscriptions and coffees.

Then she asked me if I was happy with my salary. I said I wasn't, but that I had started a side hustle last year to build extra income. She said she would love to do that but she was too tired after work. Not gonna happen, she said.

The thing that surprised me most — she told me she had never thought about retirement. No way, I thought. She is 38. I told her it was never too late to start, but she needed discipline and a plan. She said she would think about it. I hope she does.
