📋 Logistics

Cold production. No review first — students answer from memory. 4 cards, 90 seconds per card. Partner marks: (1) any "will" in the if-clause, (2) vocabulary words from M3 used or missed. Badge ⚡ after completing all 4 prompts.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus

This session consolidates ALL of M3: money habits (S1), online shopping (S2), in-store vs online (S3), financial goals (S4), and spending psychology (S5). Each warm-up prompt pulls from a different session so the teacher can immediately identify which session's content needs most attention today.

💡 Run It Fast

No correction during warm-up — collect errors on the board. Use them as live material in §4 Grammar. The exit task in §7 should be noticeably better than these first answers. That contrast IS the learning.

Colloquial Phrases — M3 Review
⏱ 8 min · 5 key phrases from all sessions
📋 Logistics

5 phrases across M3 S1–S4. For each: (1) student reads the phrase aloud, (2) explains the meaning in English without translating, (3) makes one original sentence using it. 8 minutes total — 90 seconds per phrase maximum.

🎯 High-ROI Phrases

These 5 phrases separate A2 from B1 in corporate speech. "It's easy to lose track" sounds polished in a budget review. "I try to wait 24 hours" shows discipline. "My relationship with money has changed" is MBA-level self-reflection.

💡 Chain Challenge

Extra challenge: student connects any 3 phrases into a single continuous response. Example: "My relationship with money has changed — I used to buy without thinking, but now I try to wait 24 hours. When I don't, it's easy to lose track."

"I'm more careful with money now."
Describes a change in attitude toward spending — shows personal growth and financial maturity
"I'm more careful with money now. If I see something I want, I check my budget first. I used to buy immediately."
"It's easy to lose track."
You stop monitoring something closely — most common with money, time, and subscriptions
"With online shopping, it's easy to lose track of what you spend. If I don't review my card statement, I'll never know where the money went."
"I try to wait 24 hours before buying."
A discipline strategy against impulse buying — shows control and planning
"I try to wait 24 hours before buying anything non-essential. If I still want it the next day, I'll buy it. If not, I'll forget about it — and I usually do."
"I'm saving for…"
Natural way to state a financial goal — more conversational and personal than "My objective is to..."
"I'm saving for an apartment. If I stay consistent, I'll have the down payment in three years. It keeps me from spending on things I don't need."
"My relationship with money has changed."
Reflective phrase about financial mindset evolution — sounds mature and self-aware in any professional context
"My relationship with money has changed a lot in the last two years. If I build on this now, I'll be in a very different place at 40."

Grammar — First Conditional: Full Review
⏱ 12 min · Reference table · Poll · Activity A · Error Correction Wall
📋 Logistics

REFERENTIAL. Table always visible. Poll: 3 questions, 10 pts each. Activity A: 3 MC items. Error Correction Wall: 4 real-error cards — student identifies and corrects. Badge 🎯 at 5+/6 combined score on A + Error Wall. Uses errors collected on the board in §1.

🎯 Key Points — M3 First Conditional
  • RULE 1: Never "will" in the if-clause — "If I get a raise" NOT "if I will get"
  • RULE 2: Result clause = will + base verb or won't + base verb
  • RULE 3: Order is flexible — result first: "I'll save more if I get a raise."
  • RULE 4: Negative if-clause: "If I don't save now, I won't have anything later."
💡 GT Original

"If I will save" is the single most persistent error at B1.1. It appears because Spanish "si ahorro" feels future to students, but English maps it to Present Simple. One clear rule: the if-clause is the condition (present state), not the future result. "If it rains" doesn't mean it's raining — it means IF the rain comes, THEN…

FIRST CONDITIONAL — REFERENCE TABLE

FormIf-clauseResult clauseExample
AffirmativeIf + Present Simplewill + base verb"If I save $2,000 this month, I'll reach my goal."
Negative ifIf + don't/doesn't + basewon't + base verb"If I don't track my expenses, I won't know where my money goes."
Negative resultIf + Present Simplewon't + base verb"If I invest early, I won't have to worry about my pension."
Reversed order—Result first, then if-clause"I'll buy an apartment if I save the down payment by 2027."
❌ NEVER"If I will get a raise…"—NO "will" in the if-clause — ever.

I've been thinking a lot this week about my relationship with money. Honestly? It's changed more in these past few sessions than in the last five years.

Let me tell you what I've learned. First: if I don't track my spending, I'll never understand where my money goes. I used to just check my bank balance and hope for the best. That doesn't work.

Second: online shopping. I've returned so many things I bought impulsively. Now I use a simple rule — if I still want it after 24 hours, I'll buy it. If I forget about it, I didn't really need it. It sounds obvious. But most of us don't do it.

Third: in-store versus online. If I'm buying clothes or shoes, I'll always go to the store. Touch and feel still matters for some things. But for electronics or books? Online is faster and cheaper. I've learned to choose the right channel for the right product.

Fourth — and this is the big one — financial goals. I now have a specific ladder. If I complete my emergency fund by March, I'll start investing immediately. If I invest consistently for three years, I'll have the down payment for an apartment. Step by step. It's not complicated — it just requires patience and discipline.

My advice? If you start thinking about your financial future today — even in small ways — you'll be in a completely different place in five years. That's not motivational talk. That's math.
