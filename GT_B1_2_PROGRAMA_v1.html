Who is this program for?

  • Students who completed B1.1 at Gabriel Turner English Training
  • Professionals who can communicate in English but want to sound more natural and confident
  • Adults who are tired of "textbook English" and want to speak like real people do
  • Corporate employees who want to lead discussions, express opinions, and debate in meetings

By the end of B1.2, students will be able to:

  • Speak independently on complex, real-world topics without preparation
  • Use Present Perfect Continuous, Reported Speech, Passive, Second Conditional, and Modal Deduction naturally
  • Use 5+ colloquial phrases per module in real conversations
  • Understand challenging, fast-paced audio with two speakers at natural pace
  • Express and defend opinions with fluency, nuance, and personality
  • Transition confidently to B2 level study

Colloquial Phrases — Module 1
  • "I'm not gonna lie"
    Used before an honest or surprising opinion: "I'm not gonna lie, I was really intimidated."
  • "That's such a mood"
    Agreeing because something resonates: "He hates Mondays. That's such a mood."
  • "Fair enough"
    Accepting something you may not love: "She needs more time. Fair enough."
  • "It's a bit much"
    Something excessive: "Three meetings before 9 AM? That's a bit much."
  • "Honestly though"
    Adding weight to a sincere opinion: "Honestly though, I think he was right."
Functional English
  • To be honest with you, I don't think that's fair.
  • I see where you're coming from, but I disagree.
  • That's a good point — I hadn't thought of it that way.
  • I've been dealing with this for a while, to be honest.
  • Have you noticed how different people handle pressure?
  • I really struggle with that, honestly.
  • I think the bigger issue here is confidence.
  • It's not as simple as it looks from the outside.
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🗣️ Always use click-to-reveal AFTER the student speaks — never before. The model answer is a target, not a script to copy
  • 💼 S5 imposter syndrome: this topic is deeply personal for professionals — very high engagement. Give students a minute to think silently before speaking
  • ⚠️ PPC error: "I'm working here for 5 years" → I've been working here for 5 years. Correct immediately and naturally in context
  • 🔥 S2 gossip: frame the question as a debate — half the class defends it, half attacks it. Creates natural argument language
  • 🎯 S4 (boss is wrong): this generates the most authentic, unscripted speaking of the module — let it run
Exit Task M1
2-minute unscripted talk: Describe a workplace situation you've been dealing with lately. Use Present Perfect Continuous at least twice and at least 2 colloquial phrases from this module. CEFR rubric: fluency · vocabulary range · naturalness · grammar in context.

Colloquial Phrases — Module 2
  • "No way"
    Expressing shock or disbelief: "He earns that much? No way."
  • "That's sketchy"
    Something feels dishonest or suspicious: "That investment scheme sounds sketchy to me."
  • "I'm broke"
    Informal for having no money: "I can't go this weekend — I'm broke."
  • "It adds up"
    Small amounts accumulate: "One coffee a day seems fine, but it adds up."
  • "Not gonna happen"
    Something is impossible or very unlikely: "A raise this quarter? Not gonna happen."
Functional English
  • She said she was seriously thinking about quitting her job.
  • He told me he hadn't discussed his salary with anyone.
  • They mentioned they were planning to launch something new.
  • She said it wasn't worth the risk for her.
  • He told me he'd never go back to working for someone else.
  • I was told the project would take at least six months.
  • She asked me if I was happy with what I was earning.
  • They said they had been working on it for over a year.
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 💰 S2 salary transparency: this is genuinely taboo in Mexico — expect strong opinions and real emotional investment. One of the best debates of the program
  • 📱 S1 crypto: most students have an opinion. Ask who has actually invested and who hasn't — creates an instant debate with real stakes
  • ⚠️ Reported Speech tense shift: "I am" → he said he WAS is the most common error. Drill this contrast naturally through the speaking activities
  • 🎯 S3 side hustles: for corporate students, this often touches real frustration about salary. Handle with energy — it generates the best Reported Speech practice
  • 🏷️ S4 luxury brands: bring 3 branded items to class (real or printed). Ask students to justify or attack the price. Works brilliantly

GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🤖 S1 AI jobs: ask students to name 3 jobs they think will disappear in 10 years. Generates instant, motivated discussion and perfect passive structures

Colloquial Phrases — Module 4
  • "It's a red flag"
    A warning sign of a real problem: "He got angry when I set a boundary. That's a red flag."
  • "That's messed up"
    Something unfair or shocking: "They fired her without warning? That's messed up."
  • "I called it"
    Claiming you predicted something correctly: "I told you this would happen. I called it."
  • "At the end of the day"
    What ultimately matters most: "At the end of the day, it's your decision."
  • "That hit different"
    Something had a strong, unexpected emotional impact: "That conversation hit different after what happened."
Functional English
  • If I were the manager, I'd make mental health a real priority.
  • What would you do if you had to report your own boss?
  • If the culture were healthier, there would be less turnover.
  • I'd speak up even if it made things uncomfortable.
  • Even though it's awkward, we need to talk about this.
  • What if the rules were different for everyone in the room?
  • If I could change one thing about society, it would be…
  • I'd want more honesty, even if the truth is hard to hear.
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🧠 S1 mental health: normalize the topic from the start — use it in the context of work performance, not personal crisis. Students open up more this way
  • 🔥 S2 gender roles: this is the most debate-friendly session of the module. Create two sides and let students switch positions halfway through
  • 😴 S3 quiet quitting: most corporate students have done it or seen it. Ask them to describe without using the words "quiet" or "quitting" — forces real vocabulary use
  • ⚠️ Second Conditional error: "If I would be the boss" → If I were the boss. Very common — correct it consistently and warmly
  • 🌍 S4 immigration: frame carefully — ask "What would you need if you moved to another country?" before the debate. Builds empathy before opinion

Colloquial Phrases — Module 5
  • "That's so extra"
    Overdramatic or over the top: "He cried during the interview. That's so extra."
  • "They're clout-chasing"
    Seeking attention and online fame: "She only posted that to clout-chase."
  • "It went viral"
    Spread rapidly and massively online: "The clip went viral within hours."
  • "Shade was thrown"
    Indirect criticism or subtle disrespect: "She didn't mention his name once. Shade was definitely thrown."
  • "No cap"
    Informal for "no lie, I'm being serious": "That was the best finale I've ever seen, no cap."
Functional English
  • She must be lying — the story has too many holes.
  • He can't have known about this in advance.
  • They might be hiding something — it just doesn't add up.
  • It must be exhausting to live that publicly every day.
  • That can't be the whole story. There's something we don't know.
  • She might not even write her own content.
  • They must have an entire team managing all of this.
  • He can't be that different in real life from his image.
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 📸 S1 influencers: ask students to name one influencer they follow and explain why. Immediate engagement and authentic vocabulary in context
  • 📺 S2 reality TV: ask who watches it (most will say no) and then who actually does. The gap between what people say and do is the speaking topic
  • 🧠 S3 fake news: bring 3 headlines to class — one real, one partially fake, one completely invented. Students debate which is which. Excellent critical thinking + deduction modal practice
  • ⚠️ Modal error: "He must to be lying" → He must be lying. No "to" after modal verbs. Correct immediately and move on
  • 🎬 S5 pitch session: give students the speaking topic at end of S4 so they can prepare. One of the most memorable sessions of the level — they remember this
Exit Task M5
Choose a public figure, trend, or news story from this week. Talk about it for 2 minutes using modal deduction language: "they must be / they can't have / they might be." Use at least 2 colloquial phrases. No notes. Deduction, opinion, and natural spoken English required.

Linking Words & Discourse Markers
"Even though I failed, I learned something important."
"Despite working hard, she didn't get the promotion."

Colloquial Phrases — Module 6
  • "It's a game-changer"
    Something that completely transforms a situation: "That promotion was a real game-changer for me."
  • "I'm figuring it out"
    In the process of finding direction: "I don't have all the answers yet — I'm figuring it out."
  • "That's the goal"
    What you're aiming for: "Own my own business by 40? That's the goal."
  • "I've come a long way"
    Reflecting on real personal progress: "My English used to be terrible. I've come a long way."
  • "No looking back"
    Committed to moving forward without regret: "I left that job and I haven't looked back."
Functional English
  • Even though I didn't get the job, I learned something valuable.
  • Despite working hard for years, I didn't get the recognition I expected.
  • As a result of that failure, I decided to change direction.
  • Although it was difficult, it was absolutely the right decision.
  • I've been working towards this for longer than I can remember.
  • Whereas before I was afraid to speak, now I actually enjoy it.
  • Because of that experience, I see things completely differently now.
  • In spite of the challenges, I'm genuinely proud of where I am.
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🎓 S1 university: for corporate students already working, this hits differently — many paid for degrees they "don't use." Very personal and motivating

  • 💪 S3 failure: the most emotionally honest session of the level. Model vulnerability first — share your own example briefly before asking students to share theirs
  • ⚠️ Linking word error: "Despite she worked hard" → Despite working hard (no subject after despite). Very common — correct consistently
  • 🎯 S5 CAPSTONE: give the topic at end of S4. Record if possible. Use the full CEFR rubric. This is the final proof of fluency — celebrate it
