Warm-Up Scenario
Speaking activation · 5 min · Teacher-led Q&A
📍 Context — Monday Morning
It's 8:30 AM. You're at your desk. It's Monday morning. Your computer is on. Your coffee is ready. There are some new emails in your inbox.

Teacher: Point to the student and ask naturally — no reading, no preparing.
Q1 · Opener
"What time do you usually start work? Is it earlier or later than 8:30?"
Q2 · Habit
"What do you do first when you arrive at work — check your email, talk to a colleague, or something else?"
Q3 · Workplace
"How many people do you work with every day? Are there many meetings?"
Q4 · English at Work
"Can you do anything in English at work right now — read emails, write reports, speak in meetings?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • DO NOT pre-teach vocabulary. Let the student attempt real language first — errors here are diagnostic data, not failures.
  • If the student says "I start to work" or "I go to job" — note it, don't correct yet. Correct form will emerge in §2.
  • Listen for: missing 3rd person -s, use of present continuous for habitual actions ("I am checking my email every day"), L1 transfer (calcos).
  • This warm-up takes 5–7 minutes maximum. Don't let it run long — it's activation, not assessment.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Tell me about a typical Monday for you — step by step, from when you arrive to when you have lunch." (Open prompt if student is confident.)
  • For quieter students: use Q1–Q4 as scaffolding, one at a time. Accept short answers and build on them.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Connect warm-up errors directly to §2: "You used 'check' — let's look at that verb in more detail." This creates continuity.
  • End with: "After this session, you'll be able to describe everything you just told me — but in complete, professional English."

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Present each verb card: student reads the word aloud → IPA pronunciation check → reads the example → substitutes with their own real workplace sentence.
  • Drill 3rd person rule specifically: "The card says 'she starts'. Why not 'she start'? Show me the pattern — what changes?" Student must articulate the rule, not just repeat it.
  • Spelling alert: finish → finishes (not finishs). Ask: "What ending does 'finish' have? -sh. What does -sh take? + es."
  • Strips: frequency adverbs are in violet (same as lesson color) — this is intentional. Time expressions in gold. Ask student to make 3 combo sentences: [freq. adverb] + [verb] + [time expression].
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Which of these 8 verbs describes your job best? Pick your top 3." Student produces real sentences. You produce the contrasting 3rd-person sentence in response.
  • For frequency adverbs: "Give me a true sentence about your supervisor using 'usually'." Then flip: "Now with 'sometimes'." Changes the adverb, same structure.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Note which verb gives the student the most trouble (common: attend for L1-Spanish speakers who say "assistir"). Flag for revision in S1B.
  • Combine: "You start at 7 and she starts at 8. Who usually finishes first?" Forces comparison using simple present spontaneously.

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Flip cards: student reads the word and example sentence aloud, then substitutes their own real workplace details. "There is a meeting room on the 2nd floor in my office."
  • Spinner: student spins → gets verb + time → makes a TRUE sentence. Emphasize TRUE — invented sentences are not accepted. "I always check my messages at 7 AM because I start early."
  • Watch for: missing -s on 3rd person ("she check"), wrong adverb placement ("I check always"), "cans" for can.
  • If student struggles on spinner: "Use 'check' — it's the easiest. Say: I always check my email... at what time? Good. Now spin again."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Each spin = one real sentence about your actual life. No invented sentences — everything must be true about your real job."
  • If student gets 8 different verbs in 8 spins: "8 unique verbs, 8 true sentences. That's professional fluency — you just described your entire work routine."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Note: the spinner gives the student a combination they might not choose on their own. This creates productive discomfort — forced production with unfamiliar combos builds deeper encoding.
  • End of §3: "So far you've used 8 verbs and 8 locations. Next we look at the grammar rules underneath all of this." Bridge to §4.

Grammar Focus
Simple Present · There is/are · Can · 3 activities
🔷 Pattern 1 · Simple Present
I / You / We / They+VERB (base)→I start at 8 AM.
He / She / It+VERB + s / es→She starts at 9 AM.
He / She / It+doesn't+VERB (base)→He doesn't work on Sundays.
🔷 Pattern 2 · There is / There are
There is+singular noun→one kitchen / a parking lot
There are+plural noun→5 colleagues / 20 emails
🔷 Pattern 3 · Can / Can't
Subject+can+VERB (base)→She can read emails in English.
Subject+can't+VERB (base)→He can't write reports yet.
Simple Present — Spelling Rules
SubjectRuleExample
I / You / We / Theybase formI work in an office.
He / She / It+ -s (most verbs)She works in a factory.
He / She / It-sh/-ch/-x/-ss/-o + -esHe finishes at 6 PM.
He / She / Itconsonant + y → -iesShe studies English.
⚠️ Negative: I / You / She / He + don't / doesn't + base verb → "She doesn't attend meetings on Fridays."
There is / There are
TypeAffirmativeNegativeQuestion
SingularThere is a desk.There isn't a printer.Is there a meeting room?
PluralThere are two computers.There aren't any chairs.Are there any pens?
Can / Can't — Ability
AffirmativeNegativeQuestion
I can use the computer.I can't write reports in English.Can you help me?
She can attend meetings.He can't speak English yet.Can they send the file?
⚠️ Can NEVER changes with he/she/it — "She can" ✓   "She cans" ✗
⚠️ 5 Common A1.2 Errors — Know These
✗ "She check her email every morning."
✓ "She checks her email every morning."
→ 3rd person singular always adds -s/-es
✗ "I check always my email."
✓ "I always check my email."
→ Frequency adverb goes BEFORE the main verb
✗ "She cans help me."
✓ "She can help me."
→ Modal verbs (can, must, should) never change form
✗ "There is two computers in the office."
✓ "There are two computers in the office."
→ Use 'are' with plural nouns
✗ "My boss he starts work at 8 AM."
✓ "My boss starts work at 8 AM."
→ Don't repeat the subject with a pronoun

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Grammar tables: don't read them aloud — ask the student to read them and explain the rule back to you in their own words. "What does this table say? Explain it to me."
  • 5 errors box: show one at a time. Student identifies the problem before you explain it. "What is wrong with this sentence? Why?"
  • Activity 1 (MC): options are randomized. Don't reveal the answer before the student attempts. After wrong answer: "Which error from the box does this match?"
  • Activity 3 (Build): student must say the sentence aloud after building it correctly. Pronunciation counts.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Ana's routine in Activity 2 — does it sound similar to your routine? What's different?" Cross-link the reading to real experience.
  • For fast finishers after Activity 3: "Now make YOUR OWN sentence with the same structure as sentence 1. Use a real habit from your job."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • The 5 errors box is the most important reference in this section. Photograph it mentally — these 5 errors will reappear in S1B, S2A, and S2B revision stages.
  • If student makes Error 2 (adverb placement) in speaking: stop, don't correct immediately. Finish the thought, then: "You said 'I check always' — which rule does that break?" Student self-corrects.

Reading — Sofia's Workday
Simple present · Verbs highlighted · 2 MC + 4 T/F
Sofia
Marketing Company · León, Guanajuato
Team of 5 · Monday meetings · Takes the bus
📖 Story Character — M1
in a marketing company in León, Guanajuato. She starts work at 8:30 AM every day. First, she checks her email and reads the new messages. There are usually 10 or 15 new emails in the morning. She can read English emails, but she can't write long reports in English yet.

At 10:00 AM, she attends a team meeting. There is one meeting every Monday. She works with five colleagues. They always send their reports on Fridays. At 1:00 PM, Sofia leaves the office for lunch. She finishes work at 6:00 PM. There is a parking lot in the building, but she usually takes the bus.
Reading MC · 2 questions
1. What time does Sofia start work?
2. What can Sofia do in English at work?
Reading T/F · 4 statements
1. Sofia works in a factory.
2. There is one meeting every Monday.
3. Sofia works with 10 colleagues.
4. Sofia always drives to work.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • First pass: student reads silently. Second pass: student reads aloud — monitor pronunciation of highlighted verbs. "Attends" and "finishes" are common trouble spots.
  • Use color coding as a discussion tool: "Why is 'can't' in red? What does that color mean?" Student identifies negative function from context.
  • Gold highlights (time expressions): "Which time expression tells us frequency? Which tells us a specific clock time?" Student distinguishes between the two types.
  • After T/F: ask the student to CORRECT the false statements orally. "Sofia doesn't work in a factory. She works in a ___."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "What does Sofia's routine have in common with yours? What is different?" 2 minutes of spontaneous comparison. This is the most productive moment in §5.
  • If student reads fluently: ask them to retell Sofia's story from memory without looking at the text. Give 3 prompts: time / verb / location.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Sofia is a recurring character in M1 materials. Student will see her name again in S2A and S3A — continuity builds familiarity and reduces cognitive load.
  • Comprehension check language: resist using "Did you understand?" (yes/no). Instead: "Tell me one thing Sofia does in the morning. What can't she do in English?" Open questions reveal real comprehension.

Listening — Marco's Daily Routine
ElevenLabs · Sarah · 0.82x · ~80 sec · 2 MC + 3 T/F
Marco
Manufacturing Company · Bajío Region
Team of 8 · Daily morning meetings · Production floor
🎧 Listening Character — M1
🎧 Marco's Daily Routine
Manufacturing company · Bajío region · Simple present
📜 LISTENING SCRIPT (Teacher Mode only) My name is Marco. I work at a manufacturing company in the Bajío region. I start work at seven AM every day. First, I check the production line and talk to my team. There are eight people in my team. At nine AM, I attend a short meeting with my supervisor. There is one meeting every morning, from Monday to Friday. I can speak some English at work, but I can't write reports in English yet. At twelve-thirty PM, I leave the production floor for lunch. I usually eat in the company kitchen. There are fifteen tables in the kitchen — it is very big. I finish work at four-thirty PM and leave the factory.
Listening MC · 2 questions
1. What time does Marco start work?
2. How many people are in Marco's team?
Listening T/F · 3 statements
1. Marco works in an office.
2. He attends a meeting every morning.
3. Marco can write reports in English.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Pre-listening: "You will hear a man describe his work routine. Listen for: (1) what time he starts, (2) how many people are in his team, (3) what he can or can't do in English." Give purpose before playing.
  • First listening: no pausing — full audio. Student answers MC immediately after. Second listening: student can request pause at specific moments.
  • T/F statement 1 is a classic listening trap — Marco works at a manufacturing company, not an office. Test for careful listening vs. inference.
  • If ElevenLabs is unavailable: read the script yourself at natural pace. First time: read straight through. Second time: pause after each sentence for student to confirm understanding.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Compare Marco and Sofia — who works longer hours? Who has more people in their team?" Forces both listening recall and reading recall simultaneously.
  • "Now imagine you are Marco. Tell me your routine." Student retells Marco's routine in first person — high-value production activity.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Marco is in the Bajío region — same as the student. This localization is intentional: relatable content reduces the cognitive distance between input and real life.
  • Rate 0.82 is slightly slower than natural speech. By S3, Marco will appear again at 0.90. Listening pace is a progression variable across the module.

🔑 Key Summary — A1.2 · M1 · S1A
▸Simple present describes habits, routines, and facts. Use it for anything that happens regularly at work.
▸3rd person rule: add -s (works) or -es (finishes) for he / she / it. Never for I, you, we, they.
▸Frequency adverbs go BEFORE the main verb: "I always check my email." "She usually leaves at 6."
▸There is / There are: singular → is · plural → are. Question: Is there / Are there?
▸Can / can't: modal verb — never changes. "She can attend." NOT "She cans attend."

⭐ GT Originals — Teacher Recommendations
Extension A: "My colleague's routine." Student describes a colleague's routine using full 3rd person — 5 sentences minimum. Forces sustained 3rd person production outside of a controlled activity.
Extension B: "Before and after." Student makes two lists: what they can do in English NOW, and what they want to be able to do in English in 6 months. Links the grammar (can/can't) to real personal motivation.
Revision flag: If student struggled with frequency adverb placement, open S1B with a 2-minute review: "Yesterday you said 'I check always my email.' What's the correct version? Say it." Micro-revision at session start reinforces retention.
Cultural note: "Parking lot" (American English) is used throughout — consistent with GT brand. Do not switch to "car park" (British). Monitor for British spellings in student writing at higher levels.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Flashcard Wall: don't show both sides simultaneously. Student reads base form → produces she/he form aloud → flips to confirm. If wrong: return to the card 2 rounds later.

Capa 2 · Engagement
