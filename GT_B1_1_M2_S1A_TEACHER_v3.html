§ 02
Speaking Topic — Your Working Environment
8–10 min
M2 · S1 — Work & Environment
"Describe your ideal workplace.
Does your current one match it?"
This is the session's main speaking objective. Students will revisit this question after §3 vocabulary, §4 colloquial and §5 grammar. Their answer should improve each time.
Conversation Prompts — push for depth
Round 1 — current reality: Describe your current workplace. What's the atmosphere like? Is the space open or private? How do you communicate with colleagues?
Round 2 — ideal vs reality: What would your ideal workplace look like? What would you change first? Why? What does the research say about productivity and environment?
Round 3 — personal experience: Has the environment at your job changed recently? Have you ever worked somewhere with a really different atmosphere? Which one worked better for you?
B1.1 Model Answer — "Your Working Environment" · 2.5 min
"My current workplace is actually quite open plan — we don't have private offices. At first I didn't like it, but now I've already gotten used to it. The atmosphere is generally relaxed, which I appreciate. People collaborate easily because there are no physical barriers.

But if I'm honest, my ideal workspace would be a bit different. I think I'd prefer a hybrid layout — open areas for team work, and quiet spaces for focused tasks. Right now we haven't created any real quiet zones yet, which can be a problem when I need to concentrate.

What I really value in a workplace is the vibe — and I mean that beyond just the physical space. The morale of a team affects how productive you feel. I've worked in environments where the energy was really negative, and it affected my performance dramatically.

I think companies have recently started to understand that environment motivates people. Some have already invested a lot in ergonomics, natural light, plants, quiet rooms. It makes a real difference. I haven't seen all of that at my company yet, but we're getting there."
🏷 Colour key: green = S1 vocab in use · teal = Present Perfect (just/already/yet) · gold = colloquial phrase · Count: 8 PP uses, 5 S1 nouns, 3 S1 verbs, 2 colloquial phrases

  • Model Answer: NO la reveles todavía. El alumno solo la ve al final de §7 Production, después de intentar responder por sí mismo. El contraste es el aprendizaje.

§ 03
Vocabulary — 8 Nouns · 8 Verbs · Work Environment
10–12 min
B1.1 Rule: Every word is immediately usable in today's speaking topic. Read the definition. Say the example. Then use the word in your own sentence about YOUR workplace before moving on.
8 Nouns — Work Environment
environment/ɪnˈvaɪrənmənt/
the physical and social conditions in which you work — "A positive work environment improves performance."
atmosphere/ˈætməsfɪər/
the general mood or feeling in a place — "The atmosphere in our office is very relaxed."
culture/ˈkʌltʃər/
the values, behaviours and practices of a company — "We have a culture of open communication."
space/speɪs/
the physical area where you work — open / private / shared space — "I prefer a quiet, private space."
layout/ˈleɪaʊt/
the arrangement of furniture and areas in the office — "The open-plan layout encourages collaboration."
communication/kəˌmjuːnɪˈkeɪʃn/
how people share information and ideas at work — "Communication is the foundation of a healthy team."
morale/məˈrɑːl/
the confidence, enthusiasm and motivation of a group — "Low salaries have damaged team morale."
vibe/vaɪb/
(informal) the feeling or energy of a place or group — "The vibe at that startup is really creative and energetic."
8 Verbs — Work Environment
describe/dɪˈskraɪb/
to give a detailed account of something — "How would you describe the culture at your company?"
improve/ɪmˈpruːv/
to make or become better — "The new manager has already improved team communication."
create/kriˈeɪt/
to produce or design something — "We've created a more flexible working arrangement."
support/səˈpɔːt/
to help, encourage or assist someone — "Good managers support their team's development."
collaborate/kəˈlæbəreɪt/
to work together with others on a shared goal — "In an open office, it's easier to collaborate."
motivate/ˈməʊtɪveɪt/
to encourage someone to take action or perform well — "A positive environment motivates people naturally."
build/bɪld/
to develop something gradually over time — "Leaders must build trust before anything else."
design/dɪˈzaɪn/
to plan and create a space or system intentionally — "They've redesigned the office to support hybrid work."
⚡ Rapid personalisation: After each word, student finishes the sentence about their OWN workplace: "The atmosphere at my company is… / The layout of my office is… / What we still haven't created is…"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.1 vocabulary approach: NOT a list to memorise. Each word is a tool the student uses immediately. Read → example → student produces their own sentence. If they can't → that's the word to return to in §7.
  • "morale" — most Spanish speakers don't have this word. It's high-value at B1.1 — connects to management, HR, leadership conversations. Spend extra time here.
  • "vibe" — informal but essential at B1.1. Native speakers use it constantly. Normalise its use: "I know it sounds casual, but this word appears in business conversations all the time."
  • Pronunciation trap: "collaborate" — stress on 2nd syllable /kəˈlæbəreɪt/. Students often say "COLlaborate". Drill it immediately.
  • These 16 words appear in the model answer at §2. Student will recognise them when they read it at §7.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Each of these words is something you'll use today — not in an exercise, but in a real conversation about your actual workplace. That's the difference between B1.1 and any language app."
§ 04
Colloquial English — 3 Phrases for This Topic
5–7 min
These are phrases real English speakers use in everyday conversations about work. Say them aloud. Use them naturally. They make the difference between textbook English and real English.
"The atmosphere is really good."
A natural, simple way to describe a positive work environment. Understated but effective at B1.1.
"I've worked in worse places, but here the atmosphere is really good. People actually talk to each other."
"If I could change one thing…"
A powerful opener for expressing wishes or opinions about your workplace without sounding critical.
"If I could change one thing about our office, it would be the noise level. We haven't solved that yet."
"We're getting there."
Optimistic expression for a situation that's improving but isn't perfect yet. Very natural in British and American English.
"The culture hasn't fully changed yet, but we're getting there. The new management team has already made a big difference."
💬 Drill: Maestro da una situación → alumno responde con la frase correcta. "Your team morale has improved a lot but isn't perfect yet → ?" / "Your office has a great energy → ?" / "You want to talk about one improvement you'd make → ?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "We're getting there" — this phrase appears in the model answer. When the student reads it later, they'll have a personal connection to it. That's deliberate pedagogical sequencing.
  • "If I could change one thing…" — this is also a Second Conditional structure (would + infinitive). At B1.1 we introduce it as a CHUNK, not as grammar. Grammar explanation comes in B1.2. For now: teach the phrase, not the rule.
  • Make students say each phrase OUT LOUD twice. Pronunciation matters — these are high-frequency spoken phrases, not written ones.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These 3 phrases will make your English sound natural. Not more complex — more natural. That's the B1.1 goal: real English, not perfect English."
§ 05
Grammar (Referential) — Present Perfect: just / already / yet / never / ever
10–12 min
B1.1 Grammar Philosophy: Grammar is referential — clear explanation + 2 focused activities. No drilling tables. The goal is to use these adverbs naturally in the speaking topic, not to memorise rules.
Present Perfect + Adverbs — Precision and Timing
just
Very recently — a short time ago. Position: after have/has.
"We've just started a new hybrid policy — it only began this week."
"I've just spoken to the manager about the layout."
already
Before expected or before now. Often implies surprise. Position: after have/has.
"They've already redesigned the office — faster than we expected."
"I've already tried working from home."
yet
Until now — used in negatives and questions. Position: end of sentence.
"We haven't created quiet zones yet — it's a real problem."
"Have you spoken to HR about it yet?"
never / ever
never = at no time in your experience. ever = at any time? (questions). Position: after have/has.
"I've never worked in a fully remote environment."
"Have you ever had a really toxic boss?"
Position summary: just · already · never · ever → after have/has, before the past participle
"I have already tried it." · "She has never worked remotely." · "We have just moved offices."
yet → always at the END of the sentence: "I haven't tried it yet." · "Have you seen it yet?"
Common Errors at B1.1
❌ "I haven't never worked remotely."→✅ I've never worked remotely.
Double negative — not used in English. never alone is enough.
❌ "I already worked there." (still working there)→✅ I've already worked there for 3 years.
Simple Past vs Present Perfect — if the action connects to now, use PP.
❌ "Have you spoken yet to the manager?"→✅ Have you spoken to the manager yet?
yet always goes at the END of the sentence.
❌ "She have already improved the culture."→✅ She has already improved the culture.
3rd person: have → has. Always.
Referential Activity 1 — Choose the Correct Adverb
Click the adverb that fits the context naturally. Consider meaning AND position.
The manager has ___ changed the team layout — literally yesterday.
We haven't created any quiet zones ___. It's still an open plan only.
Have you ___ worked in a company with no hierarchy at all?
They've ___ redesigned the office — I'm surprised they did it so quickly.
I've ___ worked in a truly toxic environment — I've been fortunate.
Has the company introduced the flexible hours policy ___?
Referential Activity 2 — Correct or Incorrect?
Is this sentence grammatically correct AND natural in English?
"I haven't never worked in an open-plan office."
"She has already improved the team morale significantly."
"Have you ever yet spoken to the HR manager?"
"We've just started a new remote work policy — this week."
"I never have worked there." (with have AFTER never)
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.1 grammar is referential: 10 minutes max. Explain the adverbs with real workplace examples. Do the 2 activities. Then IMMEDIATELY bridge to speaking: "Now use just one of these adverbs in a sentence about YOUR workplace."
  • The most important error: "I haven't never…" — double negative. This is a Spanish interference pattern. Address it explicitly but briefly: "In English, one negative is always enough."
  • "yet" at the end: drill position specifically. "We haven't created quiet zones yet" — the YET goes at the end. Have students tap the end of the sentence as they say it. Physical reinforcement.
  • Don't get lost in grammar tables. 2 activities, then move to §6. Grammar serves speaking — not the other way around.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These 4 adverbs are the most common Present Perfect words in English. Native speakers use them every day. 'Has the meeting started yet?' / 'I've already sent it.' That's the level we're building to."

MONOLOGUE — NATURAL B1.1 PACE — PRESENT PERFECT ADVERBS IN CONTEXT

"I've been thinking about this a lot lately. The company where I work has already made some improvements — we just moved to a new building last month, which is great. The space is much better. More natural light, better vibe.

But there are things we haven't changed yet. The layout is still completely open-plan. I don't mind it for meetings and collaboration, but for focused work, it's difficult. I've never been able to concentrate in a noisy environment. I need quiet to do my best work.

My ideal workplace? Something hybrid. I think the best companies have already figured this out — a mix of open areas for teamwork and quiet zones for concentration. The atmosphere matters more than the physical space, though. I've worked in beautiful offices with terrible morale, and in ugly ones with amazing energy.

Have I ever worked somewhere perfect? No. But we're getting there. The culture at my current company has improved a lot since the new manager joined. There's more trust, more communication. It matters."
