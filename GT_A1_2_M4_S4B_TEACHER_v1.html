🔥 Hot Take
Zero Tolerance — Should Accidents Cost Jobs?
Timed opinion · 90 seconds · Have to + should in production
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note
This topic is deliberately provocative to generate strong opinions and rich grammar production. Monitor for: "have to to" — still the top error. Cold call 3 students before the vote. Award bonus points to any student who correctly uses "don't have to" in their argument — it shows full command of the modal system.
🔥 Today's Hot Take
"If a worker causes a serious safety incident because they didn't follow the rules, they should lose their job. Zero tolerance."
90 seconds to form your opinion. Vote: Agree or Disagree. Give one argument using "have to," "should/shouldn't," or an imperative. Include a reason with "because."

⏱ 90 seconds — Form your position
"I agree/disagree because companies should/shouldn't… Workers have to… Don't punish…"
⚡ Debate
Safety Policy — 3 Structured Rounds
Argue your side · Have to + imperatives + should in full production
⏱ 18 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note — Engagement
Assign sides randomly. Award 1 point per round for: using "have to" correctly (no double "to"), using an imperative (no "don't to"), using "should" (no "to" after). 3 points max per round. Track on leaderboard. The best debate uses all 3 grammar tools in one argument.
Round 1 — Position (60 sec each)
Team A: "Companies have to enforce zero tolerance policies because safety rules exist to protect lives. Don't ignore them. Workers who break rules should face serious consequences." — Team B: argue against. 60 seconds.
💡 Must use: have to + imperative + should. 3 points if all three are correct. Score on leaderboard.
Round 2 — Rebuttal (45 sec each)
Challenge the other team's argument directly. Start with: "I understand, but..." Use at least one "don't have to" or "shouldn't" — the negative forms. Reference the incident report from the relay section.
💡 "I understand, but workers don't have to lose their job for a first incident. Companies shouldn't punish without investigating the cause first."
Round 3 — One-Sentence Closer (20 sec)
Your team's most powerful sentence. Must include at least one modal (have to / should / don't have to / shouldn't). End with a safety vocabulary word. Class votes: which closing was most convincing?
💡 "Companies have to protect workers — and that means investing in training, not just punishment, because prevention should always come before penalties."

🔍 Speed
Speed Round — Safety Grammar Blitz
8 questions · 3 seconds max · No hesitation
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Speed Round Rules
Teacher reads each prompt aloud. Students answer immediately — no thinking time. 1 point per correct answer. Reveal after 2–3 responses. This is automaticity training — speed is the goal, not perfect accuracy at full pace.
8:00
Speed Round — 8 minutes total
8 questions · 1 point each · No hesitation allowed
1. Fix the error: "You have to to wear your helmet." — Go!
✅ "You have to wear your helmet." — have to + base verb, never "to" twice.
2. What is the difference between "have to" and "should" in safety English?
✅ "Have to" = mandatory rule — you must do it. "Should" = strong recommendation — you are expected to do it but it may not be mandatory.
3. A visitor enters the plant without PPE. Give them one formal prohibition — use "Do not."
✅ "Do not enter this area without PPE." / "Do not proceed without your safety equipment." — formal, no contraction.
4. "Goggles are optional in the administrative office." Say this with "don't have to."
✅ "You don't have to wear goggles in the administrative office." — don't have to = no obligation.
5. What is a "hazard"? Give one example from a real workplace. Fast!
✅ A hazard is something dangerous that could cause injury. Examples: a wet floor, an exposed cable, a chemical spill, a heavy object at height.
6. Fix the safety briefing: "Don't to enter the restricted area." — Correct it!
✅ "Don't enter the restricted area." — Don't + base verb, never "to" after Don't.
7. What does PPE stand for? Say it and give 3 examples of PPE.
✅ Personal Protective Equipment. Examples: helmet, goggles, gloves — also: vest, boots, mask, ear protection.
8. What should you do first when you see a chemical spill? Three-word answer!
✅ "Call it in." / "Don't touch it." / "Evacuate the area." All three are correct three-word safety responses.
🏆 Solo
Solo Challenge — Your Safety Briefing & Report
Individual production · All 3 grammar tools · Real workplace context · No notes
⏱ 12 MIN
⚡ Solo Rules
No partners, no notes, no script from the listening. Students write using real information from their own workplace. Peer-check after: 3 targets — (1) have to + base verb, (2) Don't / Do not + base verb, (3) should + base verb. Zero tolerance for "to" after any of these three. Teacher circulates silently — mark only, do not correct until all are done.
Zone 1 — Safety Briefing (3 rules)
Write 3 safety rules for your real workplace or area. Use each grammar tool once: one "have to" rule, one "don't / do not" prohibition, one "should" recommendation. Add "because" to at least one.
Check: (1) "have to" + base verb — no double "to"? (2) "Don't" or "Do not" + base verb — no "to" after? (3) "should" + base verb — no "to"? All 3 structures present? Full marks if yes.
Zone 2 — Incident Report (real or imagined)
Write a 4-sentence incident report for a real or imagined workplace incident. Include: what happened, where, what immediate action you took, and one recommendation using "should" or "have to."
Check: (1) 4 sentences present? (2) Time and location included? (3) Immediate action described? (4) Recommendation using have to or should — without "to" after? All 4 = full marks.
Zone 3 — 60-Second Spoken Briefing (no written notes)
Your partner acts as a new international visitor. Give them a 60-second verbal safety briefing for your area. Include: 2 "have to" rules, 1 prohibition imperative, 1 "should" recommendation. Partner marks errors silently.
After the verbal delivery: partner checks for the 3 forbidden errors — "have to to," "don't to," "should to." Each error = minus 1 point. No errors = full marks for Zone 3.

Wrap-Up — Certificate & My Win Today
3 takeaways · Checklist · Certificate · Preview S5 — Module Exit
⏱ 8 MIN
01
Three tools, zero "to" after any of them. Have to wear. Don't enter. Should report. None of these take "to" after the modal or imperative. This rule is now permanent in your English — no exceptions.
02
Incident reporting is a professional skill. The 4-sentence structure you practised today — what, where, action, recommendation — is used in real safety reports globally. You can write it in English now.
03
Don't vs Do not — register matters. "Don't enter" = verbal, casual. "Do not enter" = signs, written rules, formal instructions. Both are correct. Choose based on the situation — but never add "to" to either.
