§ 01
Warm-Up — Scenario
5–8 min
📊 Performance Review — 3:00 PM

Imagine: Your manager stops by your desk and says: "Before your formal review next week, I want to hear it from you directly. What have you accomplished this year? What are you most proud of? Has your team met its targets?"

You have 30 seconds. What do you say? How do you describe your year in English?

📌 Teacher Follow-Up Questions
"Have you ever led a project from start to finish this year?"
"Have you exceeded any of your targets?"
"What hasn't gone as planned — what haven't you finished yet?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • This is B1.1's entry into present perfect. The performance review scenario is ideal because native English speakers use present perfect almost exclusively for year-end recaps. The student will likely respond in simple past — that's the diagnostic data you need.
  • Listen for: simple past used where perfect is correct ("I finished the project" → should be "I have finished"), attempts at present perfect ("I have achieved…" → reward immediately), mixing tenses randomly.
  • Do NOT correct in the warm-up. The goal is to surface the gap. The student should feel the conceptual discomfort of "I know what I want to say, but this feels different." That discomfort is exactly what §4 resolves.
  • Key signal: if the student spontaneously uses "I have…" even once correctly, note it and celebrate explicitly in §4: "Remember that sentence you said in warm-up? That was already present perfect."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "I'll ask you this exact question again at the end of class. The difference in your answer will be significant — and you'll hear it yourself."
  • "Every performance review in English uses this grammar. Today you get the exact language your international colleagues use."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Present perfect is the grammar that separates intermediate from advanced in English. Today is that line. This is the moment when your English starts sounding genuinely professional."

§ 04
Grammar — Present Perfect
15–18 min
Structure — Present Perfect
FormStructureCorporate example
Affirmative (I/You/We/They)Subject + have + past participleI have exceeded my targets this year.
Affirmative (He/She/It)Subject + has + past participleShe has led three major projects.
Negative (I/You/We/They)Subject + haven't + past participleWe haven't launched the second phase yet.
Negative (He/She/It)Subject + hasn't + past participleHe hasn't submitted the final report yet.
Question (I/You/We/They)Have + subject + past participle?Have you ever led a cross-functional team?
Question (He/She/It)Has + subject + past participle?Has the team met its annual target?
Short answersYes, I have. / No, I haven't.
Yes, she has. / No, she hasn't.		"Have you finished the report?" — "Yes, I have."
Key formula: have / has + past participle (NOT the simple past form)
✅ "I have written the proposal." — NOT "I have wrote"
✅ "She has driven the strategy." — NOT "She has drived"
✅ "We have delivered 10 projects." — "delivered" = regular past participle

Note: Regular verbs use -ed as both simple past AND past participle. "I delivered it yesterday." (simple past) = "I have delivered it." (present perfect) — same -ed ending, different grammar.
Key Adverbs with Present Perfect
ever
In questions — any time up to now
"Have you ever exceeded 120% of target?"
never
Not at any time up to now
"I have never missed a deadline."
just
Very recently — before the verb
"We have just closed a major deal."
yet
Until now — end of negative/question
"I haven't submitted the report yet."
already
Before expected — before the verb
"She has already exceeded her Q3 target."
for / since
for = duration · since = start point
"I have led this team for 3 years / since 2021."
When to Use Each — Present Perfect vs. Simple Past
Present Perfect — use when…
No specific time given: "I have achieved my target."
Experience at any point in life: "Have you ever led a team?"
With just/already/yet/ever/never/for/since
News / recent results still relevant now: "We have just signed a new client."
Simple Past — use when…
Specific time stated: "I achieved my target in Q2."
Finished time period: "I worked at FEMSA last year."
Sequence of past events: "She applied, interviewed, and got the job."
With: yesterday, last week, in 2020, ago
Golden rule: If you can answer "When exactly?" with a specific past time → simple past. If the time is open, unspecified, or up to now → present perfect.
⚠ Common Errors — B1.1
✗ I have worked here since 5 years.→✓ I have worked here for 5 years. (duration = for)
✗ Did you ever go to that conference?→✓ Have you ever gone to that conference?
✗ I have went to three client meetings today.→✓ I have gone to three client meetings. (irregular participle)
✗ I have exceeded my target last month.→✓ I exceeded my target last month. (specific time = simple past)
✗ I'm working here since 2020.→✓ I have worked here since 2020. (since = present perfect)

Read and decide.
"I have exceeded my target last year" is correct present perfect.
"Have you ever led a team?" uses "ever" correctly in a question.
"For" is used with a start point in time (e.g., for 2021).
"We have just launched our new product" means the launch happened very recently.

      § 05
      Reading — Year-End Performance Review
      8–10 min
      B1.1 · Present Perfect · Corporate Document
      Annual Performance Review — Laura Mendez, Operations Director · FY 2024
      Watch for: present perfect (green) · simple past + specific time (purple) · adverbs: just / already / yet / ever

      Laura Mendez has delivered an outstanding year of results. She has exceeded her operational efficiency targets by 18%, and has successfully led the integration of two new production lines into the Celaya facility.

      Laura has built a high-performing team of 22 engineers since she took the role in January 2023. She has already secured three strategic supplier agreements for Q1 next year — ahead of the December deadline.

      The team has just launched the new quality control dashboard, which has reduced reporting time by 40%. Laura has driven a culture of continuous improvement across all departments.

      There are areas for development. Laura hasn't yet completed her leadership certification program, though she enrolled in June. She also acknowledges that the team has not expanded into the northern region as originally planned.

      Overall, Laura has made a significant and measurable impact. She has never missed a critical deadline in her tenure, and her commitment to scalable results has become a model for the organization.

      Comprehension
      Answer the questions.
      By how much has Laura exceeded her operational efficiency targets?
      What has the new quality control dashboard achieved?
      Laura has already completed her leadership certification program.
      Laura has never missed a critical deadline during her time in the role.
      Laura took the Operations Director role in January 2023 — simple past with specific time.
      The team has expanded into the northern region as planned.
      Capa 1 · Pedagogical
      • Green = present perfect. Purple = simple past with specific time. Ask student to count both colors. Present perfect should dominate (12+). This reinforces why performance reviews use present perfect — they describe results without pinning them to specific moments.
      • R3 is pedagogically critical: "Laura took the role in January 2023" uses SIMPLE PAST because "January 2023" is a specific time. Contrast with "She has built a team" (no specific time = present perfect). This is the contrast table in a real document.
      • R1 trap: 18% efficiency. Students may confuse this with the 40% reporting time reduction. Teach scanning for specific numbers paired with the correct achievement.
      • After reading: "Write 3 sentences about YOUR professional year using present perfect. Model them on this document."
      Capa 2 · Engagement
      • "This document is indistinguishable from a real Fortune 500 performance review. You now understand 100% of it — and you know why every verb is in present perfect."
      Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
      • "In S1B you'll write and deliver your own version of this document — live, spoken, to the teacher acting as your director. That is the B1.1 milestone for this module."

      🎧
      Q4 All-Hands — Annual Results Presentation
      B1.1 · Single speaker · ~110 seconds · American English

      ▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

      Good afternoon, everyone. Thank you for joining our year-end recap. I want to share what we have achieved as a team this year — because it has been an exceptional year.

      Let me start with the big wins. We have exceeded our annual revenue target by 22%. That is the first time we have ever done that in the company's history. The sales team has secured 14 new enterprise accounts — 6 more than we planned. And our operations team has reduced costs by 11% without cutting any headcount.

      On the product side, we have just launched our new platform last week. Early results have already shown a 30% increase in user engagement. The product team has worked incredibly hard and has delivered something we can all be proud of.

      Now, I also want to be honest about what we haven't achieved yet. We haven't expanded into the international market as we planned. That is now our number one priority for Q1 next year.

      I want to close by saying: I have never been more proud to lead this team. You have all made this possible. Thank you.

      ⏱ Target: 105–115 sec · Voice: Sarah · Speed: 0.85 · Count present perfect uses: 12+ instances

      Listening Activities
      Listen, then answer.
      By how much has the company exceeded its annual revenue target?
      What has the company NOT yet achieved?
      This is the first time the company has ever exceeded its annual revenue target.
      The sales team has secured 8 new enterprise accounts this year.
      The new platform has already shown a 30% increase in user engagement.
      Capa 1 · Pedagogical
      • Listen 1: global — "What is the overall message? Is it a positive or mixed year?" Listen 2: comprehension questions. Listen 3 (optional): count every present perfect use (12+ instances across ~110 seconds — this is the density of real professional English at this register).
      • L2 trap: "14 new enterprise accounts — 6 more than planned" → not 8 as the T/F implies. Students often hear "6" and associate it with the total. Train careful listening for the relationship between numbers.
      • After: "Can you do a 60-second version of this presentation about YOUR team's year? Use at least 6 present perfect sentences." — oral production transfer at B1.1 density.
      • Note: the script includes "I have never been more proud" — an excellent model of present perfect with "never" for an emotional peak. Ask the student to find it and explain why it's present perfect.
      Capa 2 · Engagement
      • "That speech was 110 seconds. Count: 12 present perfect uses. That's the density of real executive English. You understood all of it."
      Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
      • "In S1B, you'll deliver your own version of this speech — about your actual year, to the teacher acting as your CEO. 90 seconds, 8 present perfect uses minimum."

      Today I Learned
      • Present perfect = have/has + past participle
      • Negative: haven't / hasn't + past participle
      • Questions: Have / Has + subject + past participle?
      • Specific time (last year, in 2020) = simple past
      • No specific time / up to now = present perfect
      • ever / never / just / yet / already / for / since
      • Key irregular participles: gone, done, led, driven, built
      Key Summary
      Affirmative
      I have achieved · She has exceeded
      Negative
      I haven't finished yet · He hasn't launched
      Question
      Have you ever led a team? · Has she delivered?
      Golden Rule
      Specific time → simple past · No time / up to now → present perfect
