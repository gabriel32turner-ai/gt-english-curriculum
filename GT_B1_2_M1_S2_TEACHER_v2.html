B1.2 · Instant opinion

Click your answer — then justify it in one PPC sentence.

This
Gossip is a natural part of office life — everyone does it, and it builds connection.
VS
That
Gossip is always damaging — even "harmless" gossip has consequences you can't control.
This

🎯 Teacher: After each pick, demand a PPC justification: "Tell me why — using 'I've been thinking that…' or 'I've been noticing that in my office…'". Don't accept opinions without a PPC attempt. This is the grammar hook for §4.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Pair 1 is about normalisation vs harm — both are defensible at B1.2. The "correct" answer doesn't exist. Push students to argue the opposite of their first instinct once they've stated it. "Now defend the other side."
  • Pair 2 is the conceptual core of the session. The distinction gossip/venting will appear in §3 (Card Sort) and §5 (Reading). Prime them now: "Keep your answer in mind — we'll test it later."
  • PPC requirement: after each answer, push for "I've been thinking…", "I've been noticing…", "This has been affecting my team because…". Log errors for §4 grammar correction.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "In 10 seconds — which side? No explanation. Just point." Then after: "Now defend it in 45 seconds. I'm timing."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention

GT Originals — The Line That Moves
The gossip/vent distinction is culturally loaded. In many Mexican workplaces, "desahogarse" (venting) is considered a team bonding behavior. The challenge for B1.2 students: when they translate this habit into English, they may not realise that the English-speaking professional context draws the line differently — and earlier. The question isn't "is it wrong?" — it's "what is it doing to trust?" That's the B1.2 insight.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Card Sort: the 2 "wrong" answers are deliberately ambiguous (scenarios 3 and 5). When a student gets one wrong, don't just reveal — ask: "What makes you say that? What's the key factor?" Push them to the third-party rule: if someone who isn't in the conversation is being evaluated/judged, it's gossip.
  • Reputation Tracker: each chain ends with a PPC sentence requirement. Don't move to the next chain until student has produced one. Minimum: 1 complete PPC sentence with a subject, a verb-ing, and a consequence.
  • Tracker is also a grammar diagnostic: "What tense are you using? Is the damage over? Is it still happening?" → Forces PPC vs past simple distinction in context.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Has any of these chains happened in your team? I'm not asking you to name names — just tell me which chain feels most familiar. Then tell me in English what's been happening as a result."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Upsell: "S4 — Your Boss Is Wrong — uses the same vocabulary and the same grammar. But now the stakes are higher: it's not just about reputation, it's about hierarchy and professional survival. Today is the foundation for that."
§ 04
Grammar — PPC in Team Dynamics
have/has + been + -ing applied to ongoing office situations · Duration of damage · Observed patterns
04
12–15 min
✅
✅ Describing ongoing situations
"Rumors have been spreading since the director left."
"Trust has been deteriorating across the whole floor."
"She's been overhearing conversations she shouldn't have."
🚫
🚫 What has NOT been happening
"Nobody has been addressing the real issue."
"Management hasn't been communicating effectively."
"The team hasn't been confronting the problem directly."
❓
❓ Investigating the situation
"How long has this been going on?"
"Who has been spreading this — do you know?"
"Have you been noticing the tension in the team?"
💡
💡 Why PPC — not simple past
"The rumor spread" = one event, done, past.
"The rumor has been spreading" = still spreading NOW, effect is ongoing.
B1.2 precision: the damage is not over yet.
💡 B1.2 insight — PPC signals the situation is unresolved:
"Gossip damaged her reputation." → Past. It happened. It's over (or we're describing a completed event).
"Gossip has been damaging her reputation." → It started before and is still happening now. The wound is open.

In professional contexts, choosing PPC sends a stronger message: this is a current problem that needs attention now.
⚠️ Most Common B1.2 Errors — S2 Context
❌ "The rumor has been spread by him."→✅ "He has been spreading the rumor." (PPC uses active voice — not passive)
❌ "Trust is deteriorating since last month."→✅ "Trust has been deteriorating since last month." (PPC needed for since + ongoing)
❌ "They have been spread rumors."→✅ "They have been spreading rumors." (verb-ing, not past participle)
❌ "How long this has been going on?"→✅ "How long has this been going on?" (auxiliary before subject in questions)
✏️ Activity 1 — Choose the Correct Form
Select the form that best describes an ongoing, unresolved situation.
The tension in our department ___ ever since the merger was announced.
Nobody ___ the issue directly — and it's getting worse every week.
"How long ___ this rumor ___ around the office?"
She ___ to warn him about it, but he hasn't listened.
✏️ Activity 2 — True or False
Decide if the use of PPC in each sentence is correct and natural.
"The gossip has been spreading" implies the gossip is still spreading now, with current effects — not a past event.
"The rumor has been spread by him" is a correct example of Present Perfect Continuous in the active voice.
"I've been noticing this pattern for weeks" is more precise than "I noticed this pattern" when the observation is still ongoing.
"They have been spreading rumors since 2020" is grammatically incorrect — FOR must be used with periods of time, never SINCE.
✏️ Activity 3 — Reorder the Sentence
Drag pieces into the correct PPC sentence order.
  1. ⠿in our department
  2. ⠿Trust has been
  3. ⠿for the past six months.
  4. ⠿deteriorating
  1. ⠿overhearing
  2. ⠿conversations she should not have access to?
  3. ⠿Has she been
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Q3 is the high-value item: "How long has this been going on?" — this is the most natural, high-frequency question form in professional English. Drill it until it's automatic.
  • T2: "has been spread" is passive — PPC is active. This error comes from students confusing PPC with passive voice. If they get it wrong, drill: "PPC = someone IS ACTIVELY DOING something continuously. Passive = it was done TO something."
  • After activities: "Take 3 situations from your REAL office that have been developing over the past month. Give me a PPC sentence for each. No inventing." This bridges grammar to their professional reality.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Speed round: I name a situation, you give me the PPC sentence in 8 seconds. Situation 1: your colleague has been arriving late every day for 3 weeks. Go."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In S3 and S4 of this module, you'll use this same grammar to discuss disagreeing with your boss and dealing with workplace conflict. The grammar is the same — the stakes get higher."
§ 05
Reading — The Email That Changed Everything
A manager describes what happened when a venting conversation became documented gossip · PPC in narrative
05
10–12 min
Jorge Castillo
Operations Manager · NovaMex Solutions · CDMX
Personal Reflection
Watch for: PPC (green) · gossip vocabulary (highlighted) · boundary / backfire / damage · FOR and SINCE markers

I have been thinking about this incident for almost a year. I still don't know if I handled it correctly.

It started with a team lunch in February. Two colleagues — friends, really — were talking about a third colleague who wasn't there. One of them said he wasn't pulling his weight, that the team had been carrying him for months. The other agreed. At the time, it seemed like harmless venting. Nobody recorded it. Nobody escalated it.

Except one of them sent a voice note summarizing the conversation to a group chat.

Within 48 hours, the voice note had been circulating through three departments. The colleague found out. He came to me. By the time I got involved, the rumor had been evolving — what started as a complaint about workload had become a statement about his character, his intentions, his loyalty to the team.

I spoke to both people who had the original conversation. They were genuinely shocked by what the rumor had become. "We were just venting," they said. "We didn't mean for this to happen."

And that's the thing about gossip: you don't have to mean for it to happen. It has been taught to me through this experience that the difference between venting and gossip isn't your intention — it's the boundary of who hears it. The moment a third person is involved, the moment it leaves the room, it becomes something you can no longer control. And by then, the damage has already been accumulating — quietly, out of sight, in ways you can't undo.

The colleague has been working in a different team since the incident. He asked to be moved. I don't blame him.

✏️ Comprehension — Inference & PPC
Answer based on the text. Focus on the WHY behind each use of PPC.
Why does Jorge use "had been circulating" rather than "circulated" when describing the voice note?
What, according to Jorge, is the real difference between gossip and venting?
The two colleagues who had the original conversation intended for it to become a damaging rumor across three departments.
"The damage has already been accumulating" — this use of PPC implies the damage started before Jorge noticed and is still having effects now.
The colleague asked to be moved to a different team because Jorge handled the situation badly.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Key sentence: "The damage has already been accumulating — quietly, out of sight, in ways you can't undo." Ask: "What's the effect of using PPC here instead of 'accumulated'?" — PPC conveys ongoing, hidden, unstoppable process. This is B1.2 stylistic awareness.
  • After RQ2: revisit the warm-up answers. "Did your answer change? You said [their answer from §1]. Now after reading — do you still think the line is clear?" This creates a reflective loop and adds depth to the debate in §7.
  • Retell task: 90 seconds, no looking at text. "Tell me what happened in Jorge's office — from beginning to end. Use PPC at least 3 times."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Jorge says 'I still don't know if I handled it correctly.' What would YOU have done differently? And when — which moment would you have intervened?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "This is the type of situation that ends careers or creates them. Knowing how to talk about it — 'The damage has been accumulating' vs 'things got bad' — is a professional skill. That's what we're building today."

Office Dialogue — Two Colleagues After a Team Meeting
B1.2 · M1 · S2 · Dialogue · ~100 seconds · Natural pace · Gossip line crossed

🔈 Audio placeholder. Replace with Google Drive embed.
ElevenLabs: Sarah + Michael · Rate: 0.87 · Stability: 0.60

▶ Show ElevenLabs Script

Sarah: Did you notice how quiet Daniel was in the meeting today?

Michael: I know. He's been like that for weeks. Since they gave the project to Ana, actually.

Sarah: I've been wondering if there's something going on with him. I mean — he's been underperforming for a while, hasn't he?

Michael: Yeah. The team has been covering for him, honestly. Nobody's been saying it out loud, but we've all been noticing it.

Sarah: Has anyone talked to him directly?

Michael: Not that I know of. I think people have been avoiding it — you know how he gets defensive. I actually heard from Carla that he's been complaining to people outside the team about Ana's management style.

Sarah: Wait — Carla told you that?

Michael: She mentioned it. I mean, I don't know if it's accurate. You know how these things get distorted as they travel.

Sarah: Okay, but — this is gossip now, right? We started talking about Daniel, and now we're talking about what Carla said Daniel said about Ana. That's three people deep.

Michael: You're right. I shouldn't have brought it up. The thing is — it's been bothering me, and I had to say it to someone.

Sarah: I get it. But the moment Carla's name came in, it stopped being venting. We've crossed the line. Let's not take this any further.

⏱ Target: 95–105 sec · PPC: has been like that / I've been wondering / he's been underperforming / has been covering / have been noticing / have been avoiding / has been complaining / it's been bothering me · Rate: 0.87

✏️ Listening Activities
Listen and answer. Focus on where the conversation crosses the line.
At what point does Sarah say the conversation crosses the line from venting to gossip?
Why does Michael say he brought it up, even though he knows he shouldn't have?
The team has been openly discussing Daniel's performance in team meetings — it's not a hidden issue.
Sarah correctly identifies the moment the conversation crossed the line — when a third person's name entered the conversation.
"It's been bothering me" uses PPC correctly — Michael's discomfort started before and is still ongoing at the moment of speaking.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Pre-listening task: "How many people are being talked about in this conversation? Count them as you listen." Answer: Daniel (the absent person), Ana (mentioned), Carla (third party). 3 absent people — each addition moves it further from venting.
  • Key moment: Sarah says "three people deep." This is a memorable phrase for B1.2 — introduce it as an idiomatic way to describe chain gossip. "How deep does your office gossip usually go?"
  • LQ1: the answer requires understanding the structure of the conversation, not just surface comprehension. If student chooses the third option ("when Sarah says 'this is gossip now'"), probe: "Was that the MOMENT it crossed, or just when she noticed it had crossed?"
  • Post-listening roleplay: student plays Sarah, you play Michael. Run the same conversation — but student must STOP it at the right moment. "When does it cross the line for you? Stop me."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Has this dialogue happened in your office? Not the exact words — but this exact dynamic? When did YOU realize a conversation had crossed the line?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Homework: replay the audio 3× and write every PPC sentence. "8 PPC instances in 100 seconds. This is what natural, professional B1.2 English sounds like in real conversation."

🗣 Debate Challenge — ⏱ 2-Minute Timed
Format: Oxford-style · Choose a side · Defend it · Concede when necessary
"Gossip has been destroying professional environments for years — and companies have been enabling it by ignoring it."
✅ Arguments FOR the motion
Reputations have been damaged by unchecked rumors for decades.
HR has been ignoring informal communication networks — that's complicity.
The least-heard employees have been the most affected.
Companies haven't been training managers to identify and stop gossip chains.
❌ Arguments AGAINST the motion
Gossip has been present in workplaces since organizations existed — it's human.
Informal communication has been filling the gap where official channels have failed.
Companies have been improving — DEI, anonymous reporting, open-door policies.
The problem has been misdiagnosis: gossip is a symptom, not the disease.

▶ Show B1.2 Model Debate Answer (reveal AFTER student speaks)
"I'm arguing FOR the motion — and honestly though, I think most people in this room have been watching this happen for years without naming it. Companies have been treating gossip as a personality issue — 'oh, that's just how she is' — when actually, the real issue is that management hasn't been creating enough transparent communication. When people don't get information officially, they fill the gap. And what has been filling that gap? Rumor. Speculation. Damage. I'm not gonna lie — I think the company is as responsible as the individual. Fair enough, there will always be people who gossip more than others. But if the culture has been enabling it — through silence, through closed doors, through favoritism — then the company has been complicit. That's the motion I'm defending."
