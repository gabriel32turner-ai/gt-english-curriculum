Plans Change — What Now?
When your plans don't work out · Rescheduling, postponing, changing · Real corporate context
⏱ 5–8 MIN
📋 Think back: Last week, did any of your plans at work change? A meeting was postponed? A deadline moved? Tell your partner what happened — in English or Spanish, doesn't matter yet.
💬 Quick contrast: Which is correct? (a) "The meeting is at 10 AM" or (b) "I'm going to have a meeting at 10 AM" — When do you use each one? Think about the difference.
🧠 Translation challenge: "Vamos a reprogramar la junta para el viernes." → "We're going to _____ the meeting _____ Friday." Can you fill the blanks?
⚡ Teacher note: S2 deepens "going to" with CHANGE vocabulary (postpone, reschedule, cancel, change, delay) and introduces the going to vs Simple Present contrast explicitly. The key distinction: "I'm going to call the client" (my plan) vs "The flight leaves at 6 PM" (fixed schedule).
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds
Say this NOW: "We're going to reschedule the meeting — the client cancelled."

Changing Plans at Solaris
Valeria's project hits obstacles · How to communicate changes professionally
⏱ 8 MIN
Context: Valeria's Q2 project plan just changed. The supplier delayed materials, a key team member is sick, and the client wants to move the deadline forward. She needs to communicate these changes.
Round 1 — Changed Plans
Think of 3 plans that changed at your work recently. Tell your partner: "We were going to [original plan], but now we're going to [new plan]."
💡 Model: "We were going to launch in March, but now we're going to launch in April."
Round 2 — Fixed vs Flexible
Describe your schedule: which things are FIXED (the class starts at 9) and which are YOUR PLANS (I'm going to prepare the slides before lunch)? Give 2 of each.
💡 Key: Fixed schedule = Simple Present · Personal plan = going to
Round 3 — Be Valeria
You are Valeria. Email Daniel about 3 plan changes. Use: "We're going to reschedule / postpone / cancel [something] because [reason]." Include 1 fixed schedule item.
💡 Mix: "We're going to postpone the meeting" + "The training starts at 2 PM" (fixed)
"Daniel, I have some updates. We're going to postpone the supplier visit to next Thursday — they're not ready. I'm going to reschedule the budget review for Wednesday morning instead. We're not going to change the launch date — it's still March 28th. Note: the safety training starts at 2 PM tomorrow (fixed, not my plan)."

Project Update Chunks
5 natural phrases for communicating plan changes · Sound professional instantly
⏱ 5 MIN
"Change of plans."
= The plan is different now (quick announcement)
"Change of plans — we're going to meet at 3 PM instead of 2."
"Heads up —"
= I want to let you know in advance
"Heads up — the client is going to postpone the launch."
"I'm going to follow up on that."
= I'll check on it and give you an update
"The supplier didn't respond. I'm going to follow up on that today."
"We're going to push it back."
= We're going to delay it / move the date later
"The deadline is too tight. We're going to push it back a week."
"I'm going to keep you posted."
= I'll update you when I know more
"We don't have the final numbers yet. I'm going to keep you posted."

§5
Going To vs Simple Present
The key distinction · Personal plan vs fixed schedule · Interactive classifier
⏱ 15–18 MIN
🚨 Real Situation — Monday Morning Email
Valeria writes to Daniel:

"The training starts at 9 AM. I'm going to prepare my slides before that. The meeting room is booked from 2 PM to 4 PM. I'm going to invite the supplier."
❓ Why does Valeria use Simple Present for some things and "going to" for others? What's the rule?
✅ The Rule: Two Types of Future
Going to = Your personal plan — YOU decided: "I'm going to call the client."
Simple Present = Fixed schedule — NOT your decision: "The flight leaves at 6 PM."

⚡ Test: Can YOU change it? → Going to. Is it FIXED by someone else? → Simple Present.

⚠️ Top errors — correct immediately:
✗ "The meeting is going to start at 10" (when it's a fixed schedule) → ✅ "The meeting starts at 10"
✗ "I prepare the slides tomorrow" (when it's your plan) → ✅ "I'm going to prepare the slides"
✗ "The flight is going to leave at 7" → ✅ "The flight leaves at 7" (fixed timetable)

GT Original — "Schedule" Pronunciation
  • 🔊 American English: /ˈskɛdʒuːl/ (SKEJ-ool) · British English: /ˈʃɛdjuːl/ (SHED-yool). In México, either is fine — American is more common in the Bajío corporate context.
  • ⚠️ Translation trap: "La junta empieza a las 10" → "The meeting starts at 10" (NOT "is going to start") — fixed schedules use Simple Present.
  • 📊 Ahead / behind / on schedule: These three phrases are extremely useful in manufacturing standup meetings. Drill them: "Are we ahead of schedule?" — "No, we're behind schedule because of the supplier delay."
  • 💼 Push back: Very common in US corporate English. "We're going to push back the deadline" = more casual than "We're going to postpone the deadline." Both correct at A2.1.

Project Update — Changes This Week
A2.1 · M4 · S2A · Voice: Sarah (American English) · Speed: 0.82
Good morning, team. I have some updates about this week. A few things changed since our last meeting.

First, we're going to reschedule the supplier visit. The supplier called yesterday — they're not going to be ready until next Thursday. So I'm going to move our visit from Monday to Thursday.

Second, the safety training starts at 9 AM tomorrow. That's fixed — we can't change it. I'm going to prepare the materials tonight. Everyone needs to attend — it's mandatory.

Third, we're going to push back the budget report deadline. The original deadline was March 15th, but there's going to be a delay with the Q1 numbers. I'm going to ask Daniel for an extension to March 22nd.

Good news: the production line is ahead of schedule. We're going to complete the first batch by Wednesday — two days early. The delivery truck leaves at 6 AM on Thursday — that's the fixed schedule from logistics.

One more thing: the client is NOT going to cancel the order. I confirmed this morning. We're on track.

Any questions? Remember, the all-hands meeting is at 4 PM today. That doesn't change.
