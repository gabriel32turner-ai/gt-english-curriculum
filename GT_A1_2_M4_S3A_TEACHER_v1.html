§1
Warm-Up — Does Your Body Hurt at Work?
Activate symptoms vocabulary · Bridge S2 → S3 · Surface ergonomics problems
⏱ 5–8 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note — Pedagogical
S3 shifts focus from acute illness (I am sick today) to chronic workplace health issues: posture, eye strain, repetitive strain, and office environment. Students already know have/has + My [body part] hurts. Today they apply these in the context of daily work routines and ergonomics — a vocabulary set that is highly relevant to coordinators, engineers, and office workers in manufacturing environments.
Question 1 — Your Body Right Now
Think about how your body feels when you sit at your workstation for 3 hours without a break. What hurts? What feels tired or stiff? Try to describe it in English.
💡 Use S1–S2 language: "My [body part] hurts / aches / feels tired after [time]."
Question 2 — Your Daily Work Routine
Describe your typical work morning: what time do you start, what do you do in the first hour, and do you take any breaks? What health habits do you have — or not have — at work?
💡 Use Simple Present: "I start at… / I usually check… / I don't take breaks because…"
Question 3 — Your Workstation
Look at your workstation (or imagine a typical one). Is your chair at the right height? Is your monitor at eye level? Is the lighting good? What is the biggest ergonomic problem in your office?
💡 New word today: ergonomic = designed to fit your body and prevent injury
Question 4 — Health Habits at Work
How many times do you stand up or stretch during a work day? Do you drink enough water? Do you have a lunch break? Which healthy habit do you find hardest to maintain at work?
💡 Use: "I always/usually/sometimes/never… / I don't have time to… / The hardest thing is…"
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds
Say this right now: "My [back/neck/wrist/eyes] hurts/hurt from [typing/sitting/screens] all day. I have a [mild/bad/terrible] [symptom]."
Use a real problem from your own body. Not a translation — a real sentence about your real work life.
§2
Speaking Topic — Workplace Ergonomics & Health Routines
Real problems · Real routines · have/has + My body hurts in production
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note
Pairs. Both partners share first, then one person summarizes the other's health issues using "She/He has a [problem]. His/Her [body part] hurts because…" — this forces third-person have/has which is the core A1.2 accuracy target. Monitor for "he have" errors and correct immediately.
Round 1 — Body Scan
Close your eyes for 10 seconds. Now describe every physical feeling in your body right now, from head to toe. Use: "I have a [symptom]" and "My [body part] hurts/aches/feels tired." Give at least 4 sentences.
💡 "My neck feels stiff. I have mild eye strain. My shoulders are tense. I have a slight headache."
Round 2 — Your Health Routine at Work
Describe your real health routine at work. Use Simple Present: when do you have breaks? Do you stretch? Do you drink water regularly? Does your company have any wellness programs? Are they effective?
💡 Use: "I usually take a break at… / I don't always remember to… / My company has/doesn't have…"
Round 3 — Chronic vs Acute: Know the Difference
In your experience: what health problems at work are temporary (a headache one day) and which ones come back every day? How do you know when a work-related health problem is serious enough to report to HR?
💡 New today: chronic = it comes back every day / acute = it happens once. "My back pain is chronic — it hurts every day after lunch."
Round 1 model (for teacher reference):
"Right now, my shoulders feel very tense — I have tight muscles from looking at the screen. My eyes ache a little — I have eye strain. My lower back hurts because my chair is not adjusted correctly. I also have a slight headache. My wrists feel okay today — I used a wrist support this morning."

§4
Colloquial — How Professionals Talk About Work Pain
5 natural phrases · What real workers say · Informal but professional
⏱ 5 MIN
"My back is killing me."
Very informal — "killing me" means the pain is very bad right now. Common in any English workplace. Not rude. Use with colleagues, not with HR in formal contexts.
"I have been in meetings since 8 AM. My back is killing me."
🕐 When: complaining about pain casually to a trusted colleague
"I need to step away for a bit."
Professional way to say you need a break — without explaining why. Works for physical or mental health. "Step away" = leave your desk temporarily. Very common in English-speaking companies.
"I have been on screens for 4 hours. I need to step away for a bit."
🕐 When: needing a break during work hours — polite and non-dramatic
"My eyes are shot."
Informal — "shot" means completely worn out or not working well. Common for digital fatigue after long screen time. Not used in formal documents, but very natural in spoken English with colleagues.
"Five video calls today. My eyes are shot — I can barely read."
🕐 When: digital fatigue is severe and you need to explain why you're struggling
"I could use a stretch."
"Could use" = it would be helpful / I want it. "A stretch" = a few minutes to stretch your body. Very common in teams when someone wants to suggest a short physical break. Friendly and light.
"We have been in this room for 2 hours. I could use a stretch — anyone else?"
🕐 When: subtly suggesting a break during a long meeting or work session
"It's been bothering me for a while."
Natural way to say a pain or problem is chronic — it has been happening for some time. "Bother" = to cause discomfort repeatedly. Used when reporting a recurring issue to a manager or HR without sounding dramatic.
"My wrist has been bothering me for a while — since we got the new keyboards."
🕐 When: reporting a chronic pain or ergonomic issue to a manager or HR coordinator
💡 Drill Note
Drill the formal vs informal contrast: students translate each colloquial phrase into formal HR English. "My back is killing me" → "I have chronic lower back pain." "My eyes are shot" → "I have significant eye strain." This teaches register awareness, not just vocabulary.

Grammar — have/has + My Body Hurts · Health Routines
Problem → Tool · Ergonomic Workstation Checker (new visual) · 2 activities
⏱ 12–15 MIN
🚨 Real Situation
Your manager asks: "How are you doing? You look a bit tired."

You want to say: "Tengo dolor de espalda crónico y me duelen los ojos del monitor. Necesito ajustar mi silla y tomar descansos más seguido."
What grammar structures do you need to say this naturally in English?
✅ The Tools
Tool 1 — have/has + symptom: I have chronic back pain. / She has eye strain.
Tool 2 — My body part + hurts/aches: My back hurts. / My eyes ache after long screen time.
Tool 3 — Simple Present for routines: I take breaks every 90 min. / She doesn't stretch enough.
Tool 4 — Imperative for advice: Adjust your chair. / Don't look at the screen without breaks.

⚠️ Common errors — correct immediately:
✗ "She have eye strain" → ✅ She has eye strain (3rd person = has)
✗ "My eyes hurts" → ✅ My eyes hurt (plural body part = hurt, not hurts)
✗ "My back is hurt" → ✅ My back hurts (not passive — active present)
✗ "I have the fatigue" → ✅ I have fatigue (no article with uncountable health nouns)

Listening — Monica's Ergonomics Assessment with HR
Monica reports chronic workplace health issues · have/has + My body hurts throughout
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Listening Note
Two passes: (1) listen for Monica's health problems — students list them, (2) listen for the grammar forms — students mark every "have/has" and "hurts/aches" they hear. Compare lists after. Speed 0.80 — natural pauses kept.
Monica's Ergonomics Assessment — HR Coordinator Laura
A1.2 · M4 · S3A · Voice: Sofia (Mx English) · Speed: 0.80
[HR — Laura]: Good morning Monica. You requested this ergonomics assessment. Tell me — what physical problems do you have at work right now?

[Monica]: Thank you for seeing me, Laura. I have several issues. First, my back hurts every afternoon — it is a chronic problem. I think it is because my chair is too low and I sit in a bad posture all morning.

[Laura]: Okay. Do you have any other symptoms?

[Monica]: Yes. My eyes ache after about two hours on the screen. I have eye strain — the lighting near my workstation is very bright and there is a lot of glare on my monitor. I also have wrist pain from the keyboard position. My wrist hurts especially in the afternoon.

[Laura]: And does your neck feel stiff?

[Monica]: Yes, my neck is very stiff. The ventilation near my desk is poor — there is a fan that blows directly on me. I think that makes it worse.

[Laura]: I see. Let me give you some immediate recommendations. First, adjust your chair so your feet are flat on the floor. Second, raise your monitor to eye level — this reduces neck and eye strain. Third, move your keyboard closer so your wrists are flat, not bent up.

[Monica]: And the lighting?

[Laura]: Use a screen filter for glare. Take a five-minute break every hour — look away from the screen and blink slowly. And drink more water — dehydration makes fatigue and headaches worse.

[Monica]: Thank you. My whole team has similar problems. Is it possible to request new chairs?

[Laura]: Yes. Complete this ergonomics request form. If more than three team members have the same issue, we can process a group request.

§SP
Speaking Practice — Ergonomics & Workplace Health in Production
6 rounds · have/has + My body hurts + imperatives + Simple Present routines
⏱ 10–12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Monitor for: (1) "she have" — third person have/has error, (2) "my eyes hurts" — singular/plural body part agreement, (3) missing article in health nouns. Correct after each Q. Switch at Q3.
Question 1 — Your Ergonomic Report
You are doing your own ergonomics assessment. Describe your workstation: 3 problems you have AND 2 things that work well. Use "I have [problem]" and "My [body part] hurts/aches because…" for the problems.
💡 "I have chronic eye strain because my monitor is too bright. My back hurts every afternoon. But my keyboard is in a good position — my wrists don't hurt."
Question 2 — Describe a Colleague
Think of a real colleague (or invent one). Describe their health situation at work using third-person he/she. Use "He/She has [problem]" + "His/Her [body part] hurts because…" + one imperative recommendation for them.
💡 "She has terrible posture. Her back hurts every day. She sits at her desk for 6 hours without a break. Adjust your chair, Maria — and take a break every 90 minutes!"
Question 3 — HR Role Play: Request New Equipment
Partner A is Monica. Partner B is HR coordinator Laura. Monica requests new ergonomic equipment for her team. She must explain: 3 health problems the team has, how many people are affected, and what equipment they need. Laura asks 2 follow-up questions.
💡 Monica uses: "My team has… / Five of us have… / Our backs hurt because…" Laura uses: "How many people have this problem? / How long has this been an issue?"
Question 4 — Wellness Routine Design
Design a 5-point Workplace Wellness Routine for your team. Use Simple Present for what the team DOES and imperatives for what they SHOULD DO. Example: "We take a 10-minute break at 11 AM. / Stand up and stretch every 90 minutes."
💡 Mix: "The team has… / We do stretching… / Drink water at your desk. / Don't work through lunch."
Question 5 — Chronic vs Acute
Describe 2 work health problems: one that is chronic (happens every day) and one that is acute (happened once). Use "I have chronic [X]" vs "Yesterday I had [Y]." How are they different? When do you report each to HR?
💡 "I have chronic eye strain — it happens every day after 2 PM. But yesterday I had a sharp pain in my shoulder — it was acute. I reported the chronic one to HR."
Question 6 — 60-Second Ergonomic Report ⭐
You are reporting to your manager. In 60 seconds: describe 3 health problems you or your team have, give 3 imperative recommendations to fix them, and say which one is chronic. Use all 4 grammar tools from today. No notes.
💡 "My team has [X]. My [body part] hurts every day. We don't take enough breaks. Adjust the monitors. Take a 10-minute break every hour. Don't sit for more than 90 minutes."

3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
Workplace health English · Immediate real-world application
PHRASE 1
"I have chronic [symptom]. My [body part] hurts every day after [time/activity]."
The professional way to report a recurring health problem to HR or your manager. "Chronic" signals it is not random — it is a pattern that needs action. Use this to initiate an ergonomics assessment.
PHRASE 2
"Adjust your [workstation element]. It will reduce your [symptom]."
Imperative + result — gives specific, actionable ergonomic advice. Replace with: monitor / chair / keyboard / lighting. This sounds like a professional wellness recommendation, not just "take a break."
PHRASE 3
"My whole team has [problem]. Can we request an ergonomics assessment?"
Escalates an individual complaint to a team-level HR action. "My whole team has" + plural noun = professional collective report. This phrase opens the door for company investment in ergonomic equipment.
§7
Wrap-Up — Session Checklist
3 key takeaways · I can now · Preview S3B
⏱ 5–8 MIN
01
have vs has is not optional. I/You/We/They = have. He/She/It = has. "She have eye strain" is always wrong. The s on "has" is the same s as "she works" — it's the 3rd person rule you already know.
02
Plural body parts → hurt (no s). "My eyes hurt" not "my eyes hurts." The verb agrees with the subject — eyes is plural, so hurt has no s. Same rule: "My shoulders hurt." "My knees hurt."
03
Chronic = pattern, not one day. "I have chronic back pain" tells HR this is serious and recurring. "My back hurts today" tells them it is temporary. The word "chronic" changes what HR does next.
I can use have/has correctly: "I have eye strain" / "She has chronic back pain"
I know plural body parts use "hurt" not "hurts": "My eyes hurt / My shoulders hurt"
I can describe a chronic workplace health problem: "I have chronic [X]. My [body part] hurts every day after [Y]."
I can give 3 ergonomic imperative recommendations using today's vocabulary
I can request an ergonomics assessment in English for myself or my team
