🎯 S1B Scenario — Respond with 2nd Conditional
"You are three weeks into a new job. Your manager calls you aside and tells you, in complete confidence, that the company will announce layoffs next Friday — and your entire department is affected. Your teammates don't know. They are working hard, making career plans, talking about upcoming projects. You are not authorized to say anything. What would you do?"

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • This scenario is calibrated to force 2nd conditional IMMEDIATELY: "If I told my teammate, I would risk..." / "If I stayed silent, I would feel..." Both branches require the structure. The situation is also genuinely uncomfortable — that's intentional. Discomfort activates authentic language.
  • Q5 is the vocabulary re-activation trigger. Target words: accountability, integrity, transparency, dilemma, navigate. If student uses any of these in a 2nd conditional sentence in the first 30 seconds — that is the S1B benchmark moment. Note it.
  • Common error to watch: "If I will say something..." → Redirect: "After 'if' in 2nd conditional, no future. Which form do we use? Past simple. Try again."
  • If student says "I would to tell them..." → missing base verb rule. Quick fix: "Would is already the modal — no 'to' after would. 'I would tell.'"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "This is not a grammar exercise. This is a real situation that happens in real companies. Have you ever been in a position where you knew something your colleagues didn't? Tell me — in 2nd conditional."
  • Micro-celebration trigger: If student produces a fluent 2nd conditional in 30 seconds without notes — stop everything. "Did you hear that? No script. No hesitation. That was B1.2 English. That's what today is about."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In S1A you learned the structure. Today you're going to use it in a role-play where there is no correct answer — only stronger and weaker arguments. At the end of class, you'll handle a real business negotiation entirely in English, under time pressure, with no script. That's the exit task."
  • Upsell seed: "The professionals who can argue competing positions in English are the ones who get asked back to the table. That's what we're building today."

Sales
The Overpromise
Your company promised a client a product feature in 30 days. Your tech lead just confirmed it requires 90 days minimum. The contract is already signed. The client presentation is next week.

Dilemma 2
Ethics
The Silent Partner
You notice a colleague is doing paid freelance work for a competitor during company hours, using company equipment. You know them well. They have never caused problems before.
If you discovered this, what would you do? Would you speak to them first or go directly to management?
Try: accountability · speak up · navigate
Dilemma 3
HR
The Unfair Review
Your manager asks you to write a formal performance review for a colleague. They hint at what rating to give — a rating lower than the person deserves — because a budget decision has already been made.
If your manager pressured you to write an unfair review, how would you respond? What would you write?
Try: integrity · push back · set boundaries
Dilemma 4
Vendor
The Personal Gift
A key supplier sends you an expensive watch as a personal gift — two weeks before the annual contract renewal process begins. There is no note, but the timing is clear.
If a vendor gave you this gift, what would you do? Keep it? Return it? Report it?
Try: conflict · boundary · transparency
Dilemma 5
Team
The Weekend Ask
Leadership sends a company-wide message: all managers are "expected" to work weekends until the Q2 project closes. No extra pay. No timeline. The message says it is "voluntary" but the pressure is obvious.
If this message arrived today, how would you handle it? What would you say to your team?
Try: pressure · expectations · push back · set boundaries
Dilemma 6
People
The Job Hunter
Your most talented team member confides that they are actively interviewing at a competitor. They ask you to keep it private. You know their departure would significantly damage the team's upcoming project.
If your best employee told you this, what would you do? Would you tell your manager? What would you tell the employee?
Try: navigate · defuse · mediate · tension
Dilemma 7
Reference
The Glowing Reference
A former manager — someone who helped your career significantly — asks you to write them a professional reference. You know their performance in their last role was consistently poor. They had a serious conflict with their team.
If a former mentor asked you for a reference you could not honestly give, what would you do?
Try: integrity · accountability · handle · speak up
Dilemma 8
Finance
The Data Request
Your manager asks you to "adjust" the way historical sales data is presented in a report that goes to investors. The data is accurate — but the new presentation makes results look significantly better than they were.
If your manager asked you to frame data in a misleading way, what would you do?
Try: transparency · integrity · escalate · speak up
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Cards 1–3 are entry-level dilemmas with clear institutional stakes. Cards 4–6 are personal-professional boundary situations. Cards 7–8 are the hardest — they involve loyalty vs. integrity conflicts where there is genuinely no clean answer.
  • Move fast: 2 cards per minute is the target. The goal is production volume, not deep analysis. Push the student to give a 2nd conditional response and move to the next card. "Good — next one."
  • If the student freezes on a card (blank response for 5+ seconds) — give them the sentence starter: "If I were in that situation, I would..." and let them complete it. Do not let silence run more than 8 seconds.
  • Card 8 is the pre-warm-up for the §SP role-play. If student reaches it, use their response to set up the role-play: "Great. That exact dilemma — that is exactly what we are going to role-play in §SP. You'll need to argue both sides."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Which of these 8 dilemmas is closest to something real in your work? Don't tell me which card — tell me the real version. In 2nd conditional. What would you do?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "These 8 situations are not invented. They are real dilemmas from real corporate environments. The professionals who handle them well — in English, with precision — are the ones who get promoted. You are practicing that skill right now."

Grammar — Error Correction & Production
No re-teaching · find the error · build the structure · apply under pressure
04

⚠ B1.2 Error Pattern Reminder — Before the Activity
The 3 most persistent errors at this level: (1) "would" in the if-clause — especially under speaking pressure. (2) Present simple instead of past simple in the if-clause ("if she handles"). (3) Forgetting "were" in formal contexts with all persons ("if I was" in a formal argument).
✏️ Activity 1 — Error Correction
Each sentence has ONE 2nd conditional error. Identify the corrected version. Click the correct option.
❌ "If I would be in charge, I would restructure the entire team."
❌ "If she handles the situation differently, the conflict would not escalate."
❌ "What would you do if your manager asks you to compromise your integrity?"
❌ "If we address this problem now, we would avoid a much bigger issue later."
❌ "I would set clearer expectations if I am in your position."
✏️ Activity 2 — Sentence Order
Click items in the correct order to build the complete sentence. Then check your answer.
would you speak up
or stay silent?
If your manager
asked you to hide the truth,
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • EC1: "would" in the if-clause. This is the primary B1.2 error. If student selects "If I was in charge, I will restructure" — two errors in one sentence. Address them separately: (a) "was" vs "were" in formal conditional → "were"; (b) "will" in main clause → "would." Never conflate two corrections into one fast comment.
  • EC3: The trap is "asks" (present simple) vs "asked" (past simple in if-clause). Students who understood S1A grammatically often still default to present simple under time pressure. Use EC3 as your marker: if they get this wrong, they need explicit attention before §SP.
  • Drag & Drop: The question form "Would you... if your manager asked" is harder than the standard declarative because the if-clause comes at the end. This mirrors real spoken English and the §SP role-play structure.
  • After both activities: quick oral production drill. Say a prompt, student responds in 2nd conditional in under 5 seconds. 3 rounds: "If you were the CEO of your company right now..." / "If your salary doubled tomorrow..." / "If your manager quit next week..."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "You caught 5 errors that B1 students make every day. In §SP, you are going to need to produce clean 2nd conditional under pressure — in real time, in a role-play, with a position to defend. The error pattern you just identified? That is exactly what I will be listening for."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "B2 candidates are expected to self-correct these errors in speaking in real time. You are building that skill right now. By S2A, I want you to catch your own errors before I do."

Rodrigo Fuentes
Operations Manager · Grupo Norteño Industrial · Monterrey, NL
LinkedIn · 3,241 reactions
Color key: ■ 2nd Conditional   ■ S1A Vocabulary   ■ Discourse Markers

Three months ago, I found something in a report I was not supposed to see. A supplier had been overbilling us for over a year — not a small discrepancy — and someone on our side had been approving it. At that point, I did not know what to do.

If I stayed quiet, on one hand, I would protect my relationship with the team. But I would also be complicit in something that was damaging the company. If I escalated this to management, on the other hand, I would risk my position — because the person approving the invoices was my direct manager. That was my dilemma.

I sat with that conflict for two days. I thought about transparency. I thought about what it would mean for my integrity. In the end, I decided to speak up. Not because I was not afraid — I was. But if I had said nothing, I would have lost something more important than a job title: my own accountability.

I escalated the issue to the legal department and provided all the evidence I had. It was not easy. But looking back, I would make the same decision again.

If you were in this situation today — what would you do? I am genuinely curious. Because the answer says a lot about how you navigate pressure.

#Leadership #Integrity #ProfessionalEthics

I know. But if we called the client now, we would lose everything we built this quarter. Two years of relationship, gone.

MIGUEL

And if we said nothing and they found out after signing — what would happen then?

DANIELA

I do not know... maybe we could manage it.

MIGUEL

"Maybe we could manage it"? If I were in the client's position, I would want to know before I signed anything. Period.

DANIELA

And if you were in my position — with the team's targets and the Q2 bonus on the line — what would you do?

MIGUEL

I would bite the bullet and call them. Tonight.

DANIELA

That is easy to say.

MIGUEL

Daniela, if we compromised our integrity on this deal, what would that do to the next one? And the one after that?

DANIELA

(pause) Fine. If I called them tonight, what would we say?

MIGUEL

We say the truth. "We identified an issue and we would not be doing our job if we did not address it before tomorrow."

DANIELA

You know what? If they respected us for being honest, it would actually strengthen the relationship.

MIGUEL

That is what accountability looks like.

🎭 Role-Play Setup
Situation: A major proposal for a 2-year client is scheduled for tomorrow at 9am. Last night, the Operations Manager (Role A) discovered that a key deliverable in the proposal requires 90 days — not the 45 days promised. The Sales Director (Role B) has been managing this client relationship for 2 years. It is 7pm. They have one hour to decide: call the client tonight, or present tomorrow as planned.

Goal: Each person argues their position using 2nd conditional. The conversation ends with a joint decision — stated in 2nd conditional.
Role A — Operations Manager
Carlos Reyes
Operations Manager · 6 years at the company · direct report to the CEO
You discovered the delivery problem at 5pm. Your tech lead confirmed: 90 days minimum, no exceptions. You believe presenting false information to a client is a clear integrity violation — regardless of the financial consequences.
Your GoalCall the client tonight. You must convince Role B that transparency now is less costly than discovery later.
"If we sent this proposal as written, we would be starting the relationship with a lie."
"If they found out after signing, the damage to our integrity would be irreversible."
"If I were the client, I would want to know before I signed anything."
Use: integrity · accountability · address · speak up · escalate
Role B — Sales Director
Fernanda Cruz
Sales Director · 4 years managing this account · Q2 targets at stake for the team
You have invested two years building this client relationship. This contract represents 30% of Q2 revenue. You believe the team can find a solution after winning the deal — and that calling tonight would destroy years of trust-building.
Your GoalPresent tomorrow as planned. Convince Role A that they can manage the timeline after signing — and that the risk of losing the client is greater than the timeline problem.
"If we called the client now, we would lose two years of relationship building overnight."
"If we won this contract first, we would have the resources to find a real solution."
"If I were you, I would not make a career-ending call based on a problem we can solve."
Use: navigate · handle · pressure · expectations · play it by ear
📋 Guided Speaking Prompts
P1
Carlos — open the conversation. Explain what you discovered and why it is a problem. Use at least one 2nd conditional sentence to show what would happen if they stayed silent.
Fernanda — listen, then react. Do not agree immediately.
P2
Fernanda — defend your position. Use 2nd conditional to show what would happen if they were transparent with the client tonight. What is the real cost?
Carlos — listen, then counter. Use "If I were the client..."
P3
Both — What would your manager say if they found out you knew about the problem and said nothing? Say it in 2nd conditional, in character.
P4
Fernanda — use ONE colloquial phrase from S1A naturally in your argument. It must fit the context — not forced.
Options: bite the bullet · play it by ear · rock the boat · drop the ball
P5
Carlos — use "accountability" and "transparency" in your argument. They must be in 2nd conditional sentences, not just mentioned.
P6
Both — reach a final decision. State it together: "We decided that if we [action], we would [outcome]." One sentence. Joint ownership. No notes.
🔥 Hot Seat Questions — Drop In at Any Point
To either role: "If YOU were the client and found out about this problem after signing — what would you say to this company? In character. Go."
After P6: "If this conversation became public — if a journalist wrote about it — what would the headline say? One sentence. 2nd conditional."

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.2 Role-Play benchmark: 6+ 2nd conditional instances across the role-play (combined) + 1 colloquial phrase used naturally + student sustains their position through counter-argument without switching to L1. If student achieves all three: S1B passed at level.

  • P6 is critical: joint decision in 2nd conditional. If student produces "We decided to call the client" (past simple, no conditional) — pause and redirect: "Good decision — now say it in 2nd conditional. What would you do IF you were in this situation right now?"
  • Closing statement benchmark: 90 seconds, 3 conditional sentences minimum, 1 colloquial phrase, personal position clearly stated. If student achieves this without notes → this is the testimonial moment.

Capa 3 · Growth & Retention

📐 2nd Conditional — Application Rules
⚡
Under Pressure
Even when speaking fast, the if-clause stays in past simple — never "would" or present simple. That is the B1.2 test.
🎭

GT Originals — Instructor Insight
S1B is the proof of concept. S1A builds the grammar. S1B proves the student can use it. If a student produces fluent 2nd conditional under role-play pressure — with vocabulary, with a colloquial phrase, defending a position they did not choose — they are ahead of 90% of B1 learners in Mexico. Name that out loud. That moment is your testimonial.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • S1B benchmark summary: if student produced 6+ correct 2nd conditional instances across the session (error correction + role-play + closing statement) and used 1+ S1A vocabulary word naturally in speaking context → S1B is complete at B1.2 standard. Note this for S2A warm-up reference.
  • Flashcard wall: do not skip, even if running late. 10 cards × 5 seconds each = less than 1 minute. The ritual creates closure and cements vocabulary at the session end. It is part of the GT product identity.
  • Checklist: have student do it themselves — tap each item they actually achieved. Items they cannot check are notes for S2A warm-up. Do not mark it for them.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Closing ritual: "One sentence. Your real job. A real situation that is happening right now — or happened recently. In 2nd conditional. No preparation. Go." If they produce it cleanly: "That is it. That is what we built today. That sentence did not exist in your professional English two classes ago."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Upsell: "S2A is a completely different topic — different vocabulary, different grammar focus. But the role-play skill, the dilemma-handling, the 2nd conditional as a tool for real argument? That carries through the entire program. You did not just learn a grammar point today. You built a professional communication skill."
  • Testimonial trigger: "That sentence you just gave me — the real one, no notes. Say it one more time. Clean. That is your before-and-after moment. Remember how you felt trying to say it in Week 1."
