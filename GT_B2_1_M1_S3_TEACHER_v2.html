§1 · Warm-Up

Rapid Fire

10 questions · 15 seconds each · no preparation · speak first, think later

⏱ 5 min
Rapid Fire: Team Edition
S3 Rotation. No discussion, no deliberation. The student answers in 15 seconds — you move on. The goal is to activate instincts, not to get correct answers. Speed is the method.
5:00
Warm-Up
Teacher Protocol

Read each question once. Student answers immediately. If they go over 15 seconds, cut them off — "Time. Next question." No elaboration, no correction. After all 10, use 1–2 answers as a bridge into §2. This is activation, not assessment.

Q 01
Whose job is it to motivate the team?

Q 02

Q 03

⏱ 15 sec
Q 04

⏱ 15 sec
Q 05

⏱ 15 sec
Q 06
Should a team leader always know everyone's role — or is that micromanagement?
⏱ 15 sec
Q 07

⏱ 15 sec
Q 08
What makes someone truly step up — skill, ambition, or pressure?
⏱ 15 sec
Q 09
Should teams be built around strengths — or built to cover gaps?
⏱ 15 sec
Q 10

⏱ 15 sec
🔵 Facilitation
🩷 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • The 15-second rule is real — enforce it. The discomfort of the cutoff is pedagogically useful; it teaches students to commit to an answer before it's fully formed. That's what real professional conversations require.
  • Q 04 and Q 07 will produce the richest material. Note the student's exact words — you'll recycle them in §3 Phase 3 as a discussion anchor.
  • Bridge out: after Q 10, say — "Every single one of those questions comes down to two things: roles and trust. That's what we're working with today."
  • S3 Rotation: new format, new pace. If students have done S1 and S2, they're already calibrated. Confirm verbally before starting.
  • Students take too long on Q 01: Expected. It's deceptively broad. Push — "First instinct. Go." Don't let them turn it into a discussion.
  • Q 03 answer is unclear: Some students conflate underperforming and not owning. Valid. Don't correct — note it for §3.
  • Monosyllabic answers ("Yes." / "The leader."): Fine for questions 1–3. After Q 4 push slightly — "One more word — why?" Never more than one follow-up.
  • Q 07 creates debate: If two students strongly disagree in real time, cut it — "We're coming back to this. Move on." Save energy for §3.
  • After the round, ask one student to reformulate their answer to Q 3 or Q 4 using a conditional: "If nobody takes ownership, then..." — plants Third Conditional instinct before §3.
  • For groups with strong B2.1 fluency: run a second round at 10 seconds per question. The reduced time forces cleaner syntax.

§2 · Vocabulary

20 Words

10 nouns + 10 verbs · team roles lexical set · click any word to reveal definition

⏱ 10 min
Vocab Activation: Team Roles Set
Click any word to reveal its contextual definition. Teacher-paced. In Student Mode, definitions are hidden. These 20 words must appear in §3 and §4 — track production actively.
10:00
Vocabulary
Nouns (10)
roleThe specific function or position a person holds within a team or organization
strengthA skill, quality, or ability that someone consistently delivers at a high level
dynamicThe pattern of interaction, energy, and behavior between people in a team
contributionThe specific value or effort that someone adds to a team or project outcome
gapA missing skill, resource, or function that the team needs but doesn't currently have
expectationWhat someone is assumed or required to deliver in their role, often unspoken
ownershipTaking full responsibility for a task or outcome — even when it's difficult or unassigned
alignmentWhen team members share the same goals, priorities, and understanding of their roles
trustThe belief that a colleague will do what they say, reliably and with good intentions
conflictTension or disagreement between people — often over roles, expectations, or resources
Verbs (10)
assignTo officially give a person a task, role, or area of responsibility
delegateTo transfer a task or decision to someone else, while keeping accountability
rely onTo depend on a person or process because you trust they will deliver
step upTo voluntarily take on more responsibility — especially when no one else does
coverTo temporarily take on someone else's tasks or responsibilities to fill a gap
underperformTo consistently deliver results below what is expected for your role
recognizeTo formally or informally acknowledge someone's contribution, effort, or strength
leverageTo use a strength, resource, or advantage to get a better result
supportTo help a teammate do their job better without taking over their responsibility
collaborateTo work jointly with others — sharing information, decisions, and outcomes
Activation Activities
Activity A — Team Diagnostic (4 min)

Each student selects 3 words — one noun and two verbs — that best describe their team at work right now. They share why, one sentence per word. No justification needed beyond the sentence itself.

Target Output — B2.1

"Right now, the biggest gap in my team is that nobody really wants to step up when the manager isn't watching — and we never recognize contributions out loud."

Activity B — Verb Pairs (3 min)

Ask students to contrast pairs of verbs: What's the difference between delegate and assign? Between support and cover? Between collaborate and rely on? They discuss in pairs — 45 seconds per pair.

Key Distinctions

Delegate vs. Assign: You delegate authority along with the task. You assign just the task — authority stays with you.

Support vs. Cover: Support = help them do it. Cover = do it for them because they can't.

Collaborate vs. Rely on: Collaboration is mutual. Relying on = one-directional dependency.

Activity C — Vocabulary Tracking (3 min)

Tell students to use at least 5 of these 20 words during §3 and §4. You will note every instance on a scratch list. At the end of §4, the Fluency Self-Check asks them to self-report. This creates conscious attention to production — not just passive recognition.

🔵 Facilitation
🩷 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • Priority words for tracking: ownership, alignment, step up, leverage, dynamic — these appear in the Info Gap and Discussion Questions by design. Students who internalize them early will produce more authentic output.
  • Relay on is two words as a phrasal verb. Write "rely on" on the board — never "relyon." State this once.
  • Time allocation: Activity A = 4 min / B = 3 min / C = 3 min. Activity B is the most productive per minute — prioritize it if time is tight.
  • The Vocabulary Tracking in Activity C is not punitive — frame it as a game: "I'm keeping score. Every word you use naturally — not forced — counts."
  • "Alignment" feels abstract: Ground it — "When everybody on the team is talking about the same goal, the same deadline, the same definition of success — that's alignment. When they're not — that's misalignment."
  • "Ownership" confusion with legal ownership: Clarify immediately — "In this context, ownership doesn't mean you own something. It means you own the responsibility. You're the one who has to make it happen."
  • "Leverage" feels business-jargon: Valid. Use it in a concrete sentence — "If Elena is the best presenter on the team, we should leverage that strength in the client meeting — not leave her in the back office."
  • Collocations extension: what goes with role? → define a role / step into a role / take on a role / fill a role / blur the lines of a role
  • For strong students: ask them to use one vocab word in a Third Conditional structure before §3 begins — "If we had leveraged Elena's strength earlier, the dynamic would have been completely different."

§3 · Discussion Questions

Discussion

3 phases · personal → professional → provocative · 15 minutes total

⏱ 15 min
Team Roles & Strengths: Discussion
Three escalating phases. Each phase targets a different conversational register. The Third Conditional appears naturally in Phase 3 — do not introduce it explicitly. Harvest student sentences and use them live.
15:00
Discussion
Phase 1 · Personal
Pairs · 3 min
Discussion Q1
What role do you typically take on in a team — without being asked? And what role do you always avoid?
"Push: What would change in the team dynamic if you stopped covering that gap? Or if someone finally recognized the role you play?"
Discussion Q2
Think of a team experience — work or otherwise — where strengths were genuinely well-distributed. What made it different?
"Push: What was the alignment like? Did anyone have to underperform their actual potential to fit the team structure?"
Phase 2 · Professional
Group · 5 min
Discussion Q3
In your current team or company: are roles clearly assigned — or does ownership get assumed and confused?
"Push: Who actually delegates in your team? Who assigns without authority? Is there a gap between the org chart and the real dynamic?"
Discussion Q4
What's one strength that exists in your team that nobody is leveraging properly right now?
"Push: Why hasn't it been leveraged? Is it a visibility problem, a trust problem, or a role problem?"
Phase 3 · Provocative
Group · 5 min
Discussion Q5 — The Pressure Question
"If someone on your team has been underperforming for months — and nobody has said anything — who is really responsible for that?"
↳ Push for 3rd Conditional: "If we had addressed it earlier…"
"Push: Was it a gap in ownership? A conflict the team avoided? Would the outcome have been different if someone had stepped up sooner? Who had the responsibility to recognize it — and chose not to?"

Target sentence: "If we had addressed the gap earlier, the dynamic wouldn't have deteriorated the way it did."
Teacher Harvest — Phase 3

When a student produces a Third Conditional sentence naturally — write it on the board immediately, uninterrupted. After Phase 3, read 2–3 of the harvested sentences back to the group: "This is what B2.1 English sounds like in a real conversation. You just produced it." No grammar explanation — just affirmation of production.

🔵 Facilitation
🩷 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • The phase structure is linear — do not skip or reorder. Personal → Professional → Provocative is a deliberate escalation. Phase 1 builds psychological safety for Phase 3.
  • Phase 1 in pairs: While students talk, you circulate and listen. Don't intervene — note 2–3 interesting sentences for live use in Phase 3.
  • Phase 3 timing: Give it the full 5 minutes. This is where the session earns its depth. If Phase 2 ran long, cut it — not Phase 3.
  • Third Conditional is embedded in the push question of Q5 — never frame it grammatically. If students produce it naturally, harvest it. If they don't, model one sentence yourself and move on.
  • Phase 1 stays surface-level: Pairs are being polite. Prompt — "Stop being kind. What role do you actually avoid?" Naming the avoidance unlocks real conversation.
  • Q3 stays abstract: Ground it — "Think of a specific project from the last 6 months. Who owned it on paper? Who owned it in reality?"
  • Phase 3 produces silence: Expected. Let it sit for 4–5 full seconds before prompting. Silence in response to a difficult question is linguistically significant — it means students are processing.
  • Students blame the underperformer: Push back gently — "But the team knew for months and said nothing. What does that tell us about ownership and alignment?"
  • For advanced students in Phase 3: ask them to argue the opposite position — that it was NOT a team failure, only an individual one. Forces adversarial reasoning.
  • If a student produces a particularly strong sentence in Phase 3, use it as the basis for the §6 Speaking Challenge prompt — personalize the challenge to their output.

§4 · Speaking · Info Gap

Info Gap

Nexo Solutions team profile · Card A & Card B · reconstruct, exchange, recommend

⏱ 20 min
Info Gap: Team Nexo Solutions
Student A and Student B each hold partial information about a real team. They cannot show their card. They must exchange information orally, complete the team profile, and jointly produce a recommendation.
20:00
Info Gap
Step-by-Step Instructions for Teacher
  1. Assign roles (1 min): Tell one student they are "Student A" and the other "Student B." For groups of 3+, assign multiple A/B pairs and run simultaneously.
  2. Card reveal (1 min): Show Student A their card view on-screen. Show Student B their card view. They have 60 seconds to read — they cannot show each other their screen.
  3. Information exchange (8 min): Students ask questions to fill in the gaps. They may NOT read directly from their card — they must rephrase and explain. Monitor: are they using vocabulary? Are they asking clarifying questions?
  4. Shared reconstruction (4 min): Both students together reconstruct the full team profile from memory — no card reference. They complete a joint verbal summary.
  5. Recommendation (4 min): Based on the full profile, they agree on ONE team recommendation to give Elena Voss. They must justify it using at least 2 vocabulary words and one conditional sentence.
  6. Debrief + Fluency Self-Check (3 min): See self-check panel below.

Recommendation Task
Joint Prompt for Students A + B

"You've just completed the team profile for Nexo Solutions. Elena Voss has 10 minutes with you. What is your single most important recommendation for the team — and why?"

Requirements: use at least 2 vocabulary words · include at least 1 conditional sentence · agree on ONE recommendation, not multiple.

Target Recommendation Examples
  • "The team needs to redefine role boundaries — specifically between Elena and Rodrigo. If they hadn't let the overlap continue, the trust issue wouldn't have deepened."
  • "Elena needs to delegate more and leverage Marco's technical strength in client-facing contexts. If she had done that from the start, the dynamic would have been stronger."

🔵 Facilitation
🩷 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • The 60-second reading phase is crucial — students must form expectations before asking. If they skip it and go straight to questions, the language becomes less precise.
  • Monitor during the exchange phase for reading aloud from the card — the most common shortcut. Enforce rephrasing: "Don't read it — tell me what it says."
  • For groups of 3: assign one student as an "observer" who takes notes on vocabulary use during the exchange. They report back during the Fluency Self-Check.
  • Recommendation phase: enforce the ONE recommendation rule. Students often produce lists — push — "If you could only say one thing to Elena Voss, what would it be?"
  • The Fluency Self-Check is student-operated — reveal it after the recommendation, not during. It should feel like a reflection, not a checklist to complete while speaking.
  • Students can't form questions: Model one — "What can you tell me about the Tech Lead's gap?" Then let them continue.
  • Vocabulary doesn't appear in the exchange: Stop after 3 minutes and ask — "Can anyone use the word 'alignment' to describe something in your card?" One prompt is enough.
  • Recommendation is vague: Push — "More specific. What should Elena Voss do on Monday morning?"
  • Fluency Self-Check: students all tick everything: Ask them to give you an example of the conditional or discourse marker they used. If they can't — the box unchecks itself.
  • Role reversal: after the first round, swap cards. Student A gets Card B and vice versa. They now argue from the opposite perspective — generates a second recommendation and tests real comprehension vs. card-reading.
  • Advanced: ask students to write Elena Voss a 3-sentence email summarizing the recommendation — using Third Conditional and at least 3 vocab words. This becomes a written speaking-to-writing bridge.

§5 · Colloquials + Idiom Spotlight

Real Language

5 colloquial phrases · 3 idioms · register & context · production prompts

⏱ 5 min
Colloquial Phrases: Team Register
5 phrases native speakers use in real team conversations. Present each with context — then ask students to use them immediately in a sentence about their own team. Speed matters here.
5:00
Colloquials
"That's not in my lane."
InformalRole boundary
Used when declining a task because it falls outside your assigned role or area of expertise. Implies awareness of role clarity — or sometimes, avoidance of responsibility.
Context: "Elena asked me to handle the client presentation, but honestly — that's not in my lane. That's Rodrigo's."
"I'll take the lead on this."
Neutral–FormalOwnership · Step up
Used to voluntarily claim ownership of a task or initiative. Strong signal of confidence and team contribution — the opposite of waiting to be assigned.
Context: "Nobody said anything in the meeting. So I said: I'll take the lead on this. And suddenly everyone was relieved."
"We dropped the ball."
InformalTeam failure · Collective ownership
Acknowledging a collective failure — something the team was responsible for and didn't deliver. Key: "we" — this is about shared accountability, not individual blame.
Context: "The deadline was last Friday. No one followed up. We dropped the ball — and now the client is asking questions."
"That's a team effort."
NeutralRecognizing contribution
Distributing credit across the team — not just the visible contributor. Can be genuine recognition or a polite deflection when someone receives individual praise they want to share.
Context: "The board loved the presentation. But honestly — that's a team effort. Marco built the data behind every single slide."
"I've got your back."
Informal–WarmTrust · Support
Expressing active support and reliability. A trust signal — it means: if something goes wrong, I will support you and not leave you alone. High emotional value in team culture.
Context: "She was nervous presenting to the director. I told her before the meeting: don't worry — I've got your back. And I was there to answer the technical questions."
💡 Idiom Spotlight — 3 Team Idioms
▼ Expand

Three idioms that appear in real company conversations about teams. Present each with the dramatized scene — ask students to identify the meaning before you reveal it.

Wear too many hats
To take on multiple roles or responsibilities simultaneously, often to the detriment of focus or performance.
Scene — Nexo Solutions, Monday morning: Elena sits at her desk at 7:45 AM. She's writing a technical spec, answering a client email, and reviewing a sales proposal at the same time. Marco walks in: "Are you okay?" She doesn't look up. "Someone has to do it." — She wears too many hats, and eventually, everything she's wearing starts to slip.
Carry the whole team
To contribute so much more than others that you're essentially doing the work for the whole group — often with resentment building underneath.
Scene — Q3 review, Nexo Solutions: Rodrigo closes four new accounts in a single quarter. The team celebrates. But at the post-mortem, he says quietly — "I don't want to celebrate. I can't keep carrying the whole team. If the alignment doesn't improve, I'm out." Nobody says anything. The dynamic shifts in the room.
Be on the same page
To share the same understanding, goals, or expectations — especially before beginning a project or making a decision.
Scene — Nexo Solutions kickoff, Project Latigo: Elena starts the meeting: "Before we go any further — are we all on the same page about who owns what?" Silence. Marco thinks Sales leads delivery. Rodrigo thinks Tech does. Elena thought everyone had read the brief. They were not on the same page. The project nearly collapses in the first week.
Quick Production Round (inside §5 — 2 min)

One prompt per student. They use one colloquial phrase OR one idiom in a sentence about their actual team — not a hypothetical. 20 seconds each, no hesitation.

Prompt

"Use one of today's phrases or idioms to describe something real that's happening — or has happened — on your team. Go."

🔵 Facilitation
🩷 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • Move fast through the 5 phrases — 30 seconds per phrase max. Context line + one student sentence = done. If you linger, you lose §6.
  • Idiom Spotlight: expand it live in class. Cover the definition — show only the scene. Ask students to define the idiom from the scene before you reveal the definition. This is discovery-first, not explanation-first.
  • "That's not in my lane" and "I'll take the lead" are the two highest-yield phrases for B2.1 corporate learners in Mexico — these appear constantly in multinational English. Spend one extra sentence on each if time permits.
  • These phrases bridge colloquial and formal — make the register explicit: "You'd say this in a team meeting, not in a formal client proposal."
  • Students don't have a sentence ready: Use the Elena Voss / Nexo Solutions context from §4 — "Use the phrase about Elena Voss. Or Rodrigo." The fictional team is a safe production context.
  • "Drop the ball" is confused with sports: Brief clarification — it comes from American football. In English, it's now used in any context where someone fails to follow through. Don't over-explain.
  • Idiom Spotlight scene is too long: Read it dramatically — don't just read it. Pause at the key moment. The theater matters more than the speed here.
  • Ask students to write their own dramatized scene (3–5 sentences) for one idiom, using a situation from their real workplace. This becomes homework option 3 in §6.
  • Register challenge: can they make "I've got your back" formal? ("I'm fully committed to supporting your success on this initiative.") — connects back to the Register Switch from S2.

Global Feedback Note — Teacher Script

After the challenge round, deliver ONE group-level observation — under 45 seconds:

"Today, the group [STRENGTH — specific vocabulary or structure produced]. One area to develop before M2 is [GAP — forward-facing, not retrospective]. In Module 2, we move into [PREVIEW]."

🩷 Challenges
✨ Extension
  • The 60-second timer is non-negotiable — run it per student individually using the per-student timers above. Visible time creates urgency. Students who know the timer is on them produce 30–40% more sustained output than in open prompts.
  • Use the Third Conditional requirement as a live production check — if a student finishes without producing one, stop them: "Wait. Give me the conditional sentence now. What would have been different?" They add it before the round closes.
  • If a student finishes before 60 seconds: "Keep going. Add one more example." Never let them sit in silence when the timer is running.
  • Homework: assign based on what was missing in the session — if students struggled with Third Conditional, HW2 is mandatory. If colloquials didn't appear in production, HW3.
  • Student can't reach 60 seconds: Scaffold — "Tell me a second example. Now tell me what would have been different. Now use one of the phrases." Each prompt adds 10–15 seconds.
  • Third Conditional not produced in the challenge: After their minute, ask directly — "Give me one conditional sentence about your team. Just one." Production under direct prompt still counts.
  • No homework engagement: Assign HW1 as a minimum — it requires only observation, not language production. Students who resist writing can voice-note instead and transcribe in S4.
  • Strong students: add constraint to the 60-second challenge — they must use all 5 colloquial phrases in their answer. Forces creative integration and demonstrates mastery.
  • For very high performers: ask them to produce the speaking challenge in the formal register — no colloquials, no idioms — formal B2+ vocabulary only. Demonstrates register flexibility.
