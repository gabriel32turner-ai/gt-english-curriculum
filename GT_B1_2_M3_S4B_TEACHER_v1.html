🛡️ SIDE A — Privacy is a Right
Your Data Cannot Be Sold Without Real Consent
"We are being watched, tracked, and monetized without our meaningful agreement. The law is being ignored by corporations who profit from our ignorance."
Sentence starters with Passive:
"Our data is being collected and sold without our real knowledge..."
"Consent forms are designed to confuse — not to inform..."
"People are being profiled without ever being told what's built from their data..."
"Regulations have not been enforced strongly enough to protect users..."
"Billions of dollars have been made from information that was never truly given freely..."
💱 SIDE B — Data Is the Price You Pay
Free Services Have Always Been Paid for With Data
"These apps are offered for free. In exchange, data is shared with partners. The deal is transparent if you read the terms. The question is: are you willing to pay money instead?"
Sentence starters with Passive:
"The terms are published and can be read by anyone willing to look..."
"Services worth billions are given to users at zero cost — something has to cover that..."
"Data collection has been normalized precisely because most users find the trade acceptable..."
"If data were truly protected, these services would have to be paid for..."
"Regulations are being written — but they take time. The system isn't perfect, but it isn't criminal..."

§3
T&C Decoder — Relay Round
5 real-style Terms & Conditions clauses · Each in Passive Voice · Decode from legal to human · Accept or reject
⏱ 8 MIN
📜 TERMS & CONDITIONS DECODER
Each clause is written in legal Passive Voice. Relay: one student per clause — read it aloud, decode it to plain English, then vote: ACCEPT or REJECT?

Click Decode to reveal the plain-English version and the grammar insight. Earn 🎤 badge when all 5 are decoded.

Clause 1 — Data Collection
"Information may be collected from your device, including but not limited to location data, browsing history, and contact lists, and may be used to improve the services offered by affiliated partners."
Plain English: "We will collect your location, browsing history and contacts. We might share this with other companies."
Grammar: "may be collected / may be used" — Modal Passive. The word "may" makes it sound optional — it isn't. The agent ("we") is completely absent. Who collects? Who uses? The passive hides both.
Verdict question: Would you accept this? What would you need to know before clicking "Agree"?
Clause 2 — Third-Party Sharing
"User data may be shared with third-party service providers who are contractually required to maintain appropriate security standards. Such sharing is considered necessary to operate the platform."
Plain English: "We share your data with other companies. They promise to keep it safe. We decided this is necessary — you don't get a say."
Grammar: Three passives — "may be shared / are required / is considered." Notice "is considered" — by whom? The passive removes the decision-maker entirely. This is corporate language designed to sound inevitable.
Verdict question: "Are contractually required" sounds strong — but who enforces this contract? The passive hides the answer.
Clause 3 — Your Rights
"Requests to access, correct, or delete personal data will be processed within a reasonable timeframe. Some data may not be deleted where retention is required by applicable law or legitimate business interest."
Plain English: "We'll try to delete your data if you ask. But we might not. If we decide to keep it, we'll say it's for 'business interest' — which we define ourselves."
Grammar: "will be processed / may not be deleted / is required" — three passives that progressively remove your rights while sounding cooperative. "Reasonable timeframe" is undefined. "Legitimate business interest" is self-defined by the company.
Verdict: The passive voice makes your rights sound protected — but the agent who decides whether to respect them is invisible.
Clause 4 — Security Breach
"In the event that personal data is accessed by unauthorized parties, affected users will be notified in accordance with applicable regulations. The Company cannot be held responsible for losses arising from such incidents."
Plain English: "If your data gets stolen, we'll tell you eventually. But we're not paying for the damage — that's your problem."
Grammar: "is accessed / will be notified / cannot be held responsible" — the final passive is the most revealing. "Cannot be held responsible" uses passive to convert a legal escape clause into something that sounds like a fact of nature. It isn't — it's a choice.
Verdict question: If the company cannot be held responsible, who CAN be? The passive makes responsibility disappear entirely.
Clause 5 — Changes to Terms
"These terms may be updated at any time without prior notice. Users are considered to have accepted the revised terms if the service is continued to be used following notification."
Plain English: "We can change the rules anytime. If you keep using the app, we'll say you agreed — even if you didn't read the new terms."
Grammar: "may be updated / are considered / is continued to be used" — the most complex passive chain in the set. "Are considered to have accepted" is a legal fiction: your continued use is reframed as active consent. The passive makes this seem neutral and inevitable.
The B1.2 insight: Passive Voice in legal language doesn't just hide agents — it manufactures consent that was never really given.
⚡ Teacher Note

Award 🎤 when all 5 are decoded. Debrief question: "After reading all 5 clauses — do you think the Passive Voice is being used accidentally in legal language, or deliberately? What does your answer tell you about who wrote these terms and what they wanted to hide?" This is the highest-level metacognitive moment of the module.

What does "consent" mean in data privacy?
Your voluntary agreement to allow something to be done with your data.
"Your profile ___ built from your browsing habits." (Present Perfect Passive)
"Your profile has been built from your browsing habits."
What is a "data breach"?
When private data is accessed by unauthorized people — security is violated.
Use "That's sus" in a data privacy sentence.
"The app asked for access to my microphone just to show me recipes — that's sus."
"Millions of user records ___ exposed last year." (Past Passive)
"Millions of user records were exposed last year."
Why do companies use Passive Voice in Terms & Conditions?
To hide who is responsible for data use — making it sound natural and inevitable, not chosen.
What does "surveillance" mean? Use it in a Passive sentence.
Being watched systematically. "We are being surveilled by apps we agreed to without reading the terms."
"Your data ___ sold to three companies since you installed this app." (Perfect Passive)
"Your data has been sold to three companies since you installed this app."

"The terms were designed to confuse users." (agent hidden — deliberately)

§6
Source Analysis — 4 Voices on Data Privacy
Same data scandal · Four perspectives · Different Passive structures · Different agendas
⏱ 8 MIN

All four sources respond to the same event: a major app was found to have sold location data to insurance companies. Read the language — analyse the Passive and the agenda behind it.

📱 Twitter / X — @PrivacyMatters · 1.8M followers
"EXPOSED: Your location has been sold to insurance companies WITHOUT your consent. Premiums are being raised based on where you live and where you go. This was allowed to happen because regulators failed us."
💬 Discussion: Three passives — each one assigns blame differently. "Was allowed to happen" — by whom? Who failed? How does passive make systemic failure sound like personal betrayal?
📄 App Company — Official Statement
"Data sharing practices are governed by our published Privacy Policy, which is made available to all users at the time of registration. All partnerships are conducted in compliance with applicable law. User trust is taken very seriously."
💬 Discussion: Four passives — zero mentions of "we." "Is taken very seriously" — by whom? How does this statement use Passive Voice to sound accountable while committing to nothing?
💬 WhatsApp — Tech Industry Insider (private)
"Look, this has been happening for years. Everyone knows location data is traded between companies. The difference is this time someone got caught. The law wasn't built to stop this — it was built to make it look regulated."
💬 Discussion: The last two passives are the most cynical: "wasn't built to stop this / was built to make it look regulated." Who built the law? The passive here implies institutional conspiracy. Does that make it more or less credible?
📰 Investigative Report — Digital Rights Review
"Location data was sold to at least seven insurance providers over a 14-month period. Users were never notified. The data is being used to adjust premiums — a practice that has been documented in three other markets and was flagged by regulators in 2021 without action being taken."
💬 Discussion: This is the highest density of Passive Voice in the set — six structures. Why? This source is reporting facts, not opinions. The passive here sounds most credible precisely because it focuses on actions and results, not accusations. Compare this to the Twitter source — same passive forms, opposite emotional register.

Metacognitive Question — earn your 🔍 badge:

"Across all 4 sources, which use of Passive Voice do you trust most — and which do you trust least? Use Passive Voice to explain: 'In Source [X], responsibility is assigned to... In Source [Y], the agent is hidden because...'"

Students who can do this are demonstrating genuine B1.2 critical language awareness, not just grammatical competence.

Session Complete — 3 Phrases + Certificate + My Win
Take the language out of the classroom · Session 4B done
⏱ 5 MIN
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
Phrase 1
"Your data is being collected right now."
Use this to open any conversation about digital privacy. Present Continuous Passive makes it immediate and personal — not abstract. Stops the conversation dead and makes people listen.
Phrase 2
"This was designed to confuse — not to inform."
Use when analysing terms, contracts, or policies that are deliberately complex. Past Passive + contrast structure ("not to X, but to Y") sounds analytical and confident. Works in meetings and emails.
Phrase 3
"No real consent was ever given."
The most powerful phrase from this session. Past Passive + "ever" + negative makes it absolute and final. Use when challenging any process where someone claims agreement was obtained. Legally precise, rhetorically devastating.
