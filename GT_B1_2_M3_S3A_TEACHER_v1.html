§1
Have you ever cancelled someone?
Warm-Up · Activate opinion language · Connect to the Passive Voice in media context
⏱ 5 MIN
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds

Say this out loud right now in English: "In my opinion, cancel culture is [fair / unfair / complicated] because..." — one sentence. No notes. Just your real opinion.

5:00
Warm-Up
Question 1
Can you think of a famous person or brand who was "cancelled" in the last 2 years? What did they do?
💡 Try to use Passive: "He was accused of..." / "She was dropped by..."
Question 2
Is cancel culture "justice" or "mob mentality"? Where's the line between holding someone accountable and destroying them?
💡 "Accountability was demanded by..." / "The decision was made by..."
Question 3
Have you ever stopped following, buying from, or supporting someone because of something they said or did online? What happened?
💡 Be honest — this is the most authentic speaking you'll do today.
Question 4
Do cancelled people deserve a second chance? What would they need to do to be "forgiven"? Is forgiveness even possible in the internet age?
💡 "Forgiveness can be given if..." / "The apology was accepted / rejected by..."
⚡ Teacher Note

This topic generates the most authentic, unscripted speech of the module — let it run. Students who usually say little will have strong opinions here. Collect 3–4 Passive Voice structures on the board as students speak (e.g., "He was dropped by his sponsor"). Use them in §5 to connect grammar to real speech.

§2
Cancel Culture — Justice or Mob?
Speaking Topic · Carlos Mendez perspective · 3 structured rounds
⏱ 8 MIN
Today's Topic
Cancel Culture
Carlos Mendez, executive coach in Monterrey, has been asked to comment on a high-profile cancellation case involving a famous CEO in the tech industry. The CEO was fired, publicly shamed, and his company lost 40% of its stock value — all within 72 hours of a viral post. Carlos must decide: was this justice, or did the system go too far? Today you will take sides, argue, and use the Passive Voice to describe what happened — and why it matters.
Round 1 — Context
What was this CEO accused of? Do the facts justify what happened to him?
Use: "He was accused of... / The company was investigated for... / He was fired by..."
"He was accused of covering up workplace discrimination. The company was investigated by three different organizations. He was fired by the board within 48 hours — not by a court, not by a legal process, but by public pressure."
Round 2 — Debate
Is what happened to this CEO justice — or did the punishment go too far? Defend your position.
Use: "He deserved to be held accountable because... / The reaction was exaggerated because..."
"In my opinion, accountability was necessary — the problem was ignored for years. But the way it was handled was brutal. His entire career was destroyed before any facts were confirmed. Context was completely missed by the media."
Round 3 — Your Verdict
If you were Carlos Mendez, what would you say publicly? Defend or criticize cancel culture in 3 sentences.
Use: "Cancel culture can be used as a tool for... / It should be replaced by..."
"Cancel culture is sometimes used as a shortcut for accountability — but real accountability takes time. Systems need to be built, not just reactions. People should be judged by their actions over time, not destroyed by a single viral moment."
⚡ Teacher Note — After Round 2

Listen for Passive Voice attempts — reward them immediately. If a student says "Twitter destroyed him," write it on the board and ask: "How would this sound in a news article?" → "He was destroyed by public opinion." This is the organic grammar bridge.

§4
How They Actually Talk About It
Module 3 colloquial phrases · Natural in debate, opinion and reaction contexts
⏱ 5 MIN
"I'm hooked"
You can't stop consuming something — a show, a drama, a thread. Often used to admit digital addiction.
"I'm hooked on this whole cancellation drama — I've been reading about it for hours."
"It blew up"
Something became massive and viral very fast — a post, a controversy, an argument. Often before people can react intelligently.
"The whole thing blew up overnight — one tweet and suddenly everyone had an opinion."
"That's sus"
Sus = suspicious. Something feels wrong, dishonest, or questionable. Very common in digital conversations about public figures.
"He deleted all his posts right before the story broke? That's sus, honestly."
"I ghosted them"
To stop responding to someone completely — no explanation, no goodbye. Can apply to people, brands, or platforms.
"After they refused to apologize, I just ghosted them — unfollowed, unsubscribed, done."
"Low-key obsessed"
Quietly or secretly fascinated by something you might not want to admit publicly. Perfect for describing guilty pleasures.
"I'm low-key obsessed with following these cancellations — it's like a reality show."
⚡ Drill Note

Run a quick "Reaction Drill": give a scenario (e.g., "A CEO deleted all his tweets before a scandal broke") and students must react using one of these phrases naturally. The goal is spontaneous production, not memorization. "That's sus" and "It blew up" will appear naturally in S_B debates.

§5
Passive Voice — Cancel Court
Grammar referential · Interactive case files · Why Passive matters in media, ethics, and accountability
⏱ 12 MIN
REF Passive Voice — Why it's chosen in cancel culture language

FORM

TensePassive structure
PresentHis account is being reported right now.
PastShe was fired by the board last Friday.
PerfectHe has been accused of covering up the truth.
FutureThe case will be investigated by authorities.

WHY IT'S CHOSEN — The Metacognitive Insight

Hides the agent: "He was cancelled" — by whom? Everyone? No one? This creates power in language.

Focuses on the victim: "She was fired" puts the spotlight on the person affected, not the person who acted.

Sounds more formal/objective: News media uses passive to sound neutral — even when they're not.

⚖️ CANCEL COURT
Read each case file. Choose your verdict. See how Passive Voice carries the argument.

Each fact is written in Passive Voice. Click a verdict — then read the grammar insight that appears.

CASE FILE 01
The Tech CEO
He was accused of covering up sexual harassment complaints for 3 years.
His company was investigated by 4 journalists simultaneously.
He was fired by the board before any charges were filed.
His name has been removed from all company materials.
Grammar Insight

Notice: "He was fired by the board" — we know who acted. But "He was accused" — by whom exactly? The passive hides the source. In cancel culture, this ambiguity is often intentional.

"Accountability was demanded." → By whom? The public? One person? The passive generalizes.
"He was removed from all platforms." → One company? All of them? Passive makes it sound total.
CASE FILE 02
The Fitness Influencer
A video was shared of her mocking a fan's weight in a private chat.
She was dropped by 3 supplement brands within 24 hours.
Her apology was criticized as "fake" and "performative" by thousands.
Her account was never banned — she lost 40% of her followers instead.
Grammar Insight

Here the passive focuses on the result, not the agent: "She was dropped" — the brand decision matters, not the brand's name. The passive makes the punishment feel impersonal — like the universe punishing her, not specific humans.

"Her apology was criticized" → by fans, media, everyone simultaneously. Passive = collective judgment.
Active: "Brands dropped her." Passive: "She was dropped." Which sounds more final?
CASE FILE 03
The Sports Coach
A tweet was found from 2011 containing a homophobic joke.
The tweet was shared by an activist account with 2M followers.
He was suspended from his coaching role "pending investigation."
His team's championship was overshadowed by the controversy.
Grammar Insight

"A tweet was found" — notice: was found sounds accidental, neutral. But someone chose to search for it. The passive hides intention. This is how language shapes perception of guilt.

"He was suspended pending investigation." → No one is responsible. The system acts alone.
Compare: "The school suspended him" vs "He was suspended." Which feels more powerful?
CASE FILE 04
The Fashion Brand
An ad campaign was pulled after being called "tone-deaf" on social media.
The CEO was criticized for not responding for 72 hours.
Their products were returned or burned by hundreds of customers.
A donation was announced — but was called "too little, too late."
Grammar Insight

"Products were returned or burned" — this is the passive used for extreme collective action. The brand's image was destroyed not by one person, but by thousands acting together. Passive Voice captures that scale perfectly.

"A donation was announced" → Who announced it? The passive keeps the company distant from its own PR.
In PR crises, passive voice is used to sound responsible without admitting fault directly.

"She was fired" and "She was being fired" mean the same thing in English.
In cancel culture language, the Passive is often used to hide who is responsible for an action.

§6
The Podcast Debate — Was It Justice?
Two hosts argue about a recent cancellation case · Passive Voice throughout
⏱ 8 MIN
The Digital Dilemma Podcast — "Cancel Culture: Justice or Mob?"
B1.2 · M3 · S3A · Voice: Ryan (American) & Emma (British) · Speed: 0.85
Ryan: Welcome back to The Digital Dilemma. Today we're talking about cancel culture — and I want to start with a case that was discussed everywhere last week. A well-known sports journalist was accused of making racist comments during a private event. The comments were recorded without his knowledge and then shared on Twitter. Within hours, he was dropped by his network, his sponsors, and even his podcast platform. No investigation was conducted. No statement was requested. He was simply erased.

Emma: And you're framing this as unfair, but I'd argue the opposite. If those comments were made — regardless of whether they were recorded privately or not — they represent who this person really is. Accountability was finally demanded in a situation where institutions would have protected him. Without social media, nothing would have been done.

Ryan: But the speed is the problem. He was judged, convicted, and punished before any facts were verified. Real due process was completely bypassed. In a legal system, evidence has to be examined, context has to be considered. In cancel culture, a 10-second clip is enough.

Emma: I take your point. But context is often used as an excuse to avoid accountability. "Oh, he was misunderstood." How many times has that phrase been used to protect powerful men? I think when people are given the kind of platform he had — millions of followers, national broadcast — they are held to a higher standard. And they should be.

Ryan: Fair point. But I'd argue that the real power here wasn't the public — it was the algorithms. That clip was amplified by recommendation systems designed to maximize outrage. So really, the question is: was justice served by the public? Or was the whole thing orchestrated by a platform that profits from controversy?

Emma: That's a really good point, actually. So maybe the passive voice that should be used here is not "he was cancelled by the public" — but "the public was manipulated into cancelling him." That changes everything, doesn't it?

Ryan: Exactly. And that's why this conversation is so important. Because if we're going to talk about accountability, we need to ask: who is ultimately being served by all of this?

★
3 Phrases You Can Use This Week
Passive Voice in real situations · Professional, digital, and social contexts
Phrase 1
"He was never given a chance to respond."
Use this when discussing unfair situations — at work, in the news, or online. The Passive focuses on what was denied, not who denied it. Sounds analytical and measured.
Phrase 2
"The whole thing was blown completely out of proportion."
Perfect for describing overreactions — in meetings, in debates, or when something small was made into a huge issue. Passive + degree adverb ("completely") sounds sophisticated.
Phrase 3
"The full context was ignored."
Essential for any argument where someone misunderstood a situation. By not saying "who" ignored it, you sound less accusatory — but just as powerful. Use in debates, emails, and reports.
§7
Session Wrap-Up
What you can do now · 3 key insights · Preview Session 3B
⏱ 5 MIN
01
Passive hides — and reveals.
You can choose what to hide (the agent) or reveal (by + agent). This is a strategic choice, not just grammar. In cancel culture, passive is used as a weapon — or a shield.
02
All tenses work in Passive.
"is being accused" / "was fired" / "has been removed" / "will be investigated" — you now have all four. Each shifts the timeline and urgency of the story.
03
Context changes everything.
"He was cancelled" tells you nothing. "He was cancelled by an algorithm-driven mob" tells you everything. The language you choose shapes what people believe about the story.
