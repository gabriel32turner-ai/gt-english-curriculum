Who is this program for?

  • Students who completed A2.1 at Gabriel Turner English Training
  • Professionals who can handle past and future but struggle with modals and nuance
  • Corporate employees who need to express obligation, advice and possibility at work
  • Adults ready to close their full A2 level and move towards real intermediate English

By the end of A2.2, students will be able to:

  • Talk about past habits and career changes using used to
  • Express workplace rules and obligations with must and have to
  • Give professional advice and suggestions using should and could
  • Express possibility and confirmed plans with may/might and present continuous
  • Compare products, candidates and options using comparatives and superlatives
  • Introduce themselves and communicate naturally at a networking event

01
Habits Then & Now
Past habits · Life and career changes · Used to vs Simple Past · Then vs now
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Describing how your career or daily life was different in the past
  • Talking about old habits that no longer exist
  • Comparing past and present work situations
  • Describing how companies or industries have changed
  • Using not any more to mark a change from past to present
  • Telling a short career narrative: where you started vs where you are now
Grammar
Used to — full system (aff. / neg. / ?) I used to / I didn't use to / Did you use to…? Short answers: Yes, I did. / No, I didn't. Used to vs Simple Past — repeated habit vs single past event Not any more — marking change: I don't work there any more. Simple Past (consolidation from A2.1)

Functional English
  • I used to work in sales, but now I work in operations.
  • Did you use to travel a lot for work? — Yes, I did. I visited clients every week.
  • I didn't use to like early meetings, but now I prefer them.
  • She used to live in Monterrey. Now she works remotely from Celaya.
  • We used to have paper reports. These days everything is digital.
  • I don't commute any more — I work from home three days a week.
  • What did you use to do before this job? — I used to be a teacher.
Everyday English
  • "Things have changed."
  • "That was a long time ago."
  • "Back in the day…"
  • "Times change!"
  • "I'm not the same person I was."
Career Narrative (chunks)
  • "I used to work in X. Now I lead Y."
  • "I started as a… and moved into…"
  • "I've been here for… years."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🔴 False Friend: "I am used to" ≠ "I used to" — completely different structures. Raise awareness at A2.2, no full analysis needed
  • ⚠️ Error: "I use to go" → I used to go — the D is always required in affirmative form
  • ⚠️ Error: "I didn't used to" → I didn't use to — no D after didn't, same rule as Simple Past auxiliaries
  • 💼 LinkedIn bio use: "I used to work in X. Now I lead Y." — natural career narrative students can apply immediately
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "used to" /juːst tə/ — the D is barely audible in natural speech, commonly sounds like "yoosta"
Exit Task M1
Talk about 3 things you used to do at work or in your life that are different now. Use used to (positive and negative) plus not any more. Answer 3 teacher questions across used to and Simple Past. (Speaking · 4 min)
02
Rules & Obligations
Workplace policies · HR language · Safety rules · Permissions and prohibitions
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Describing workplace rules and company policies
  • Explaining what is required, optional or forbidden at work
  • Discussing health and safety procedures in the office
  • Asking about and clarifying obligations with colleagues or HR
  • Writing simple policy statements using must and have to
  • Distinguishing strong prohibition from absence of obligation
Grammar
Must — strong obligation and internal rule Mustn't — prohibition (it is forbidden) Have to — external obligation (company or law requires it) Don't have to — no obligation (optional, not necessary) Must vs Have to — internal vs external obligation Mustn't vs Don't have to — the key contrast at A2.2 Do you have to…? / Does she have to…? + short answers
Vocabulary

Functional English
  • You must sign in at reception before entering the building.
  • Employees must not share confidential information externally.
  • Do I have to attend all the meetings? — Yes, you do. They're compulsory.
  • You don't have to wear a suit — business casual is fine.
  • I have to submit this report by 5 PM. It's a hard deadline.
  • You mustn't use your personal phone during client meetings.
  • Does she have to travel to the client? — No, she doesn't. It's a video call.
Everyday English
  • "It's compulsory." — no choice
  • "It's up to you." — your decision
  • "That's the rule."
  • "No exceptions."
  • "It's not a big deal."
HR Policy (chunks)
  • "Employees must…"
  • "It is not permitted to…"
  • "Attendance is compulsory."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • ⚠️ The critical contrast: "You mustn't do it" (forbidden) vs "You don't have to do it" (not necessary) — one of the most common A2/B1 errors globally, always drill with real examples
  • 💼 HR policy language: "Employees must… / Employees are not permitted to…" — connects directly to real employee handbooks students use at work
  • 🌍 Cultural note: in US/UK companies, rules are written as "must/must not" in documents but spoken as "have to/don't have to" — teach both registers
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "have to" /hæftə/ in natural speech — the V becomes an F sound before T
  • 📋 Workplace policy activity: teacher and student write 5 real rules from the student's company using must/have to — immediate corporate output
Exit Task M2
Describe 4 rules from your real workplace using must, mustn't, have to and don't have to (one of each). Explain why each rule exists. Then write a 4-line company policy statement. (Speaking + written · 5 min)
03
Advice & Suggestions
Should/Shouldn't full system · Could for suggestions · Career and professional advice
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Giving and receiving professional advice at work
  • Making suggestions for solving a work problem
  • Advising a colleague on career decisions
  • Recommending actions for daily life and health situations
  • Asking for advice and responding to it naturally
  • Distinguishing recommendation from strong obligation
Grammar
Should / Shouldn't — full system (aff. / neg. / ?) Should questions: Should I call or email? / Should we postpone? Short answers: Yes, you should. / No, you shouldn't. Could — polite suggestions (not ability, not past tense) Why don't you / Why don't we — informal suggestions Should vs Must — recommendation vs strong obligation Giving reasons with should: You should prepare because…
Vocabulary
Career & Professional Development
CV / résumécover letterskillsexperiencequalificationpromotionraiseinterviewapplicationreferencenetworkLinkedInperformance review
Advice & Recommendations
advicesuggestionrecommendationoptionsolutionapproachideatipimprovement
Daily Life (mix)

Functional English
  • You should update your CV before the interview.
  • Should I send the proposal today or wait until Monday? — You should wait.
  • You shouldn't promise something you can't deliver.
  • Could you try calling instead of emailing? It's faster.
  • Why don't we set up a short meeting to discuss the options?
  • I think you should talk to your manager about the workload.
  • Should we ask for more time? — Yes, I think we should.
Everyday English
  • "If I were you…" — fixed chunk opener
  • "Have you thought about…?"
  • "That's not a good idea."
  • "It's worth trying."
  • "My advice would be…"
Interview Advice (chunks)
  • "You should prepare examples."
  • "You shouldn't be late."
  • "Why don't you research the company?"
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 💼 Career advice context: give advice for a job interview, a difficult email, a promotion request — high-value scenarios with real professional stakes
  • ⚠️ Error: "I suggest you to update" → You should update — "suggest" doesn't take "to + infinitive" — use "should" as the safe alternative at A2.2
  • 🔴 False Friend: "I recommend you to…" → use "I recommend + -ing" or simply "You should…" — cleaner and safer at this level
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "should" /ʃʊd/ — silent L, same family as would/could — always drill all three together as a set
  • 📋 LinkedIn recommendation language: "You should include…" / "I would recommend adding…" — connects to real student professional needs
Exit Task M3
Give advice to a colleague who has: (1) a job interview next week, (2) a difficult client situation, (3) too much work and no time. Use should, shouldn't and could in each answer with a reason. (Speaking · 4 min)
04
Possibility & Arrangements
May / Might · Present continuous for future · Uncertainty · Confirmed plans
10h
5 sessions  ·  Business
Communicative Topics
  • Expressing uncertainty about upcoming work events or decisions
  • Describing confirmed work appointments and travel arrangements
  • Asking and answering about possibility vs certainty
  • Booking and describing business travel itineraries
  • Contrasting what is certain, possible or uncertain in the future
  • Using present continuous for future in professional contexts
Grammar
May — possibility (around 50%+): The client may call tomorrow. Might — less certain (around 30–40%): She might be late. May not / Might not — negative possibility May/Might vs Will — certainty contrast (100% / 50% / 30%) Present continuous — confirmed future arrangements: I'm meeting him at 10. Present continuous vs Going to — arrangement vs personal intention No "to" after may/might — bare infinitive rule applies to all modals
Vocabulary
Travel & Hotel
flightboardingcheck-inhotelreservationdeparturearrivalconnectionlayoverluggagecarry-onvisaitineraryexpense report
Uncertainty & Possibility
maybeperhapspossiblyprobablyI'm not sureit dependswe'll seelikelyunlikely
Arrangements
appointmentreservationbookingconfirmationscheduleavailabilityarrangeset upcancelreschedule
Functional English
  • I might travel to Guadalajara next week — it depends on the client.
  • The meeting may be cancelled. I'm waiting for confirmation.
  • She might not be available on Monday — she may have a conflict.
  • I'm meeting the regional director on Thursday at 9 AM. It's confirmed.
  • We're flying to Mexico City on Tuesday — the tickets are booked.
  • Are you joining the conference? — I'm not sure. I might, but I may have another commitment.
  • What are you doing next Friday? — I'm having lunch with a client. It's already arranged.
Everyday English
  • "We'll see." — not certain yet
  • "It depends."
  • "I'll keep you posted."
  • "TBD." — To Be Determined
  • "It's in the diary." — confirmed
Travel Arrangements (chunks)
  • "My flight is at [time]."
  • "I'm checking in on Monday."
  • "I might need to extend my stay."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 🔵 May/Might vs Will: use a probability scale — "I will (100%) / I may (50%) / I might (30%) / I won't (0%)" — visual reference students always remember
  • ⚠️ Error: "He might to come" → He might come — no "to" after any modal verb — reinforce with the full modal set
  • 💼 Business travel: booking a hotel, checking the itinerary, confirming a flight — practical vocabulary + present continuous for confirmed arrangements
  • 🌍 Cultural note: "I'm meeting the client" (present continuous) sounds more professional and committed than "I'm going to meet the client" — teach this register difference
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "might" /maɪt/ vs "meet" /miːt/ — common listening confusion, always drill in clear context
Exit Task M4
Describe your schedule for next week using present continuous for future (3 confirmed plans). Then talk about 2 things that might or may happen but aren't certain yet. Contrast all three future forms. (Speaking · 4 min)

Comparing & Choosing
Comparatives · Superlatives · Products · Candidates · Travel options · Cities
10h
5 sessions  ·  Versatile
Communicative Topics
  • Comparing two or more products, services or suppliers
  • Evaluating job candidates using comparative language
  • Choosing between travel options for a business trip
  • Describing and comparing cities or locations
  • Justifying a choice with comparative reasoning
  • Using superlatives to describe the best or worst option
Grammar
Comparatives — short adjectives: faster / cheaper / bigger Comparatives — long adjectives: more expensive / more efficient Irregular comparatives: good→better / bad→worse / far→farther Than in comparisons: This option is better than the other one. As…as (equality) / Not as…as (inequality) Superlatives — short: the fastest / the cheapest / the best / the worst Superlatives — long: the most expensive / the most efficient Comparative adverbs: more quickly / more carefully / better
Vocabulary
Products & Services

Candidates & Jobs

Cities & Travel (mix)
safecrowdedmodernexpensiveconvenientwell-connectedcleanlivelyquietdirect (flight)
Functional English
  • This model is more reliable than the previous one, but it's also more expensive.
  • The second candidate is more experienced, but she's less available than the first.
  • Which option is better for us? — Option B is cheaper and faster, but A has better quality.
  • Mexico City is the most connected city in the country for business travel.
  • Our new product is the most competitive in the market right now.
  • Is the direct flight more expensive than the connecting one? — Yes, but it's much faster.
  • I think this proposal is better than the last one. The terms are more flexible.
Everyday English
  • "It's a no-brainer." — obvious choice
  • "There's no comparison."
  • "It's a better deal."
  • "It's worth the extra cost."
  • "The pros outweigh the cons."
Decision Language (chunks)
  • "I think option A is better because…"
  • "The best choice for us is…"
  • "It's not as good as the other one."
GT Originals — Exclusive Teacher Notes
  • 💼 Business comparison context: compare two proposals, two suppliers, two candidates — immediate corporate application with real decision-making stakes
  • ⚠️ Error: "more better" → better — never use "more" with irregular comparatives / "the most best" → the best
  • ⚠️ Error: "This is more good than…" → better than — irregular forms must be memorized as a set: good/better/the best
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "cheaper" /ˈtʃiːpər/ — the -er ending reduces to /ər/ in natural speech
  • 🌍 Cultural note: in business English, comparing always needs a justification — "I think option A is more practical because…" — the reasoning is as important as the comparison
Exit Task M5
Compare two real options from your work: two suppliers, two products, two candidates or two cities for a business trip. Use at least 4 comparatives and 2 superlatives with reasons. Present your recommendation. (Speaking · 5 min)
