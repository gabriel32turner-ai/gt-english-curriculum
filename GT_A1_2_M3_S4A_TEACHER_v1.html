§1
Warm-Up — Your Last Flight
⏱ 5–8 min · Quick-Fire + Quick Win
📋 Logistics

90-second Quick-Fire. Students answer from personal travel experience — no right or wrong answers here. The goal is to activate airport lexicon before formal input. Collect any object pronouns students use naturally (or fail to use) for §5 follow-up.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Q1: airport vocabulary activation — what can students already say?
  • Q2 forces object pronoun ("show me / tell me") in a functional context
  • Q3: prepositions of movement — elicit "past," "through," "along" if possible
  • Q4: student → teacher — reverses dynamic, builds confidence
💡 Corporate Link

Many students in Celaya and Bajío industries travel for supplier visits to Monterrey, CDMX, or Guadalajara — or receive international clients at GTO airport. This is immediately relevant professional English.

⚡ QUICK-FIRE · 90 SECONDS PER STUDENT

🟣 Teacher → Student
Q1 — Think about an airport you know — León/Bajío, CDMX, or Monterrey. What areas do you pass through when you arrive? Name as many as you can.
Push: "What do you do first? Where do you go after baggage claim?"
⭐ Student → Teacher
Q2 — A client from Chicago arrives at the airport and calls you: "I'm lost. I can't find the exit." What do you say to help them? (Notice: "help them" — object pronoun!)
Push: "Tell me the instructions step by step. Use: Go to… / Turn… / Follow the signs for…"
🟢 Student → Student
Q3 — You are at the gate with your partner. How do you get to the duty-free shop? Give directions using: Go straight / Turn left / Walk past / Go through
Push: "How long does it take to walk there? Use: It takes about… minutes."
🟣 Teacher → Student
Q4 — Your company is sending a client to Celaya from the US. They land at GTO Airport. How do you tell them to get from the airport to your plant? What do you say?
Push: "Give me 4 instructions. Start with: 'Take the exit to…' / 'Turn right on…'"
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds

Say this out loud right now: "Excuse me — where is the baggage claim? Can you show me?" That's airport English with a perfect object pronoun.

Speaking Topic — Receiving a Client at the Airport
⏱ 8 min · 3 rounds · Mónica Herrera context
📋 Logistics

3 rounds, 3 min each. Students speak about a real or imagined client arrival situation. Push for object pronouns in every sentence. Model answer is B1-level — use to show aspiration, not as the benchmark.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Round 1: personal airport experience — activate vocabulary
  • Round 2: giving instructions to a real person — imperatives + object pronouns
  • Round 3: "How long does it take?" — forces duration question practice
💡 Color Code

Blue = key vocab · Gold = grammar structure · Green = colloquial · Orange = imperative · Light violet = object pronoun

✈️
Receiving a Client at the Airport
Mónica Herrera needs to receive a client from San Antonio, Texas at the Bajío International Airport (BJX). The client doesn't speak Spanish. Mónica needs to help him find her — and then guide him to her car.
Round 1 — Your Airport Experience
Tell your partner about the last time you went to an airport. What did you do? Where did you go? How long did it take?
💡 Use: "I went to… / I had to… / It took about… minutes / The flight was…"
Round 2 — Guide the Client
Mónica texts the client: "I'm outside. Follow these instructions to find me." Write and say 5 step-by-step instructions using imperatives. Include: walk past / turn left or right / go straight / follow the signs for / take the exit to.
💡 Use: "Walk past the baggage claim. Turn left at the pharmacy. Follow the signs for arrivals."
Round 3 — How Long Does It Take?
The client asks: "How long does it take to get to your office from the airport?" Answer in full sentences. Then ask your partner: "How long does it take you to get to work every day?"
💡 Use: "It takes about… minutes / It takes [him/her] around… hours / It doesn't take long."

"Hi, I'm Mónica. Walk out of customs and follow the signs for Ground Transportation. Don't take the taxi stand — walk past it. Turn left at the exit and look for a white ConnectPro sign — that's me. You can't miss it. It takes about 45 minutes to get you to our office from here."

📋 Logistics

12 airport words — all with "Suena:" pronunciation (A1.2 = NO IPA). TTS button per card. Quiz: 5 questions, badge 📚 at 4/5. After each card, ask student to produce their own sentence before moving to the next.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Every example sentence uses an object pronoun — intentional cross-linking with §5
  • Priority words: departure gate, baggage claim, customs, boarding pass — highest frequency in business travel

"Embarcar" → students often say "to embark" in English. Correct: "to board" or "to board the plane." "Embark" exists in English but is formal/nautical — you would never say it at an airport counter.

Colloquial — Real Airport English
⏱ 5 min · 5 phrases · Say them out loud
📋 Logistics

5 phrases from real airport and business travel contexts. Student explains meaning in English — no Spanish. Every example uses a grammar point from this session (imperative, object pronoun, or How long). Push for own sentence before moving on.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus

Don't accept translation. If student doesn't know: "When do people say this? At the airport? In a taxi? On the phone?" — context clues unlock meaning. The goal is production, not passive recognition.

💡 Extension — Combine Grammar

Advanced: "You can't miss it — walk past the pharmacy, turn left, and you'll see it." → Imperative + object pronoun + direction. This is exactly what clients need from a Bajío professional.

You can't miss it.
It's very easy to find — very obvious or clearly marked
"Walk past the pharmacy and turn left. You can't miss it — the sign is huge."
I'm running late.
I'm behind schedule — I'll arrive after the planned time
"I'm running late. Please wait for me in the arrivals hall. I'll be there in 15 minutes."
It was a smooth trip.
Everything went well — no delays, no problems, easy travel
"Welcome! How was your flight? — It was a smooth trip, thank you. No delays at all."
It's a bit of a walk.
The destination is not close — you need to walk some distance
"The parking lot is a bit of a walk from the terminal — let me carry your bag."
Let me know when you land.
Send me a message or call me when your plane arrives
"Let me know when you land and I'll come pick you up immediately."

§5
Grammar — Object Pronouns + Imperatives + How Long?
⏱ 10 min · Airport Map · Activities A & B
📋 Logistics

Grammar is REFERENTIAL — brief table + Airport Navigation Map (interactive unique visual) + Activity A (MC, 5 questions) + Activity B (drag-and-drop, 2 sentences). Never drill conjugation — always connect to real airport situation.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Unique visual: click a scenario → the map highlights zones → student gives instructions using imperatives + object pronouns
  • Activity A: pronoun selection under context (not isolated grammar)
  • Activity B: sentence reorder with imperative + object pronoun
  • Poll: "How long does it take?" — forces duration structure
💡 Error to Anticipate

Students say "It takes TO me 30 minutes" → correct immediately: "It takes me 30 minutes" (no "to"). Also: "Go to straight" instead of "Go straight." The preposition "to" attaches to a destination, not to "straight."

🚨 Real Situation

Your client from Chicago just landed at BJX Airport. He calls you: "I have my luggage. Now what?" You need to tell him step-by-step where to go — and how long it will take.

❓ Which words do you need? How do you give instructions? How do you replace "your client" with one small word each time?

✅ The Tool: Imperatives + Object Pronouns + How Long

"Walk past customs. Follow the signs for arrivals. Don't take the taxi stand — walk past it. I'll meet you outside. It takes you about 5 minutes."

Imperatives = verb (base form) at the start — no subject needed. Use for instructions.
Object pronouns = replace people and things AFTER the verb. Avoid repetition.
How long = "How long does it take to get to [place]?" → "It takes about [time]."

How Long Does It Take?
QuestionAnswer PatternExample
How long does it take to get to [place]?It takes + [time]"It takes about 45 minutes."

⚠️ Top errors at this level — correct immediately:
✗ "Go to straight" → ✅ Go straight (no "to" before direction words)
✗ "It takes to me 30 minutes" → ✅ It takes me 30 minutes (no "to" before object pronoun here)
✗ "Please to wait" → ✅ Please wait (no "to" after please in imperatives)
✗ "Meet he at the gate" → ✅ Meet him at the gate (object pronoun, not subject)

Listening — Mónica's Client Is Lost
⏱ 8 min · Audio + MC + T/F
📋 Logistics

ElevenLabs placeholder — record Mónica (female, Mexican-accented English, professional, 0.80× speed). Scenario: phone call from airport — client is lost, Mónica gives step-by-step directions. 2 MC + 3 T/F. Play once for gist, twice for detail.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Target: imperatives + object pronouns in natural phone speech
  • Highlighted words in script = direct link to §3 vocab and §5 grammar
  • Speaking response: "What would you say in Mónica's position?"
💡 GT Original — False Friend in Script

"Embarcar" → students often translate to "embark." Script deliberately uses "board" — use this moment to contrast: "We board the plane" (NOT "we embark"). "Board" is always used at airports in English.

Client (James): Mónica? Hi, I just got my luggage. I'm at baggage claim. Now where do I go?

Mónica: James! Great, you're almost here. Listen — walk out of the baggage claim area. Don't go into the shops — walk past them. Follow the signs that say "Arrivals" — you can't miss it.

James: OK, I'm walking. I see a pharmacy... and security... where now?

James: How long does it take to get to your office from here?

Production — Speak, Navigate, Time It
⏱ 12 min · 4 speaking prompts · 60s Challenge
📋 Logistics

4 speaking cards — pairs. Switch roles after Q2. 60s challenge is individual: full phone call from arrival to ground transportation, no notes. Monitor for object pronoun usage — count every "him/her/them/it/us/me" heard.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus

Accuracy goal: imperatives without "to" + object pronouns after verb. Count both in the 60s challenge — 4 object pronouns minimum for badge. Partners track count on paper.

💡 Upsell Moment

At the end of this activity, mention: "The next session is Module 3 Exit — you'll present a full business trip from hotel check-in to airport navigation. This session is your preparation." That motivates students to practice between sessions.

Question 1 — Arrival Instructions
Your colleague from Mexico City is arriving at GTO Airport for the first time. She calls you from baggage claim. Give her 5 step-by-step instructions to reach your car outside.
💡 Use: imperatives + "her" + "Walk past / Turn / Go through / Follow / Don't…"
Question 2 — How Long?
Your international client asks 3 "How long does it take?" questions: to get from the airport to your office / to the hotel / to the plant. Answer all 3 in full sentences.
💡 Use: "It takes you about… / It takes us… by car / It doesn't take long if there's no traffic."
Question 3 — You Are the Driver
You're at ground transportation holding a sign. Two clients walk out. Greet them, introduce yourself, help them with their luggage, and give them 3 instructions for getting in the car.
💡 Use: "Let me help you with it." / "Please follow me." / "Put them in the trunk."
Question 4 — Lost Client!
Your client is lost. He's at security but went the wrong way and ended up at departures. He calls you. Give him new instructions to get back to arrivals. Use 3 object pronouns.
💡 Use: "Don't go through security. Walk back past…/ Turn left when you see… / I'll meet you there."

⭐ 60-SECOND CHALLENGE — No Notes

Full phone call: your client just landed. Guide him from baggage claim to your car outside — in 60 seconds. Include: 5 instructions, 2 "how long" answers, at least 4 object pronouns. Partner counts the pronouns.
