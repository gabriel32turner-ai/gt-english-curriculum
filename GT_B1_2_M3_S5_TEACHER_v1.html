Warm-Up — Digital Life in 60 Seconds
⏱ 8 min · Quick-Fire · Badge + Module Recap
📋 Logistics

Quick-Fire activation. Students answer each question in one sentence using Passive Voice. This surfaces errors before the grammar review in §4. Timer runs 30 seconds per question. Collect errors on the board — use them in §4 correction activity. 8 questions total — teacher chooses 5 based on time.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Target: activate M3 vocabulary and passive structures spontaneously
  • Collect 3–4 passive attempts — correct and star the best ones
  • Do NOT correct every error now — create curiosity for §4
💡 Improvement Over Previous S5

§1 opens with a 60-second Quick-Fire (not a standard warm-up) — this raises the energy immediately and shows the pace of a review session. Students who hesitate in the first question are given a model answer instantly, so momentum never drops.

⚡ Quick-Fire — 30 seconds per question · Answer in Passive Voice where possible

Model: "Customer service is now being handled by AI in many companies."

Use: "It blew up because..." / "It was shared by..."

Think of your phone. How many apps were designed to keep you scrolling? Can you name one?
Force the passive: "This app was designed to..." / "My attention is being directed by..."
CANCEL CULTURE
Has anyone you follow been cancelled or called out recently? What were they accused of?
Model: "She was accused of... / He was dropped by his sponsors..."
PASSIVE VOICE
Your data — do you know what it is being used for right now?
"My data is being collected by... / It is being sold to..."

⏱ 6 min · Self-Assessment · Determines Review Focus

Self-assessment before review activates metacognition — students stop being passive recipients and become active learners who know what they need. Research shows this single step improves retention by ~30% compared to uniform review.

💡 New Feature — Not in M2 S5

Grammar Review — Passive Voice + Passive Transformer
⏱ 16 min · Full review · Tense selector · Activities · Badge
📋 Logistics

Three parts: (1) Quick reference table — full Passive tenses. (2) Passive Transformer widget — student picks tense + context, widget generates the example and the WHY. (3) Two interactive activities — Activity A: identify tense + agent. Activity B: error correction. Badge at 6/7 correct across both.

🎯 Priority Correction

"My data is collected" (Present Simple) vs "My data is being collected" (Present Continuous Passive) — the most common B1.2 passive error. Correct this immediately if it appears. "Is collected" = general truth. "Is being collected" = happening now, active process. The digital context makes this contrast clear.

💡 New — Passive Transformer Widget

Instead of a static table, the Transformer lets students select a context and tense — and the system shows the structure + a real-world example from M3 topics. This makes the review active and production-oriented, not just recognition.

Activity A — Identify the Tense and the Agent's Status
Score A: 0 / 4
"Thousands of jobs are being replaced by AI every month."
Which Passive tense is this, and what does it tell us?
✓ Present Continuous Passive. "Is being replaced" = active, ongoing process — not a general truth. The agent "by AI" is revealed because we want to name the cause.
"His reputation has been destroyed — even though the investigation found nothing."
Why is Passive used here — and why is the agent hidden?
✓ Present Perfect Passive. The damage exists NOW — "has been destroyed" connects past action to present reality. Agent is hidden because responsibility is collective and diffuse.
"She was being watched without her knowledge."
What does "was being watched" imply that "was watched" doesn't?
✓ Correct. Past Continuous Passive adds duration and intention — it was a deliberate, ongoing act. "Was watched" sounds like a single event. The tense choice shapes how creepy it feels.
"More personal data will be sold to advertisers unless laws change."
Transform this into a sentence with the agent revealed — then explain why you might choose NOT to reveal it.
✓ Exactly. "Platforms will sell data" is direct and names a culprit. "Data will be sold" is impersonal — used when you want to sound like a legal or neutral warning. Both are correct; the choice is strategic.
Activity B — Spot and Fix the Passive Error
Score B: 0 / 3

1. "My data is collected right now by my phone." — What's wrong?

2. "He was accused and then the board fired him immediately." — How do you make this more impactful using Passive?

3. "The app was being designed to maximize engagement." — Is "designed" the correct past participle here?

Colloquial Review — All 5 M3 Phrases
⏱ 8 min · Review + Drill + Authentic Use
📋 Logistics

Review all 5 Module 3 colloquial phrases in context. After review: Drill — teacher reads a scenario, students react with the correct phrase in under 5 seconds. Fastest answer wins. The Poll at the end helps students vote on which phrase they actually used this week.

🎯 Authentic Use Target

By S5, students should be using these phrases without thinking. If a student uses "It blew up" or "That's sus" naturally in §7, award an instant bonus point. Colloquial fluency is a B1.2 benchmark — it signals true integration.

💡 Improvement — The Authenticity Poll

New: after the review, a class poll asks "Which phrase did you actually use this week in real life?" This makes colloquial integration a tracked metric, not just a classroom exercise. Normalizes using English outside class.

"I'm hooked"
Addicted, can't stop. Often about apps, shows, content.
"I'm hooked on this drama — my data is being harvested and I don't even care."
"It blew up"
Went viral extremely fast — often before anyone could respond.
"The video blew up — it was shared 2 million times before he was even notified."
"That's sus"
Suspicious — something feels dishonest or questionable.
"He deleted his posts the night before the investigation was announced. That's sus."
"I ghosted them"
Stopped responding completely — no warning, no explanation.
"After the brand was called out, I ghosted them — deleted the app, unsubscribed, done."
"Low-key obsessed"
Quietly or secretly fascinated — something you might not admit publicly.
"I'm low-key obsessed with following these cancel culture cases — it's like real-life drama."

After the week I've had — being interviewed by three different magazines, asked constantly about cancel culture and data privacy — I've been thinking. We say we control our digital lives. But do we?

Think about it. Your data is being collected right now. Not yesterday. Not last year. Right now. Every search, every pause on a video — it's all being tracked by systems designed not to serve you, but to predict you. And the predictions are sold to companies that want your attention and your money.

Cancel culture is the same story. A person is judged, condemned, and sometimes destroyed before any facts are confirmed. The context is almost never considered. And who benefits? The platforms. Because outrage is amplified by the algorithm — not by humans.

So what do I tell my clients? I tell them: be conscious of what you are being shown. Ask who benefits from what you believe. And remember — the most powerful thing you can do in the digital age is decide what gets your attention. Because your attention is being sold whether you like it or not. The question is — to whom?
