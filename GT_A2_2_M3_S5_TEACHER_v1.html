§2
Grammar Matrix — Both Systems, Side by Side
Advice modals vs Possibility modals · Full comparison · Consolidated review
⏱ 12 MIN
💡 Key insight for students: Module 3 has two separate modal systems. They look similar but do different jobs. "Should" gives advice. "Might" expresses possibility. A professional English speaker chooses precisely between them — and this matrix shows you how.
⚖️ Advice System — S1–S3
🎲 Possibility System — S4
should
Strong recommendation — clear advice
"You should prepare examples before the interview."
will
100% certain — confirmed fact
"The project will start on Monday — it's confirmed."
could
Gentle suggestion — optional option
"You could mention your LinkedIn experience if they ask."
may
~60% possibility — more likely than not
"The client may confirm tomorrow — I think it's likely."
shouldn't
Strong negative advice — warning
"You shouldn't make that decision without talking to HR."
might
~40% possibility — real but uncertain
"The delivery might be late — there's a transport issue."
Why don't you/we…?
Informal suggestion — collaborative
"Why don't we set up a quick meeting to discuss this?"
might not / may not
Negative possibility — probably won't
"She might not be available — she has a conflict."
If I were you… / My advice would be…
Colloquial openers — high register advice
"If I were you, I'd take the offer. My advice would be to negotiate first."
won't
0% — certain negative (it will NOT happen)
"The client won't attend — they cancelled in writing."
⚠️ Key difference — don't confuse these two: "You should call the client" = I recommend it (advice). "The client might call" = there's a 40% chance (possibility). One is about what a person should do. The other is about what might happen. Different grammatical subjects, different jobs, same word family.
⚠️ Top 4 errors — Module 3 (drill until automatic):
✗ "He might to arrive" → ✅ He might arrive — no infinitive "to" after ANY modal
✗ "I recommend you to update your CV" → ✅ You should update your CV — safer at A2.2
✗ "Maybe the client calls tomorrow" → ✅ The client might call tomorrow — modal, not adverb
✗ "It's possible that she might come" → ✅ She might come — don't double-hedge

Colloquial Flashcards — All 10 Phrases · Module 3

⚖️ Advice Phrases — Sessions 1–3
01 · Advice
"If I were you…"
Tap to reveal context →
The most natural way to give personal advice in English. It's hypothetical ("If I were you" = I'm imagining I'm in your position) and sounds empathetic, not commanding.
"If I were you, I'd take the promotion — it's a real opportunity."
02 · Advice
"Have you thought about…?"
Tap to reveal context →
Invites the person to consider an option you're suggesting without commanding. Much softer than "You should." Perfect for peer-to-peer advice at work.
"Have you thought about asking your manager for a formal review?"
03 · Advice
"That's not a good idea."
Tap to reveal context →
Direct but not aggressive. A professional way to push back on someone's plan. Always follow with an alternative using "could" or "should" — don't just shut them down.
"That's not a good idea — you could wait until after the review to bring this up."
04 · Advice
"It's worth trying."
Tap to reveal context →
Encourages action when someone is hesitating. It acknowledges risk ("might not work") but motivates anyway. Very natural in coaching and peer support conversations.
"I know it's scary, but applying for that role — it's worth trying."
05 · Advice
"My advice would be…"
Tap to reveal context →
The most formal and considered advice opener. Use when you want your recommendation to sound thoughtful and deliberate — especially in writing or presentations.
"My advice would be to document everything before the conversation with HR."
🎲 Uncertainty Phrases — Session 4
06 · Uncertainty
"We'll see."
Tap to reveal context →
Not passive — it means "the situation is unresolved and I'm monitoring it." Use after "might" statements. It's a closing phrase that signals awareness, not avoidance.
"The client might cancel — we'll see. I'll update you by Thursday."
07 · Uncertainty
"It depends."
Tap to reveal context →
Always add what it depends on — "It depends on the client / the budget / the director." Standalone "It depends" sounds evasive at A2.2 level. Complete the thought.
"Are you attending the conference?" — "It depends on the project timeline."
08 · Uncertainty
"I'll keep you posted."
Tap to reveal context →
Commits to follow-up communication. Always use AFTER expressing uncertainty — it's the professional way to leave an open loop closed enough that the other person stops worrying.
"The delivery might not arrive on time — I'll keep you posted as soon as I hear back."
09 · Uncertainty
"TBD."
Tap to reveal context →
To Be Determined. Common in agendas, emails, and project plans. Internationally understood. More direct than "we'll see" — it signals the decision is formally pending, not just unclear.
"Venue for Q4 kickoff: TBD — might be Monterrey or CDMX. Confirming next week."
10 · Uncertainty
"I'm not sure yet — let me check."
Tap to reveal context →
Honest, action-oriented, and professional. Much stronger than "I don't know" because it commits to finding out. Always pairs naturally with "might" — "I'm not sure if she might be available — let me check."
"Is Jorge joining the Monday call?" — "I'm not sure yet — let me check his calendar."

✅ FIX: "The client might call tomorrow — I'm waiting for confirmation." Use the modal.

✅ CORRECT — "Why don't we" + base verb. Collaborative suggestion, perfectly formed.

✅ FIX: "She might be late to the meeting." One expression of uncertainty is enough.

✅ FIX: "She should prepare examples for the interview tomorrow." No "to" after should.

Scenario 1 — The Colleague Ask
Your colleague Andrea asks: "I have a job interview next week. I'm really nervous. I've never interviewed in English before. What should I do?" — Give her complete advice in English. Speak for 90 seconds.
  • At least 2 pieces of advice using should + reason
  • At least 1 warning using shouldn't
  • At least 1 gentle option using could
  • End with a colloquial phrase from §5
should ×2 shouldn't ×1 could ×1 colloquial phrase
Scenario 2 — The Manager Update
Your manager asks: "How is next week looking?" You have: (1) a confirmed Monday standup, (2) a client visit that's 50% likely, (3) a supplier delivery that might not arrive, (4) a budget approval that's pending. Give your manager a complete professional update in 90 seconds.
  • Use will for the confirmed event
  • Use may for the 50% possibility
  • Use might not for the risky delivery
  • Use TBD or "I'll keep you posted" for the pending approval
will may might not TBD / I'll keep you posted
Scenario 3 — ⭐ Challenge: Mix Both Systems
A client calls and says: "I'm thinking about applying for a promotion at my company. I don't know if I'm ready. And I might have a difficult performance review next month." Respond in one continuous 2-minute turn. You must give advice AND express uncertainty about possible outcomes.
  • Give advice using should / shouldn't / could
  • Express what might or might not happen using may / might / might not
  • Use at least 2 colloquial phrases from either set
  • End with a clear closing statement that sounds professional
