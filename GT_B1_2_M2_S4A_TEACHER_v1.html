Status Symbol or Waste of Money?

💬
Speaking Topic
3 rounds · Model answer · Report + Defend + Disrupt
8–10 MIN
Today's Question
Luxury Brands — Are You Paying for Quality, Status, or a Story?
Everyone has an opinion on luxury. But what do people actually SAY when you ask them? And does what they say match what they actually do?
Round 1 · 2 min
What did the last person who bought something expensive tell you about why they bought it? Report what they said — and whether you believed them.
💡 "She told me she bought it because…" / "He said it was an investment, but I think…"
Round 2 · 2 min
Have you ever judged someone for what they wear or carry? What did you think — and what did you say? Is there a gap between what we think and what we report?
💡 "I thought she was… but I told her it looked great." / "I mentioned to a friend that…"
Round 3 · 2 min
Someone says: "Luxury brands are just marketing." Do you agree? What arguments have people given you? Report the strongest argument you've heard for AND against luxury.
💡 "The strongest argument I've heard is that…" / "People have told me…" / "But then someone mentioned…"
"Last month, my colleague told me she'd spent $900 on a pair of designer shoes. I asked her why — she said they were exclusive and the quality was unbeatable. Honestly? I thought she was paying for the logo, not the leather.

But here's the thing — my friend in Monterrey mentioned that how you dress changes how clients perceive you. She said she'd closed deals faster since she started wearing luxury brands to meetings. 'It adds up' she told me — the value is in the perception, not the product.

I'm still undecided. Someone once told me that 'you're not buying a bag — you're buying an identity.' That's either brilliant or sketchy — depending on how much you spent."

§ 04
Real Talk — Money & Spending
4 phrases with images · How native speakers actually discuss luxury · RS integration
8 min
🗣️
Colloquial
4 phrases · M2 colloquial set · Report what people really say
8 MIN
"No way."
Expressing shock or total disbelief. Stronger than "really?" — almost physical surprise. Can be positive or negative.
"He told me he'd spent $5,000 on sneakers. I said: 'No way. That's insane.'"
+ RS: She told me she'd bought a $3,000 bag. I said "No way" — and she said she'd saved for a year.
"That's sketchy."
Something feels dishonest, suspicious, or unreliable. Common when discussing counterfeit luxury, too-good-to-be-true deals, or questionable brands.
"She mentioned a site selling designer bags at 90% off. I told her that sounded sketchy."
+ RS: He said he'd found a deal online. I told him it sounded sketchy and that he should check reviews first.
"I'm broke."
Informal for having no money — temporary, not permanent poverty. Extremely common after big purchases, holidays, or end of month.
"She said she was completely broke after the Gucci sale. I told her she should've skipped the bag."
+ RS: He mentioned he was broke and that he couldn't go out this weekend because he'd spent too much.
"It adds up."
Small amounts accumulate into something significant. Used to argue that small luxury purchases become expensive over time — or that small quality investments pay off.
"She said one coffee a day seems cheap, but she told me it adds up to $1,500 a year."
+ RS: He mentioned that buying cheap things actually costs more because you replace them constantly. He said "it adds up."
  • Cada frase coloquial tiene un ejemplo con RS integrado (+RS). Pide al alumno que lea el ejemplo en voz alta, luego que lo reformule SIN leer: "Tell me what she said about…" — eso fuerza RS espontáneo.
  • "No way" — universal, alta retención. "That's sketchy" — creciente en uso global. "I'm broke" — todos lo han dicho. "It adds up" — argumentación sofisticada. Este set funciona perfectamente para luxury.

Reported Speech · Tense shift · said vs told · Poll + Activity

Reported Speech — Luxury Context
Core principle: When we report what someone said, the verb tense usually shifts one step back (backshift). "I love luxury" → She said she loved luxury. But the meaning stays the same — we're just reporting from a different time perspective.
Direct SpeechReporting verbReported SpeechTense shift
"I buy designer shoes."saidShe said she bought designer shoes.Present → Past
"I'm saving for a bag."told meHe told me he was saving for a bag.Continuous → Past Cont.
"I've spent $2,000."mentionedShe mentioned she'd spent $2,000.PP → Past Perfect
"I can't afford it."saidHe said he couldn't afford it.can → could
"I'll buy it next month."told meShe told me she'd buy it next month.will → would
⚠️ said vs told: "said" = no indirect object needed ("She said she was broke"). "told" = NEEDS an indirect object ("She told me she was broke"). "mentioned" = no object ("He mentioned he'd bought it online"). These are NOT interchangeable.
⚠️ Common Errors at B1.2
✗ "She told she was broke" → ✅ She told me she was broke (told NEEDS an object)
✗ "He said me he loved it" → ✅ He told me OR He said he loved it (never said + me)
✗ "She said she can afford it" → ✅ She said she could afford it (backshift can → could)
✗ "He mention the price" → ✅ He mentioned the price (past tense for reporting)
✏️ Quick Poll — Reported Speech
1. Direct: "I can't justify this price." → Reported: She _____ she _____ justify the price.
2. Direct: "I've already spent too much this month." → Reported: He _____ he _____ too much that month.
3. "She _____ me she would invest in quality over brands from now on." Which verb?
  • Badge 3 (⚡ Grammar Pro) se otorga cuando el alumno obtiene 3/3 en el Quick Poll. El trigger es en la función checkPoll — automático.
  • El error "told + sin objeto" es el más frecuente en B1.2 con L1 español. "She told she was broke" suena natural en español ("dijo que estaba sin dinero"). Corrige siempre: "She told WHO?"
  • Después del poll: pide al alumno que reporte algo real que alguien le dijo esta semana sobre dinero o compras. Eso conecta gramática con vida real.

The Brand Conversation — Carlos Mendez
B1.2 · M2 · S4A · Voice: Emma (British) + James (American) · Speed 0.88

📎 ElevenLabs: Voice Emma + James · Speed 0.88 · Conversational · B1.2 M2 S4A

Carlos: "So Elena, you told me before the interview that you used to spend a lot on luxury brands. What changed?"

Elena: "A few things. I remember my friend telling me I was paying for a logo, not quality. I laughed at first. But then I looked at my closet — half the stuff I'd bought, I never wore. The real value was zero."

Carlos: "But some people say luxury is an investment."

Elena: "Sure. My colleague mentioned she'd invested in a designer bag and said it had actually gone up in value. I asked her if she'd ever sell it. She said no way — she loved it too much. So it's not an investment if you never sell it — it's a purchase you've justified with a nice story."

Carlos: "What about the status argument? People say brands open doors."

Elena: "Someone at a conference told me that wearing a luxury watch had changed how clients perceived him. He said he'd started closing deals faster. I'm broke half the time, so I can't test that theory. But here's what adds up — the psychological effect is real. Whether it's worth $15,000 is another question entirely."

🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"She told me it was an investment — but I think it was just the logo."
Perfect for any conversation about spending. Combines RS + your own opinion. Sounds natural, confident, and B1.2-level precise. Use this the next time someone shows you their new purchase.
PHRASE 2
"He mentioned that perception mattered more than he wanted to admit."
Sophisticated RS with "mentioned" — the softest reporting verb. Use this in professional settings when you want to describe someone else's opinion diplomatically. The backshift ("mattered") is automatic if you practise saying it out loud 3 times.
PHRASE 3
"No way. That's either brilliant or sketchy — depending on the price."
Two M2 colloquial phrases in one sentence — "no way" + "sketchy." Use this when reacting to any surprising deal, purchase or decision. Shows natural spoken English fluency.
