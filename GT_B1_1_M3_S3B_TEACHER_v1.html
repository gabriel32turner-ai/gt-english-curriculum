You have 60 seconds to respond. Use First Conditional. Agree, disagree, or find the middle — but make your argument conditional.

📘 How to run Hot Take 30 seconds to think, then 60 seconds to speak — no notes. Requirement: at least 2 First Conditional sentences. Partner writes down every "will" they hear in an if-clause (error to correct later). The response should be conversational and natural — not a formal debate. Goal: can the student express a nuanced position using First Conditional in real time?
§2 Pitch Round — Store Defenders vs Digital Natives Two assigned sides · First Conditional arguments · 3 minutes per side ⏱ 10 MIN
💬 Motion: "If physical stores don't reinvent the shopping experience, they will disappear within 20 years." — Each side has 3 minutes. Use at least 4 First Conditional sentences. Starters provided below.
Side A — Store Defenders
"Stores will survive — if they evolve"
  • "If stores focus on experience over transactions, they'll keep customers coming back."
  • "If you can't touch and feel a product, you'll always have more returns and more doubt."
  • "If online shopping replaces everything, we'll lose the social element of buying together."
  • "If stores invest in expert staff, they'll offer something no algorithm can — real human advice."
Side B — Digital Natives
"If it's online, it's better — period"
  • "If stores can't compete on price, younger generations won't give them a second look."
  • "If the returns process gets easier, people won't need to go to stores even for clothing."
  • "If AI improves product recommendations, you'll find exactly what you want without leaving home."
  • "If delivery gets faster and cheaper, there'll be no practical reason to visit a store."
💡 Engagement After both sides, ask: "Who used First Conditional more effectively?" Then ask students to name their real position — not the assigned side. Use First Conditional: "Personally, if [condition], I'll [decision]." This metacognitive step shows that First Conditional is the language of opinion and argument, not just neutral description.
§3 Relay — The Shopping Journey 5 decision points · Each stage generates a First Conditional sentence · Rodrigo's full buying process ⏱ 8 MIN
🛒 How the Shopping Journey works: Rodrigo needs to buy a new pair of running shoes. Follow his decision process from discovery to final purchase. At each stage, a decision must be made — and every decision is a First Conditional sentence. Read the example, write your own version, then click "Show Model." The goal: 5 connected First Conditional sentences that tell a complete purchase story.
👟 Rodrigo Needs Running Shoes — Where Does He Start?
1
STAGE 1 — Discovery: "I need new shoes"
If Rodrigo searches online first, he will see hundreds of options — but he won't know which ones actually fit.
✍️ Write your own version — how do YOU start searching for a product?
"If I need something I've never bought before, I'll always check reviews first — before I set foot in any store or click anything online."
2
STAGE 2 — The Price Check: "I found a pair I like"
If the online price is more than 300 pesos cheaper, Rodrigo will buy online. If not, he will go to the store.
✍️ What's YOUR price threshold for going to a store vs buying online?
"If the online price is the same as the store, I'll go to the store — I'd rather support a local business and try them on."
3
STAGE 3 — The Try-On Decision: "I go to the store"
If they have his size in store, Rodrigo will try them on immediately. If they don't have his size, he won't buy without trying first.
✍️ Would you buy shoes without trying them on? Write your rule.
"If they don't have my size in store, I'll ask them to order it — I won't buy shoes I haven't tried on. Not after the last time."
4
STAGE 4 — The Checkout: "They fit — now what?"
If the assistant offers a store membership card, Rodrigo will sign up — if it gives real discounts. If not, he won't bother.
✍️ What's your reaction to store loyalty cards or memberships?
"If the loyalty card gives me real points, I'll sign up. If it's just for marketing emails, I won't give them my data — it's not worth it."
5
STAGE 5 — After: "He wears them — and feels pain after 2km"
If the shoes cause problems after wearing, Rodrigo will return them. If the store doesn't accept a return, he will never shop there again.
✍️ What's your return policy rule? When do you return and when do you accept the loss?
"If there's a problem, I'll always try to return it — politely, but firmly. If the store refuses and the receipt is valid, I'll escalate and leave a review. Bad return policies lose customers forever."
🗣️ Spoken Journey Challenge
Partner A narrates Stages 1–2–3. Partner B narrates Stages 4–5. No reading — use your own versions. Then switch: Partner B narrates 1–2–3, Partner A does 4–5. Time limit: 90 seconds total. First Conditional in every sentence.

§5 Solo Challenge — My Shopping Philosophy 120 seconds · Personal rules · First Conditional throughout · No notes ⏱ 8 MIN
⭐ Solo Challenge — 2 Minutes
Explain your personal shopping philosophy to a colleague who asks: "So — are you a store person or an online person?" Your answer should use at least 4 First Conditional sentences and cover at least 3 product categories.
01
Start with your general position: "It depends — if [general condition], I'll [general preference]."
02
Give your rules for at least 3 product categories using First Conditional.
03
Include at least 1 negative: "If I can't [action], I won't [result]."
04
End with your strongest conviction — a category where you'll NEVER change your preference.
05
No "will" in the if-clause. Partner marks every violation — 1 point deducted per error.
📘 Assessment Partner rates: (1) Grammar accuracy — no "will" in if-clause (0–3 pts), (2) Vocabulary from S3A — used at least 4 in-store words (0–3 pts), (3) Coherence — does the shopping philosophy make sense and flow naturally? (0–4 pts). Max 10 points. Enter scores in Leaderboard §7.
§6 Source Analysis — Shopping Language in the Wild 4 real-world formats · First Conditional in authentic contexts · Critical reading ⏱ 8 MIN
🐦 Tweet / X Post — Frustrated Customer
"Just spent 40 minutes in a store looking for a specific shade of blue shirt. They didn't have it. If stores don't improve their inventory systems, I will never go back. This is why online shopping wins. Every. Single. Time."
1. Find the First Conditional sentence. Is it grammatically correct?
2. "I will never go back" — why does the writer use "will" here instead of "I won't"? What's the emotional effect?
🧠 Metacognitive: First Conditional can be used to threaten or warn. How does the tone change when you say "I will NEVER" vs "I won't"?
💬 WhatsApp — Friend Asking for Advice
Ana: "Oye, I found these boots online for $890. If I order them and they don't fit, can I return them easily?" / Rodrigo: "Depends. If the site has free returns, you'll be fine. If not, you'll pay the shipping back. For boots I'd go to the store — if you can try them on first, you won't regret it."
1. Count the First Conditional sentences in Rodrigo's reply. Are they all correct?
2. How does Rodrigo use First Conditional to give advice — not just describe facts?
🧠 Metacognitive: Rodrigo uses three short if-sentences to structure his advice. Is this more or less persuasive than one long explanation?
📧 Email — Store Promotion
"Hi Rodrigo, we miss you! If you visit us before Sunday, you'll receive a 20% discount on your entire purchase. If you spend more than $1,500, we'll add free home delivery. Come in — our trained assistants will help you find exactly what you need. If you're not completely satisfied, we'll refund your purchase with no questions asked."
1. The store uses First Conditional 3 times. What is the strategic goal of each conditional sentence?
2. Why does the store end with a return policy guarantee in First Conditional?
🧠 Metacognitive: How does First Conditional turn a sales email into a series of promises? What happens to trust if the conditions aren't met?
⭐ Online Review — Product Page
"3/5 stars. The shoes look great but run small — I'm normally a 10 and had to return and reorder a 10.5. If you're between sizes, order the larger one. If you wear orthotics, these won't work — there's no room. If the fit is right, they're excellent quality. I'll buy from this brand again IF they sort out the sizing."
1. Find all First Conditional sentences. Are they used for advice, opinion, or warning?
2. The last sentence says "I'll buy again IF…" — why is the IF capitalised? What does that signal?
🧠 Metacognitive: Reviews use First Conditional to guide future buyers. How is this different from using First Conditional in a conversation?
💡 Discussion anchor After pairs discuss, bring the class together: "Which of these 4 sources uses First Conditional most powerfully — and why?" Push for answers that explain the grammar choice, not just the content. A student who says "The store email is most effective because each conditional is a specific promise that reduces purchase risk" is operating at B1.1+ level.

🎯 This week at work — use these 3 phrases
3 Phrases Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"It depends — if [condition], I'll [decision]."
The most sophisticated way to express a nuanced preference. Use it this week when someone asks you a binary question you can't answer simply. "Tea or coffee?" "It depends — if it's morning, I'll have coffee. If it's afternoon, tea." First Conditional makes you sound thoughtful, not indecisive.
PHRASE 2
"If they don't have [X], I won't buy it."
First Conditional negative — shows discipline and clear decision-making. Use it this week when talking about any purchase. "If they don't have the right size, I won't settle for the wrong one." Professionals who know their limits earn respect.
PHRASE 3
"If the experience is good, I'll come back."
Applies to stores, restaurants, services, apps — anywhere. Use it in any professional or personal context where quality matters. "If the onboarding is smooth, I'll recommend it to my team." Customer experience language that works in any industry.
