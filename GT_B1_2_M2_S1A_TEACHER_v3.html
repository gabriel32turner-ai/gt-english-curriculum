§ 02
Speaking Topic — Crypto & Investing
8–10 min
M2 · S1 — Money, Power & Choices
"Would you put your savings in crypto?
Smart move or gamble?"
First answer: no preparation, no vocabulary help. Just your opinion right now. Then build it across the session.
Push the conversation — 3 rounds
Round 1 — Your position: Do you invest? Do you think crypto is a legitimate investment or speculation? What makes something a "smart investment" vs a "gamble"?
Round 2 — Other people: Do you know someone who has invested in crypto? What did they say about it? What was their experience? (This is the natural Reported Speech bridge.)
Round 3 — Bigger picture: Is it fair that some people make millions from crypto while others lose everything? Should governments regulate it more? What's the bigger issue with volatility and risk?
B1.2 Model Answer — "Crypto & Investing" · 2.5–3 min · Natural & Real
"Honestly? I wouldn't put my savings in crypto. No way. I've looked at the volatility and it just doesn't make sense to me for money I actually need. The risk is too high when we're talking about real savings — your emergency fund, your portfolio for the future.

That said, I have a colleague who did invest in Bitcoin back in 2020. She told me she had put in about 10% of her salary at the time — just a small part of her total assets. She said she was treating it as a high-risk bet, not a serious investment. She mentioned she had been watching the market for months before deciding. And she was lucky — she made a gain before it crashed. But she also said it had been incredibly stressful. That's sketchy to me — the idea that your financial peace depends on something that crashes and recovers without logic.

I think the smarter move is to diversify. Put most of your money in stable things — savings, maybe a real estate fund — and if you want to speculate, do it with money you could afford to lose completely. A friend of mine said the same thing — he told me that the blockchain technology behind it is interesting, but the actual trading is a different story. I agree. It adds up — the fees, the tax implications, the emotional cost. Not gonna happen for me."
🏷 Colour key: teal = S1 vocab · cyan = Reported Speech · gold = colloquial phrases · Count: 10 vocab words, 4 RS uses (told me / said / mentioned / said), 3 colloquial phrases
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.2 diagnostic: the student speaks FIRST before vocab or grammar. This is non-negotiable. The model answer is revealed AFTER §7 only. The contrast between first attempt and final attempt = the learning arc of the session.
  • Ask: "Do you know someone who has invested?" → this triggers natural Reported Speech BEFORE the grammar lesson. Note what they say. You'll refer back to it in §5: "Remember what you said about your colleague? Let's put that into Reported Speech."
  • B1.2 standard for this topic: 2.5+ minutes · 6+ vocab words · 2+ RS instances · 1 colloquial phrase. That's the exit bar.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Has anyone in your life ever said something interesting about money or investing? Remember exactly what they said. That's what we're going to learn to report in English — precisely."

⚡ B1.2 Rapid Personalisation: After each word → use it in a sentence about a real person you know or a real situation: "My brother told me he was going to invest…" / "A friend said she had lost money when it crashed…" — This is instant Reported Speech practice before §5.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.2 vocabulary: 20 words in ~12 minutes. Pace is intentionally faster than B1.1. Read word + phonetics + definition + example → student produces 1 sentence. Don't stop for full discussion — that's §7.
  • "volatility" — this word is the key to the whole topic. Take 30 extra seconds here. "What does volatility feel like emotionally? Is it exciting or terrifying?" — this triggers authentic speaking.
  • "speculate" vs "invest" — draw the contrast explicitly: invest = calculated risk with research. Speculate = high-risk bet. The distinction drives the debate in §7.
  • The Rapid Personalisation technique is pre-loading Reported Speech: "My brother told me he was going to invest" uses RS without the student realising. Name this explicitly in §5: "You've already been using Reported Speech — here's why it works."
§ 04
Colloquial English — 5 Phrases · Module 2
7–8 min
These are phrases real people use when talking about money in English. B1.2 target: all 5 feel natural — not memorised, but yours.
"No way."
Expressing shock, disbelief, or strong refusal. Tone determines meaning — can be positive surprise or flat refusal.
"He earns that much from crypto? No way." / "Would I put my savings in Bitcoin? No way."
"That's sketchy."
Something feels dishonest, suspicious, or shady. High-frequency in English conversations about investments and online platforms.
"That investment scheme promises 30% monthly returns? That's sketchy — it sounds like a Ponzi scheme."
"I'm broke."
Informal — I have no money right now. Note: NOT the same as being poor long-term. It's temporary cash shortage.
"Don't talk to me about investing this week — I'm broke after paying rent and the car service."
"It adds up."
Small amounts accumulate into a significant total over time. Used for expenses, fees, benefits — any accumulation.
"The transaction fees seem small — 1% here, 0.5% there — but over time, it adds up to thousands."
"Not gonna happen."
Something is impossible or very unlikely. Informal but widely used in professional conversations. "Not going to happen" in natural speech.
"A raise this quarter with the current budget situation? Not gonna happen." / "Me putting my savings in crypto? Not gonna happen — I've seen what volatility does."
💬 Drill: Maestro da una situación → alumno responde con la frase correcta. "You find out a colleague earns 3x your salary → ?" / "Someone pitches you a suspicious investment opportunity → ?" / "You're at the end of the month before payday → ?" / "A friend says small daily coffees don't matter → ?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "That's sketchy" — high-value B1.2 phrase. Native English speakers use this constantly in informal business and personal conversations. It bridges business and daily English perfectly.
  • "Not gonna happen" — teach pronunciation: "gonna" /ˈɡɒnə/ not "going to". This is natural connected speech. "In formal writing you'd say 'That's not going to happen.' In conversation: 'Not gonna happen.'"
  • "I'm broke" — cultural note: Spanish speakers sometimes say "I'm poor" when they mean broke (temporary). Clarify this distinction — it affects how native speakers perceive the statement.
  • Drill: make each answer a full sentence if possible at B1.2. "No way!" alone is fine — but "No way — I had no idea he earned that much, to be honest" is the B1.2 standard.
§ 05
Grammar (Referential) — Reported Speech
10–12 min
B1.2 Grammar Philosophy: 1 grammar point per module. Explained clearly, activated immediately through the speaking topic. 2 activities — that's it. Grammar is a tool for communication, not the focus.
Reported Speech — What People Said
When you report what someone said, the tense shifts back one step. The original words change because time has passed.
Direct Speech (the original words)
"I am thinking about investing."→She said she was thinking about investing.
"I will never go back to 9-to-5."→He told me he would never go back.
"I have been watching the market."→She mentioned she had been watching it.
"I don't think it's worth the risk."→He said he didn't think it was worth it.
"Are you happy with your salary?"→She asked me if I was happy with my salary.
Tense Shift — The Rule
Present Simple→Past Simple
Present Continuous→Past Continuous
Present Perfect→Past Perfect
will→would
can→could
Reporting verbs: said · told (me) · mentioned · asked · explained · admitted
Said vs Told: "said" stands alone. "told" needs an object (told me / told her / told the team).
✅ "She said she was thinking about it." · ✅ "He told me he was thinking about it." · ❌ "He told he was thinking about it."
⚠ Common Errors — Reported Speech at B1.2
❌ "She said me she was investing."→✅ She told me she was investing.
said + no object. told + object (me/him/her/them)
❌ "He told me he will never invest."→✅ He told me he would never invest.
will → would in reported speech (tense shift)
❌ "She said that she invest in crypto."→✅ She said she invested in crypto.
tense shift: present simple → past simple
❌ "He asked me do I invest."→✅ He asked me if I invested / if I was investing.
reported questions use "if" — no direct question word order
Referential Activity 1 — Report the Statement
Choose the correct reported speech form.
Direct: "I am thinking about selling all my crypto." → She said she ___.
Direct: "I will never invest in crypto." → He told me he ___.
Direct: "Are you happy with your portfolio?" → She asked me ___.
Direct: "I have been trading crypto for two years." → He mentioned he ___.
Which sentence uses "told" correctly?
Referential Activity 2 — Correct or Incorrect?
Is this Reported Speech sentence grammatically correct?
"He told me he will put his savings in crypto."
"She said she didn't think it was worth the risk."
"He asked me if I was planning to invest."
"She said me she had been watching the market."
"They mentioned they were going to diversify their portfolio."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • B1.2: 1 grammar point, maximum 12 minutes. After the 2 activities, IMMEDIATELY bridge to speaking: "Now tell me something your colleague / friend / partner said recently about money. Use 'told me' or 'mentioned'." That's the activation.
  • Said vs Told is the #1 error. Drill it with a physical prompt: "Said = alone. Told = needs a person." Tap the desk when you say "told" and then the object person. Physical reinforcement works.
  • Tense shift: present → past is the core. Don't get into modal shifts (would, could) in depth — keep it functional. "Will → would is the most common one in real conversation."
  • Natural bridge: "Remember what you said about someone you know who invested in crypto? Let's put that into Reported Speech right now." Makes grammar immediately personal.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Reported Speech is the grammar of gossip, of relaying what happened in a meeting, of telling your partner what your boss said. It's used constantly — and most B1 students avoid it because they're not sure of the rules. You now know the rules."

"OK so I'm going to tell you my crypto story. I invested in Bitcoin back in 2021 — terrible timing, as it turned out.

A friend of mine had been talking about it for months. He told me he had already made a 200% return on his initial investment. He said it was the most obvious financial opportunity he'd ever seen. No way was he selling, he told me. He was just going to hold.

So I put in about 5% of my savings. I felt smart. Two months later, the market crashed — hard. I lost about 40% of what I'd put in. My friend mentioned later that he had sold just before the crash. He said he had been watching the volatility and something felt off. He told me he was sorry he hadn't said anything earlier. That's sketchy, honestly — why didn't he warn me?

The market recovered eventually, and I got most of it back. But the lesson for me is clear: diversify. Don't put more than you can afford to lose into any single asset. My financial advisor asked me if I had a plan before I invested. I didn't. That's the real problem. It adds up — the stress, the emotional cost, the time you spend checking prices obsessively. Not gonna happen again for me at that scale."

Production — Your Full Answer · 2–3 Minutes
10–12 min
Speaking Practice — B1.2 Main Event
Would you put your savings in crypto? Smart move or gamble?
The student has vocabulary (§3), colloquial phrases (§4), Reported Speech (§5) and a model story (§6). This is the full 2–3 minute answer. No notes. Real opinions. Natural English.
Round 1 — Your position with vocabulary
Are you pro or against crypto as an investment? What makes it appealing or dangerous? Use: volatility, risk, return, portfolio, speculate.
Target: 4+ vocab words used naturally in your argument
Round 2 — Reported Speech in action
Tell me about someone you know (or have heard about) who invested or refused to invest in crypto. Report what they said about it.
Target: 2+ Reported Speech instances using said / told me / mentioned / asked
Round 3 — Colloquial + opinion
Is crypto different from traditional investing, or is it the same kind of risk? What advice would you give someone who asks you about it?
Target: at least 1 colloquial phrase + a nuanced opinion (not just "it's good" or "it's bad")
