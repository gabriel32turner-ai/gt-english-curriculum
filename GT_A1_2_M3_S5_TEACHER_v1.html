§1
Warm-Up — The Full Business Trip
⏱ 8 min · Quick-Fire · Full module activation
📋 Logistics

90-second Quick-Fire. Today's warm-up activates ALL of Module 3: reception (S1), hotel (S3), directions (S2), airport (S4). Each question targets a different session's core content. Run as a race — first complete answer wins the round.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Q1: reception language — activate S1 content
  • Q2: hotel check-in — activate S3 content
  • Q3: giving directions — activate S2 content
  • Q4: airport navigation — activate S4 content
💡 S5 Purpose

This is not a new lesson — it's a performance session. Students should feel confident from the start. If the warm-up shows gaps in specific sessions, note them privately and adjust the Exit Task focus. Never re-teach in S5 — redirect to "remember what we did in S3?"

⚡ QUICK-FIRE · 90 SECONDS PER STUDENT

🟣 Reception — S1 Review
A client from the US arrives at your company's reception. You didn't meet them before. What are the first 4 things you say? Use object pronouns: "I'll take you to… / Let me call… / Please follow me…"
Push: "How do you offer them a drink? How do you introduce them to your manager?"
⭐ Hotel — S3 Review
You arrive at a business hotel in Monterrey at 10 PM. Give 5 sentences: check in, ask for Wi-Fi, ask about breakfast, request an early receipt, say goodbye. Use "me/it/them" at least 3 times.
Push: "What do you say if there's a problem with the room? Use: 'Can you fix it for me?'"
🟢 Directions — S2 Review
A visitor calls you from outside your building: "I'm at the traffic lights on Blvd. Adolfo López Mateos. How do I get to your office?" Give them 5 step-by-step instructions using imperatives and tell them how long it takes.
Push: "Can you add 'you can't miss it' naturally? Which imperative verbs work best for directions?"
🟣 Airport — S4 Review
Your international client just landed at BJX. She has her luggage and is at baggage claim. Call her and guide her all the way to your car using at least 4 imperatives and 3 different object pronouns.
Push: "What if she can't find you? What's your fallback instruction? Use 'Let me know when you see…'"

Speaking Topic — Your Real Business Trip
⏱ 12 min · 3 rounds · Personal experience · Full integration
📋 Logistics

3 rounds integrating all Module 3 contexts. Students use real experience — no imaginary scenarios needed. Push for object pronouns in every sentence. Model answer shows B1-level aspiration — never as benchmark, always as "here's where you're going."

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Round 1: what students can now say in English that they couldn't 5 sessions ago
  • Round 2: real client visit — full reception + directions sequence
  • Round 3: business trip story — hotel + airport + how long
💡 Retention Moment

Ask: "If you had to explain to your manager what you can do in English now that you couldn't do in Module 2, what would you say?" This is the ROI conversation — plant it here. Use their answer as the Exit Task framing.

🌐
Your Module 3 World — Integrate Everything
Round 1 — What I Can Do Now
Tell your partner: "Before Module 3, I couldn't say… Now I can say…" Give 3 specific examples using real English phrases from the module. Partner confirms: "I heard you use [object pronoun] correctly."
💡 This builds metacognitive awareness AND creates evidence of learning you can share with your manager.
Round 2 — A Client Visit to YOUR Company
Describe the last time (real or imagined) an external client visited your company. Cover: How did you receive them? Where did you take them? How long did it take to get from their hotel to your plant? What did you say?
💡 Use: "I met them at… / I took them to… / It took us about… / Please follow me…"
Round 3 — Your Best Business Trip Story
Tell a 5-sentence story about a business trip you took (or imagine one). Include: where you went, how you got there, the hotel, one problem, and how long the travel took. Use at least 5 object pronouns and 2 imperatives.
💡 Partner marks tally: one mark per object pronoun heard. Target: 5+ marks in 2 minutes.

"Last month I received a client from Chicago at BJX Airport. I picked him up at arrivals and took him to Hotel Ejecutivo in Silao — it took us about 40 minutes. The hotel gave him the wrong room, so I called the manager and asked them to change it. The next day I took him to our plant. Please follow me, I said — and he did. It was a smooth trip, overall. He said he'd come back again."

Colloquial — The 5 That Stick
⏱ 10 min · Best-of Module 3 · Student explains in English
📋 Logistics

These are the 5 highest-frequency colloquial phrases from M3. S5 doesn't introduce new phrases — it consolidates. Student explains each phrase in English WITHOUT translating. Then creates their own sentence. If they get stuck: "Where did you hear this? In a hotel? At the airport? On a call?"

🎯 Pedagogical Focus

Push for object pronoun integration in every student-generated sentence: "You can't miss it" → "You can't miss it — I'll wave when I see you." This creates the bridge between colloquial fluency and grammatical accuracy.

💡 Module 3 Identity

These 5 phrases are the student's professional identity in English travel situations. Ask: "Which one will you use THIS WEEK? In what situation?" Commitment to use → retention rate doubles.

You can't miss it.
It's very easy to find — very visible or clearly marked
"Walk past the pharmacy and turn left. You can't miss it — the sign is huge."
I'm running late.
I'm behind schedule — arriving after the planned time
"I'm running late — please wait for me in the lobby. I'll be there in 15 minutes."
It's a bit of a walk.
The destination is not very close — you need to walk some distance
"The parking lot is a bit of a walk from the terminal — let me carry it for you."
It was a smooth trip.
Everything went well — no delays, no problems, easy travel
"How was your flight? — It was a smooth trip, thank you. No delays. They gave us a good connection."
Let me know when you land.
Send me a message or call when you arrive
"Let me know when you land and I'll come pick you up immediately."

Grammar — Full Module 3 Review
⏱ 14 min · Poll + Activity A (MC) + Activity B (drag) + Dice
📋 Logistics

Grammar review is REFERENTIAL — brief tables from S1–S4 + 3 polls + Activity A (5 MC crossing all module grammar) + Activity B (drag-and-drop) + Hot Seat Dice (the production bridge to §7). Badge 🎯 at 4/5 Activity A. No new grammar introduced.

🎯 Module 3 Grammar Map
  • S1: Can I help you? / object pronouns (first exposure)
  • S2: prepositions of movement / imperatives for directions
  • S3: object pronouns in hotel context / Can you give me…?
  • S4: imperatives + object pronouns + How long does it take?
💡 Error Patterns to Address in S5

By S5 these errors should be disappearing — but monitor closely: "Give to me" → "Give me" · "Call to her" → "Call her" · "Meet he" → "Meet him" · "Go to straight" → "Go straight" · "It takes to me" → "It takes me". If still present: note for M4 warm-up.

Last month I received a client from our Chicago office — his name is James Weber. I picked him up at BJX Airport on a Tuesday afternoon.

When he called me from baggage claim, I gave him directions: "Walk out of the area, follow the signs for arrivals, and look for me — you can't miss me." It took him about five minutes to find me.

I took him to Hotel Ejecutivo in Silao. I had booked a room for him — a single room with good Wi-Fi. The receptionist gave him the key card and he asked her for the Wi-Fi password immediately. I'm running late for my meeting, so I said goodbye and told him: "Call me if you need anything."

The next morning I picked him up from the lobby at 8 AM. He asked me: "How long does it take to get to your office?" I told him: "It takes us about 20 minutes." And it did — no traffic that day. It was a smooth trip.

I showed him our plant and introduced him to our team. He was very happy. He said: "Let me know next time you're in Chicago — I'll pick you up." I said yes, of course.

James found Mónica at arrivals in about 5 minutes.

§7 — MODULE EXIT TASK
The Full Business Trip — Your Performance
⏱ 16 min · No notes · 3 min minimum · Real context · Module evidence
📋 Logistics

This is the Module 3 Exit Task. Students choose ONE scenario (A, B, or C) and perform it with their partner for 3 minutes minimum. No notes. Teacher only marks — does NOT correct during performance. After: 2-minute feedback using the rubric cards. Badge 🌟 for completing the exit task.

🎯 Exit Task Rubric
  • ✅ 4+ imperatives used correctly (no "to" before direction verbs)
  • ✅ 4+ object pronouns used (no subject form after verbs)
  • ✅ At least 1 "How long does it take?" used naturally
  • ✅ At least 1 colloquial phrase (You can't miss it / Let me know / etc.)
  • ✅ Context is real or realistic — not invented
💡 Corporate Evidence Moment

This performance is the evidence that justifies renewing the program. Record it (with permission). Use the best 30-second clip for: HR report · Manager presentation · LinkedIn testimonial · Program renewal conversation. A student saying "I picked him up at the airport and showed him our plant" in fluent English is the product. This is it.

SCENARIO A — The Client Visit
A client from the US is visiting your company in Celaya or Silao. You received them at the airport, took them to a hotel, and showed them your plant. Tell the full story as Mónica would — 3 minutes, first person.
💡 Include: airport navigation, hotel check-in, giving directions to your plant, how long everything took.
SCENARIO B — The Phone Guide
Your client is lost at BJX Airport. Their phone is at 5% battery. They call you. You have 3 minutes to give them complete instructions: from wherever they are to your car outside. Partner plays the client — ask questions to make the student work harder.
💡 Partner asks: "Where exactly? How long? I see a pharmacy — now what?" Student must respond with imperatives + pronouns throughout.
SCENARIO C — The Hotel Problem
Your colleague arrived at their hotel in Monterrey. They call you with 3 problems: Wi-Fi doesn't work, the room is wrong, and there's an error on the bill. You help them resolve all 3 by phone — giving instructions for each problem. Partner plays the colleague.
💡 For each problem: identify it, give an instruction, use an object pronoun. "Tell them about it. Ask them to fix it."
