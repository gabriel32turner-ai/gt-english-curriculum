Warm-Up — Quick-Fire Review
⏱ 8 min · 4 questions · 90 sec timer
📋 Logistics

Final session of Module 2. Warm-up reviews the must/mustn't/have to/don't have to system from S1–S4 without framing it as a test. Energy high. Badge ⚡ triggers on timer completion.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Q1: must — internal rule, student gives a real example from their company
  • Q2: student asks teacher — mustn't, forces student to produce a prohibition
  • Q3: have to vs don't have to — the optional/compulsory contrast
  • Q4: student-to-student — real workplace policy from their experience
💡 GT Original — The Critical Contrast

"You mustn't do it" = it is forbidden. "You don't have to do it" = it is not necessary but allowed. This is one of the most common A2/B1 errors globally. Any confusion here today = address in §8 global feedback.

Push: "Is attendance at all meetings compulsory or optional at your company?"
🟣 Teacher → Student
Q4 — What is a rule at your company related to confidential information or privacy?
Push: "Use: Employees must / must not share…"

Speaking Topic — Workplace Rules
⏱ 12 min · 3 rounds
📋 Logistics

3 rounds, 3–4 min each. Student speaks about real rules at their workplace. Teacher only redirects. Model answer only if student is completely blocked. Target: use all 4 modals in natural speech.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus
  • Round 1: basic rules — what is compulsory, what is forbidden
  • Round 2: optional vs required — push for don't have to / have to contrast
  • Round 3: opinion — which rule is most important and why?
💡 Color Code in Model

Orange = key vocab · Gold = grammar · Green = colloquial · Red = prohibition language

📋
Workplace Rules at My Company
Round 1 — Required & Forbidden
Tell me: what must employees do at your company? What must they NOT do? Give real examples.
Round 2 — Optional vs Compulsory
What do you have to do that you think should be optional? What don't you have to do but you do anyway?
Round 3 — Your Opinion
Which rule at your company is the most important? Which one do people ignore most often?

At my company, all employees must sign in at reception when they arrive — that's the rule, no exceptions. We also must wear a badge at all times in the building. It is a safety and access requirement.

Employees mustn't share confidential information externally — not by email, not by phone. That is the most important rule.

We have to attend the Monday morning meeting — it's compulsory. But we don't have to wear a suit. It's up to you — business casual is fine.

The most important rule? Confidentiality. It's not a big deal to forget your badge sometimes — but sharing confidential data? That is a serious policy violation.

📋 Logistics

🎯 Pedagogical Focus

These phrases are what managers and HR use in real workplace conversations. At A2.2, students who know "It's compulsory" and "It's up to you" can handle policy conversations with confidence.

💡 Extension

Advanced: combine two phrases. "Attendance is compulsory — no exceptions. But the dress code? It's up to you."

It's compulsory. / Attendance is compulsory.
No choice — everyone must do it. HR and policy language.
"Do I have to attend the safety training? — It's compulsory. All employees must complete it before working in the plant."
It's up to you.
Your decision — no obligation. Informal way to say "don't have to."
"Do I have to wear a suit for the presentation? — It's up to you. You don't have to — business casual is fine."
That's the rule. / No exceptions.
Used to close a discussion about policy — firm and final.
"But why do I have to sign in every day? — That's the rule. No exceptions. Employees must show their badge at reception."
It's not a big deal.
Something minor — not important or serious.
"You forgot your badge today? It's not a big deal — just sign in at the front desk. But sharing confidential data? That IS a big deal."
It is not permitted to… / Employees must not…
Formal HR policy language — written rules in handbooks
"It is not permitted to use personal devices during client meetings. Employees must not access restricted areas without authorization."

§5
Grammar — Must · Mustn't · Have to · Don't have to
⏱ 10 min · Reference + Poll + 2 Activities
📋 Logistics

REFERENTIAL. Modal cards + contrast table visible throughout. Poll: 3 questions, 10 pts each. Activity A: choose the correct modal (3 items). Activity B: rewrite as formal HR policy (3 items). Badge 🎯 at combined 4+/6.

🎯 Pedagogical Focus — GT Original

THE critical contrast: "You mustn't do it" = FORBIDDEN. "You don't have to do it" = NOT NECESSARY (but allowed). These look similar in Spanish — "no tienes que" is used for both, which causes the error. Point at the table every time.

💡 GT Originals from Program
  • ⚠️ mustn't ≠ don't have to — the #1 error at A2/B1 globally
  • 💼 HR register: documents use "must/must not"; speech uses "have to/don't have to"
  • 🌍 US/UK: written = "Employees must…"; spoken = "You have to…" — both registers matter
  • 🔊 "have to" /hæftə/ — V becomes F sound before T in natural speech
MUST
Strong internal obligation · personal or company rule
You must sign in at reception.
Employees must wear a badge.
MUSTN'T
Prohibition · it is forbidden · NOT allowed
You mustn't share confidential data.
Employees must not enter restricted areas.
HAVE TO
External obligation · company or law requires it
I have to submit this by 5 PM.
Does she have to travel? — Yes, she does.
DON'T HAVE TO
No obligation · optional · NOT necessary (but allowed)
You don't have to wear a suit.
He doesn't have to attend — it's optional.

THE KEY CONTRAST — REFERENCE TABLE

ModalMeaningExample
Mustn'tFORBIDDEN — do NOT do thisYou mustn't share your password. (It's prohibited.)
Don't have toNOT NECESSARY — optional, allowedYou don't have to wear a suit. (It's your choice.)
MustStrong obligation — internal/personal ruleYou must sign in before 9 AM. (Company rule.)
Have toExternal obligation — law or company requirementI have to complete the safety training. (Required by law.)

Good morning everyone, and welcome to the company. I am going to explain the most important policies you must follow as new employees.

First — access and badge. You must wear your badge at all times inside the building. You mustn't lend your badge to anyone — not even a colleague. No exceptions. If you forget your badge, you have to sign in at reception and get a temporary pass.

Second — confidential information. You must not share any company data, client information or passwords with people outside the organization. This is a legal requirement, not just a company policy.

Third — attendance. The Monday morning briefing is compulsory. You have to be there at 8:30. The Friday afternoon review is optional — you don't have to attend, but it is recommended.

Finally — dress code. You don't have to wear a suit or formal clothes every day. Business casual is fine. It's up to you. But in client meetings, please dress professionally. That's the rule.

MULTIPLE CHOICE

1. What must employees do if they forget their badge?

2. What is the status of the Friday afternoon review?

TRUE / FALSE

Employees can lend their badge to a colleague if they forget theirs.

The confidentiality rule is a legal requirement, not just a company policy.

Employees must wear formal clothes every day — business casual is not allowed.

What rules at your company are similar to the ones in the briefing? Tell your partner two examples using must / have to / don't have to.

Starter: "At my company, employees must… / We don't have to… / It's compulsory to…"
