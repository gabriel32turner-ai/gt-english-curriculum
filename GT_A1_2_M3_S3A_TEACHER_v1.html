§1
Checking Into a Business Hotel
Activate hotel language · Connect object pronouns to real travel scenarios · Personal experience
⏱ 5 MIN
🏨 Quick memory: Think about the last time you stayed at a hotel for work or personal travel. What was the first thing you said at reception? Try to say it in English!
💬 Partner question: Tell your partner: "When I travel for work, I usually stay at _____ hotel." What is important for you — Wi-Fi, breakfast, or location?
🧠 Think about this: Your colleague from the US visits your company. You booked a hotel for him. How do you tell your boss: "I booked it for him"? What are the small words you need?
⚡ Teacher note: S3 focuses on hotel check-in as the real-world context for object pronouns in action. The small words (me, him, her, it, us, them) are the heroes today. Collect student attempts on the board — note who drops the object pronoun entirely.
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds
Say this out loud right now: "I have a reservation. My name is [your name], from [your company]." That's it — you just checked in!
🧠 Pedagogical: Students at A1.2 often omit the object pronoun completely ("I booked for colleague" instead of "I booked it for him"). Today's session drills the habit of always including the pronoun. The hotel context makes this natural because check-in involves constant references to people and things.
§2
Business Travel — Hotels & You
Personal travel experience · Hotel preferences · Object pronouns in natural speech
⏱ 8 MIN
Mónica Herrera is a sales manager at ConnectPro International in Guadalajara. She travels to client offices in Monterrey, México City, and sometimes San Antonio, Texas. She always stays at business hotels near the client's office.
Today's question: What makes a good business hotel? What do you need when you travel for work?
Round 1 — Your hotel preferences
When you travel for work, what is most important in a hotel? Wi-Fi? Breakfast? Location? Quiet room? Tell your partner using: "For me, the most important thing is..."
💡 Try: "I need a room with good Wi-Fi. Can you give me a quiet room?"
Round 2 — A hotel experience
Tell your partner about a hotel you stayed at for work (or vacation). Was it good? What did you like? What was the problem? Use: "The hotel was..." and "They gave me/us..."
💡 Try: "The hotel was near the office. They gave us a nice room."
Round 3 — Book for a colleague
Your company sends a colleague to Celaya. YOU need to book a hotel for him/her. Tell your partner: What hotel? What room? Why? Use object pronouns: "I booked it for him/her."
💡 Try: "I booked a room for her at Hotel XYZ. I sent her the confirmation."
"When I travel for work, the most important thing is Wi-Fi and location. I need a hotel near the client's office. Last month, I stayed at a hotel in Monterrey. It was very good — they gave me a quiet room on the 5th floor. When my colleague visits Celaya, I book a hotel for him near our plant. I always send him the reservation by email."
🎯 Engagement: Round 3 is critical — it forces students to use third-person object pronouns (him/her) in a natural context. If students say "I booked for colleague" without the pronoun, correct immediately: "I booked it for him."

Everyday Hotel English
5 phrases you hear in every business hotel · Click each card to hear · Real life, not textbook
⏱ 5 MIN
"Do you need anything?"
The receptionist asks if you want help with something
Answer: "Yes, can you give me extra towels?" or "No, I'm fine. Thank you."
"Right this way."
Follow me — I'll take you there
"Your room is on the 3rd floor. Right this way — let me show you."
"It's not far."
The place is close — don't worry about distance
"The business center? It's not far — I'll take you there."
"Have a good trip!"
A friendly goodbye when someone is traveling
"Thank you for staying with us. Have a good trip!"
"I'm here for a client visit."
Standard arrival phrase at hotel or reception
"Hi, I'm here for a client visit. My company booked a room for me."
🎯 Engagement: Have students role-play: one is the receptionist, the other is the guest. The receptionist uses the colloquial phrases naturally. Switch after 90 seconds. This builds confidence for real hotel situations.
§5
Object Pronouns — At the Hotel
The small words that make you sound professional · me / you / him / her / it / us / them
⏱ 10 MIN
🚨 Real Situation — Business Trip to Monterrey
You arrive at the hotel in Monterrey at 10 PM. You're tired. The receptionist asks for your passport. You want to say:
"Aquí tiene. ¿Puede darme la llave de mi habitación? Mi empresa la reservó para mí."
❓ How do you say "darme" and "para mí" in English? What small words do you need?
✅ The Tool: Object Pronouns
"Here you go. Can you give me the key card? My company booked it for me."

Rule: Object pronouns go after the verb or after a preposition (for, to, with).
Pattern: Verb + object pronoun  ·  Preposition + object pronoun

⚠️ Top A1.2 errors with object pronouns — correct immediately:
✗ "Give to me the key" → ✅ Give me the key (no "to" before me after give)
✗ "Call to her" → ✅ Call her (no preposition needed after call)
✗ "I booked for he" → ✅ I booked it for him (object form, not subject form)
✗ "Send she the email" → ✅ Send her the email

Listening — Mónica Checks In
Mónica Herrera arrives at a business hotel in Monterrey · Listen for object pronouns and hotel vocabulary
⏱ 8 MIN
Mónica's Hotel Check-In — Monterrey
A1.2 · M3 · S3A · Voice: Sarah (American English) · Speed: 0.80
Receptionist: Good evening! Welcome to Hotel Ejecutivo. How can I help you?

Mónica: Hi, good evening. I have a reservation. My name is Mónica Herrera, from ConnectPro International.

Receptionist: Let me check... Yes, I can see it. A single room for two nights. Is that correct?

Mónica: Yes, that's right. My company booked it for me.

Receptionist: Perfect. Can I see your ID, please?

Mónica: Of course. Here you go.

Receptionist: Thank you. Your room is 412, on the 4th floor. Here is your key card. Don't lose it!

Mónica: Thank you. Can you give me the Wi-Fi password?

Receptionist: Of course. The password is "hotel2025." I'll write it down for you.

Mónica: Great. And does the room include breakfast?

Receptionist: Yes, we serve it from 6:30 to 10 AM in the restaurant on the first floor.

Mónica: Perfect. One more thing — I need to check out early tomorrow. Can you prepare the receipt for me? I need it for my company.

Receptionist: No problem. We'll have it ready for you at the front desk. Do you need anything else?

Mónica: No, that's all. Thank you very much.

Receptionist: You're welcome. Have a good night!
📎 ElevenLabs: Voice — Sarah (American English) · Speed 0.80 · Pitch 1.0 · A1.2 M3 S3A

At the Hotel — Your Turn
Role-play hotel situations · Use object pronouns + hotel vocabulary · Accuracy is the goal
⏱ 12 MIN
📊 Opinion Meter
"Hotels are more important than the meeting itself when you travel for business."
Agreement: 50%
💬 How to run this: Students work in pairs — one is Guest, one is Receptionist. Switch roles after Q3. Teacher monitors for object pronoun accuracy. Correct every "give to me" → "give me" immediately.
Question 1 — Check In
You arrive at a business hotel. Tell the receptionist: your name, your company, and that you have a reservation. Ask for the Wi-Fi password and your key card.
💡 Use: "I have a reservation. My name is... Can you give me...?"
Question 2 — You need things
Your room is missing extra towels, there's no soap, and the Wi-Fi doesn't work. Call reception and ask for help using "Can you bring me...?" and "Can you help me with...?"
💡 Use: "Can you bring me extra towels? / Can you help me with the Wi-Fi?"
Question 3 — Book for a colleague
Your boss asks you to book a hotel for two colleagues visiting from Chicago. Call the hotel and reserve rooms. Use him/her/them: "I need to book a room for them."
💡 Use: "Can you book it for them? / Send them the confirmation."
Question 4 — Check Out
It's morning. You need to check out, get the receipt for your company, and ask for a taxi to the airport. Use at least 3 different object pronouns in your conversation.
💡 Use: "Can you prepare it for me? / Call a taxi for us."

🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"I have a reservation. My name is [name], from [company]."
Your opening line at any hotel check-in worldwide. Confident, clear, professional. No hesitation needed — you just learned it!
PHRASE 2
"Can you give me the Wi-Fi password, please?"
The first thing every business traveler asks. Perfect object pronoun use: "give me." Use this at hotels, cafés, and client offices.
PHRASE 3
"Can you prepare the receipt for me? I need it for my company."
Two object pronouns in one sentence! "for me" + "I need it." This is what professional English sounds like. Use it at checkout.

⚡ 📚 🎯 🎧 🏆
01
Object pronouns replace people and things. me / you / him / her / it / us / them — always AFTER the verb or preposition.
02
Hotel check-in = object pronoun practice. "Give me the key" · "Book it for him" · "Show us the room" — every interaction uses them.
03
Never use subject form after a verb. "Give me" NOT "give I." "Call her" NOT "call she." "Help us" NOT "help we."
