§ 01
Warm-Up — This or That: Workplace Permissions
5–7 min
This or That. The teacher reads each scenario. The student picks one option and explains the rule using must, mustn't, have to, or don't have to. No right answer — the goal is to start talking about permissions and rules right away.
01 · Phone policy in meetings
📵 Put away
phones must be off / face-down
OR
📱 Keep out
no phone rule / silent is enough
At your company, must you put your phone away during meetings? Or don't you have to?
02 · Sharing project files
🔒 Restricted
must use official channels only
OR
📤 Open
can share via email / WhatsApp
Do employees have to use a specific system to share files? Or can they use any platform?
03 · Client visits
🪪 Badge required
visitors must sign in at reception
OR
🚪 Open access
no formal registration process
Do visitors have to sign in before entering your building? Is it compulsory?
04 · Weekly reports
📊 Mandatory
must submit every week, no exceptions
OR
✅ Optional
don't have to — only when needed
Do you have to submit a weekly report at your job? Is it required or optional?
Layer 1 — Errors & Redirects
  • If the student says "I must to put my phone" → no infinitive after must: "I must put". Redirect gently: "Almost — no 'to' after must."
  • If they say "I have to not share" → it's "I don't have to share" (no obligation) or "I mustn't share" (forbidden). Ask: "Is it forbidden, or just not required?"
  • Timing: 5–6 min. Do all 4 pairs. If the student is slow on one, move on — don't get stuck.
Layer 2 — Micro-Celebrations
  • When they use don't have to correctly: "Excellent — that's the hard one. You've already got it."
  • When they self-correct: "You caught that yourself. That's a B1 skill."
  • When they give a real example from their job: "That's exactly how native speakers use this."
Layer 3 — GT Originals & Upsell
  • This session extends S1A/B into new permission vocabulary. Students who completed M2 S1 will notice the same modal system applied to a new domain — that's consolidation.
  • Gentle upsell: "In S2B we'll do a full debate about whether strict workplace rules help or hurt companies — you'll need today's tools for that."

adj.

adj.
"Only authorized personnel can enter that area."
forbidden
adj.
"It is forbidden to share client data with third parties."

adj.
"Attendance at the annual compliance review is mandatory."
Key Phrases for the Workplace
No exceptions
phrase
"This rule applies to everyone — no exceptions."
It's up to you
phrase
"You don't have to come to the team lunch. It's up to you."
strictly enforced
phrase
"The data privacy policy is strictly enforced. Violations have consequences."
no exceptions allowed
phrase
"You must sign in before entering. No exceptions allowed."
⚡ Quick Speak — 2 min
Think of one thing at your job that is compulsory. Say it out loud using must or have to.
Think of one thing that is forbidden. Say it using mustn't or prohibited.
Think of one thing that is optional. Say it using don't have to or it's up to you.
Layer 1 — Key Distinctions
  • Compulsory / mandatory / required are synonyms — teach all three because students will see them in real documents. Mandatory is slightly more formal.
  • Prohibited vs forbidden: both mean "not allowed." Forbidden is slightly more forceful in tone. Both appear in real employee handbooks.
  • Permitted vs authorized: permitted = allowed in general; authorized = officially approved, usually with a formal process.
  • Pronunciation focus: com-PUL-sory (students stress wrong syllable), pro-HIB-it-ed.
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "These 8 adjectives will let you read any HR policy document in English and understand it immediately. That's worth knowing."
  • When they use compulsory in the micro-speak: "That sounds very professional. Exactly the word an HR manager would use."
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • 🌍 Register note: In spoken English, people say forbidden; in documents, they write prohibited or not permitted. Teach both registers here.
  • 💼 Application: "A2.2 students can now read and write a basic HR policy. That's a real-world output we'll use at the end of today's session."
§ 03
Grammar — The Full Permission Map
10–12 min
The problem: An international colleague emails you about your company's data policy. They need to know: what can they share? What is forbidden? What is optional? You have to explain these rules clearly — in English, using the right modal for each situation.
The Four Modal Slots — Which one to use?
SituationModalMeaningExample
Strong internal rule / obligationmustThe company or I require thisYou must log out of all systems before leaving.
External rule / law requires ithave toThe law / HR system demands thisWe have to submit the audit report by Friday — it's a legal requirement.
Forbidden / prohibitedmustn'tIt is not allowed — do NOT do thisYou mustn't share client passwords with anyone.
Not required / optionaldon't have toNo obligation — your choiceYou don't have to wear a suit on Fridays.
Asking about permissions — Question forms
QuestionShort Answer (Yes)Short Answer (No)
Do I have to submit a report every week?Yes, you do.No, you don't.
Does she have to attend the safety briefing?Yes, she does.No, she doesn't.
Do we have to use the company VPN?Yes, you do — it's compulsory.No, you don't. It's optional.
Important: We use do/does + have to for questions — not must. In natural speech, we almost never ask "Must I attend?" — it sounds very formal or old-fashioned. Use "Do I have to attend?"
⚠️ The Critical Contrast — Don't confuse these
MUSTN'T = FORBIDDEN ❌
You mustn't share this.
It is prohibited. If you do it, there are consequences.
"You mustn't enter that server room without authorization."
DON'T HAVE TO = NOT REQUIRED ✅ (optional)
You don't have to share this.
It is optional. You can share it, or not — your choice.
"You don't have to attend the optional training session."
⚠️ Common Errors at A2.2
❌ "I must to attend the meeting."→✅ "I must attend the meeting."
No infinitive "to" after modal verbs (must, should, can, will...).
❌ "I don't must go."→✅ "I don't have to go."
We don't negate must with "don't" for absence of obligation — use "don't have to."
❌ "You mustn't use the printer if you don't want."→✅ "You don't have to use the printer if you don't want."
Mustn't = forbidden. The student means "not necessary" — so don't have to is correct.
Layer 1 — Teaching Focus
  • The key addition of S2A vs S1A: the question form with "Do you have to…?" — this is new production territory. Drill it explicitly.
  • The contrast (mustn't vs don't have to) was introduced in S1A. Here, students should be able to produce it — not just recognize it. Raise the bar.
  • If the student hesitates between must and have to: "Both are OK — the difference is: must = I decide this rule; have to = the company or law decides."
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "The question 'Do you have to…?' is one of the most useful questions in any workplace conversation. Once you have it, you can navigate any new job in English."
  • Celebrate when they ask the question correctly. It's a production milestone.
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • 🔊 Pronunciation: "have to" /hæftə/ — the V sounds like F, and "to" reduces to "tə". Drill: "Do you haf-tuh?" not "have-to".
  • 💼 Real-world hook: "A2.2 students who master this question form can handle entire onboarding conversations in English at a new client site."
§ 04
Reading — Company Policy Update Email
10–12 min
Read the internal company email. Identify all rules: what employees must / mustn't do and what they have to / don't have to do.
From:HR Department — Nexcore Industrial Solutions <hr@nexcore.mx> To:All Staff <allstaff@nexcore.mx> Subject:Updated Workplace Security & Access Policy — Effective November 1st

Dear Colleagues,


Following our recent security audit, we are updating several workplace policies effective November 1st. Please read the following carefully.


Access & Badges

All employees must wear their access badge visibly at all times while on company premises. Visitors have to sign in at reception and collect a temporary badge before entering any department. Employees mustn't lend their badge to any other person — this includes colleagues.


IT & Data Security

You must use the company VPN when connecting to internal systems from outside the office. You mustn't store confidential client data on personal devices. You don't have to use the encrypted email system for general internal communication — it is optional for non-sensitive topics. However, you must use it for any client-facing or financial data.


Working Hours & Remote Work

Engineers and coordinators have to notify their line manager at least 24 hours before working remotely. Sales representatives don't have to provide advance notice for remote days — this is flexible for the sales team. All staff must be reachable by phone during core hours (9 AM – 2 PM).


If you have questions about any of these policies, please contact the HR department directly.

Best regards,
Sandra Flores
Human Resources Manager · Nexcore Industrial Solutions
Activity 1 — Comprehension: True, False or Not Mentioned
Read each statement. Click the correct answer based on the email above.
1. All employees have to wear a badge at work.
2. Employees can lend their badge to a colleague in an emergency.
3. Using the encrypted email system is compulsory for all internal messages.
4. Sales representatives have to notify their manager 24 hours before working remotely.
5. The company's security audit found serious data breaches last year.
Activity 2 — Modal Analysis: Find the rule
Choose the correct modal for each situation described.
6. Using the VPN from home — it is required by the company. What modal do you use?
7. The Friday team lunch — you can go if you want. You are not required to attend. What modal do you use?
8. Sharing client data on WhatsApp — it is prohibited. What modal do you use?
Layer 1 — Reading Strategy
  • Have the student read the email first silently (2 min), then read it aloud sentence by sentence. Correct pronunciation as they go.
  • The color-coding is student-visible — use it to spot-check understanding. Ask: "Why is 'must' in red? Why is 'don't have to' in green?"
  • For Q3: the email says encrypted email is optional for non-sensitive topics but required for client/financial data. If they answer False, confirm why — partial reading is a common error at A2.2.
  • Answer key: Q1 True · Q2 False · Q3 False · Q4 False (sales don't have to) · Q5 Not Mentioned · Q6 must · Q7 don't have to · Q8 mustn't
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "This email is the type of message you'll receive on your first day at any English-speaking company or on a multinational project. You just read and understood all of it."
  • If they get Q3 wrong: "Good — that one is tricky. That's exactly why we read policies carefully."
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • 🌍 Culture note: In US/UK companies, HR policies are typically written in a formal, impersonal tone. Teach the student to recognize the register difference between "must" in writing and "have to" in speech.
  • 💼 "The student just processed a real corporate HR document. At the end of this module, they'll write one of their own."

Activities — Workplace Scenarios
10–12 min
Scenario A · Factory Floor
Safety Rules at the Plant
Workers must wear a hard hat and safety glasses at all times.
You mustn't use your phone near moving machinery.
Supervisors have to report any accidents to management within 2 hours.
Gloves don't have to be worn in the administration area.
Scenario B · Corporate Office
Meeting & Client Protocols
You must arrive on time for client meetings — no exceptions.
Employees mustn't discuss competitor information with clients.
Coordinators have to send a meeting agenda 24 hours in advance.
You don't have to wear formal attire on internal team days.
Drag Activity — Put the policy statement in order
Rearrange the sentence segments to form a correct and logical company policy statement. Drag the items into the right order.
  • ⠿at any time during work hours.
  • ⠿their access badge visible
  • ⠿All employees must keep
Drag Activity 2 — Build the prohibition
Arrange the segments to form a correct prohibition sentence.
  • ⠿outside the designated smoking area.
  • ⠿Employees mustn't smoke
  • ⠿or use e-cigarettes
Activity 3 — Permission or Prohibition?
Read each workplace rule. Choose the correct modal verb.
9. The rule: "Do not take photos inside the production area." → Employees _____ take photos in the production area.
10. Lunch break is flexible — you can eat at any time between 12 and 2 PM. → You _____ take your lunch at exactly 12:00.
11. The government requires all companies to submit a tax report every quarter. → We _____ submit this report — it's a legal requirement.
12. The company policy states: "All visitors are required to sign a confidentiality agreement." → Visitors _____ sign the NDA before entering.
⚡ Micro-Speaking — 3 min
Look at Scenario A (factory) or Scenario B (office). Tell the teacher 3 rules from that workplace — one must, one mustn't, one don't have to. Try from memory — don't read.
Now tell the teacher: does your real workplace have any rule that is similar to one of the scenarios? Describe it.
Layer 1 — Activity Management
  • Answer key: Q9 mustn't · Q10 don't have to · Q11 have to · Q12 must
  • For the drag activities — if on iPad and drag doesn't work, student can read the segments and tell you the correct order verbally. The content is what matters.
  • The scenario cards are reading + listening prep — student reads rules aloud, you correct pronunciation, especially mustn't /ˈmʌsnt/ and prohibited /proʊˈhɪbɪtɪd/.
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • Micro-speaking from memory is a key milestone. Celebrate: "You just produced 3 modal sentences without looking. That's real English."
  • If they connect to their real workplace: "That's the goal — when the grammar leaves the classroom and goes to your job."
Layer 3 — GT Originals
  • 📋 At the end of the module (S5 review), students write a 4-line company policy using today's vocabulary. Start priming them: "Think about which rules from your job are most important to communicate in English."

§ 07
Speaking Practice — Workplace Rules: Debate
10–12 min
Speaking · Debate Format · S2 Rotation
Do Strict Rules Make Better Companies?
Format: structured mini-debate. Teacher and student take opposite sides. Use must, mustn't, have to and don't have to to argue your position. Then switch sides and argue the opposite. 10–12 min total.
DEBATE TOPIC
Companies with strict rules and many obligations perform better than companies with flexible policies.
"A company where employees must follow detailed rules and have to report everything performs better than one where staff don't have to follow many procedures. Do you agree?"
Side A — In Favor (Strict Rules Work)
Employees must follow clear procedures — it prevents mistakes and accidents.
When everyone has to follow the same rules, it's more fair and transparent.
Data protection policies must be strict — one breach can destroy a company's reputation.
Clients have to trust that your company follows regulations. Rules build that trust.
Side B — Against (Flexibility is Better)
Employees don't have to follow 100 rules to do their job well — trust is more effective.
Too many obligations slow down decision-making. Sometimes you don't have to ask permission for everything.
Creative teams work better when they don't have to report every small action.
If the company mustn't experiment or take risks, it can't innovate.
💡 Teacher tip: start with the student arguing their real opinion first (easier), then have them defend the opposite side (more challenging — and that's the real language workout).
Question 01
Does your company have too many rules, not enough, or about the right amount?
Use: "I think we must… / We don't have to… but maybe we should…"
Question 02
What is one rule at your company that you think is very important? Why does it exist?
Use: "Employees must… because… / It's compulsory because…"
Question 03
Is there a rule you think is unnecessary? What would happen if it didn't exist?
Use: "I think we don't have to… / It's optional and that's fine because…"
Question 04
What is one thing employees mustn't do in your workplace? Have you ever seen someone break that rule?
Use: "In my company, you mustn't… / If someone does it, they have to…"
Question 05
If you were the HR manager, what is one new rule you would create? What would employees have to do?
Use: "I would say that employees must… / They would have to… because…"
⚡ Speaking Challenge — 2 min independent
"Describe the 3 most important rules at your company using must, mustn't, and have to. Explain why each one exists. Go."
No preparation. Student speaks directly. Teacher only corrects modal errors — let the content flow. This is free production territory.
Layer 1 — Debate Management
  • If the student struggles with Side B (against strict rules): give them a sentence starter from the scaffold card. Don't let them stay silent — one sentence is enough to get momentum.
  • Time the debate: 2 min per side, then 2 min Q&A, then the challenge. Total: ~10–11 min.
  • Modal error during debate → don't interrupt. Make a mental note. Correct after the student finishes their argument: "You said 'I don't must go' — try that sentence again."
Layer 2 — Motivation
  • "You just argued a position in English — and then defended the opposite. That's something most English students can't do until B1+. You're ahead."
  • After the challenge: always give one specific piece of positive feedback ("Your use of 'compulsory' was perfect") before any correction.
Layer 3 — GT Originals & Upsell
  • 💼 "This debate skill maps to real business English: performance reviews, policy discussions, supplier negotiations. You're building the vocabulary and the confidence for those moments."
  • 🎯 Upsell: "In S2B we continue with this — but you'll do a full onboarding role play as the HR manager explaining rules to a new employee. You'll need everything from today."
  • Testimonial trigger: "A lot of students tell me that after this session, they notice workplace rules in English all around them — on signs, in emails, in contracts. That's when the language starts working for you."
