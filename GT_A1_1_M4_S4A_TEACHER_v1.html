Speaking Topic — Describe It Precisely
Color + location + this/that · The full precision toolkit · Real office objects
⏱ 8 MIN
S4 Theme — Full Description
Color + Location = Precision
In quality, logistics, and engineering environments across the Bajío, saying "the red folder in the blue cabinet next to the printer" — not just "that thing over there" — is what separates functional from professional English. Today you complete the full description system.
Round 1 — 2 min
Describe 4 objects to your partner using COLOR + LOCATION. No pointing — only words. Partner identifies the object from your description alone.
💡 "The blue notebook is on my desk, next to the black laptop."
Round 2 — 3 min
Partner A holds two similar objects of different colors. Partner B says: "This [color] one is [X]. That [color] one is [Y]." Distinguish by color + this/that. Switch after 90 seconds.
💡 "This black pen is here. That blue pen is on the shelf."
Round 3 — 3 min
Teams call: Partner A (WFH) needs a file. B describes exactly which one — color, where it is, and this/that. A confirms "Got it!" only when they understand exactly which object it is.
💡 "It's the grey folder — that one in the cabinet, between the red and the blue one."

Teacher model: "The contracts are in the blue folder — this one, on my desk, in front of the monitor. There is also a yellow copy in the cabinet behind me — that one, between the black binder and the grey folder on the top shelf."

📌 Perfect combination: color + this/that + prepositions + there is. That's the S4 target sentence.

§4
Colloquial — Describing Things Naturally
How native speakers pinpoint objects by color in real conversation
⏱ 5 MIN
"The blue one — not the red one."
El azul — no el rojo.
Use "[color] one" to avoid repeating the noun. "Which folder?" → "The blue one." Not "The blue folder." Natural, efficient, native-sounding. Very common in corporate settings.
"The one right there — the grey one."
Ese de allá — el gris.
"Right there" = very close to where you point. Combine "that one" + "right there" for exact location. Use on Teams calls when pointing at your screen during a screenshare.
"It's the black one — on the left."
Es el negro — a la izquierda.
Perfect for phone/Teams. Color + location in one sentence = clearest possible description. "It's the black one on the left" tells WHAT and WHERE simultaneously. No confusion possible.
"I mean the yellow one — not that one, this one."
Me refiero al amarillo — no ese, este.
"I mean" = quiero decir — use to correct a misunderstanding. Then color + this/that to specify. "I mean the yellow folder — not that one, this one on my desk." Very natural for clearing up confusion quickly.
📌 Drill Note

Drill "the [color] one" until automatic: teacher holds up two differently colored objects → student says "the [color] one." Then combine: "The blue one, on the left, next to the printer." That's how professionals sound. 6 rapid-fire rounds, then pairs continue.

§5
Grammar — Color + This/That/These/Those + Review
Adjective order · Color before noun · This/that + color · Full M4 integration
⏱ 15–18 MIN
🚨 Real Situation

Your US client calls: "I need the safety inspection report from last quarter — the one we signed in March."

You know exactly where it is: "Está en la carpeta roja — esa, la que está encima del archivero gris, entre los dos folders azules."

❓ How do you say this in English? You need color + this/that + prepositions — all three working together.

✅ The Tools: Color + This/That + Preposition

"It's in the red folder — that one, on top of the grey cabinet, between the two blue folders."

Rule: Color BEFORE the noun → "the red folder" — never "the folder red"
This/that: This red folder (close, singular) · Those blue ones (far, plural)

⚠️ Top errors — correct immediately:
✗ "The folder red" → ✅ The red folder — color before noun in English
✗ "This folders are red" → ✅ These folders are red (plural = these/those)
✗ "That one blue" → ✅ That blue one — color before "one"
✗ "These ones" → ✅ Just these — "ones" is redundant at A1.1

Listening — The Wrong Color
Ana helps Carlos find the right folder by color and location · Full M4 integration
⏱ 10–12 MIN
The Wrong Color — Solaris Manufacturing, Celaya
A1.1 · M4 · S4A · Voice: Sarah + James (American) · Speed: 0.78
Carlos: Ana, I need the Q3 safety report. Do you know where it is?

Ana: Yes! It's in a red folder — this one, right here on my desk, next to the grey monitor.

Carlos: Are you sure? I thought it was in the blue folder.

Ana: No, the blue one has the supplier contracts. That's in the cabinet — that grey cabinet behind the door, on the left. The red folder is the safety reports. This is the one you need.

Carlos: OK, so this red one on your desk? Not that blue one in the cabinet?

Ana: Exactly. These red folders are all safety reports — Q1, Q2, and Q3. Those blue folders in the cabinet are supplier contracts. Different systems.

Carlos: And where are the yellow ones? I see some on the shelf behind you.

Ana: Those yellow binders? Those are the quality manuals — ISO standards. Top shelf, between the black ones and the white ones. There are eight of them.

Carlos: This office has a lot of color coding! Thank you, Ana.

Ana: No problem! And Carlos — the grey box next to the printer? That one has the 2022 archive. Don't open it by mistake — wrong year!

3 Phrases You Can Use at Work This Week
Starting tomorrow — precise, professional, natural
Phrase 1
"It's in the red folder — that one on the shelf, next to the grey cabinet."
The complete S4 sentence. Color + that + location + preposition. Use it the next time anyone asks where a document is. In person, on Teams, or on the phone — this sentence is complete and professional.
Phrase 2
"The blue one — not that one, this one."
Use "[color] one" to avoid repeating the noun. Add "not that one, this one" to clarify. Native-sounding, efficient, and completely natural in any corporate setting.
Phrase 3
"These [color] folders are for [purpose] — those [color] ones are in the cabinet."
Use when explaining your filing system to a visitor or new colleague. These/those + color + location = the full explanation. Sounds like someone who owns their workspace in English.
