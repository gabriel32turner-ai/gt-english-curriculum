§1
Do You Know What They Know About You?
Warm-Up · Activate data privacy intuitions · Connect to Passive Voice in corporate language
⏱ 5 MIN
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds

Say this out loud right now: "My data is being collected every time I use [name an app]. I think it is being used to [say something]." One sentence. Your real opinion. Go.

5:00
Warm-Up
Question 1
When did you last read the Terms & Conditions before clicking "I agree"? Do you actually know what you consented to?
💡 Try passive: "I was asked to agree to..." / "My data was shared with..."
Question 2
Which apps do you use every day that are probably selling or sharing your data? How does that make you feel — angry, indifferent, or powerless?
💡 Use: "My location is being tracked by..." / "My behavior is monitored when..."
Question 3
Is "free" really free? If you don't pay for the product, what ARE you paying with? Is that a fair exchange — or is it exploitation?
💡 Use: "Users are being monetized by..." / "Attention is sold to..."
Question 4
Should companies be required to tell you exactly what they collect and why? Or should people simply read what they sign? Who is responsible here?
💡 Use: "Companies should be regulated by..." / "Users are never clearly informed when..."
⚡ Teacher Note

Data privacy is simultaneously abstract and deeply personal — the key is to make it real fast. Before discussing: ask students to open their phone settings and check location permissions for 3 apps. Real data, real reaction. The Passive Voice emerges naturally: "My location is being accessed by..." This warm-up always generates strong opinions from quiet students.

§2
Your Data Is Being Sold Right Now
Speaking Topic · Carlos Mendez scenario · 3 structured rounds · Passive in every answer
⏱ 8 MIN
Today's Topic
Your Data Is Being Sold
Carlos Mendez uses a free fitness app, a social media platform, and a navigation app every day. This morning he received an advertisement for a product he had never searched for — but had mentioned out loud in a conversation near his phone. He now suspects his microphone is being activated without consent. As his executive coach, help him understand what is happening, what his rights are, and what he should do about it.
Round 1 — What Is Happening?
Explain to Carlos what is probably happening with his data — and why the microphone ad is not as surprising as it seems.
Use: "Your data is being collected by..." / "A profile has been built from your..." / "Your behavior has been tracked for..."
"Carlos, you're not being hacked — you're being profiled. A detailed behavioral model has been built from your location data, search history, and app usage. That ad was served to you because you match a profile that was sold to an advertiser. Your microphone may or may not be accessed — but your data is already being used in ways you never consented to."
Round 2 — What Were His Rights?
What should Carlos have been told when he signed up? What was he agreeing to — and what was never clearly explained?
Use: "He should have been informed that..." / "His data was sold without being clearly..." / "He was never told that..."
"He should have been given a clear explanation — not 47 pages of legal language. Users are technically told their data can be shared with 'third-party partners,' but the real meaning is hidden. His consent was obtained through a design that was built to confuse, not inform."
Round 3 — What Should He Do?
Give Carlos 3 concrete recommendations. What should be changed, deleted, or demanded? Use Passive Voice for each recommendation.
Use: "Permissions should be reviewed..." / "Data should be deleted from..." / "Companies must be required to..."
"First: all app permissions should be reviewed — location and microphone should be set to 'never' for apps that don't need them. Second: his data should be requested from each company under GDPR. Third: companies should be required by law to explain in plain language exactly how data is monetized."
⚡ Teacher Note

After Round 1, pause and ask: "How many of you use a free app every day without knowing how your data is used?" Show of hands. Then ask one student to describe what they discovered in the warm-up (location permissions check). This makes the speaking personal and the grammar automatic.

Module 3 Phrases — In Context
5 colloquial phrases applied to data privacy situations · All 5 appear naturally in today's topic
⏱ 5 MIN
"I'm hooked"
The app is designed for this. You're not weak — you've been engineered to feel this way.
"I know my data is being sold but honestly? I'm hooked on the app. I can't delete it."
"It blew up"
Data breaches and privacy scandals go viral fast — the news cycle is faster than regulation.
"The WhatsApp privacy change blew up. Millions of accounts were deleted the same week."
"That's sus"
When companies write Terms & Conditions in language no one understands — that's sus.
"The new update asked for access to my microphone and contacts. That's sus. I declined."
"I ghosted them"
Sometimes the only answer to a data-hungry app is to disappear completely.
"After the breach was announced, I ghosted TikTok — deleted the app, requested my data, blocked the domain."
"Low-key obsessed"
Once you start learning about data collection, you can't stop. It's everywhere once you see it.
"I'm low-key obsessed with reading privacy policies now. It's disturbing what's being collected."
⚡ Drill Note

Run a "phrase auction": give a data privacy scenario and students must use one of these phrases to react — first to speak wins a point. The goal is spontaneity, not perfection. "That's sus" and "I ghosted them" are most natural in this topic context.

§5
Terms & Conditions Decoder
Grammar referential · Passive Voice in corporate language · Read the clause, find the passive, reveal what it really means
⏱ 12 MIN
REF Passive Voice — Corporate & Legal Language
TenseCorporate passive example
Present Simple"Data is collected to improve user experience."
Present Continuous"Your activity is being monitored in real time."
Present Perfect"Consent has been obtained through use of the service."
Future"Information may be shared with trusted partners."
Past Simple"The policy was updated on January 1st."

WHY Companies Choose Passive

Hides responsibility: "Data is collected" — who collects it? They do. But you'd never know from the sentence.

Sounds neutral and technical: "Consent is obtained" sounds like a natural process, not a decision they made.

Creates legal distance: If "data may be shared," no one is promising anything — or admitting anything.

🔍 TERMS & CONDITIONS DECODER

Each card shows a real-style clause from a Terms & Conditions document. Identify the Passive Voice structures (tagged in green), then click to decode what the company is actually saying in plain English.

📱 Mobile App — Section 3.4
"Personal information may be collected through your use of the Service and may be shared with affiliated entities and trusted third-party partners as deemed necessary to provide the services described herein."
may be collectedmay be shareddeemed necessarydescribed herein
Plain English Translation
We will collect your personal data. We will sell it or give it to other companies. We decide what is "necessary" — not you. You have no idea which companies will receive it.
💬 Discussion: Why is "may be shared" more dangerous than "will be shared"? What does "may" do to the user's ability to know what will happen?
🌐 Social Network — Section 7.1
"By using this platform, it is understood that consent has been provided for the processing of all data generated through your interactions. This policy may be updated at any time without prior notice."
is understoodhas been providedgeneratedmay be updated
Plain English Translation
Using the app = consenting. You didn't actively agree to anything — it was assumed. Everything you do creates data we own. We can change these rules without telling you.
💬 Discussion: "Consent has been provided" — by whom? When? The passive hides that YOU never actively consented. How is this being used to manipulate legal language?
🎯 Advertising Company — Section 12
"Data is collected across partner sites to build interest profiles. These profiles are used to serve targeted advertisements. The accuracy of inferences cannot be guaranteed but will be applied regardless."
is collectedare usedcannot be guaranteedwill be applied
Plain English Translation
We track you everywhere on the internet. We build a personality profile. Our guesses about you might be wrong — but we will use them to target you anyway, and you can't stop us.
💬 Discussion: "Cannot be guaranteed but will be applied" — what is the ethical problem here? If inferences may be wrong, why is the passive used to make them sound inevitable?
📧 Email Provider — Data Breach Notice
"It has come to our attention that certain user information may have been accessed by an unauthorized party. Steps are being taken to ensure this does not reoccur. Affected users will be notified in due course."
has come to our attentionmay have been accessedare being takenwill be notified
Plain English Translation
Hackers stole your data. We don't know exactly what was taken. We're doing something about it (unspecified). We will tell you eventually — when we feel ready. Don't ask when.
💬 Discussion: Count the passive structures. Not a single active sentence. Why? How does the passive make this company sound responsible while saying almost nothing of substance?

✏️ Activity A — Identify the Passive: tense and strategic purpose
1. "User data is being processed to improve service delivery." — What tense and why passive?
2. "Consent has been obtained through continued use of the platform." — What is the company implying?
3. "This policy may be updated at any time without prior notice." — Why is "may" + passive particularly dangerous here?
✏️ Activity B — True or False: Data Privacy & Passive Voice
"Your data is being sold" uses Present Continuous Passive correctly.
When a company writes "data may be shared," they are legally committed to sharing it.
"Consent has been obtained" (Present Perfect Passive) suggests consent happened at some point before now and still applies.
The main reason companies use passive in legal language is to hide who is responsible for the action.
"The breach was announced" and "We announced the breach" convey exactly the same information and emphasis.

The Investigation — What They Found
A tech journalist investigates how Carlos's data was collected, sold, and used · Passive Voice throughout
⏱ 8 MIN
The Data Investigation — "What Was Done With Carlos's Information"
B1.2 · M3 · S4A · Voice: Emma (British) · Speed: 0.85
Welcome to Digital Dispatch. I'm Emma Chen, and today I want to tell you about something that was discovered during a three-month investigation into how personal data is collected, sold, and used by free apps.

The investigation was conducted by our team in partnership with a European digital rights organization. We focused on six popular apps — all free, all used by millions of people every day. What was found surprised even us.

In the first month, data was being collected continuously — not just when the apps were open, but in the background. Location data was tracked every four minutes on average. Microphone access was activated by two of the six apps — not for calls, but to capture ambient audio that was then analyzed by AI systems.

By the second month, this data had been sold. Our team was able to trace the data to over 340 third-party companies. The original users had never been informed that their data would be shared beyond the apps themselves. Profiles had been constructed — detailed enough to predict purchasing behavior, political views, and even emotional states.

In the third month, we reached out to all six companies. Five did not respond. The sixth issued a statement — written almost entirely in passive voice — in which no agent was identified, no apology was made, and no commitment to change was offered. The statement ended with: "User privacy is taken seriously by our organization."

What should be done? That's the real question. Stronger laws need to be passed. Companies must be required to disclose exactly how data is monetized. And users — like you — need to be given real tools to protect themselves. Not just a 47-page document that was designed to confuse, not inform.

Your data matters. It's time it was treated that way.

★
3 Phrases You Can Use This Week
Passive Voice in professional and personal contexts · Applicable Monday morning
Phrase 1
"This information is being used without my knowledge."
Use this when something about you — your data, your work, your words — is being shared or used without your awareness. Professional, clear, and powerful. Works in meetings, emails, and HR conversations.
Phrase 2
"Consent was never clearly obtained."
When someone claims you agreed to something you didn't properly understand — in a contract, a policy, a work agreement. The passive makes this sound analytical rather than accusatory. High-value in legal or HR contexts.
Phrase 3
"Greater transparency needs to be demanded."
Use this to advocate for openness — about data, decisions, or processes at work. Passive + "needs to be" sounds less personal and more systemic. Perfect for making a structural argument without pointing fingers.
§7
Session Wrap-Up
3 key insights · Checklist · My Win · Preview S4B
⏱ 5 MIN
01
Passive hides corporate responsibility.
"Data is collected" removes the company from the sentence. This is not a grammatical accident — it is a legal and PR strategy. Reading Terms & Conditions is now a grammar skill.
02
"May be" + Passive = maximum corporate protection.
"Data may be shared" commits to nothing. The combination of modal uncertainty + passive voice is the most powerful legal deflection structure in English.
03
Passive can also be used to demand accountability.
"Companies must be regulated." "Transparency must be demanded." The same grammar that hides responsibility can be flipped to demand it. You now have both tools.
