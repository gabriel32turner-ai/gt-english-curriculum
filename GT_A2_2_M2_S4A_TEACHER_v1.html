Warm-Up
Dilemma Board — Asking for advice in professional life
5–8 MIN
🤔 Real situation: Someone at work comes to you with a problem: "I have two job offers. One pays more money. The other is closer to home. Should I take the higher salary?" — In S3A you learned to give advice with "You should...". But today: how do YOU ask for advice in English? What question do you use?
💬 Partner activity: Think of a real dilemma from your life (work decision, difficult person, time problem, career choice). Tell your partner the situation. Your partner asks you: "Should you...? / Why don't you...? / What about...?" Don't worry about grammar — just try the questions. (Teacher: collect the questions on the board. Focus on the question word order with "should".)
🧠 Think about the difference: "You should apply for the job" vs "Should I apply for the job?" — same word, completely different function. One is advice. One is a question. Today we install the full system: questions + reasons + suggestions.
⚡ Teacher note (S4A): S3A/S3B covered the giving advice side. S4A adds: (1) "Should I/we…?" questions — asking for advice, (2) "I think you should / I don't think you should" — softened opinion, (3) "Shouldn't you…?" — gentle confrontation, (4) "Why don't you/we…?" — indirect suggestions. These are the forms students need for RECEIVING, requesting, and suggesting — not just delivering. Collect authentic student dilemmas — they'll become the content for §3 and Speaking.
⚡ Quick Win — 30 seconds
Ask your partner a real question right now using "Should I...?" — about anything from work or your life. Then your partner answers with "I think you should..." or "I don't think you should..." Just try it!
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • ACCIÓN: Di "Should I accept this job offer?" — y pregunta al alumno qué respondería. Anota su respuesta. Usa esa respuesta como ejemplo vivo en §3. El contexto personal ancla la gramática.
  • El diagnóstico clave de S4A: ¿el alumno ya puede preguntar "Should I...?" o solo puede dar advice con "You should"? La mayoría de A2.2 tiene el segundo pero no el primero — ese es exactamente el gap que S4A cierra.
  • "Why don't you...?" — muchos alumnos dicen "Why you don't...?" (orden de Simple Present). Detecta este error en el warm-up y corrígelo en §3 con la tabla de estructura.
  • Collect 3 student dilemmas en el board — se convierten en el content de §3 Activity B (scenarios) y Speaking §SP.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "In S3A you learned to give advice. Today you learn to ASK for it — and to suggest, not just tell. These are the sentences you need in meetings when you say 'What do you think we should do?' That's A2.2 professional English."
Capa 3 · Retención & Upsell
  • "S4B es la sesión de producción pura bajo presión — role plays de conversaciones reales donde el alumno pide y da advice en el mismo intercambio. S4A instala las herramientas que S4B va a exigir sin soporte."

Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • ACCIÓN: Para "in my opinion" y "personally" — practica el contraste. "In my opinion, you should accept" vs "Personally, I wouldn't accept." Ambas expresan opinión pero el segundo tiene más matiz. A2.2 necesita este nivel de sofisticación.
  • "Worth it" — chunk fijo, enseña como bloque. "Is it worth it?" / "I think it's worth it." / "It's not worth the risk." Tres formas, un chunk. Alta frecuencia en conversaciones de decisión.

§3 — Grammar
The Full Advice System — Questions, Reasons & Suggestions
15–18 MIN
🔢
Grammar
Should questions · Shouldn't + reasons · Why don't you/we
15–18 MIN
🚨 Real Situation — Monday morning
Your manager says: "We have a budget problem. The client wants the project finished faster. The team is already exhausted. What do you think we should do?"

You want to say: "¿Deberíamos hablar con el cliente primero? Creo que no deberíamos prometer más rápido sin hablar con el equipo. ¿Por qué no reorganizamos las prioridades?"
❓ How do you form these three different structures in English? They all use "should" — but in three completely different ways.
✅ The Tools — Three Layers of the Advice System
"Should we speak to the client first?" ← asking for advice/suggestion
"We shouldn't promise faster without talking to the team." ← advice with reason
"Why don't we reorganize the priorities?" ← indirect suggestion
Structure — The Full System
FormStructureProfessional exampleFunction
Should I…?Should + I/we/you + base verb?"Should I send the email now or wait until Monday?"Asking for advice or permission
Should we…?Should + we + base verb?"Should we tell the team about the change today?"Asking for group decision/advice
Shouldn't + reasonSubject + shouldn't + base verb + because/since"You shouldn't accept without reading the contract because there may be hidden clauses."Strong advice with justification
Why don't you…?Why don't + you/we + base verb?"Why don't you talk to HR directly? They can help with this."Gentle, indirect suggestion
I think you shouldI think + subject + should + base verb"I think you should take the offer — it's a great opportunity."Softened, personal opinion
I don't think you shouldI don't think + subject + should + base verb"I don't think you should resign before you have another offer."Softened negative advice
SHOULDN'T vs DON'T HAVE TO — The Classic Trap
These look similar but mean very different things:
"You shouldn't be late." = It's a bad idea / I advise against it
"You don't have to be early." = It's not required / optional
⚠️ "You shouldn't wear a tie" = it's bad idea
"You don't have to wear a tie" = it's optional — totally fine either way
WHY DON'T YOU vs YOU SHOULD — Register difference
Both give advice — but "Why don't you" is softer and more collaborative:
"You should talk to the client." = direct recommendation
"Why don't you talk to the client?" = gentle suggestion / collaborative
✅ "Why don't you/we" is very common in meetings when you want to suggest without imposing. Use it when the relationship matters.

⚠️ Top Errors — Correct Immediately
✗ "Why you don't call him?"→✅ "Why don't you call him?" (inversion: Why don't + subject)
Most common word order error. "Why don't you" is a fixed structure — don't put subject first.
✗ "I don't think you shouldn't go."→✅ "I don't think you should go." (negative already in "don't think")
Double negative error. "I don't think you should" already means the same as "I think you shouldn't" — never use both.
✗ "Should you to call?" / "Should you calling?"→✅ "Should you call?" (modal + base verb, never to/ing)
✗ "You shouldn't do that because is a bad idea."→✅ "...because it's a bad idea." (don't drop the subject "it")

Subject: I need your advice — urgent

Lucía,

I have a really difficult situation and I don't know what to do. A startup in Monterrey offered me a position — Operations Director. It's a big title, good salary, but it's a new company with only 18 months of history. Should I accept this offer even though the company is so new? My current job is stable but not exciting. Should we talk this weekend? I really need a second opinion.

— Andrea

From: Lucía Morales · To: Andrea Soto
Subject: RE: I need your advice — urgent

Andrea,

Of course — call me Sunday. But here's my initial reaction:

Why don't you research the startup's investors first? If serious investors are behind the company, the risk is lower. You could also ask for a 3-month trial period before signing a long-term contract.

You shouldn't accept immediately just because the title sounds impressive. You shouldn't resign from your current role until you have a signed contract in your hand — because startups can change plans very quickly.

I think you should be excited about this — it's a real opportunity. But I don't think you should decide this weekend. Take two weeks. Make a list: risks vs opportunities. Then decide.

You're ready for this level. Trust that.

— Lucía

Career Coach Call — Should I Change Direction?
A2.2 · M2 · S4A · Voice: Sarah (American) · Speed: 0.82
▼ Show ElevenLabs Script
Coach: Good morning, Andrea. So — you said you have a big decision to make.

Andrea: Yes. I received a job offer. Operations Director at a startup. And I don't know — should I accept it? The salary is good but the company is new.

Coach: Interesting. First question: should we look at this from a risk perspective or an opportunity perspective? Both are valid.

Andrea: Both, I think. That's exactly my problem.

Coach: Okay. Why don't you start by telling me what you'd lose if you stay where you are?

Andrea: Growth. Challenge. I feel — stuck.

Coach: Right. You could also ask yourself: in five years, which decision will I regret more? Taking the risk, or not taking it?

Andrea: When you put it that way... probably not taking it.

Coach: Should I tell you what I think honestly?

Andrea: Please.

Coach: I think you should apply. But you shouldn't resign from your current position until you have a signed contract. That's non-negotiable. You also shouldn't make this decision alone — involve your family. It affects them too.

Andrea: Should I ask for a trial period before committing long-term?

Coach: Yes. Why don't you propose a 90-day probation where both sides can evaluate the fit? It reduces the risk for you and shows them you're serious about commitment.

Andrea: That's really helpful. Should I call them today or wait until Monday?

Coach: I don't think you should call them on a Friday afternoon. It looks rushed. Why don't you send a brief email today saying you're very interested and will call Monday morning? That's professional and gives you the weekend to think.

Andrea: Perfect. I know what to do now. Thank you.

§ Speaking Practice
Ask · Advise · Suggest — The Full Cycle
10–12 MIN
🗣️
Speaking Practice
Full advice cycle — ask + give + suggest in one conversation
10–12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Students use ALL FOUR structures in their answers. Partner marks: ✓ "Should I/we" correctly formed ✓ "Shouldn't + reason" ✓ "Why don't you" (not "Why you don't") ✓ "I think/don't think you should." Switch after Q3. Teacher: don't interrupt — mark silently, correct after each Q.
Question 1 — Ask for Advice
Think of a real dilemma you have right now at work or in your personal life. Tell your partner the situation and ask: "Should I…?" Partner responds with "I think you should…" or "I don't think you should… because…"
💡 The person asking should ask at least 2 follow-up questions: "But what if…? / Should I also…? / Why don't we…?"
Question 2 — Gentle Suggestion Practice
Your partner describes a work problem (difficult colleague, too much work, unclear deadline). You can ONLY use "Why don't you/we…?" to suggest solutions. No "You should" allowed. Give 3 different suggestions using this structure only.
💡 This is harder than it sounds — it forces you to think about the collaborative register. Notice how it feels different to "you should."
Question 3 — The Two Sides
Partner A: describe a career decision (job change, promotion, extra project, training course). Partner B: give advice using BOTH "I think you should…" AND "I don't think you should…" — one for each side of the decision. Then switch.
💡 Model: "I think you should take the promotion because it's a great opportunity. But I don't think you should accept without discussing the salary increase first."
Question 4 — Shouldn't + Reason
Think of 3 pieces of advice you would give a new employee at your company — things they absolutely shouldn't do. Give each piece of advice with a reason: "You shouldn't… because…" Partner checks: is the reason specific and realistic?
💡 Example: "You shouldn't send informal messages to senior managers because it can seem disrespectful in this culture."
Question 5 — Team Decision
Your team needs to decide: should you ask for a budget extension, or deliver the project as planned (with lower quality)? Have a 2-minute team conversation using "Should we…?" questions and "Why don't we…?" suggestions. Both options must be discussed.
💡 Both people ask AND give advice. Target: at least 6 sentences total. No monologues — real back-and-forth conversation.
Question 6 — 60-Second Coach ⭐ Challenge
You are a career coach. Your client has 60 seconds to describe their problem. Then you have 90 seconds to give professional advice using at least 3 of the 4 structures: Should I/we? · Shouldn't + reason · Why don't you? · I think / I don't think you should. No notes.
💡 Model opening: "Okay, I understand your situation. Here's my advice. I think you should… You shouldn't… because… And why don't you also try…?"
🎯 This week at work
3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
PHRASE 1
"Should I [action], or would you recommend something different?"
Use this the next time you want to ask for advice professionally. It combines "Should I" with an open invitation for alternatives — which is exactly how a collaborative professional sounds. Use it in your next meeting or email with your manager.
PHRASE 2
"Why don't we [suggestion] before deciding?"
The most collaborative sentence in English. Use it in meetings when you want to slow down a decision and propose a better process. It's indirect, respectful, and signals leadership thinking. Replaces "I think we should" when the relationship matters.
PHRASE 3
"I think you should [X], but I don't think you should [Y] yet."
The complete advice structure — both sides in one sentence. Use it when someone asks for your opinion and you want to give nuanced, professional input. This is what a mentor or trusted colleague sounds like in English. Use it this week when someone asks what you think.

Wrap-Up
Key Points · Checklist · What Comes Next
5–8 MIN
01
Questions with should. "Should I/we + base verb?" — NEVER "Should I to do" or "Should I doing." The question asks for advice or decision support. Inversion: Should + subject + verb.
02
"Why don't you/we" = collaborative. This is indirect advice — softer than "you should." The word order is fixed: Why don't + subject + base verb. Never "Why you don't."
03
I think / I don't think you should. The negative is in "think" — not in "should." "I don't think you should go" = correct. "I think you shouldn't go" also acceptable. Never both.
