§2
Speaking Topic — Give Me Directions
From where you are → where they need to go · Inside and outside the building
⏱ 8 MIN
S3 Theme — Navigation
Getting people where they need to go
In the Bajío manufacturing corridor — Celaya, Silao, Salamanca — international visitors arrive daily at plant receptions. Being able to say "Go straight, turn left, the office is at the end of the corridor" is not optional at A1.1. It's Day 1 corporate English.
Round 1 — 2 min
Partner A is a visitor at your company's reception. Partner B is the receptionist. Visitor asks: "Excuse me, where is the HR office?" Give real directions from your reception to a real space in your building.
💡 Use: "Go straight… turn left/right… it's on the [left/right]… at the end of the corridor."
Round 2 — 3 min
Outside the building now. Partner A asks: "Is there a bank/pharmacy/restaurant near here?" Partner B gives simple street directions to a real place near your office. Switch after 90 seconds.
💡 Use: "There is a [place] near here. Go straight… turn [left/right]… it's on the [left/right], opposite the…"
Round 3 — 3 min
Teams call scenario: your colleague from another city is coming to your plant tomorrow. They ask: "How do I get from the main gate to your office?" Give step-by-step directions over the "call."
💡 Use: "When you arrive at the gate, go straight… take the elevator to floor [X]… turn right… my office is next to / between / in front of…"

Teacher model: "When you arrive at reception, go straight along the corridor. Take the elevator on the left — it's next to the security desk. Go to the second floor. Turn right when you exit the elevator. My office is the third door on the left, between the meeting room and the break room. There is a sign on the door."

📌 Key structures used: go straight / take the elevator / turn right / it's the third door on the left / between / next to / there is

GT Original — Direction Traps for Mexican Learners
  • 🔊 "straight" — the GH is silent: /streɪt/ — NOT "es-traight". Students add a vowel at the start (Spanish phonology).
  • ⚠️ "opposite" ≠ "in front of" exactly — "opposite" implies across a street or corridor; "in front of" can be the same side. Teach with gestures.
  • 🇲🇽 "cuadra" = "block" in English — not a 1:1 translation but close enough for A1.1 directions. "Two blocks from here" is natural AmE.
  • 📱 Real use: WhatsApp "I'm at the main entrance — coming up now!" — combine vocab with digital English from S1.

§4
Colloquial — Real Direction Phrases
What people actually say when giving directions in corporate English
⏱ 5 MIN
"You can't miss it."
No te puedes perder.
Use after giving directions to reassure someone it's easy to find. "Turn left, go straight — you can't miss it, there's a big sign." Extremely common in North American English.
"It's just down the hall."
Está al fondo del pasillo / está cerca en el pasillo.
Casual office phrase meaning "not far, just along this corridor." More natural than "at the end of the corridor" when the distance is short. Common in informal office conversations.
"I'll take you there."
Te llevo / Te acompaño.
Offer to physically walk someone to the destination. Much warmer than just pointing. Great for first-time visitors. Note: "take" here = accompany, not carry. Very natural in Mexican corporate culture.
"Excuse me, do you know where [X] is?"
Disculpa, ¿sabes dónde está [X]?
The polite way to ask for directions from a stranger or colleague you don't know well. "Excuse me" is essential before any question to a stranger in English — skipping it sounds abrupt.
📌 Drill Note

Drill "You can't miss it" + "It's just down the hall" as a pair: teacher points at the classroom door and says "Turn left. Go straight. The cafeteria is just down the hall — you can't miss it." Students repeat, then produce their own 2-sentence version for a real space in their building. 90 seconds total.

Grammar — Giving Directions + There is/are + Any
Direction imperatives · There is/are in context · Any in questions and negatives · Review of prepositions
⏱ 15–18 MIN
🚨 Real Situation
Your plant manager is on a call with a Japanese client visiting tomorrow. The client asks: "Is there a hotel near your plant? And how do I get from the hotel to your office?"

You need to answer both: (1) confirm there IS a hotel and where it is, (2) give step-by-step directions from the hotel to your plant.
❓ You need "there is/are" to answer the first question — and direction imperatives for the second. Today you get both.
✅ The Tools: Directions + There is/are

"Yes, there is a hotel — it's on Avenida Tecnológico, opposite the park.
Go straight along the main road. Turn left at the traffic lights. Take the first right. The plant entrance is on the left — there is a big sign. There are security guards at the gate."

⚠️ Top direction errors — correct immediately:
✗ "Go to the left" → ✅ Turn left (we say "turn", not "go to")
✗ "Are there some restaurants?" → ✅ Are there any restaurants? (any in questions, not some)
✗ "There aren't some chairs" → ✅ There aren't any chairs (any in negatives too)
✗ "The straight" → ✅ Go straight — "straight" is an adverb here, not a noun

4. To give a direction: "_____ left at the elevator and the HR office is on your right."

Listening — The Lost Visitor
Ana helps a visiting supplier find the conference room · Directions + there is/are throughout
⏱ 10–12 MIN
The Lost Visitor — Solaris Manufacturing, Celaya
A1.1 · M4 · S3A · Voice: Sarah (American) + James (American) · Speed: 0.78
Visitor: Excuse me. I'm looking for the conference room. Is it on this floor?

Ana: Hi! Welcome. Yes, there is a conference room on this floor. Are you here for the ten o'clock meeting with Mr. Ochoa?

Visitor: Yes, that's right. I'm a bit early.

Ana: No problem. OK, so from here, go straight along this corridor. Don't turn — just keep going straight. At the end of the corridor, you'll see the elevator on your left.

Visitor: At the end — elevator on the left. Got it.

Ana: The conference room is just before the elevator — it's the last door on the right. There is a sign on the door — it says "Conference Room B."

Visitor: Last door on the right. And is there a restroom near there?

Ana: Yes! There are restrooms on every floor. From the conference room, turn left and go past the elevator. The restrooms are at the end, between the IT room and the break room.

Visitor: Perfect. And one more thing — is there a café or coffee machine anywhere? I didn't have breakfast.

Ana: There isn't a café on this floor, but there is a coffee machine in the break room, next to the restrooms I just mentioned. It's free for visitors. Help yourself!

Visitor: That's great. Thank you so much, Ana.

Ana: You're welcome! I'll let Mr. Ochoa know you've arrived. Enjoy your coffee — you can't miss it!

Question 1 — Reception Role-Play
Partner A: visitor arriving at your company for the first time. Ask for directions to: the meeting room, the restroom, and the cafeteria. Partner B: receptionist — give complete directions to all three. Switch.
💡 Every direction must include: a movement verb (go/turn/take) + a location (on the left/right, at the end, next to)
Question 2 — Outside: Is there a…?
Partner A asks: "Is there a [bank / pharmacy / restaurant / supermarket] near your office?" Partner B answers with "Yes, there is — go straight… turn…" OR "No, there isn't, but there is a [alternative]." Use real places near your actual workplace.
💡 Include "any" in at least one question: "Are there any restaurants nearby?"
Question 3 — Draw & Describe
Draw a quick map of your company building or floor on paper (30 seconds). Then describe it to your partner in English — where the spaces are, how to get from reception to your desk. Partner draws what you say and compares.
💡 Use ALL of: there is/are · prepositions (next to/in front of/between) · direction words (turn/go/take)
Question 4 — Teams Call
Simulate a Teams call. Partner A is driving to your plant from the hotel. They call and ask: "I'm at the traffic lights on [street] — where do I go?" Give turn-by-turn directions to the main entrance. Include: is there parking? Which entrance to use?
💡 "There is parking on the left when you enter. Go straight to the security gate. Turn right — the reception is in front of you."
Question 5 — Full Building Tour
Give your partner a complete spoken tour of your real or imaginary company building. Start at reception. Visit 4 spaces. End at your desk. Use: there is/are for each space, directions between spaces, and prepositions to locate objects in each room.
💡 Challenge: use "You can't miss it" and "It's just down the hall" at least once each.
Question 6 — 60s Visitor Brief ⭐
In 60 seconds: welcome an imaginary international visitor to your company. Tell them: where things are (3 spaces), how to find the meeting room, and where to get coffee. Use there is/are + directions + prepositions. No notes. Partner counts direction phrases.
💡 "Welcome! There is a reception here. To get to the meeting room, go straight… The coffee machine is next to…"

3 Phrases You Can Use at Work This Week
Starting tomorrow — use these in real situations with real people
Phrase 1
"Go straight, turn left — you can't miss it."
The complete mini-direction in one sentence. Use this next time a visitor or new colleague asks where something is. End with "you can't miss it" to reassure them. Simple, natural, professional.
Phrase 2
"There is a [place] — it's just down the hall, on the left."
Combines "there is" + direction in one smooth sentence. Use when someone asks if something exists AND where it is. This is how fluent speakers answer both questions at once — no need for two separate sentences.
Phrase 3
"Are there any [places] near here? — Yes, there is one just around the corner."
Practice the full question-answer cycle. "Any" in the question, "there is" in the answer. Use with international colleagues who are new to Celaya. It's the most complete A1.1 location exchange you can produce.

⏱ 5 MIN
01
Direction imperatives. Go straight · Turn left/right · Take the elevator · It's on the left/right. These are commands — no subject, no auxiliary. "Turn left" not "You should turn left."
02
"Any" in questions and negatives. "Are there any restaurants?" / "There aren't any spaces." Any = we're not sure if it exists. Some = we know it exists. Never "Are there some…?"
03
Combine them all. "There is a meeting room — go straight, turn left, it's next to the elevator. You can't miss it." That's S1 + S2 + S3 in one sentence. That's real English.
