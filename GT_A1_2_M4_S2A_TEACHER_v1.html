Warm-Up — Talking to HR About Your Health
Bridge from S1 · Surface HR vocabulary · Introduce allergies + imperatives
⏱ 5–8 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note — Pedagogical
S2 deepens the HR communication context. Students already have: have/has + symptoms + possessive pronouns. Today they add: imperatives for health advice and the allergy vocabulary needed for real HR forms. The warm-up bridges S1 to S2 by asking students to recall phrases they earned in the previous session.
Question 1 — Review S1
From last session: how do you tell your manager you are sick? Say the full sentence — symptoms, what you will send, and who to contact.
💡 Target: "I have a [symptom]. I will send my certificate to HR."
Question 2 — HR Scenario
HR sends you a form: "Are you allergic to anything?" What do you write? Do you know the word "allergic" in English? Have you filled in a health form at work in English?
💡 New today: allergic / allergy / I am allergic to…
Question 3 — Imperatives
Your colleague has a terrible headache. Give them short, direct advice. Do not use "you should" — just give the command directly. How does it sound different?
💡 "Take a painkiller!" vs "You should take a painkiller." — same advice, different tone
Question 4 — Possessive Review
HR asks: "Whose absence form is this?" Point to your own form and say the correct sentence. Now point to a colleague's. What changes?
💡 "It's mine." / "It's his." / "It's hers." — which pronoun matches which person?
⚡ Quick Win — 20 seconds
Say this right now: "I'm not feeling well. I am allergic to penicillin. Please send my form to HR."
You just used 3 key A1.2 M4 structures in one go. Today we master all three.
§2
Speaking Topic — HR Communication & Workplace Wellness
Real HR scenarios · Imperatives vs should · Absence forms · Return to work
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Teacher Note
Pairs. Show model answer only AFTER both partners attempt. Focus on whether students use imperatives (Take / Rest / Drink) vs should. Note any student who says "Take to" or "Don't to drink" — imperative errors are common at A1.2.
Round 1 — HR Intake Form
HR asks you to complete a health form before your first day. It asks: "Do you have any allergies?" and "Do you have any health conditions that affect your work?" What do you write?
💡 Use: "I am allergic to… / I have [condition]. It affects my work because…"
Round 2 — Advice with Imperatives
You are the team leader. Three colleagues report health problems: (A) terrible back pain, (B) eye strain after 6 hours on screen, (C) mild fever and sore throat. Give each person 2 direct instructions using imperatives — not "should."
💡 "Take a painkiller and rest. / Look away from the screen. Blink often. / Go home now. Don't come back until your fever is gone."
Round 3 — Return to Work
You were sick for 2 days. Today is your first day back. HR calls you: "How are you feeling? Did you send your certificate? Do you have any medical restrictions?" How do you answer all three?
💡 "I am feeling much better. Yes, I sent my certificate. I do not have any restrictions / I should not lift heavy things this week."
Round 2 — Imperatives for each colleague:
A (back pain): "Take a painkiller. Don't sit in the same position for more than 30 minutes."
B (eye strain): "Look away from your screen every 20 minutes. Blink often."
C (fever): "Go home now. Don't come in tomorrow if you still have a fever."

§4
Colloquial — Imperative Health Advice Phrases
How people really give health advice at work · Direct · Natural · Corporate-ready
⏱ 5 MIN
"Take a break."
Simple imperative — direct advice to stop working and rest. Natural in all levels of business English.
"Your eyes look really tired. Take a break — go get a coffee."
🕐 When: a colleague is overworking or showing signs of eye strain or stress
"Drink lots of water."
The most universal health imperative. Works for headaches, fever, sore throat — almost any symptom.
"You have a fever? Drink lots of water and stay in bed."
🕐 When: someone reports any illness — this advice is always appropriate
"See a doctor."
Direct imperative for serious symptoms. More urgent than "You should see a doctor." No article — "a doctor" means any doctor.
"You have had that pain for three days? See a doctor today."
🕐 When: symptoms are serious, repeated, or lasting more than 2–3 days
"Rest and get better."
Warm closing imperative — combines advice (rest) with a wish (get better). Very common in WhatsApp messages to sick colleagues.
"Thanks for letting me know. Rest and get better — see you when you are back."
🕐 When: closing a sick day message or call with a colleague or team member
"Don't push too hard today."
Negative imperative — caring advice to an employee on their first day back after illness. Professional and warm.
"Welcome back. Don't push too hard today. Take it easy and ask for help if you need it."
🕐 When: welcoming a colleague back after sick leave or a difficult week
💡 Drill Note
Drill imperatives vs should as a contrast pair: teacher says should version, student gives imperative version. "You should drink water" → "Drink water!" Negative imperatives: "Don't + base verb" — never "Don't to" or "Doesn't."

Problem → Tool · Allergy Alert Card Builder (new visual) · 2 activities

You want to write: "Soy alérgico a la penicilina. Por favor, informa a mi supervisor. No me des penicilina."
What structures do you need to complete this form correctly in English?
✅ The Tools
Tool 1 — Allergy: I am allergic to [noun] → "I am allergic to penicillin."
Tool 2 — Imperative (aff): [Base verb] + object → "Inform my supervisor."
Tool 3 — Imperative (neg): Don't + base verb → "Don't give me penicillin."

Listening — Monica's Return-to-Work Meeting with HR
Allergies · Imperatives · Possessive pronouns throughout · Real HR scenario
⏱ 8 MIN
⚡ Listening Note
Two passes: (1) listen for the main purpose of the meeting, (2) listen for the possessive pronouns — students write down every time they hear mine/yours/ours/his. This trains attention to form in natural speech. Speed 0.80 — pauses kept natural.
Monica's Return-to-Work Meeting — HR Coordinator Laura
A1.2 · M4 · S2A · Voice: Sofia (Mx English) · Speed: 0.80
[HR Coordinator Laura]: Good morning, Monica. Welcome back. Please sit down. How are you feeling today?

[Monica]: Good morning, Laura. Thank you. I am feeling much better. My throat is fine now and my temperature is normal.

[Laura]: That is great to hear. I have your absence form here. Is this yours?

[Monica]: Yes, it is mine. I filled it in this morning.

[Laura]: Perfect. And I have the medical certificate too. It says here you are allergic to penicillin. Is that correct?

[Monica]: Yes, that is correct. I am allergic to penicillin and also to dust. My eyes react badly to dust — they get very red and itchy.

[Laura]: Thank you. We need this information for your employee file. The file is ours — HR keeps it confidential. Now, your doctor says you should rest your voice this week. Don't give long presentations. Take short breaks every hour. And drink a lot of water.

[Monica]: I understand. I have a presentation on Thursday — is that a problem?

[Laura]: Talk to your manager. The decision is his. But take care of yourself first. Don't push too hard this week.

[Monica]: Thank you, Laura. I appreciate it.

[Laura]: Of course. Welcome back, Monica. Rest and get better completely.

§SP
Speaking Practice — HR Conversations and Health Advice
Imperatives + possessive pronouns + allergies in real production
⏱ 10–12 MIN
💬 How to run this: Monitor for: (1) "Don't to" — imperative negative errors, (2) "it's my" instead of "it's mine", (3) missing article in "allergic to penicillin." Correct after each question, not during. Switch roles at Q3.
Question 1 — Your HR Health Profile
Complete your HR health profile out loud: "I am allergic to ___." — use a real allergy or invent one. Then: "My [body part] is affected when I ___." Then give one imperative instruction for the company.
💡 If you have no allergies: "I am not allergic to anything. But my back hurts if I sit without breaks. Give me an ergonomic chair."
Question 2 — Manager Gives Instructions
You are the manager. Three team members report health problems: (A) sharp wrist pain from typing, (B) a dust allergy that affects eyes, (C) mild fever. Give 2 imperative instructions for each — one affirmative, one negative.
💡 A: "Use a wrist support. Don't type without breaks." B: "Use an air purifier. Don't sit near the window." C: "Go home now. Don't come back until your fever is gone."
Question 3 — HR Return-to-Work Role Play
Partner A is the HR coordinator. Partner B is Monica returning after sick leave. HR asks: "Is this form yours? Are you allergic to anything? What should we know?" Monica answers using today's structures. Then switch roles.
💡 HR must use at least 2 possessive pronoun questions. Monica must answer with "Yes, it's mine" and "I am allergic to…"
Question 4 — Identify the Owner
Teacher places 4 imaginary items on the desk: form, certificate, folder, laptop. Students point and say who they belong to. Use all 6 possessive pronouns: mine / yours / his / hers / ours / theirs. Add one context sentence each.
💡 "That is hers — she submitted it yesterday." / "These are ours — the HR team prepared them."
Question 5 — Workplace Wellness Policy
Your company wants to create a Workplace Wellness Policy. Give 4 imperative rules — 2 affirmative, 2 negative. Include at least one about allergies and one about workstation ergonomics.
💡 "Take a break every 45 minutes. / Don't eat near computers. / Report all allergies to HR. / Don't ignore back pain — see a doctor."
Question 6 — 60-Second HR Update Challenge ⭐
You are back at work after 3 sick days. Give HR your complete return update: how you feel, your allergies, what you can and cannot do this week, and whose documents they have. Use all 4 grammar tools from today. No notes.
💡 "I am feeling better. I am allergic to… My [body part] is still… Don't ask me to… Those forms are mine."

3 Phrases You Can Use Starting Tomorrow
HR English · Immediate real-world application
PHRASE 1
"I am allergic to [noun]. Please inform my supervisor."
Use this on any HR health form. This structure is standard in English-language companies globally. Replace with your real allergen. The imperative "inform" sounds professional and direct.
PHRASE 2
"Take a break. Don't push too hard today."
Two imperatives — one positive, one negative. Use when a colleague looks tired or unwell. Sounds natural, caring, and completely professional. This is how English-speaking managers talk to their teams.
PHRASE 3
"That form is mine. / The decision is his."
Possessive pronouns make you sound fluent and professional — not "it's my form" but "it's mine." Use in any HR situation, meeting, or WhatsApp group where document or decision ownership comes up.
§7
Wrap-Up — Session Checklist
3 key takeaways · I can now checklist · Preview S2B
⏱ 5–8 MIN
01
Imperatives: no "to." "Take a painkiller." Never "Take to a painkiller." "Don't come in." Never "Don't to come in." The base verb follows directly — always.
02
Allergies: no article. "I am allergic to penicillin." — no "the." Substances and materials do not use an article after "allergic to." This is the most common error on real HR forms.
03
Possessive pronouns standalone. "Whose is this?" — "It's mine / his / hers / ours / theirs." Never "It's my." The pronoun replaces the noun completely — it stands alone.
✅ I Can Now… — Click each one when confident
I can give health advice using imperatives: "Take a painkiller. / Don't come in today."
I know that "Don't to" is wrong — the correct form is always "Don't + base verb."
I can say "I am allergic to [noun]" correctly — without an article after "to"
I can use all 6 possessive pronouns: mine / yours / his / hers / ours / theirs
I can handle a return-to-work HR conversation in English — symptoms, allergies, documents
